Namibian regulator rejects Starlink again

SpaceX's low-Earth orbit (LEO) satellite company, Starlink, has once again been denied an operating license in Namibia.

Matshepo Sehloho,Associate Editor,Connecting Africa

3 Min ReadElon Musk's Starlink has received another rejection from Namibia. (Source: Timothy Sithole)

The Communications Regulatory Authority of Namibia (CRAN) has rejected appeals for SpaceX's low-Earth orbit (LEO) satellite company, Starlink, to obtain a telecommunications service license in the Southern African nation.

The Namibian regulator first rejected Starlink's application for both a Class Comprehensive Telecommunications Service License and a Spectrum License for fixed satellite services in March 2026.

"Following our decision of March 2026 to decline Starlink's license application, we received 624 requests for reconsideration from members of the public. Every submission received was reviewed and assessed in accordance with the Communications Act as well as applicable regulations," CRAN CEO Emilia Nghikembua explained in a media briefing this week.

She said 622 of the requests didn't meet the legal requirements necessary to trigger a valid reconsideration process.

"In simple terms, they didn't provide the information required by the law for the Authority to lawfully revisit and reassess its decision," she continued.

"The Authority, therefore, decided to decline all these 622 requests; secondly, there were two requests out of the 624 requests that satisfied the procedural threshold and were therefore considered on their merits," she added.

She said after a comprehensive assessment, the Authority found that neither request presented new evidence nor demonstrated a material error in the original decision.

Nghikembua said CRAN therefore upheld and reaffirmed its March decision to decline Starlink's license application.

CRAN CEO Emilia Nghikembua confirmed that the regulator has upheld its previous decision to decline Starlink's license application. (Source: CRAN)

According to the CRAN CEO, the issue has never been whether satellite technology is beneficial.

She said the regulator acknowledges that LEO satellite can play a meaningful role in advancing the country's connectivity goals to underserved areas.

"The issue before the Authority, is whether the application as submitted complied with the requirements of the Communications Act. For completeness, we reiterate that for an applicant to receive a license there are six criteria that they must meet," she explained.

"For Starlink, there were three criteria they didn't meet, which include ownership and control, the compliance history of the applicant, as well as national security and public safety considerations," she added.

CRAN's reasons for rejecting Starlink's appeal

Nghikembua told the media that CRAN also received an application by Starlink Internet Service Namibia itself on June 8, 2026.

"After careful consideration, the Authority found that the request has been lodged outside the statutory period prescribed by law. Put more clearly, the law requires that the application must be submitted 30 days from the date of the decision and in this case the date of the decision was March 23, 2026, which means that the 30 days to submit the application lapsed on April 22, 2026," she said.

Nghikembua further stated that neither the Communications Act nor the applicable regulations provide CRAN with the authority to condone late applications for reconsiderations.

"Consequently, the Authority was legally precluded from considering the merits of the application and that application, too, has been dismissed," Nghikembua continued.

She said that there was also a petition signed by 5,500 citizens, which was received on June 17, 2026, after the statutory deadline for reconsideration had expired.

Starlink is still trying to get an operating license in South Africa but is also battling ownership rules that apply to Namibia's neighbor.

Starlink is set to launch its services in Côte d'Ivoire in July 2026 after receiving a 12-month provisional license.

Across Africa its services are available in Lesotho, Zimbabwe, Zambia, Eswatini, Malawi, Mozambique, Madagascar, Botswana, Ghana, Nigeria, Niger, Liberia, Benin, Guinea-Bissau, Sierra Leone, Chad, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Senegal, Somalia, Central African Republic, Kenya, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Burundi as well as the island nations of Cape Verde and São Tomé and Príncipe.
