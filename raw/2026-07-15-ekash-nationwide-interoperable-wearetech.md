Rwanda Launches eKash to Enable Nationwide Instant Interoperable Payments

Rwanda launched interoperable real-time person-to-person (P2P) transfers through its national eKash payment infrastructure on July 14.

The platform enables instant transfers across banks and mobile money providers while capping transaction fees at 20 Rwandan francs and transaction values at 10 million Rwandan francs (about $7,000).

Rwanda reported that 96% of adults had access to formal financial services in 2024, while 68.5% owned mobile money wallets, underscoring the country's push toward a cash-lite economy.

The National Bank of Rwanda (BNR) announced on Monday, July 13, that the service would become operational on July 14. The central bank described the rollout as another milestone in strengthening the country's digital payments ecosystem.

Unlike consumer-facing payment applications, eKash operates as a national payments infrastructure. The platform connects banks and electronic money institutions through a single network. Customers will continue using their existing bank accounts or mobile money wallets, but they can now make instant transfers across providers, including bank-to-bank, wallet-to-wallet and bank-to-mobile money transactions.

The National Bank of Rwanda has capped transaction values at 10 million Rwandan francs (about $7,000) and maximum transfer fees at 20 Rwandan francs. However, the regulator allows financial institutions to charge lower fees or waive them altogether. The central bank will also oversee system availability, transaction performance, consumer protection and complaint resolution.

Culmination of a National Digital Payments Strategy

The eKash rollout marks the latest phase of Rwanda's long-term strategy to build an open and interoperable national payments infrastructure.

The country developed the platform with support from partners including AfricaNenda. The system relies on an open-source architecture, which allows Rwanda to retain control over critical payment infrastructure while reducing its dependence on foreign technology providers.

The launch comes as Rwanda reports one of Africa's highest levels of financial inclusion. According to the National Bank of Rwanda, 96% of adults had access to formal financial services in 2024, compared with 48% in 2008, largely because of the rapid expansion of mobile money.

Mobile wallets have become the country's primary gateway to financial services. The central bank said 68.5% of adults now own a mobile money wallet, while only 5% hold a traditional bank account.

Removing the Remaining Barriers Between Payment Networks

Despite strong growth in digital financial services, transactions have remained fragmented across banks and mobile money operators. As a result, users have often needed multiple platforms to transfer funds.

The government expects eKash to eliminate those barriers by enabling real-time interoperability across all participating financial institutions. The platform should reduce transaction friction, simplify everyday payments and increase competition among financial service providers.

The authorities also view the initiative as more than a money transfer system. They expect eKash to support Rwanda's transition toward a less cash-dependent economy, improve access to digital financial services, strengthen confidence in electronic payments and provide the foundation for new digital financial and public services.
