# Digital Public Infrastructure at the G20: tensions, commitments and continuities | ODI: Think change

> ## Excerpt
> This essay examines the role the G20 can play in global technology governance and cooperation, especially analysing the way in which digital public infrastructure (DPI) was threaded through India, Brazil and South Africa presidencies.

---
However, the forum's informal, consensus-driven structure, designed for agility, also makes sustained, substantive progress structurally difficult. This tension is most visible in the realm of digital and data governance. The 2026 presidency returns to the United States, during a time of heightened geopolitical tension is an apposite time to understand the evolution and efficacy of the forum.

This essay examines the role the G20 can play in global technology governance and cooperation, especially analysing the way in which digital public infrastructure (DPI) was threaded through India, Brazil and South Africa presidencies. The essay concludes with suggested pathways for greater coherence and collaboration through the digital agenda in going forward.

## **The** e**volution** **of** **the ‘DPI’ agenda at** **the G20**

While a distinct digital agenda is relatively new to the G20, it has increasingly become a central feature of the forum’s discussions. The Japanese presidency proposed the influential [Data Free Flow with Trust (DFFT)](https://www.csis.org/analysis/operationalizing-data-free-flow-trust-dfft) , in 2019, to combine privacy and security of personal data with enhanced cross border data flows. Notably, in the 2019 Osaka declaration, DFFT was signed off by most countries except [India](https://www.livemint.com/news/india/india-says-no-to-free-flow-of-digital-data-at-g20-meeting-11600787726265.html), Indonesia and South Africa, who cited the need to develop its own data governance frameworks and raising questions of sovereignty, especially as imagined through data localisation. Similarly, Indonesia’s presidency as part of the post-COVID global recovery with a focus on [digital financial inclusion.](https://www.g20.in/en/docs/2022/G20%20Bali%20Leaders%27%20Declaration,%2015-16%20November%202022.pdf)

However, it was during India’s presidency in 2023 that digital issues gained serious traction on the G20's agenda. For India, the flagship concept was “Digital Public Infrastructure”. The Indian presidency had pitched DPI as a framework for thinking about foundational, interoperable and open digital infrastructure to drive inclusive development, modelled on India’s experiences in developing digital identity (Aadhaar) and digital payments (unified payments interface, UPI). India’s G20 presidency, occurring soon after the Ukraine war and the COVID-19 pandemic, highlighted the need for digital sovereignty and connectivity through open platforms that allow governments and the private sector to deliver goods and services, especially for the Global South.

India’s presidency anchored DPI as an idea around which the G20 could make progress on digitalisation, leading to agreement on a [definition of DPI](https://carnegieendowment.org/research/2023/09/decoding-the-g20-consensus-on-digital-public-infrastructure-a-key-outcome-of-indias-presidency). Here, DPI, as a set of shared, interoperable and secure digital systems built on open standards and specifications to provide equitable access to services at societal scale, was elevated as a priority for global economic cooperation. Along with principles, more concrete outputs such as knowledge products to assist countries to adopt DPI like the [LMIC playbook](https://www.undp.org/sites/g/files/zskgke326/files/2023-08/undp-the-dpi-approach-a-playbook.pdf) were also released, along with a [DPI repository](https://www.dpi.global/) to guide countries towards their own DPI instantiations. India also declared the [One Future Alliance](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleseDetailm.aspx?PRID=1931670&reg=3&lang=2), a voluntary initiative to shape and fund DPI deployment across the globe. India’s G20 presidency and its outcomes provided legacy and momentum to India’s ambition to institutionalise DPI as a global approach to technologically driven development.

India’s DPI remained on the agenda for the Brazilian and South African presidencies, carried through as part of the troika. The troika overlapped with other intergovernmental organising groups - [BRICS](https://www.brics2026.gov.in/) and [IBSA](https://www.ibsa-trilateral.org/), thus adding more opportunities to coordinate their agendas. There was an effort amongst the three presidencies to sustain a Global South Agenda: digital transformation - and DPI - became part of this effort, retained across the presidencies’ distinct agendas. For example, in 2024, [Brazil](https://g7g20-documents.org/database/document/2024-g20-brazil-sherpa-track-digital-economy-ministers-ministers-language-g20-dewg-maceio-ministerial-declaration) built on India’s commitments, and acknowledged the importance of DPI in improving the responsiveness, effectiveness and transparency of the public sector in the digital era. In Brazil there was a [joint communique](https://www.mea.gov.in/bilateral-documents.htm?dtl/38551/Declaration+on+Digital+Public+Infrastructure+AI+and+Data+for+Governance++Joint+Communiqu233+by+the+G20+Troika+India+Brazil+and+South+Africa+endorsed+by+several+G20+countries+guest+countries+and+international+organizations) between India, Brazil and South Africa on DPI, although this was quite short and unspecific. Following this, [South Africa](https://www.g20.utoronto.ca/2025/250929-digital.html) continued in the same vein, as the Digital Economy Working Group (DEWG) recognised DPI as a key enabler of digital transformations, and built a public value measurement framework for DPI.

## **Shared commitments** **or** i**nstitutional** i**ncoherence?**

On face value, these efforts across the three presidencies indicates continuity of agenda. However, continuity of nomenclature is not the same as continuity of conviction. Not only did neither the Brazil nor South African presidency bring to DPI the same institutional energy and support as India, but also each country approached the notion of DPI differently - Brazil from the idea of the _public_ sector, and South Africa from the lens of _public value._

DPI has been [intrinsically linked to India,](https://openfuture.eu/publication/digital-public-infrastructure-at-a-turning-point/) its government and its priorities, and in the past was referred to as the “India Stack” (it was renamed to enhance global adoption). Despite the definitional ambiguity of DPI, it is often perceived as an idea emerging from India that was fit into the presidencies of Brazil and South Africa. As part of the G20 DPI amplification, many of the concerns about India’s DPI experience such as [exclusion](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3971724) or [unregulated competition](https://www.medianama.com/2025/02/223-npci-competition-regulation/) or [potential for surveillance](https://techpolicy.press/public-infrastructure-and-private-surveillance-in-indias-aadhaar-system) have been papered over, adding to scepticism about what DPI might achieve.

Further, DPI has been adopted enthusiastically by a range of multilateral and philanthropic organisations, such as the [Gates Foundation](https://www.gatesfoundation.org/our-work/programs/global-growth-and-opportunity/digital-public-infrastructure), [UNDP](https://www.undp.org/digital/digital-public-infrastructure) and [World Bank](https://id4d.worldbank.org/), all of which have invested deeply in the DPI agenda beckons the questions on who is driving the DPI agenda from the India presidency onwards. Further, the DPI narrative props up the agenda as a counter point to the hegemony of US Big Tech on digital infrastructure, yet even India’s experience of DPI has not been able to escape US firms.

This narrative fails to acknowledge how in India the payments platform has been monopolised by [Google and Walmart,](https://www.hindustantimes.com/opinion/break-the-duopoly-in-the-upi-payments-ecosystem-101714059757257.html) and how the propelling of the India DPI approach has been facilitated by [tech companies](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/technology/tech-news/google-creates-dpi-in-a-box-what-is-this-digital-india-toolkit-for-the-world-and-more/articleshow/113915487.cms). These partnerships actively challenge the promise of digital independence and sovereignty - especially for Global South countries - made by the DPI agenda within the G20.

The Global South G20 Presidencies have indicated the potential to confront some of these challenges. The South Africa G20 advocated for the concept to be limited to a set of [digital systems that need to be tailored to context.](https://www.g20.utoronto.ca/2025/250929-digital.html) This potentially emerged under South Africa as several African countries have [pushed back](https://citizenshiprightsafrica.org/fr/kenyas-controversial-digital-id-scheme-faces-push-back/) [against](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/data-and-policy/article/mistrust-of-government-within-authoritarian-states-hindering-user-acceptance-and-adoption-of-digital-ids-in-africa-the-nigerian-context/6511B7AE871C328F316912ACAD27274F) the export of India’s digital infrastructure, and its [trade-offs](https://www.centerforfinancialinclusion.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/DPI-Unspoken-Tradeoffs-Brief-September-2025.pdf) are being discussed more openly. While the semantic elasticity of DPI allows for the broad agreements between countries, it also serves to dilution and actual buy-in as implementation requires both operational specificity and resources which many countries are unwilling to invest despite the hype cycle.

These forms of resistance and hesitation towards the DPI agenda are not irrational. Sovereignty over digital infrastructure remains a real and significant issue, and one that even India has prioritised given that it sat out the DFFT discussion. Foundational systems – for identity, payments and data exchange – are not merely technical artefacts. They encode assumptions about governance, privacy, and the relationship between the state and the citizen and private sector.

It is understandable that countries would seek control over the design and governance of such systems, and to be wary of frameworks developed elsewhere and promoted with the full weight of multilateral and philanthropic resources. Thus, while at a narrative and high level DPI was threaded through the [India, Brazil and South Africa G20 presidencies](https://odi.org/en/insights/from-india-to-brazil-and-beyond/), it remains contested by states and non-state actors. As a result, alongside the sustained discursive commitment to DPI within the G20, questions remain about ownership and agency of the discourse, and sovereignty of digital infrastructure, and the G20 commitment to DPI has not led to concrete buy-in from countries.

While the G20 has helped to cement DPI within global discourses as it was carried through India, Brazil and South Africa, challenges largely beyond the G20’s scope remain. For DPI to be a serious global development approach by states, DPI must move from a small group of enthusiastic proponents to an agenda that is evidenced and responsive to concerns rather than dismissive of them. This necessity reflects a wider trade-off for the G20: between pushing for a shared global discourse, while also acknowledging and grappling with the diverse and practical challenges of its member states.

At a multilateral level, the G20 DPI agenda reveals both the potential – and the challenges – of aligning global digital governance processes. Even as DPI has been taken up within other bodies – not the least the UN with the [DPI Safeguards](https://www.dpi-safeguards.org/) initiative, it arguably still lacks effective linkages to the priorities of parallel multilateral processes.

The [Global Digital Compact (GDC)](https://www.un.org/digital-emerging-technologies/content/digital-public-infrastructure) was being negotiated and adopted through the India presidency and was finalised in 2024, where DPI safeguards were discussed and finalised. The South Africa Digital Economy Working Group acknowledged existing multilateral and multistakeholder initiatives in the digital economy, including the WSIS+20 review, and encouraged continued international collaboration on digital governance with an inclusive, consensus-based approach consistent with the Global Digital Compact.

Yet even still the relationship between G20 digital discussions and GDC processes can be understood as largely improvised rather than institutionally anchored. There is no formal architecture for ensuring that G20 commitments inform GDC negotiations, or that GDC frameworks shape G20 workstreams.

In early 2026, with the [India AI Summit](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2230663&reg=6&lang=1), DPI again emerged as a key theme, yet even here the links between DPI and the ongoing G20 activities are not established. This disjuncture is striking; even India’s government appears to approach these events as separate. The result is a fragmented landscape – with and across countries and multilateral spaces – in which similar concepts, like DPI, are debated in multiple forums through overlapping sets of actors, but without necessarily being accompanied by meaningful cross-fertilisation that would improve the quality of outcomes in either of these multilateral spaces.

## **Ways** f**orward** **for** **the** **G20’s** d**igital** a**genda**

In the current geopolitical environment, where the collapse of the [rules based order is a consistent refrain](https://www.orfonline.org/expert-speak/the-inglorious-death-of-the-international-rules-based-order) and ‘great power’ politics appears to be resurging to take its place, there is a need and an opportunity to reimagine the G20 as a space for true collaboration: one that goes beyond vague narrative continuity for the digital agenda. Many [ideas](https://www.global-solutions-initiative.org/article/towards-reconstitution-of-the-g20/) for change to the G20 have been suggested in recent months, filling the gap that has been created with the US presidency as it takes forward a stripped down G20 process.

This has become a moment to rethink the forum from both a functional and a conceptual perspective, and strengthen its added value to the global political landscape. We propose two interconnected areas of change are needed for the G20 to play a serious and genuinely global role in digital governance: first, a more strategic and consensus-based identification of priority issues, and second, dedicated process to ensure continuity and coherence in the G20’s debates and leadership on global digital governance.

### **1\. Identifying** s**hared issues for a sustained G20** l**eadership role**

The DPI agenda has been vast and politicised. This has made it difficult for the G20 to develop and advocate a coherent agenda and leadership role around DPI: it is simultaneously too abstract and too detached from competing national priorities.

However, there are connected issues that could be a stronger foundation for a concrete, sustained G20 leadership role around global digital governance. Data governance, crudely the third ‘pillar’ of DPI, offers an alternative conceptual framing for G20 leadership around digital governance. Data governance was raised through various past presidencies, including under the banner of data justice with Brazil’s presidency, but was largely neglected in G20 priorities. It was overtaken by Presidencies’ specific agendas – like DPI – or more attractive headline issues like artificial intelligence.

Yet, data governance is critical to the current and future operations of all G20 countries; it _underpins_ the latest innovations: from digital currencies to AI. Every G20 member has a stake in how data crosses borders, its stores and processes, how it is shared between public and private actors, and how issues of individual privacy and agency over data are handled. Data governance has also been an area where governments have actively tried to develop regulation mechanisms which have quickly become outdated in the context of the fast developing AI ecosystem.

Yet, from presidency to presidency, data governance as a policy area has been scattered across different interest areas and working groups in the G20, with little direct channel to the highest-level negotiations in the Sherpa and Financial tracks. Efforts such as [OECD’s data access](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/g20-compendium-on-data-access-and-sharing-across-the-public-sector-and-with-the-private-sector-for-public-interest_df1031a4-en.html) compendium were made in Brazilian G20, but did not percolate beyond. Because of data governance’s strategic and foundational role to wider digital governance issues, it offers a strategic basis as a priority issue shared amongst member-states, and, therefore a critical area where the G20 could contribute to global governance processes in important, sustained and global ways.

### **2\. Establish institutional continuity**

Related to the question of establishing substantive coherence for the G20’s agenda, the second recommendation to the G20 is primarily around _how_ it can do so. It is not enough for member-states to recognise a shared interest in data governance, there must be the processes and resources to sustain G20 leadership across such critical, cross-cutting digital issues from year to year, solidifying and building its role.

Ideas have been put forward about how to build on existing G20 multi-stakeholder structures. Under Brazil’s G20, the idea of a separate engagement group, [Data20 (D20)](https://www.gov.br/g20/en/news/call-to-action-why-the-g20-needs-a-data20). A D20 could capitalise on the G20’s existing structural framework with its engagement groups, and bring together academics and civil society organisations to anchor different strands of data issues, and support and steward discussions about data governance across G20 working groups and engagement groups. This sort of structural reform could build internal synergies amongst G20 entities, and enable the G20 to be a more stable and continuous space for discussions over global data governance.

Yet, while a Data-20 could help to build an internal multi-stakeholder foundation on data governance, it is not enough to translate into external influence on the global stage. We propose the G20 requires a permanent cross-presidency working group for digital governance. The current rotating format is structurally incompatible with sustained progress on complex technical and regulatory questions, and a rapidly moving ecosystem. A working group that persists across presidencies, with a stable secretariat, a rolling work programme, resourcing and the mandate to engage systematically with parallel multilateral processes including the Global Digital Compact and the UN Global Dialogue would provide the continuity that individual presidencies cannot.

This is not an unprecedented innovation: the [Financial Stability Board,](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/strengthening-cooperation-for-a-changing-world-the-evolving-role-of-the-g20-in-global-economic-governance/) which grew out of G20 deliberations, provides a model for how a standing body can translate political commitments into sustained technical work and meaningful international coordination. Reinstating action-oriented communiqués with clear timelines would complement such a structure, ensuring that the working group's outputs feed into commitments that can be tracked and evaluated.

### **Conclusion**

The G20's influence in global digital governance is not guaranteed. Rather, it depends on its capacity to translate political will and turmoil into outcomes that matter and not just high-level communiques. As argued here, the limitations of the G20 DPI agenda to move beyond high-level shared narratives, advancing global digital governance efforts and gaining traction amongst member-states, reinforces the need for more structural and sustained shifts in how the G20 approaches digital governance both substantively and procedurally.

At the moment, there is a real risk that digital governance discussions at the G20 become a ritual, or worse steamrolled by those that play by power and not rules. The G20 must be more than a space for countries to make commitments without corresponding action or to wait for their presidency to publicise their own agenda.

To avoid this, we propose an alternative approach to the G20’s role in digital governance:

1) Identification of critical, cross-cutting issues, like data governance that are critical across member-states.

2) Accompanying structural reform to ensure leadership on such core issues last beyond a single presidency and are embedded into the forum in ways capable of building towards actual engagement and buy-in.

Digital governance questions that the G20 has been attempting to address - including how data flows across borders, how foundational digital infrastructure is built and governed, and how the benefits of artificial intelligence are distributed - are among the most consequential of our time. They deserve more than the current approaches within multilateral spaces, and the G20, with its diverse membership, stakeholder participation and structural potential, is well-placed for it.
