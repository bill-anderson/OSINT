Today, data has become the power fueling digital economies, and few things will shape the future of digital societies more than how we govern it.

For countries like Ethiopia, getting data governance right is not about following the latest global trend. Rather, it’s about laying the groundwork for digital sovereignty, inclusive innovation, and trustworthy public institutions, each of which are critical to fostering an effective [digital public infrastructure (DPI)](https://dial.global/good-dpi/) approach.

This year, Ethiopia is wrapping up its current Digital Transformation Strategy and on the cusp of introducing its next phase of Digital Ethiopia 2030. With preparation underway for this new wave, the country is developing its first ever national data governance strategy – a milestone that holds significance well beyond its borders.

What makes the data governance strategy notable isn’t just its emerging content, but the process shaping it: **led by national institutions with key support – and collaboration – from third-party experts.**

## As a key step in this process, the government brought together local stakeholders to share their experiences, perspectives, and expertise.

While drawing on global best practices adds value by offering tested insights and lessons, the direction, purpose, and pace should be defined and driven by national leadership. In an era when many data governance strategies mirror global templates, Ethiopia’s homegrown approach offers a powerful example of how to get digital policy right—from the inside out.

The development of the data strategy began under the Office of the Prime Minister, with a technical committee drawn from Ethiopia’s own institutions: the Information Network Security Administration (INSA), the Ministry of Innovation and Technology (MiNT), the Ethiopian Artificial Intelligence Institute, and Addis Ababa University.

As the first step in the process, the technical committee conducted an assessment of the country’s data ecosystem. Leveraging their diverse expertise, they examined existing practices in data management, sharing, classification, and access – identifying what was working well, emerging opportunities, where challenges remained, and the impact these choices have had on decision-making. They also reviewed safety and security principles, identifying where Ethiopia’s current practices could be reinforced. These insights were then consolidated into a draft data governance framework, a high-level overview that highlighted key technical considerations, priorities, and stakeholders.

## Working alongside local implementers, we helped further strengthen the process - by promoting an inclusive and representative approach.

At the Digital Impact Alliance (DIAL), we were invited to help build on this foundation. Our primary role was to help expand this framework into a full draft data strategy, complete with inclusive implementation guidance, evidence-based policy decisions, and alignment with international standards.

As part of this effort, we focused on bringing in perspectives from stakeholders often left out of formal government policy-making processes, including civil society groups, academic researchers, tech entrepreneurs, and regional actors. Through a series of virtual consultations and face to face interviews, we helped gather insights about how data is actually used (or not used), where trust gaps exist, and what kind of governance models would serve the ecosystem as a whole.

In addition, we also drew on key learnings from regional and global standards for data governance, such as the AU Data Policy Framework, as well as our recent engagements in [Sierra Leone](https://dial.global/work/strengthening-national-data-strategy-sierra-leone/), [South Africa](https://dial.global/research/the-dpi-roadmap-playbook/), [Nigeria](https://dial.global/work/ai-dpi-nigeria/), and beyond. Through these inputs, we highlighted global best practices in data governance and strategy development, including the importance of:

- **Institution building,** which is a pillar of successful data governance policies
- **Capacity development**, which is essential for strengthening ownership
- **Leadership commitment** and diverse **stakeholder engagement**, both of which strengthen data sharing practice and promote effective governance
- **Alignment with emerging approaches like DPI,** where data is the foundation and data governance is a critical enabler.

When we presented our findings back to the technical committee in a review workshop, the draft data governance strategy was crafted with these broader voices – and considerations – in mind. By incorporating these insights, we’re helping ensure the final data governance strategy will be technically sound, socially grounded, and based on real world learnings.

## By combining local ownership with targeted expert support, Ethiopia is ensuring widespread buy-in, trust, and commitment.

This locally led, deliberative, and collaborative approach has been key to the strategy’s success, for several different reasons.

First, data governance strategies that are created by those who will be responsible for implementing them are much more likely to be put into practice. Hence, setting the context and ownership right from the onset is crucial. When processes are driven by consulting firms or support organizations, they can perpetuate continued dependency on external technical expertise, only loosely anchored on existing systems, institutions, and processes. This can result in prescriptive strategies and documents that risk limited ownership, local buy-in, and lack of implementation.

Secondly, national ownership builds long-term commitment. With its approach, Ethiopia is successfully avoiding many of the common challenges associated with externally driven processes, including the failure to consider:

- The social, cultural, and political contexts within Ethiopia’s policy environment
- Public sector execution capabilities
- Developmental goals and ambitions of the ecosystem
- The need to build long term sectoral linkages and circumvent siloes

Finally, by including DIAL in the drafting process, Ethiopia was able to combine the technical committee’s knowledge and expertise with that of a reliable, impartial third-party partner. This collaboration has been key in filling knowledge and implementation gaps, while still ensuring the draft strategy is targeted and tailored to Ethiopia’s unique digital, political, and cultural landscape.

By exerting clear ownership over the process while also incorporating insights from trusted experts, Ethiopia is signaling that this is not just another policy exercise, but part of a broader, sovereign agenda to shape the future of digital development in line with national priorities.

## For Ethiopia, the process matters as much as the product.

We often judge policy success by the quality of the final document. But in digital transformation — especially when it comes to advancing a DPI approach — the process is just as important. Ethiopia’s experience demonstrates that when local institutions lead and ecosystem actors are consulted meaningfully, it doesn’t just result in better strategy, it makes implementation possible.

It’s tempting to see data governance as a niche topic. But, in reality, it is central to every other digital investment a country might make. Whether it’s digital ID, payments, registries, or citizen-facing services; governance of data underpins trust, interoperability, and usability across the board. That’s why Ethiopia’s approach is also an early step toward a DPI-aligned digital transformation journey. It creates the rules, safeguards, and institutional clarity needed to unlock future digital infrastructure investments. Most crucially, it also lays the foundation for public trust. Citizens are more likely to engage with digital systems when they know their data is being handled transparently, fairly, and securely. That’s as true in Addis Ababa as it is in Nairobi, Accra, or Johannesburg.

## Ethiopia’s experience holds practical lessons for other countries embarking on similar journeys:

- National institutions should drive strategy development, but support from partners can help fill gaps and amplify reach – especially when done collaboratively.
- Governance models must reflect the needs of all stakeholders, especially those with the least power or access to data. This is one good example of [designing for inclusion](https://digitalprinciples.org/principles/design-for-inclusion/) rather than simply efficiency.
- A good strategy is not a one-off event. It must be embedded in institutional processes and regularly updated as needs and technologies evolve. The current version of the Ethiopia Data Strategy is far from over. It will continue to entertain and consider additional perspectives and voices on a regular basis to ensure inclusive and on-going iterations.

For other African countries pursuing a DPI approach, this is not just a story about data. It’s a story about how to build the right foundation. With the right leadership, inclusive engagement, and a clear-eyed view of local needs, digital policies can do more than guide technology; they can help build stronger, more accountable public institutions for the digital age.
