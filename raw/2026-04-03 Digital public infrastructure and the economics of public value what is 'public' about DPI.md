## ABSTRACT

Digital Public Infrastructures (DPI) are gaining prominence in policy and academic debates, with governments, international organisations, philanthropies, and private actors increasingly funding and building them. Yet these transformations are not neutral; they embody directions and values. This paper examines how DPI can be created and governed for the common good by maximising public value. It identifies the normative values associated with DPI development, while arguing that values alone are insufficient. Maximising public value also requires a more proactive state and stronger governance. The paper offers policymakers and researchers a framework for understanding and designing DPI governance to enhance value creation.

## 1\. Introduction

Society is currently at a crucial turning point in how it perceives the potential of digital technologies. For decades, digital transformation was associated with digitising public and private services, developing software and platforms to solve specific needs, and using data to make better decisions. Some companies made substantial profits from the new technologies and business models that emerged – hence Big Tech – but the perspective on digital is expanding. Instead of focusing on products, specific technologies or digital services, attention has been directed at deeper layers – the shared digital systems, increasingly referred to as digital public infrastructure (DPI), that underpin a digital economy and the operation of 21st-century societies.

For 10,000 years, public physical infrastructure has been fundamental to economic and social development. Roads, electricity, water and plumbing all became public infrastructure because they were essential to the functioning of society. In the 21st century, societies run on software. Digital systems that coordinate vaccine distribution, provision social welfare, manage identity records, make payments and share medical data are no longer optional, they are critical infrastructure. At this moment, new digital societal structures and capabilities, such as digital public infrastructure (DPI), are not just being regulated, but funded and created by governments, philanthropies, transnational organisations and the private sector. However, these transformations are not neutral; they have a direction. This paper addresses how to ensure that DPI is not only regulated, but created and governed for the common good by maximising public value creation.

Understanding these transformations requires an economics that goes beyond the standard market failure framework. The economics behind DPI must learn, first, from the economics of *market shaping*, which rejects the idea that the state merely corrects for market failures and instead places the direction of innovation, and how it is co-created from the start, at the centre of economic analysis (Mazzucato [Citation2016](#)). It should also draw on the *economics of public value*, in which the state creates value not by fixing markets but through purposeful investment driven by public purpose (Mazzucato and Ryan-Collins [Citation2022](#)). And finally, it can benefit from a compass for building an *economy driven by the common good*, which moves beyond both the “public good” and “commons” approaches to foreground how collective goals are set, how processes of collaboration and participation are structured, and how the rewards of collectively created value are shared (Estonian Innovation Agency, [Citation2026](#); Mazzucato [Citation2024](#)). This paper brings these three frameworks to bear on DPI, arguing that maximising the public value of digital infrastructure requires not only attention to its technical attributes and societal functions, but a governance architecture oriented towards the common good.

DPI officially entered the global policy lexicon in 2023, when the G20 Digital Economy Working Group published a consensual joint statement outlining DPI’s relevance for inclusive economies and the SDGs ([Citation2023](#)). Although no single definition exists, there is consensus around DPI being reusable digital components that enable public benefits at societal scale. Three components serve key functions for participation in a digital society – digital ID, payment and data exchange systems – documented in the DPI Map (Map [Citation2024](#)), which studies DPI attributes in real-world deployments.

Early evidence suggests DPI can boost SDG commitments and financial inclusion. India’s Unified Payment Interface (UPI), for example, has brought down digital transaction cost and made financial services accessible for hundreds of millions (Parkin [Citation2023](#)). The UNDP ([Citation2023](#)) estimates that by 2030, a DPI approach could lead to 20–33% growth acceleration through financial DPI, circa 4% carbon emission reduction through carbon trading DPI, and 28–42% increase in access to justice.

Despite the growing practice-oriented literature and vast number of countries implementing DPI in the past decade, DPI is still emerging in the scholarship domain. The earliest frameworks, measures and narratives – mainly originated in the policy space – have still been unable to capture the broader “public” and “common” value of DPI. Meanwhile, the scholarship on the digital transformation of the public sector has primarily focused on the digitisation of public services and efficiency gains enabled by technology (Eaves and Rao [Citation2025](#) <sup>1</sup>; Meijer and Bekkers [Citation2015](#); Mergel, Edelmann, and Haug [Citation2019](#)), although there has been an increasing acknowledgement of the importance of embedding the notions of public value in technological solutions for the public sector (Bannister and Connolly [Citation2014](#); Bonina and Cordella [Citation2009](#); Cordella and Bonina [Citation2012](#); Mazzucato, Entsminger, and Kattel [Citation2020](#)).

This gap points to a deeper problem: the economics of DPI remains rooted in a market failure framework that treats public intervention as a correction to otherwise efficient markets, saying nothing about the direction of investment or the state’s capacity to shape markets (Mazzucato [Citation2013](#)). A “public value” and “common good” lens (Mazzucato [Citation2024](#), [Citation2026](#)) foregrounds the purposeful co-creation of value, the directionality of public investment, and governance structures for equitable benefit-sharing (Mazzucato and Ryan-Collins [Citation2022](#)). It also requires rethinking economic categories that have reduced infrastructure to a “public good” problem (Mazzucato [Citation2018](#)). Applying this lens to DPI means asking not only what makes digital infrastructure technically “public”, but how its governance can maximise public value towards the common good.

The increased interest in DPI raises profound questions about how “public” is understood. The definition of “public” in DPI is not neutral – it is embedded with values, assumption and directions of impact. Eaves and Sandman ([Citation2023](#)) argue that publicness derives from guaranteeing essential capabilities for digital participation, while others emphasise openness or social value (Zuckerman [Citation2020a](#), [Citation2020b](#); Center for Digital Public Infrastructure [Citation2023](#)). The varied framing must be unpacked to understand how DPI creates value and to support DPI players in designing more effective infrastructure.

Therefore, this paper aims to contribute to the gap in the literature on DPI by clarifying a fundamental question: what makes DPI *public*? The goal is to understand what values have been associated with DPI and how to maximise DPI’s public value creation potential.

The paper’s original contribution is threefold. First, it systematically examines the normative values embedded in competing conceptions of DPI’s “publicness” – a gap in the existing literature, which has focused on technical definitions and implementation. Second, it demonstrates that neither attribute nor functional framings are sufficient for public value maximisation, advancing the debate beyond definitional questions towards the political economy of digital infrastructure. Third, it brings the economics of market shaping, public value and the common good into the DPI domain for the first time, offering an actionable governance framework that connects the literature on the state’s role in value creation to the practical challenge of governing digital infrastructure for the common good. This paper reviews the literature on traditional infrastructure to reflect on how debates on public value have shaped scholars’ understanding of public infrastructure more generally. We identify two high-level frames for value creation – attributes and functional perspectives – and argues that “publicness” in DPI implies normative assumptions that should be made explicit. We argue that the “common good” lens (Mazzucato [Citation2024](#), [Citation2026](#)) is more aligned with public value maximisation than the vague notion of “public interest”, capturing the role of government as shaping markets and setting collective goals in the DPI space. This paper discusses how a theory of the “common good” might help strengthen the public value creation process and inform the governance of emergent DPI. This paper does not intend to engage in the definitional debate over DPI, though shedding light on what *public* means might help clarify the origins of the distinct visions behind the concept of DPI.

The paper proceeds as follows: [section 2](#s0002) outlines key concepts of traditional and digital infrastructure; [section 3](#s0003) synthesises literature on public value in digital transformation processes preceding DPI; [section 4](#s0004) discusses the contested nature of publicness in DPI, identifying attribute and functional framings; [section 5](#s0005) introduces the “common good” and its relevance for DPI governance; and [section 6](#s0006) concludes with suggestions for further research.

## 2\. Back to basics: from traditional to digital infrastructure

### 2.1. What is infrastructure?

Given the ubiquitous use in policy and technology circles, the term infrastructure is surprisingly contested. The etymology derives from Latin, meaning “underneath the structure”. French engineers began using it in the 19th century to describe physical structure supporting railings and other “superstructures”.[Footnote <sup>1</sup>](#) Subtly, the notion of infrastructure was expanded beyond its literal meaning of “underneath the structure” to incorporate a vaguer, but essential, idea of collective use.

Today, infrastructure has broadened to social, institutional, digital and other categories. The Oxford Dictionary defines it as “the basic physical and organisational structures and facilities needed for the operation of a society or enterprise.”

Frischmann’s functional definition of infrastructure (Frischmann [Citation2012](#)) as “shared means to many ends” is a valuable starting point for two reasons. First, it is holistic in that it groups any infrastructure – not only physical but also social, institutional, and digital. Second, it emphasises elements we consider essential in the analysis of any infrastructure: communal use (“shared means”) and the numerous possibilities of use (“to many ends”). According to Frischmann ([Citation2012](#)), infrastructure should satisfy three criteria:

The resource must be consumed non-rivalrously for an appreciable range of demand.

Social demand for the resource is driven primarily by downstream productive activities that require the resource as an input.

The resource may be used as an input into a wide range of goods and services, which may include private goods, public goods and social goods.

The first criterion reflects the “shareable” nature of infrastructure – not always a pure public good, since high demand may exceed capacity (e.g. roads cannot accommodate all cars worldwide, in digital domain, if it relies on limited storage or processing power, it is an impure public good).[Footnote <sup>2</sup>](#) The second associates infrastructure with “means”: capital goods or intermediate resources. The third emphasises genericness of purpose. This definition is used throughout the paper.

### 2.2. What does infrastructure mean in the digital domain?

Put simply, infrastructures in the digital domain are digital resources, such as systems and specifications, built as shared means to many ends. In other words, digital resources are seen as infrastructure when different societal actors can leverage them for several possibilities.

When the term digital infrastructure is used, many associate it with physical telecommunications – internet cables, data centres, transmission networks – or with the internet itself. The internet is the most prominent example of how software and hardware combine as digital infrastructure: any person or entity can use it for research, commerce, communication and innumerable other activities. It is a “shared means to many ends”.

This paper does not focus on internet protocols and the physical infrastructures that support them. Instead, it focuses on an emerging layer of digital infrastructure that currently sits above the hardware and internet protocols layers, and is emerging as a new form of infrastructure essential to the operation of modern markets and society, such as digital IDs, payments and data exchange systems.

The literature on digital infrastructure dates to the early 2000s, when the terms information infrastructure (Hanseth and Lyytinen [Citation2008](#), [Citation2010](#)) or e-infrastructure (Edwards et al. [Citation2009](#)) were used to describe infrastructures like the internet: networked, interoperable, heterogeneous.[Footnote <sup>3</sup>](#) For the sake of simplicity, this paper does not explore the technical definitions of digital infrastructure, but it is particularly useful to understand infrastructure as a digital stack (see Figure 1).[Footnote <sup>4</sup>](#) In general terms, the logic of the stack is that each layer is xconxnected to the ones above and below, and the upper layers leverage the resources created in the layers below. The infrastructure layer provides the shared resources that connect the data and hardware layers to the multiple services and use cases that might emerge. Shared infrastructure can be provided both by public and private organisations or even co-developed by different institutions (see Table 1).

## 3\. The public value of digital systems: from e-government to DPI

The impact of digital technologies in the public sector has primarily been analysed under the term “e-government”, with a focus on enhancing the efficiency of existing services (Cordella and Tempini [Citation2015](#); Linders, Liao, and Wang [Citation2018](#); Meijer and Bekkers [Citation2015](#); Siddiquee [Citation2016](#)), rather than creating new business models or on digital as a catalyst for transformational change in the public interest. Terms like digitalisation, digitisation or digital transformation are used interchangeably (Mergel, Edelmann, and Haug [Citation2019](#)). Although digitising services improves efficiency, this view underplays technology and the government’s role in creating, large-scale values.

Strauss et al. ([Citation2021](#)) developed the notion of government-as-a-platform (GaaP), and Fishenden and Thompson ([Citation2013](#)) proposed that governments could drive “open architecture” through standardised, platform-type behaviours. Yet public administration literature has focused more on digital services than on deeper layers of shared infrastructure (Brown et al. [Citation2017](#)), with even the UK – a leader in GaaP – focusing extensively on services and little on shared infrastructure.

Nevertheless, moving beyond the digital services paradigm towards a societal-scale digital transformation requires an explicit intention to create public value and reconceptualise the state’s role. A growing body of research has attempted to use the public value theory to analyse the benefits of ICT transformations (see, for example, Bannister and Connolly [Citation2014](#); Bonina and Cordella [Citation2009](#); Cordella and Bonina [Citation2012](#)). Cordella and Paletti ([Citation2019](#)) were among the first to articulate clearly how the GaaP architecture can help public administration deliver public value better. Analysing the Italian case, they suggest that public value creation requires a stronger GaaP orchestration, pointing to a potential role for the state to play.

Building public-governed digital infrastructure might allow states to develop capabilities (Cingolani [Citation2022](#)) essential to running a 21st-century society (e.g. managing identity, payments and data exchange). Digital infrastructures create opportunities for new regulatory and operational mechanisms to fight structural inequalities and offer an alternative to value extraction by corporate or foreign actors (O’Reilly, Strauss, and Mazzucato [Citation2023](#)). Nevertheless, if the direction for change is not set with purpose or, worse, set with the wrong one, DPI – and other digital transformations at the population scale – can facilitate structural exclusion and other types of problems at scale across a society. Several examples depict the potential harms. India’s Aadhaar has had several documented examples of exclusion, highlighting the risk of pairing IDs with access to welfare services (Totapally et al. [Citation2019](#)). Likewise, in Kenya, misrecognised IDs have barred migrants and refugees from essential social security programmes (Weitzberg [Citation2020](#)). Therefore, these systems’ governance and guardrails should be set to prevent the infrastructures from being misused while maximising public value creation.

As more countries embark on strategies to build DPI, it is fundamental to understand what “public” means in DPI and how a theory of the “common good” can help strengthen public value creation.

## 4\. Digital public infrastructure: what is public about it?

What makes a digital infrastructure public? Porteous ([Citation2023](#)) asked a similar question and argued that so far, seeking clarity around the “I” in DPI is more relevant than the terms digital and public, claiming that there is little to be gained now by restricting the latter two. This section argues why the “P” is more relevant than it seems.

Most, though not all, advocates for DPI seem to believe that “public” in DPI does not mean government ownership but public interest or oversight. For Bozeman ([Citation2002](#)), the notion of “public interest” has historically been too vague and lacking in content to be helpful. The same might be said about DPI. By accepting that “public interest” can be loosely defined, actors in the DPI space have struggled to find consensus in debates about what makes a digital infrastructure *public*. For example, one can argue that Mastercard, Visa and American Express payment infrastructures are DPI because their existence allows millions of people to use a credit function, generating a societal benefit, as can be argued for most digital infrastructures. These companies may also claim they are regulated, so government oversight exists. Ultimately, it becomes hard to differentiate between a digital infrastructure and a DPI; therefore, unpacking the “P” in DPI matters.

However, in practice, each organisation in the DPI space has an implicit interpretation of public interest that is not vague or neutral, but rather has implicit normative values embedded in it. For Bozeman ([Citation2007](#)), whereas the public interest is an ideal to be pursued but not tied to any specific content, public values have content and, in many cases, can be easily identified, measured and evaluated. He adds that “public values” provide a *normative consensus* about “(a) the rights, benefits and prerogatives to which citizens should (and should not) be entitled; (b) the obligations of citizens to society, the state and one another; and (c) the principles on which governments and policies should be based.”.[Footnote <sup>6</sup>](#)

How roads, streets and cities are designed affects what happens on them (Hillier [Citation1996](#); Hillier and Hanson [Citation1984](#)). Like traditional infrastructure, DPI also has a direction. Although the question about publicness might seem like a minor semantic issue, this section exemplifies that different understandings of DPI embed normative perspectives. The assumptions that are driving the direction should be made explicit. To design and implement DPI with the public interest in mind, having a clear reference to identify how organisations frame and embed values in their decisions about DPI, is essential. This is the goal of this section.

### 4.1. The two ways of framing the “publicness” of DPI

In many ways, the discussion around the publicness of DPI mimics the debates over traditional infrastructure. There are two dominant ways of framing the public benefits generated by traditional infrastructure (see, for example, Buhr [Citation2003](#); Frischmann [Citation2012](#)) that could be applied to DPI (see Table 2): by attributes and functions of the infrastructure.

### Table 2. Different ways of framing “publicness” in DPI.

In the DPI context, some groups focus on the benefits enabled by the digital infrastructure’s technical attributes, such as being built with open standards or reusable building blocks (see Public benefits of DPI associated with its attributes). The technical attributes are embedded in a normative paradigm that values dynamic efficiency and scalability. The second framing stems from a functional view of infrastructure, which understands societal benefits through the different societal purposes it achieves. For example, Eaves and Sandman ([Citation2023](#)) define DPI as “society-wide, digital capabilities essential to participation in society and markets as a citizen, entrepreneur and consumer in a digital era.” Another functional example comes from Zuckerman ([Citation2020b](#)), who emphasises the role of DPI in enabling civic life in digital spaces. Although there can be several functional perspectives, in this paper we focus on the most pronounced. The functional perspectives are more directly associated with normative values, such as social value, economic value, capabilities, human rights and essential needs.

We explore different ways in which the value of infrastructure has been associated with attributes and functions, and reflect on how they can be applicable or are limiting for DPI. In practice, most organisations do not use a single frame, but combine them to justify the different social benefits that DPI can create. Still, the exercise of unbundling the frames and analysing them in isolation is helpful to make explicit the assumptions about value creation that are embedded in the current narratives about DPI’s potential.

#### 4.1.1. Public benefits of DPI associated with its attributes

##### 4.1.1.1. Be interoperable through open standards

Traditional infrastructure generates dynamic efficiency through interoperability. The standardisation of electrical grid protocols – common norms across transformers, meters, control systems and socket ports – enabled seamless integration and facilitated the entry of multiple payers, creating a competitive environment for innovative solutions.

In the digital space, DPI may increase competition if built with interoperable standards.[Footnote <sup>7</sup>](#) Interoperability prevents lock-in and legacy problems common to IT systems (Bispal et al. [Citation1999](#)), and when digital infrastructure follows open standards, “shared means” becomes “universally shared means to many ends”. The Center for Digital Public Infrastructure ([Citation2023](#)) considers interoperability the most important feature distinguishing DPI from regular digitisation.

From this perspective, Amazon Web Services (AWS) is not considered DPI because its architecture does not follow interoperable open standards. Like other major cloud providers, AWS introduces proprietary services to foster vendor lock-in, raising concerns about limited interoperability. Put simply, AWS does not offer “universally shared means to many ends”.

##### 4.1.1.2. Be built using reusable building blocks

Digital infrastructure outlined by Yoo, Henfridsson, and Lyytinen ([Citation2010](#)) are not only modules but can be divisible into even more powerful modular subcomponents – unlike analogue infrastructures, which are technically indivisible. These reusable building blocks, like Lego bricks, are associated with power capacity for innovation and scalability. McAfee and Brynjolfsson ([Citation2017](#)) describe their potential for combinatorial innovation: “coming up with something new and valuable not by starting from scratch, but instead by putting together in new ways things that were already there”.

The Digital Public Goods Alliance’s GovStack Community of Practice (2022) has conceptualised reusable building blocks as part of “DPI ecosystems”. According to them, digital public goods (DPGs) [Footnote <sup>8</sup>](#) and building blocks constitute a country’s DPI. In their definition, building blocks are “software code, platforms and applications that are interoperable, provide a basic digital service at scale, and can be reused for multiple use cases and contexts.” In this definition, which is shared by the Center for Global Development (2021), reusable building blocks have four features, which constitute a microservices architecture:

1. Autonomous: provide a standalone, reusable service or set of services; they may be composed of many modules/microservices.
2. Generic: are flexible across use cases and sectors.
3. Interoperable: must be able to combine, connect and interact with other building blocks.
4. Iterative evolvability: can be improved even while being used as part of solutions.

#### 4.1.2. Public benefits of DPI associated with its functions

##### 4.1.2.1. Foster community and social relationships

The notion that infrastructure creates social value is based on its potential to shape communal, not individual, life. In this narrative, infrastructure’s interconnectedness and relational aspects make it public. Discourses highlighting the social value of infrastructure define it as:

- The material and organisational foundations of collective existence
- The networks, systems and institutions that link people, places and things together
- Those physical spaces in which regular interactions are facilitated between and within the diverse sections of a community, and where meaningful relationships, new forms of trust and feelings of reciprocity are inculcated among local people
- The principal function is to foster inter- and intra-communal relationships (The Bennett Institute 2021).

In the DPI space, the most prominent voice in this discourse is Zuckerman ([Citation2020b](#)), who defines DPI as “the infrastructures that let us engage in public and civic life in digital spaces.” Focusing mainly on public and social media, he argues that only some digital infrastructures are designed primarily for civic goals. He suggests that DPIs could be built with more purpose and intention toward the common good.

##### 4.1.2.2. Foster economic activity

In this perspective, typically pronounced by economists, public infrastructure is justified by the economic value it creates. One example in the non-digital space is from Buhr ([Citation2003](#)), who defines infrastructure as “the sum of all relevant economic data such as rules, stocks and measures with the function of mobilising the economic potentialities of economic agents”.

In the DPI domain, this narrative is particularly strong in developing economies. Countries tend to highlight two potentials. The first is the notion that DPI supports a digital economy with potential for economic growth. India, one of the leaders in the DPI space and the host of the 2023 G20 meetings, has used this discourse to justify building its digital ID (Aadhaar), open payments system (UPI) and a consent-based data layer. The three together constitute the India Stack (IndiaStack [Citationn.d.](#)). The second is DPI’s potential for fast-tracking economic development (Mukherjee and Maruwada [Citation2021](#)), usually focusing on the most vulnerable. For example, the World Bank’s Identity for Development (ID4D) program is structured around the assumption that producing proof of identity is associated with various dimensions of development (World Bank [Citation2022](#)).

##### 4.1.2.3. Guarantee better quality of life

Here, publicness of infrastructure is justified by guaranteeing essential human needs (Buhr [Citation2003](#)). Hospitals meet the need for medical care as a type of material infrastructure (Torrisi [Citation2009](#)). Buhr ([Citation2003](#)) categorised material infrastructure essential for physical needs (warmth, health, water, light) and social needs (security, information, education, mobility). This framing intersects with human rights: access to these infrastructure is a question of upholding human dignity and equality of opportunity.

In the digital domain, the right to an identity, prompted by the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goal (16.9) to “provide legal identity for all, including birth registration” by 2030, has been one of the most evoked arguments for providing digital IDs as DPI. This perspective is also starting to develop for sectoral DPI in areas such as healthcare, education, mobility and security, and necessitates a discourse around DPI that extends beyond its technical aspects. It advocates for DPI as a pivotal element of just societies, where the digital divide, for example, is addressed not only as a technological challenge, but also as a matter of human rights.

##### 4.1.2.4. Guarantee essential capabilities

The essential capabilities view highlights infrastructure’s potential to provide capabilities for societal agents to thrive in social, economic, political and civic roles. Influenced by Sen’s capability approach (Sen [Citation1985](#)), this discourse holds that well-being should be understood in terms of capabilities (“the doings and the beings that people can achieve”) and functionings (capabilities realised). A capability is achieved when infrastructure resources are converted into a functioning, such as participating in the digital economy.[Footnote <sup>10</sup>](#)

In the digital space, this discourse has been one of the most popular to justify the publicness of digital infrastructures. The argument follows Amartya Sen’s understanding of human rights: if a person does not have the capabilities to participate in a digital society, the person is excluded from opportunities and loses their freedom. This is the underlying assumption behind Eaves and Sandman’s ([Citation2023](#)) definition of DPI as “society-wide, digital capabilities that are essential to participation in society and markets as a citizen, entrepreneur and consumer in a digital era.” Through the capabilities’ lenses, there is consensus around three types of DPI – data sharing, digital identity and digital payment layers – although these categories are evolving.

### 4.2. Are functional and attribute perspectives to public value enough for defining the “P” of DPI?

In the subsection above, we unpacked the underlying values associated with different perspectives on the publicness of DPI, showing that DPI is not neutral. While, in practice, organisations rarely rely on a single perspective, looking at the different narratives in isolation makes explicit the assumptions about value creation embedded in how DPI potential is justified. But should one care which narrative is used? Moreover, are these categorisations satisfactory for defining the “P” of DPI?

Clearly, building DPI with an attributes perspective to public value creation instantly creates societal benefits. For example, designing DPI to have open standards, being built with reusable components and open-source software, contributes to many dynamic efficiency gains (such as increased and improved competition, network effects, and the circulation and dissemination of knowledge in the economy).[Footnote <sup>11</sup>](#) These features also foster the system’s scalability and access. However, the attributes perspective is broadly agnostic on the direction of outcomes. It assumes that innovation emerging from the infrastructures will create positive spillovers in different markets and formats, and those should not be restrained or pre-defined. While this is undoubtedly a positive consequence of the attributes outlined in this paper, its downside is to possibly waste DPI investment and implementation efforts in areas that are not policy-relevant or priority.

In contrast, functional perspectives, by definition, make explicit a direction for societal impact. For example, a needs-based discourse would justify building DPI for essential goods enabling human existence, which is a restrained scope for DPI’s impact. However, a solely functional perspective is also limiting. Suppose DPI is built with a specific function in mind, but does not comply with attributes. In that case, it might waste an opportunity for societal impact and be less resilient to political shifts. For example, building digital identities that are not reusable, interoperable and open does not maximise the impact that takes place when the digital ID is designed for multiple use cases and at scale. Features are also complicated to change over time, whereas an ill-intended leader can reshuffle governance more easily. They are thus critical to the sustainability and endurance of public values.

If one is genuinely concerned about public value maximisation, understanding the “P” of DPI through a combined functional and attribute perspective is desirable, because it expands the potential for creating public benefits. However, only answering what public values are embedded in DPI is not enough. At least two arguments can be made. The first is about governance. Although both attributes and functional perspectives alone create some form of value, as described above, none account for the processes surrounding value creation and maximisation, and the political economy implications. For example, nothing intrinsic about the attributes or functions of DPI creates inclusion, transparency and trust. These are achieved not only through features, but also through processes, i.e. governance. Second, both discourses are broadly silent on the state’s role. In the policy discourse, it is expected to find economic perspectives which see the state’s role as fixing market failures or being unable to deliver more quality than the private sector. This framing is not helpful. Achieving ambitious goals aligned with public values requires proactive governments who set the direction for the required collective action (Mazzucato and Ryan-Collins [Citation2022](#)).

So how to maximise public value? In the next section, the discussion centres on how a “common good” framework, when applied to governance, has the potential to enhance the public value creation of DPI.

## 5\. Taking the “P” seriously: governing DPI for the “common good”

The different lenses on DPI’s publicness illustrate significant value creation potential. One implication is to consider DPI’s value creation from both technical attributes and functional perspectives. However, defining normative values is insufficient for public value maximisation. As discussed in Are functional and attribute perspectives to public value enough for defining the ‘P’ of DPI?, the different notions of publicness are limiting because they are broadly silent on governance and the state’s role. We argue that these elements are essential for public value maximisation, and propose a common good framework, as developed in Mazzucato ([Citation2024](#), 2026), to strengthen DPI’s public value creation.

Before turning to this framework’s specifics, the underlying economic argument bears stating directly. The economics of DPI has been dominated by a market failure lens that frames public investment as corrective. The state provides infrastructure where the private sector underinvests and withdraws once the “failure” is remedied. DPI scholarship inherited this framing, treating digital infrastructure as a public good problem managed through regulation. Yet this approach does not put the direction of investment, quality of outcomes, or institutional conditions for value creation at the centre of discussion.

Understanding DPI from a public value and common good theory requires a different starting point. Rather than understanding value as something created by private actors and merely redistributed or protected by public ones, it recognises that value is collectively created through the interactions of public and private institutions, and that the state plays a constitutive role in shaping markets, not only correcting them (Mazzucato [Citation2013](#), [Citation2018](#)). In this framing, infrastructure – including digital infrastructure – is not a cost to be minimised or a correction to an otherwise efficient market, but a strategic public investment that shapes the conditions under which innovation, inclusion and economic participation become possible. The question is not simply whether a digital infrastructure is technically “open” or fulfils a particular function, but whether its governance, institutional architecture and investment logic are oriented towards the common good (Mazzucato [Citation2024](#), [Citation2026](#)).

This reframing has concrete implications for DPI. It means that decisions about which digital infrastructures are built, who participates in their design, how their benefits are distributed and what capabilities the state retains to govern them are not secondary governance questions but constitutive economic ones. The common good framework developed in Mazzucato ([Citation2024](#), [Citation2026](#)) offers a set of principles – purpose and directionality, co-creation and participation, collective learning, access and reward-sharing, and transparency – that translate this economic reframing into actionable governance criteria. The remainder of this section applies these principles to the DPI context.

### 5.1. From public values to public value maximisation

Consider the Mastercard, Visa and American Express examples in [Digital public infrastructure: what is public about it?](#s0004). One can argue that these companies create public value through financial inclusion and efficient transactions. Yet because merchants transfer fees to retail prices, the indirect “credit card tax” reduces annual consumer and total welfare by $7 billion and $10 billion, respectively (Wang [Citation2023](#)). Are these payments systems maximising public value creation?

First, it is not clear that they are creating public value. Public value creation, building on Bozeman’s evolution of public values, is not just about societal benefits but how value is created. The value creation process – its direction, who is involved and who guarantees it – matters. Yet this discussion typically foregrounds a state versus market dichotomy. Bozeman’s ([Citation2002](#)) works, building on Moore’s ([Citation1995](#)) public value management theory, argues that efficiency should not be the leading argument for policy delivery “by force of available analytical tools”.

If Bozeman’s argument is accepted, the question becomes: how to go past the state versus market dichotomy? The key is to understand public value creation as a process co-created by the public and private sectors, not only created by one and fixed by the other (Mazzucato and Ryan-Collins [Citation2022](#)). In practice, this conception of public value creation means that widely accepted societal goals can be achieved in collaborative innovation processes between different societal actors that co-create markets (Mazzucato [Citation2016](#)). To effectively generate such outcomes, the availability of expertise and competencies is essential in the planning, implementation, management, and coordination among various interest groups (Mazzucato and Kattel [Citation2019](#)).

In addition to public value creation, public value maximisation implies a more proactive and entrepreneurial attitude to dynamically create and shape new markets, i.e. “transformation” (Mazzucato [Citation2016](#)). A market failure policy narrative fails to account for the innovation capacities required for structural and transformative change. It also does not say anything about the direction of change. However, societal transformation requires moving away from an agnostic view of digital public infrastructure towards a more purposeful and intentional one. Thus, a revived notion of the “common good” that is not nested on fixing, but more on shaping and creating is useful (Mazzucato [Citation2024](#), 2026). So, what does the common good mean and how can it translate into market-shaping governance for DPI?

### 5.2. The common good and the role of the state

The common good is a philosophical principle that extends beyond the mere attainment of common objectives, foregrounding the common processes and relationships needed to achieve them (Dupré [Citation1993](#); Hussain and Edward [Citation2018](#)). Applied to economics, it moves beyond maximising individual preferences and creating Pareto-efficient outcomes as the ultimate goal of economic activity (Felber [Citation2018](#); Tirole [Citation2017](#)). It shows that the “how” of a journey towards a goal is just as crucial as the destination itself. A “common good” framework can strengthen public value maximisation through an impactful governance model for DPI. In contrast with the conventional views on the public good and even the broadly defined public interest, the common good is not a correction, but a bold objective reached collectively (Mazzucato [Citation2024](#), 2026).

The common good builds from the discussions around publicness outlined in [Digital public infrastructure: what is public about it?](#s0004), because the first step for the common good is to know what to consider to create public value. Only then can it move to the stage of articulating collective objectives and governing the economy for the common good. The common good is not restricted to the values created by attributes or functions of DPI, but it moves towards the collective good regarding both outcome and process. In the common good framework, the process is almost as important as the final result and, therefore, governance issues are critical. After all, markets are outcomes of governance. Thus, rather than focusing on market failure or DPI neutrality, more proactive forms of governance that can generate desirable market outcomes are needed (Mazzucato [Citation2016](#)).

A renewed understanding of the state’s role as a market shaper and entrepreneur is central to the notion of a common good. The proactive role of the state has been conceptualised in the entrepreneurial state (Mazzucato [Citation2013](#)), building on Polanyi’s ([Citation1944](#)) views of markets as co-created by the state.[Footnote <sup>12</sup>](#) This requires understanding the role of the public sector not only as market regulators, but also as a shaper and potentially even as an investor or co-investor of first resort, not last resort, capable of influencing the quality of market outcomes. Such a role requires not just bureaucratic skills, but vision and real technology-specific and sector-specific expertise. However, because the state’s contribution has been understated, the state’s full potential as an innovator and value creator has not been realised. Thinking of the state as “levelling” the playing field – instead of tilting it – has reduced its confidence in its capabilities and has made it more vulnerable to being captured by vested interests.

Another argument for a proactive state role is the need to develop sovereignty – retaining essential capabilities to deliver public value. When the state outsources functions, it loses institutional memory and implementation capacity. Unless the state engages in building DPI, essential societal functions become the exclusive property of private corporations. This does not mean governments should run all DPI components, but that a minimum mass of implementation capacity is needed for meaningful governance.

Finally, governments play a critical role in setting the direction of the process through which collective action and coordination occur (Mazzucato [Citation2024](#), 2026). If public value is created collectively for reaching the common good and the process matters as much as the intention, creating public governance structures that help different actors move collectively toward the common good is paramount (Mazzucato [Citation2024](#), 2026). This requires an understanding of how public institutions can guarantee the following five pillars (See Figure 3a):

- Purpose and directionality: Setting an ambitious direction towards which policies may be designed, public-private partnerships formed and citizens engaged;
- Co-creation and participation: Defining the rules and mechanisms for co-investment, collaboration and coordination involving a diverse group of societal actors;
- Collective learning and knowledge-sharing: Rethinking institutional practices that support collective learning and build long-term capabilities and capacities;
- Access for all and reward-sharing: Ensuring that public value is distributed equitably (inclusive growth); and
- Transparency and accountability: Winning and retaining citizen’s trust in tracking progress through practices that show commitment to transparency and accountability.

Maximising public value toward the “common good” requires three things (see Figure 3b). First, well-defined public values, which can be embedded in technical attributes and well-defined functional purposes (see [Section 4](#s0004)). Second, the five pillars of the common good can be translated into governance practices and processes. Below, we further explore the five pillars outlined above and suggest directions for application in the DPI context.

### 5.3. Purpose and directionality

In a “common good” framework, setting directionality and orchestrating the process are two fundamental capabilities in a co-creative value process. The desire for a more explicit directionality in building shared digital infrastructure is a growing trend. Zuckerman ([Citation2020b](#)) reflects that, “Our digital public infrastructures are only accidentally public infrastructures” and suggests that we should “aspire toward a set of tools that are intentionally digital public infrastructures, spaces that operate with norms and affordances designed around a set of civic values.” Similarly, Cordella and Paletti ([Citation2019](#)) argue that the adoption of a platform organisation (interpreted in this paper as a shared digital infrastructure) is not enough to deliver public value and that public administration requires a new mechanism of governance (i.e. orchestration) to guarantee the overall creation of public value.

DPI, like any form of infrastructure, is not neutral – it creates winners and losers and shapes what can be built on top of it. Once one accepts that all infrastructure possesses “directionality”, the that direction should be made explicit (as argued in [Section 4](#s0004)). Highways, initially created for military redeployment, shaped their better-known “long tail” uses – logistics, commuting, commerce – pulling billions of dollars of private capital into the process and creating vast social benefits (as costs). This same direction made other use cases (highways as locations for walkways, sports areas or open-air markets) easier to imagine.

India’s identity system, Aadhaar, illustrates purposeful directionality (Eaves and Goldberg [Citation2018](#)). The government’s main reason for establishing residents’ identity was to simplify welfare distribution, fearing substantial waste from fraud. This directionality enabled additional scaling – a scaled identity solution also streamlined “know your customer” (KYC) compliance for banking and telecommunications.

In contrast to the Indian case, the digital identity system in Jamaica was not built with an explicit, primary purpose in mind (Eaves et al. [Citation2025](#)). Initially, the Jamaican government declared an interest in building an ID system, but did not link it to a primary policy purpose. This allowed others to imagine the ID’s purpose, fostering the distrust of civil society actors, who were suspicious of the government’s intentions. It was also a wasted opportunity, as the government did not focus on developing a programme or application that would benefit its citizens.

Questions for policymakers on purpose and directionality

- What normative public values support the DPI creation?
- 1\. Is the DPI built considering priority use cases (without losing sight of broader applicability)?
- 2\. Are civil society organisations and other societal representatives involved in defin-ing purpose?
- 3\. Is the government responsible for orchestrating the DPI design and implementation process?

### 5.4. Co-creation and participation

The common good framework emphasises collaboration, coordination and co-investment between governments, private companies, civil society and international organisation. Despite suggesting a proactive state role, the framework opposes top-down governance. The process of creating highways, for example, is deeply distrusted in many places due to land expropriation from vulnerable communities to often benefit wealthier and privileged communities. This is why co-creation and participation are parallel to purpose and directionality, not subsequent steps.

Applying this pillar to DPI might mean that in the early stages of DPI conceptualisation, the state creates the institutional mechanisms for collaboration and finds a role for different societal actors to contribute ex-ante, not ex-post. The effort that this coordination requires cannot be downplayed, but it will likely significantly impact the chances for long-term policy success. It is important to note that achieving co-creation and participation around proprietary technologies, particularly if it is a commercial company and not the government with the rights to the technology, is more challenging than an open-source or digital public good option.

Brazil’s Pix exemplifies co-creative DPI. Launched in November 2020 by the Central Bank (BCB), Pix enables instant payments at any time. The team broke paradigms within the BCB by experimenting with user-centric design (Vasconcellos, Eaves, and Rao [Citationforthcoming](#)). They created the Pix Forum, a collaborative governance practice with over 130 members – banks, fintechs, civil society organisations and small business associations. Although processing feedback from so many participants was challenging, the team considers it central to Pix’s success.

Questions for policymakers on co-creation and participation

- 1\. Are explicit public values driving key design and governance decisions?
- 2\. Is there a process for gathering and meaningfully integrating the perspectives of a diverse group of societal actors?
- 3\. Is the DPI being publicly funded or co-funded?

### 5.5. Collective learning and knowledge-sharing

A genuinely collective value creation process requires that actors involved in the implementation learn from each other’s successes and failures, and evolve in their innovation process. In addition, an institutionalised learning process and accumulating institutional knowledge create long-term state capacities. Therefore, the orchestrators of the value creation process must find ways to encourage collective intelligence and knowledge-sharing.

Bangladesh’s Aspire to Innovate (a2i) (\[[Citationn.d.](#)\]) programme drives “collaborative digital innovation for the public good”, serving as a government think tank focused on technology projects supporting the SDGs. It allows government to integrate inputs from civil society, demonstrating the capacities needed to maintain digital public goods.

Open-source software and communities of code also encourage collective learning. For effective engagement of external contributors (Eaves et al. [Citation2022](#)), MOSIP (\[[Citationn.d.](#)\]) – supporting countries in implementing open-source identity systems – offers a balanced approach: some components are closely governed to maintain safety mechanisms, while others are open to external contributions covering requirements, design, coding, testing and documentation.

Questions for policymakers on collective learning and knowledge-sharing

- 1\. Are there processes for documenting learning?
- 2\. Are learnings publicly shared?
- 3\. Is the code base available for others to reuse?

### 5.6. Access for all and reward-sharing

The fourth pillar, access for all and reward sharing, is central to implementing the “common good” in policymaking and directly affects the state’s role. In this pillar, if an infrastructure is directed towards collective benefits, it must be universally accessible, and its rewards must be shared with society. These two ideas are not always aligned with private interests. Therefore, the state has a role in guaranteeing them, even if the infrastructure is privately managed. Physical public infrastructure illustrates the point. For 10,000 years, physical public infrastructure has been a fundamental pillar of social and economic development. Infrastructure like roads, electricity, water and plumbing became *public* because they became essential to the functioning of society, so governments prioritised access and inclusion over market pricing and the possibility of rent extraction.

In the digital domain, guaranteeing universal access, particularly to DPI, unequivocally implies ensuring access through analogue means. Except for seven countries [Footnote <sup>13</sup>](#) where the share of the population that uses the internet is virtually 100%, waiting for universal internet access means imposing a burden on citizens; thus, a non-digital solution is paramount. A few countries have explored creative solutions to analogue access. In Bangladesh, DPI was expanded to include an additional “access layer”, which turns DPI into “phygital public infrastructure” (Chowdhury [Citation2023](#)). The access layer encompasses physical locations and call centres, improving DPI’s accessibility for individuals with disabilities and those in underserved communities in remote rural areas. Bangladesh’s over 9000 digital centres (also known as one-stop shops), widely spread at an average of 4 km from a person’s house, are run by young local entrepreneurs (a third of whom are women). These public-private partnerships guarantee that government services reach the grassroots level. Togo’s Novissi, a COVID-19 cash transfer programme, is another example. Although the service was not available to every citizen, the Togolese government proactively reached out to the most vulnerable by leveraging the post office agencies and partnering with radio stations to communicate about the programme (Vasconcellos, Lawson, and Bakari [Citation2020](#)).

Barcelona offers inspiration for reward-sharing. Following Mayor Ada Colau’s election, the city created a “new data deal” agenda (Bria [Citation2018](#); City of Barcelona [Citation2018](#)), reviewing procurement processes and introducing “data sovereignty” clauses (Fernandez-Monge et al. [Citation2023](#)) – including the right to acquire data collected through public services and some private sector data associated with the public interest. This shows that procurement rules can be powerful policy instruments for the common good.

As DPI develops worldwide, it may boost AI algorithms. Since DPI potentialises data use and sharing, it is essential to address the inadvertent integration of biases into algorithms (Obermeyer et al. [Citation2019](#); Yan [Citation2021](#)) and the unequally distributed benefits of big data (Blumenstock [Citation2018](#); Hilbert [Citation2016](#); O’Reilly, Strauss, and Mazzucato [Citation2023](#); Strauss, O’Reilly, and Mazzucato [Citation2023](#)). Strengthening dialogue with scholarship on data democracy and digital rights will be important (Craglia and Shanley [Citation2015](#); Morozov and; Bria [Citation2018](#); Kulkarni, Chong, and Batarseh [Citation2020](#)).

Questions for policymakers on access for all and reward-sharing

- 1\. Is there a proactive effort to make the DPI universally accessible?
- 2\. Are there governance clauses regulating data for the public interest?
- 3\. Are the benefits of data use being socialised?
- 4\. Are the costs for using the infrastructure low or none?

### 5.7. Transparency and accountability

To win and retain trust in the policy process, transparency and accountability are critical for public sector organisations leading and orchestrating a DPI implementation. Unlike in monolithic organisational structures, in which accountability is vertical and hierarchical, DPI’s decentralised architecture poses challenges to accountability across government departments and levels of government sharing the same infrastructure.

If accountability has become more challenging on the one hand, on the other, DPI has the potential to improve transparency. The more systems are integrated, the more digital footprints can be leveraged for transparency. One well-known example is Estonia’s e-health portal, which allows citizens to see who has accessed their data and when (Estonian Innovation Agency [Citation2024](#)). The city of Barcelona, as mentioned previously, also worked on giving citizens more transparency about how private companies used their data. One of the ways these objectives were achieved was through using an open-source data-sharing infrastructure (Decode), enabling citizens to control their data as a common good and to share them on terms that are fair, transparent and accountable (Decode, n.d.).

Transparency also enhances access by making initiatives more well-known. However, transparency by itself does not guarantee accountability and trust – if interactions with citizens are not operationalised in a user-friendly way, the effect can be neutral or negative.

Questions for policymakers on transparency and accountability

- 1\. Can citizens and companies consent or audit how and when their data is being used?
- 2\. Are key decisions (technical and management) about the infrastructure available in an accessible language and format?
- 3\. Is the DPI governed by a public-interest entity?

To take the public value creation of DPI seriously, the five principles outlined earlier should not be seen in isolation, but rather combined to achieve the notion of the common good.

## 6\. Discussion and conclusion

In the context of a growing interest in the DPI approach for achieving sustainable development goals and other societal impacts, major investments and decisions about the design and governance of DPI are taking place. This is a crucial moment for further reflection on the choices that can increase or harm the potential for public value creation.

In this paper, we outlined one central question for analysis: what does “public” mean in digital public infrastructure? To answer this question, we argued that no infrastructure is neutral in directionality and demonstrated that DPI is no exception. We argued that the current narratives through which different groups understand the publicness of DPI, although vested under the broad concept of public interest, already embed a directionality through values generated by attributes or led by functions. We hope to have contributed to the literature and the policy world by making these values explicit (see Figure 3).

The paper’s distinct contribution lies in bridging two bodies of scholarship that have developed in parallel: the DPI literature, which lacks a grounded economic theory of value creation, and the public value and common good literature, which has not yet been applied to digital infrastructure. By connecting these traditions through five governance principles, the paper provides a foundation for future empirical research on how specific mechanisms can maximise the public value of DPI.

However, creating public value does not mean maximising public value. Although making values explicit is an important and necessary step for public value maximisation, it is insufficient. If one understands public value creation as a *process* co-created by the public and private sectors, not only created by one but fixed by the other (Mazzucato and Ryan-Collins [Citation2022](#)), then functions, attributes and even public provision are limiting. We suggest that the debate on DPI starts by making public values explicit, but that it goes beyond it. Public value maximisation must be a collective effort focusing on outcomes and processes towards the common good. In this process, the state has a renewed role in guaranteeing and orchestrating DPI. The “common good” framework (Mazzucato [Citation2024](#), 2026) and its five principles emerge as useful perspectives to strengthen DPI’s value generation process with a focus on governance.

Therefore, one implication of our paper is that if we want the “P” to be public value maximisation, there is no DPI without explicit public values, governance that follows the five pillars of the common good and a prominent role for the state (See Figure 4). We leave a call for researchers in the public administration and digital government field to consider more robust investigations on the specific policy tools and management mechanisms required to apply a common good governance framework to DPI for creating even more public value. After all, as Star and Ruhleder ([Citation1996](#)) remind us, infrastructure is only such when or while it facilitates value.
