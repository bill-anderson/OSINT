Note: analytical think-tank report (IGP, sponsored by Tech & Media Convergency); treat as named analysis, not primary evidence.

A report from the Internet Governance Project (IGP), sponsored by the Tech and Media Convergency (TMC), investigates a divergence in Tanzania's development: while internet adoption has climbed to roughly one-third of the population, expected digital economic growth has failed to materialise. The research argues the bottleneck is not a lack of infrastructure, but a self-reinforcing system of restrictive governance.

As internet use rose from negligible levels to approximately 31% today, Tanzania's services sector contribution to GDP contracted from a 2001 peak of 49% to 29% in 2024. The ICT sector has stagnated at 1.5% to 1.6% of GDP. Compared to Kenya (similar ~35% internet penetration), Tanzania's digital economy contribution is less than one-fifth. The report identifies: data localization/local ownership mandates that deter foreign investment; censorship, VPN registration and internet shutdowns (an estimated US$250 million in losses during the 2025 election period); and cronyism aligning regulatory authority with private commercial interest.

The report argues incremental reforms under President Samia Suluhu Hassan have not changed the sector's trajectory because the underlying incentive structure between regulator and regulated remains intact. It closes with ten policy recommendations calibrated to be politically survivable — revoking data localization mandates, risk-based cross-border data rules, judicial oversight for data access, liberalizing content licensing, and opening wholesale infrastructure — warning that a mounting surveillance apparatus (SIM registration and the forthcoming Jamii Namba digital ID) risks locking in a durable architecture of control. The full report is titled "The Political Economy of Digital Governance in Tanzania: Pathways to ICT Sector Growth."
