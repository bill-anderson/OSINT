## ABSTRACT

Submarine cables are the indispensable yet often overlooked global infrastructure of our digital age. While geoeconomic competition over cables has increased, its political-economic context, where private corporations dominate nearly every aspect of the network, remains poorly understood. This article examines how geoeconomic competition unfolds within the submarine cable network. We provide a comprehensive mapping of the patterns of ownership and supply of submarine cables, based on a novel open-access dataset. We find that network expansion remains largely driven by corporate interests. US hyperscalers like Google play an increasingly prominent role in high-capacity connections, although telecom carriers retain an important position in the network. US and Chinese interventions have significant, but fragmented effects. They are particularly pronounced in the oligopolistic supply sector, where corporate and state interests align more strongly. We conclude that, against the assumption of purported ‘cable wars’, commercial interests represent structural barriers to states’ instrumentalization of this network.

## 1\. Introduction

Once regarded a seemingly ‘neutral’ foundation for an integrating global economy, transnational infrastructures have become contested terrains of geoeconomic competition for power, control, and profit (Abels & Bieling, [Citation2024](#); Bernards & Campbell-Verduyn, [Citation2019](#); Schindler et al., [Citation2024](#)). Especially submarine fiber-optic cables have recently attracted greater attention as a strategic arena for state competition (Bueger & Liebetrau, [Citation2021](#); Burdette, [Citation2021](#); Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#); Abels, [Citation2025](#)). This heightened interest is hardly surprising: submarine cables are the defining communication infrastructure of the digital age, carrying more than 97 percent of global internet traffic and enabling a tremendous $10 trillion in financial transactions daily (Rossiter, [Citation2023](#); Stronge, [Citation2023](#)). But this is only the beginning. Over the past two decades, the world has seen a renewed boom in submarine cable construction. The global mushrooming of data centers to fuel advances in capacity-hungry Artificial Intelligence (AI) and cloud applications multiplies the demand for bandwidth. The relentless digitalization of our wallets, workplaces, and private lives threatens to put even more pressure on energy and digital infrastructure systems. Contrary to popular imagination, the rising demand will, for the foreseeable future, not be met by communication satellites in the sky, but by the hard-wired infrastructures buried beneath the seabed (Starosielski, [Citation2015](#)). This is also discernible from projections that expect the submarine cable market to grow from roughly $13 billion in 2022 to over $30 billion in 2030, promising annual growth rates of nearly 12% (Insight Partners, [Citation2023](#)).

Despite their critical role, our knowledge about submarine cable networks remains relatively meagre and scattered. Some studies have provided us with insights into their geographical unevenness (Malecki & Wei, [Citation2009](#)); cultural and environmental aspects (Starosielski, [Citation2015](#)); technological developments (Thomas et al., [Citation2015](#)); security aspects (Bueger & Liebetrau, [Citation2021](#)); and strategic dimensions (McGeachy, [Citation2022](#)). Our limited knowledge notwithstanding, the material relevance of submarine cable systems is growing and it intersects with intensifying geoeconomic rivalries (Babić et al., [Citation2022](#)). Submarine cable systems have become a battleground for the rivalry of Chinese and US interests as well as other regional players. An amalgamation of security and geoeconomic motives plays a role. The Snowden revelations have demonstrated that submarine cables can be (and factually are being) tapped for surveillance purposes (Khazan, [Citation2013](#)). Cables have also become the target of sabotage, prominently in the Baltic Sea (Goldenziel, [Citation2025](#)), and the possibility exists that such attacks could extend to cable laying ships or landing points (Bueger & Liebetrau, [Citation2021](#)). Yet, states have not just shown an interest in weaponizing or manipulating the cable network, but also in influencing its further expansion. China has relied on public subsidies to increase its cable connections and gain a stronger footing in the cable-laying industry. The US has used diplomatic pressure and economic statecraft to prevent this (Brock, [Citation2023a](#)), even blocking several major cable projects from landing in Hong Kong. Still, state strategies – the ‘cable wars’ – unfold within a political-economic context where private corporations dominate nearly every commercial aspect of the network, from cable planning and laying to managing capacity. Hence, we ask how geoeconomic competition impacts on the patterns of ownership and supply in the submarine cable network – and vice versa. To address this, we pick up on concepts brought forward in this Special Issue viewing geoeconomics as ‘the study of global struggles for economic expansion and control’ (introduction to this Special Issue). Most significantly – and in contrast to perspectives focused more strongly on the competition of nation states, like great power rivalry, imperialism, or techno-nationalism – we conceptualize corporations as key actors in this struggle. We argue that corporations do not constitute ‘instruments‘ that states can, more or less successfully, employ for their goals. In large parts of the literature, like weaponized interdependence, such a view of geoeconomic competition is dominant: states may have a harder time manipulating networks in some cases, but the power hierarchy remains clear (see, e.g. Farrell & Newman, [Citation2019](#); Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#)). Against this backdrop, we show empirically that, in the case of submarine cables, it is global corporations that own and control large parts of the infrastructure networks which become subject to geoeconomic contestation. By implication, prevailing corporate control poses obstacles to state efforts to manipulate the network. Geoeconomic rivalry is decisively shaped by the juxtaposition of states and corporations, not merely by the instrumentalization of one by the other. State and corporate interests may align or diverge. Depending on their position in the network, corporations will have vested interests in state interventions either distorting competition in their favour and edging out foreign rivals; or leaving their sector largely to their own strategic activity. In short: corporate agency matters for the future of the submarine cable network.

To make our claims plausible, we provide the first empirical mapping of the political economy of the submarine fiber cable network. In debates about submarine cables, the actual ownership and control patterns over time are either not discussed, only indirectly addressed through (regional) case studies, or anecdotally described (see, e.g. Bueger & Liebetrau, [Citation2021](#); Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#); Malecki & Wei, [Citation2009](#)). One central contribution of our article is to fill this gap by introducing a novel open-access dataset through which we can explore the political economy of the submarine cable network amid intensifying geoeconomic competition. The dataset combines ownership and supplier data on over 600 submarine cable systems from provider TeleGeography with manually collected capacity information on these systems. Through analyzing the data, amended with qualitative background information from media reports, we show that the expansion and ownership of the submarine cable network is still overwhelmingly driven by private corporate actors. In line with previous studies, we find that hyperscalers – large tech companies like Google and Meta that offer cloud services at a global scale – indeed play an increasingly prominent role in building high-capacity connections (Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#); Winseck, [Citation2017](#)). However, far from being the only or even dominant type of owner, major telecom carriers retain a central position in the overall network. Likewise, we observe an increased share of state-owned enterprises (SOEs) in the ownership of high-capacity cables. These findings nuance and challenge the prominent arguments about the fundamental change of the global submarine cable network due to the rise of hyperscalers (Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#)). Beyond ownership patterns, we also map and analyze the cable supply market, which gets often neglected by existing research. We show that cable supply remains highly oligopolistic, yet geoeconomic competition is starting to change its composition as Chinese player HMN Tech, after quickly gaining ground into the market, is currently being pushed out of large-scale contracts.

This study responds to recent calls within International Political Economy (IPE) to empirically scrutinize the ‘geoeconomic turn’ (Babić et al., [Citation2024](#)). It expands our understanding about the relevance – not only influence – of global firms as juxtaposed actors and the infrastructures they control, which is an increasingly crucial, yet still underexplored, area in a field long dominated by state-centric analyses (Abels, [Citation2024](#)). By introducing the dataset, it provides our analysis, and follow-up research, with the empirical means to do so. On a theoretical level, it outlines that the era of geoeconomic competition has introduced new layers of conflict into a domain largely shaped by private commercial interests. Governments intervene in networks and adjacent markets to further their geoeconomic and security interests. Yet, they cannot simply steer or instrumentalize them as central non-state network players frequently act according to a different logic. In cases where state and business interests align more clearly, like cable supply, geoeconomic competition can be expected to transform the network more profoundly.

The article is structured as follows: the first section conceptualizes the effects of private business activity on the geoeconomic-infrastructure nexus. The second section depicts the historical dynamics and market forces behind the emergence, expansion, and change of the global fiber-optic network. It also examines how geoeconomic competition, especially between the US and China, has limited effects on this overwhelmingly privately owned network. The third section details the methodology used to map the political-economic features of the submarine cable network and presents our findings on the ownership structures (section [^3].[^1]) and supply market (section [^3].[^2]) of submarine cables. Section four discusses these findings and outlines the strategic orientations of investors, tech firms, and suppliers. Finally, the conclusion sketches out possible scenarios for the future of this vital infrastructure and proposes a research agenda on the political economy of global communication systems.

## 2\. Private infrastructure development and geoeconomic competition

Transnational infrastructures like submarine cables are large technical systems that function as a ‘meso geography’ (Mayer & Acuto, [Citation2015](#), p. 672), mediating between the practices of societal actors and the broader structures of the geoeconomic order. They are not just tools of connectivity but historical artifacts that embody past power relations, projecting them into the present and shaping today’s actions (de Goede & Westermeier, [Citation2022](#)). Many cables have been laid in the 1990s, a period marked by the financialization of infrastructure and speculative investment in the ICT sector. While early cable systems cannot compete with newer ones in terms of capacity, the investment decisions made during that era continue to shape the geographical contours of the global network. This concerns the prominence of infrastructure hubs, regional disparities in internet access, and the vulnerabilities and underdevelopment of less connected areas. In return, today’s decisions, both deliberate and unintentional, reconfigure the global network, with far-reaching implications for patterns of ownership and dependency. Amid rising geoeconomic competition, which this Special Issue understands as global struggles for economic expansion and control, infrastructures are at the core of global contentions. As actors are faced with exclusionary practices on the part of competitors, they tend to react with their own strategic measures seeking to reshape networks to their advantage (introduction to this Special Issue). Prominent contributions to the debate frame these dynamics primarily in terms of intentional state-led interventions that instrumentalize networks for strategic purposes (Baldwin, [Citation2020](#); Farrell & Newman, [Citation2019](#); Gertz & Evers, [Citation2020](#)). US and Chinese foreign economic policies have been the main empirical focus for perspectives that view major powers as driving network development (Beeson, [Citation2018](#); Ho, [Citation2020](#); Weiss & Thurbon, [Citation2018](#)). Yet, recent contributions have warned not to underestimate the strategic agency of private corporations that often stand at the center of modern infrastructure networks and adjacent tech sectors. Scholars have outlined that these corporations have amassed the capital and influence to pursue independent agendas (Abels, [Citation2024](#); Rikap, [Citation2024](#)).

In this article, we address the question of how this complex interplay of state and corporate strategizing affects the geoeconomic competition over global networks. As an essential infrastructure of the modern global economy, submarine cables are a case worthy of investigation in this regard. During the era of neoliberal globalization, the development of economic and material interlinkages – at the core of which stand global infrastructure networks – followed the whims of private investment rather than that of states. Cost considerations led to the concentration of cable centers in select regions, insufficient protection of landing zones, and supplier contracts awarded according to the lowest bidder principle (Starosielski, [Citation2015](#)). Available funds were channeled into speculative projects promising high returns on investment, rather than aligning with long-term state objectives. While recent shifts in the global order have been rapid and far-reaching, more inert and immobile infrastructure networks have responded only with significant delay. Subsea fiber-optic cables are fixed, material infrastructures, made of steel, glass fiber, and plastic, that reflect past decisions and constrain present possibilities.

We contend that private corporations as developers and owners of infrastructure networks play a central role in deciding how strongly interstate competition is able to reshape the control and topography of these networks. Our article contributes to a growing body of literature, mostly from political economy, that revaluates the influence of transnational corporations by highlighting their structural power (Braun, [Citation2022](#); Rafi Atal, [Citation2021](#)) and technological capabilities (Abels, [Citation2024](#); Rikap, [Citation2024](#)). We stress the importance to consider corporations’ roles in the evolution of global networks and the underlying patterns of ownership and control. Corporations continue to shape geoeconomically relevant infrastructures in ways that escape direct state governance. This applies in particular to infrastructures whose ties and nodes are located in different regions, in international waters, or even outer space. While such corporations are not ‘non-territorial’ (Lee et al., [Citation2018](#)), their cross-border operations limit the ability of individual states to govern or regulate their infrastructures. Privatization and fiscal constraints pressure governments to rely on private capital for infrastructure development, in the process ceding substantial decisions over the design and operation of said infrastructures to private actors (Wigger, [Citation2024](#)). Given the tendency toward concentration in global (especially digital) markets, this has also led to consolidation of power in the hands of a few dominant players.

In the case of submarine cables, international, mostly private consortia have been central to the historical emergence and initial structuring of the network. As our analysis shows, this continues to be the case even under conditions of intensifying geoeconomic competition. Companies like Google and Meta, alongside major telecommunication companies, are leading a new wave of submarine cable network expansion, transforming the very logic by which these cables are financed and laid. These firms are capable of fully funding cable projects, which they integrate into their proprietary networks to support cloud-based services. Instead of sharing capacity, for example within consortia, these actors seek to control large parts of the material architecture of the global internet.

Geoeconomic competition is now entering the equation. States, in particular the great powers US and China, have displayed an interest in reshaping the network in accordance with their security and geoeconomic interests. One aspect is that they seek to manipulate cable connections to ready them for weaponization – surveillance and sabotage being the main factors here – or to increase their resilience to weaponization attempts by global rivals. As our empirical analysis illustrates, this has changed some central parameters of network evolution. First, it propels the activity of SOEs within these networks as way of foreign political intervention (Stone et al., [Citation2021](#)). Second, states seek to leverage corporations’ reliance on favourable regulatory and financial conditions to manipulate their business decisions. At the same time, while the US and China manage to change the political economy and topography of the subsea network, considerable obstacles keep them from achieving this more holistically. As stated before, this has to do with the material inertia of global networks. Another major factor to consider is the influence of central network actors that prefer different outcomes. For instance, our analysis underlines that the US government has been able to intervene in tech firms’ plans to build cables that connect to China in some instances, but not in others. It has been a recurring theme in scholarship on transnational corporations that they pursue a profit-seeking logic – as some argue, even amended with broader political objectives (Abels, [Citation2024](#)) – that differ from the geostrategic logic of states (Rafi Atal, [Citation2021](#); Rikap, [Citation2024](#)). At the core of this article stands the argument that corporations should not just be considered intermediaries of state action, but at least partially autonomous builders of networks.

As a second key argument, we stress that the success of state action within these networks depends on its alignment with the strategic orientation of the corporations it centers on. This is particularly true in an era of transnational corporations that are ‘domestic’ in terms of being headquartered in a country, but whose production networks, business operations, and assets are diversified and spread around the globe (Babić et al., [Citation2017](#)). These firms adopt strategies that either hedge their geopolitical risks or align with a certain party (Choer Moraes & Wigell, [Citation2022](#); Schindler & Rolf, [Citation2025](#)). Depending on their position in the network, corporations can have vested interests in aligning their strategic decisions with state interventions that distort competition in their favour and edge out foreign rivals. In these cases, the impact of geoeconomic competition on the network can be expected to be more substantial. Out article illustrates this using the example of the cable supply sector: Chinese market intervention has established cable laying company HMN Tech as a new sectoral player, but the alignment of US supplier SubCom with its government’s foreign policy kept HMN Tech from further increasing its market share. Therefore, geoeconomic forces are reshaping the supply sector more substantially than ownership structures. Generally, US and Chinese financial, political, and regulatory pull will increase chances for corporations to align their strategies with them. In other instances, states’ strategic interests threaten the profitability and growth strategies of structurally powerful private firms. This will offer incentives for these firms to circumvent state intervention by expanding network links in ways that serve their own interest more. Hence, the alignment of central corporate actors indeed acts as a precondition for a successful shaping and instrumentalization of the network through state actors.

Our analysis of the submarine cable dataset serves the purpose of mapping the network’s political economy in terms of patterns of growth, ownership, and supply. It is, to that extent, exploratory. However, our analysis has theoretical purchase insofar as it underlines that the observed dynamics in submarine cable markets largely correspond with the arguments presented in this section about corporations’ role in global networks. It hence also paves the way to more case-based and fine-grained investigations that might allow for a refinement of the proposed mechanisms.

## 3\. A short history of submarine cables: from neoliberal globalization to geoeconomic competition

The first submarine telegraph cable was laid across the Atlantic seabed in 1858, yet it would take nearly another century before the successful installation of the first transatlantic *telephone* cable in 1956. The development of telecommunication technology accelerated in the second half of the twentieth century. In the late 1980s, the first fiber-optic cable was laid, significantly increasing transmission capacity and laying the groundwork for the modern internet (Clark, [Citation2016](#), p. 234). These early stages of global internet infrastructure coincided with the take-off of neoliberal globalization. In the late 1980s, several major telecommunication providers, which had long functioned as monopolies in their respective home market, were privatized and deregulated, reflecting a broader ideological shift (Bulfone, [Citation2019](#)). As former national monopolists, these firms brought with them the know-how, operational capabilities, and financial resources required for the buildout of submarine fiber-optic cable systems. Large Western telecommunication firms such as AT&T, France Télécom (now Orange), and British Telecom (now BT Group) were dominant players in this era of expansion. In many cases, governments retained some form of ownership or influence in domestic enterprises (Winseck, [Citation2017](#)). Due to the high costs of building transnational infrastructures, companies often formed consortia to share the financial burden and benefits of more ambitious projects. In extreme cases like the South-East Asia-Middle East-Western Europe 3 (SEA-ME-WE-3), completed in 1999, over 90 telecom carriers joined forces to finance and operate a 39,000 km long system that would span 33 countries.

By the late 1990s, liberalization and deregulation also attracted speculative investors that sought to exploit the ongoing dot-com boom. Shareholder-driven telecom companies, facing growing competition, pursued increasingly risky infrastructure projects. This ushered in the financialization of the undersea cable network expansion, complementing the consortium model (Starosielski, [Citation2015](#), p. 47). The submarine cable market soon overheated as global capacity far exceeded actual demand for data transmission at that time.

The pace of the buildout slowed drastically after the dot-com bubble burst in the early 2000s. Between 2001 and 2003, investment in submarine fiber-optic projects dropped by a factor of 12, while new cable deployments decreased by a factor of 10 (Terabit Consulting, [Citation2014](#)). Many cable-owning telecommunication firms filed for bankruptcy, particularly US-American companies like Viatel, Global Crossing, and WorldCom. As activity in the Global North waned, the center of gravity shifted toward the Global South. Indian companies like Reliance Communications (now part of Global Cloud Xchange) and Tata Communications emerged as central players in the submarine cable sector. It was not until after the global financial crisis of 2008 that cable construction picked up again. This time, the Asia-Pacific and parts of the Global South were at the center of the buildout, not the transatlantic space. More than half of post-crisis investment in global cable systems through 2015 went to the Asia-Pacific (Winseck, [Citation2017](#), p. 241). Chinese involvement in global construction grew from just 7% between 2012 and 2015 to an estimated 20% between 2016 and 2019 (Lee, [Citation2017](#)).

Alongside this geographical shift, the 2010s also saw the growing involvement of hyperscalers as cable owners and operators. Traditionally, submarine cables were financed by consortia of telecom operators, each holding shares and corresponding capacity rights. These consortia typically contracted one of the few specialized companies that both manufacture and install submarine cables. After installation, cable capacity is monetized through long-term leases (*Indefeasible Rights of Use*) or more expensive short-term rentals. Hyperscalers such as Google and Meta have different infrastructure needs. Their cloud-based services require vast and scalable bandwidth, and these firms increasingly prefer to bypass telecom operators by owning and operating their own cables. Controlling the entire data pathway allows them to manage and scale network performance and security more effectively. This makes economic sense. The volume of data hyperscalers move is so high that, beyond a certain threshold, it becomes more cost-effective to build infrastructure rather than to lease it.

As a result, hyperscalers are increasingly financing and constructing their own cable systems – not to primarily connect densely populated areas, as telecoms often do, but to link their data centers that are distributed around the globe. This marks another significant shift within the political-economic structure of the submarine cable network, whereby hyperscalers are ‘pushing aside longstanding investment models helmed by telecommunication companies and consortia’ (McGeachy, [Citation2022](#), p. 167). While a market-logic has driven cable system buildout in recent decades, its contemporary political relevance is largely defined by the rise of global geoeconomic competition that sets this historical phase apart from previous ones (Abels & Bieling, [Citation2024](#); Babić et al., [Citation2024](#); Gertz & Evers, [Citation2020](#)). Great powers in particular seek to intervene in the development of cable connections to center the network around them geographically, to strengthen their footing in associated sectors, and to increase their resilience to the aggressive strategies of rivals (Abels, [Citation2025](#)). Yet, the very possibility of projecting power in this domain often depends on the economic ties and infrastructures forged during neoliberal globalization. In this sense, the global submarine cable network stands as a paradigmatic example of a ‘geoeconomic’ infrastructure whose interaction is driven by the interaction of market forces and state interventionism. Global tensions – between the US and China first and foremost, but also involving other highly connected countries – are increasingly centered around the construction and control of communication channels (Bueger & Liebetrau, [Citation2021](#); McGeachy, [Citation2022](#); Winseck, [Citation2017](#); Woods & Li, [Citation2024](#)). In cases where economic tools are insufficient to fulfill the security interests of powerful states, they resort to surveillance or sabotage. Most sections of the submarine cable network are, on a legal level, only insufficiently protected. Cables cross international waters and borders, and as Rossiter ([Citation2023](#), p. 4) notes, they ‘fly no flag’ (see also Figure 1). Jurisdiction and competences are fragmented or entirely absent. The 1994 United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) protects the integrity of cables only within 12 nautical miles of a state’s coastline. No legal consensus exists on the responsibilities in Exclusive Economic Zones and in high seas, where cable-laying and interference with submarine cables remain unregulated (Burnett, [Citation2021](#)). Both China and the US have shown deficient commitment to UNCLOS, with the US not even having ratified the treaty. As a result, the physical integrity of the cable infrastructure is far from guaranteed. Russia and China, in particular, have recently been accused of cutting and damaging cables in the Baltic Sea and the Taiwan Strait (Goldenziel, [Citation2025](#)). However, manipulation efforts predate the geoeconomic era. In 2013, former intelligence contractor Edward Snowden revealed that the US National Security Agency (NSA) was tapping directly into global telecommunications networks, collecting massive amounts of data on both foreign and domestic individuals through programmes like PRISM, enabled by close cooperation with Western telecommunication providers (Khazan, [Citation2013](#)). The 2018 CLOUD Act enables US law enforcement agencies to access data stored by domestic tech firms, even when that data resides outside the US – a controversial measure that has attracted widespread criticism. The US has also pivoted toward proactive intervention in cable planning and deployment. In his first term, President Donald Trump set up ‘Team Telecom’, an interagency group led by the National Security Division of the Department of Justice, to oversee foreign investments and influence in telecom infrastructures. It has blocked or rerouted multiple cables with Chinese involvement (Brock, [Citation2023a](#)). Another part of the US strategy has been to influence the investment and contracting decisions of cable consortia. It successfully intervened in the construction of the SeaMeWe-6 project, a major cable system running between Singapore and France, pushing Chinese cable supplier HMN Tech out of the cable laying contract (Brock, [Citation2023a](#)). In another significant instance, the Biden administration lobbied Vietnam to exclude Chinese firms from participating in planned upgrades to the country’s undersea cable infrastructure (Guarascio et al., [Citation2024](#)).

Overall, the political economy of submarine cables has become a battleground for contemporary global rivalries. Geoeconomic competition has found its expression in state measures that aim to effectively alter the trajectory of the submarine cable network. However, the subsequent sections demonstrate that these effects are fragmented as investment decisions by large corporate players remain central drivers of network development.

## 4\. Mapping the global submarine cable network

In the following, we map the submarine cable network to outline how it has been developing under conditions of geoeconomic competition. We do this on the basis of a dataset that amends data from TeleGeography, a provider of telecom-related information, with manually coded information. Specifically, we scraped data from TeleGeography’s regularly updated resource submarinecablemap.com, which contains all active and planned cables, 622 in total as of October 2024.[Footnote <sup>1</sup>](#) The dataset reaches as far back as 1989, which is the ready-for-service (RFS) year of the ‘oldest’ cable in our data. Our dataset includes the following variables for each cable: name, RFS year, cable length, owners, suppliers, and landing points (city and country). Detailed ownership shares are not included as they are often not publicly available. Likewise, the capacity of the cable system – how much data can be carried in a certain amount of time – was not part of the base data set, despite being a crucial metric for assessing strategic significance. To add this important information, we manually researched cable capacities and added verified values to the dataset. In total, we were able to obtain capacity data for 61% of our cables.[Footnote <sup>2</sup>](#) Figure 2 shows how the compiled data looks like in a Python dataframe.

Examining the distribution of cable lengths, we observe a strong right-skew in the data (Figure 4). Around 80% of all cables are shorter than 5,000 km, while a few outliers such as the 2Africa cable, which circles the entire African continent, reach massive lengths of up to 45,000 km. To focus our analysis on the core of the submarine cable network, we apply a cutoff and take only into account cables that are 5,000 km or longer. Submarine cables are not directly connected to each other but landing stations serve as connective nodes. The 5,000 km threshold filters out minor, more regional systems and focuses on long-haul cables that are more vital to the global economy, such as transcontinental cables.[Footnote <sup>3</sup>](#) In contrast, longer submarine cables typically connect continents or major regions and are thus more likely to become the focus of geoeconomic competition. Hence, we take cable length and the total kilometers of cables owned by companies as one key metric to assess the geoeconomic relevance of specific connections and individual actors respectively.

In a next step, we focus on the core of the network, defined above as systems that are at least of 5,000 km length.

Within this core subset, we observe both similarities and differences compared to the full network (Figure 6). Google continues to dominate, reflecting in an even higher presence across systems (16% of core cables). Firms like Global Cloud Xchange and Tata remain key players, retaining significant roles in these long-distance connections. A striking difference is the prominent presence of both Chinese SOEs, China Unicom and China Telecom, and Meta, another major hyperscaler. This might reflect the strategic importance of long-haul cable systems for these types of actors. Yet, even at the core of the global network, ownership is still primarily held by non-state and non-hyperscaler firms, as previously observed.

Figure 6. Largest owners in the core of the global submarine cable network.To better contextualize these patterns of ownership, we turn to cable capacity as an indicator of how ‘powerful’ a cable system is in terms of potential data traffic per time unit.

Figure 7 shows the distribution of cable capacities in our data. In total, we found capacity information for 61% of the cables in our initial sample. The capacity distribution is even more unequal than the cable length distribution (Gini of 8.32 compared to 0.6), heavily right-skewed with extreme outliers. A small number of cables has capacities of 500 terabits per second (Tbps) or more, while half of the systems remains below 8 Tbps. Capacity growth over time does not mirror the curve for total cable length (Figure 8). It remains relatively flat until the mid-2010s, after which we see a sharp increase, reflecting the rise of hyperscalers and their demand for high-capacity systems. The average growth rate per year between 2015 and 2025 is more than tenfold compared to the previous decade.[Footnote <sup>5</sup>](#)

Cumulative capacity growth of the submarine cable network over time (in terabits per second).Looking at ownership of cable capacity, we again observe both differences and similarities to the previous findings. On the one hand, many top capacity holders own only one or two cables. In fact, seven of the ten largest capacity owners fall into this category (Figure 9). Moreover, high-capacity cables tend to be quite short: the top three systems are under 200 km, and only three out of the top ten are longer than 5,000 km. Owners like EllaLink or Crosslake Fibre are not global players but own a few systems in their respective world regions. A final difference is that most large-capacity cables are *not* built by consortia – only five of the twenty cable systems with the highest capacity have more than one owner – which is an important distinction from the collaborative models of the 1990s and 2000s. One obvious similarity to the cable length perspective is that hyperscalers (Meta and Google) own high capacities while also being present in a double-digit number of systems.

Figure 9. Top 10 owners by cable capacity and their respective owner count in the full sample.Among cables with a capacity of 200 Tbps or more, Google is involved in thirteen out of 50, including sole ownership of ten systems – all of which have been starting service recently or are nearing operability. This underscores Google’s ongoing central role in the expansion of the global cable network. Meta also plays a significant role. It is the single owner of Anjana, the highest capacity transatlantic cable to date, and has become involved in several smaller consortia. Notably, Meta and Google frequently collaborate on major infrastructure projects connecting to East and South East Asia (Apricot, Echo, Pacific Light Cable Network) and Europe (Havfrue/AEC-2). Generally, this reflects their shared interest in increasing connectivity between the US and other economic centers. However, this should not obscure the fact that the expansion of advanced fiber-optic networks in the Global South is also driven by regional investors. Two standout projects are Medusa and Asia Connect Cable-1 (ACC-1). Medusa is currently under construction in the Mediterranean and will connect Northern Africa with several European cable hubs, providing a capacity of 480 Tbps. Medusa is fully owned by African internet service provider AFR-IX Telecom. ACC-1, a 19,000 km trans-Pacific system with 256 Tbps capacity, will link the US to South East Asia and Australia and is financed by Singaporean investor Inlingo Networks.

Another striking observation is that Chinese players (China Mobile, China Telecom, China Unicom) have a more limited presence in high-capacity networks. They are involved in a few major projects, such as India Asia Xpress (IAX) and India Europe Xpress (IEX), both in partnership with India’s Reliance Jio Infocomm. The Pakistan & East Africa Connecting Europe (PEACE) cable, linking France to Singapore via connections *inter alia* in Egypt, Saudi-Arabia, Kenya, and Pakistan, constitutes the most significant Chinese presence in the transregional cable sector. In sum, the high-capacity segment of the global cable network reveals three key dynamics: the growing influence of US hyperscalers, particularly Google; the increasing concentration of capacity in the hands of a small number of telecom ‘champions’; and the limits to SOEs’ development of high-capacity networks.

### 4.2. Construction and supply of the submarine cable network

We now turn to supplier firms as the second key category of actors. The manufacturing of tens of thousands of kilometers of cables and their deployment in the depths of the oceans requires immense operational capabilities. Hence, cable construction and laying is the most oligopolistic segment of the submarine cable sector. As of 2022, only 60 cable ships existed worldwide (International Cable Protection Committee, [Citation2022](#)). These ships are costly to maintain, and demand for them is cyclical, spiking during major global buildouts and dropping in less active phases. Consequently, just four global suppliers possess the in-house capability to both manufacture and lay submarine cables: SubCom, a US subsea telecommunications company; Alcatel Submarine Networks (ASN), a former subsidiary of French telecommunications firm Alcatel; NEC, a Japanese IT and electronics firm; and HMN Tech, previously a subsidiary of Chinese tech giant Huawei under the name of Huawei Marine Networks. As McGeachy ([Citation2022](#), p. 162) aptly notes, ‘in short, cables are essential, expensive, and not many companies produce them’. The recent boom in cable deployment has led experts to warn of a ‘cable ship capacity crunch’ due to rising demand against a small and aging fleet (Swinhoe, [Citation2022](#)).

SubCom’s history can be retraced to AT&T’s marine unit, which was involved in building the first transatlantic telephone cable (TAT-1) between Scotland and Newfoundland in 1955. From its early days, the company maintained close ties with the US military. During the Cold War, it was contracted to construct the Navy’s Sound Surveillance System (SOSUS), an underwater network of hydrophones to detect Soviet submarines. In 1997, AT&T sold its cable-laying operations and fleet to Tyco International, a security systems firm. In 2018, New York-based Cerberus Capital Management acquired the company, now branded SubCom. The cooperation has continued since, with SubCom remaining the preferred contractor for US military projects (Brock, [Citation2023b](#)).

ASN has a legacy dating back to the nineteenth century through the Société Générale des Télégraphes Sous-Marins, one of the pioneering companies in undersea telegraphy. French telecommunications company Alcatel merged with several early telegraph companies and acquired cable manufacturing sites, eventually formalizing ASN as a subsidiary in 1994. ASN began operating its own cable-laying fleet in the 2000s, quickly establishing itself as a market leader. In 2015, its parent company Alcatel-Lucent was bought up by Finnish firm Nokia. ASN is a particularly relevant case as it has recently become state-owned (Abels, [Citation2025](#)). In June 2024, the French government announced that its Agence des participations de l’État (APE) would acquire ASN from Nokia (Ministère de l’Économie, [Citation2024](#)). The deal, worth €350 million, was finalized in November 2024.

Founded in 2008 as Huawei Marine Networks, HMN Tech was initially a joint venture between Huawei and Global Marine Systems. It focused on underserved markets in South East Asia and South America, quickly gaining ground. In 2020, the company rebranded as HMN Tech after Chinese cable operator Hengtong had acquired a majority stake. In light of geoeconomic tensions, the US warned against working with HMN Tech, citing data security concerns. In 2019, both Huawei and HMN Tech were blacklisted under the US Clean Network initiative, imposing investment and export restrictions for the companies (Alper & Psaledakis, [Citation2021](#)).

We identify a total of 19 firms, yet our analysis confirms that the market is highly concentrated. The ‘big four’ suppliers account for nearly 85% of all global submarine cables currently active or under construction (Figure 10).

Figure 10. Distribution of the global suppliers of submarine cable systems by kilometers supplied.HMN Tech is the smallest by cable length, responsible for just 4.5% of total kilometers laid. If measured by the number of cables, not their length, HMN Tech is involved in 10.3% of global systems, close to NEC’s 12.5%, though still trailing SubCom (21.1%) and ASN (37.7%). This is already considerable, given that HMN Tech does not even appear in our data until 2010. While cable length is a useful metric for identifying suppliers involved in transnational infrastructures, we can also again look at cable capacity as another important measure (Figure 11).

Figure 11. Distribution of global suppliers of submarine cable systems by cable capacity supplied.Here, Swedish supplier Hexatronic lands among the top four of suppliers due to its focus on high-capacity, short-distance cables in Scandinavia. From a supplier perspective, the global high-capacity fiber-optic network is currently built with minimal Chinese involvement. Among the 94 cables in our database offering capacities of more than 50 Tbps, ASN supplied 28, SubCom 26, NEC 14, and HMN Tech only 6. Figure 12 presents a time series of cable length and capacity deployed by HMN Tech. It shows that, after a remarkable catching-up process starting 2017, the Chinese company’s supply activity has slowed down after 2021. This data point matches the timeline of US sanctions and the adding of HMN Tech to the entity list of the US Commerce Department in December 2021, which effectively barred it from access to US technology. There are at least six cases where the US has directly interfered with cable building contracts to exclude HMN tech specifically (Brock, [Citation2023a](#)). Hence, HMN Tech saw its total market share drop from over a quarter of all building and repairing activities to well under 10% after the US began to actively exclude the firm (Brock, [Citation2023a](#); FCC, [Citation2020](#)). These actions are reflected in the stagnation we see after 2021, whereas HMN Tech was widely regarded as one of the fastest-growing suppliers before US sanctioning began (Runde et al., [Citation2024](#)).

Figure 12. Cumulative length and capacity growth of the cables supplied by HMN Tech over time.## 5\. Discussion: submarine cables in an era of geoeconomic competition

One central insight from our analysis is that intensifying geoeconomic competition is only partially reflected in ownership patterns within the submarine cable network. Particularly in high-capacity networks, US-based hyperscalers have evolved from major customers of transmission capacity to initiators and owners of large, transregional cable projects. The financial muscle of firms like Google enables them to independently realize costly infrastructure developments or to partner in small consortia. Yet, not all hyperscalers are expanding their influence at the same pace, if at all. Based on our findings, Google and, to a lesser extent, Meta are the most relevant actors in this category. US firms are also not as dominant as they are in areas like cloud infrastructure, as the cable network continues to be shaped by companies from regions such as Europe, India, China, and the Middle East. Across the broader network, private telecommunication companies remain the major group of actors driving infrastructure development, albeit within a more concentrated market. This is an important finding amid widespread narratives about hyperscalers and tech firms reshaping the internet. Traditional telecom carriers operate with strategic priorities that differ from those of hyperscalers: they prefer secure, affordable, and reliable cables to deliver services to customers and sell excess capacities. Their continued presence in substantial parts of the global cable network suggests that geostrategic ambitions of other actors cannot easily be imposed upon these network structures – even though states retain more or less pronounced shares in telecommunication companies as well. One indicator of states getting more involved in contested networks is the modest but notable rise in state ownership in the cable industry. While Russian, Chinese, and other SOEs are not among the dominant players, the fact that some appear among the top bracket of various metrics signals a growing influence – assuming current trends persist. Two decades ago, these actors were virtually absent from the landscape. Today, their participation is increasingly driven by strategic goals aligned with national geoeconomic interests, in contrast to the commercial priorities of traditional telecom operators. Still, Chinese companies’ footprint in the current buildout remains modest compared to their US counterparts.

Network topography, specifically the establishment of novel links, also reflects an admixture of market dynamic with geoeconomic influences. Hyperscalers like Google and Meta are moving their data centers closer to users to provide their services with less latency, building cables that serve as a backbone to their services. One effect of these content delivery networks, coupled with the rise of economic centers beyond the West, has been a geographical decentralization: regional networks have been spread around the globe to meet local demand for web services and reduce the load on central hubs.

US–China rivalry has been an intervening factor into investors’ decisions about which cables will be built. A notable example is the Pacific Light Cable Network, a large transpacific cable owned by Google and Meta, initially planned to connect the US to Hong Kong. The US Federal Communications Commission (FCC) denied approval for these plans, and the cable was instead rerouted to Taiwan and the Philippines, leaving the Hong Kong segment unused on the ocean floor. Other instances have been reported where the US kept hyperscalers from establishing cable links to Chinese territory (Brock, [Citation2023a](#)). For example, the Apricot cable, expected to be ready for service in 2027 and owned by a small consortium including Google and Meta, will connect the US territory of Guam with many Asian economic centers, but notably routes around Hong Kong. However, while the impact of geoeconomic competition on network development is significant, it should not be overestimated. There is a clear selection bias: we hear more about cases where state intervention was effective than about those where it was not. Chinese and US firms have continued to cooperate on several projects, even under conditions of intensifying geoeconomic competition. Both the FASTER (ready for service 2016) and the New Cross Pacific (NCP) cable (ready for service 2018) connect China to the US. They involve major Chinese SOEs China Mobile and China Telecom as well as US tech firms (Google in the case of FASTER, Microsoft in the case of NCP). Meta has been cooperating with Chinese SOEs and Taiwanese player Chunghwa Telecom on regional initiatives Asia Pacific Gateway (ready for service 2016) and Southeast Asia-Japan Cable 2 (ready for service 2025), linking Taiwan, China, Japan, and other regional players. This can be interpreted as a sign that the commercial logic in the submarine cable market remains to a substantial degree intact and that US intervention, while effective, is fragmented.

Interestingly, most of the aforementioned projects shy away from contracting either SubCom or HMN Tech as cable supply, instead converging on NEC as a ‘compromise supplier’. Generally, the market for manufacturing and laying submarine fiber-optic cable is characterized by limited supply and has been more holistically restructured by geoeconomic competition. Supplier firms provide states with a degree of influence over the planning and execution of future cable projects. A larger market share for domestic cable manufacturers also ensures a reliable supply of cables and cable laying capacity. Furthermore, cable production and deployment is especially vulnerable to interference as firms could ‘potentially insert backdoors, install surveillance equipment, and place disruption triggers into the components of a cable before the cable is deployed’ (Bueger et al., [Citation2022](#), p. 31). This is another reason why states carefully pick suppliers.

Initially, Chinese subsidies and diplomatic efforts established HMN Tech as an promising competitor to market leaders ASN and SubCom. However, our analysis confirms that the US strategy to contain the Chinese company has had an impact (Figure 12). Contracts awarded to HMN Tech have declined as investors increasingly avoid HMN Tech, fearing they could lose US tech firms as customers or be affected by sanctions. In consequence, HMN Tech has shifted its focus more strongly toward Africa and Asia. The PEACE cable stands out as a notable example. 25,000 kilometer in length, it links France to Singapore via connections inter alia in Egypt, Saudi-Arabia, Kenya, and Pakistan. PEACE has been supplied by HMN Tech and is owned by a subsidiary of Chinese cable firm Hengtong, partnering with state-owned China Mobile, China Telecom, and China Unicom.

SubCom’s integration into the US military-industrial ecosystem has placed it in direct opposition to HMN Tech. Alignment is key here: a main pillar of US strategy has been to secure important contracts for SubCom, hence the company has benefited strongly from the relationship (Brock, [Citation2023a](#)). In 2022, Google and SubCom signed a long-term deal for SubCom to manufacture and deploy what is supposed to become the world’s largest data network, privately owned by Google (Brock, [Citation2023b](#)). The consequences of France’s acquisition of ASN are not yet foreseeable, yet it indicates that European countries recognize the sector for submarine cable construction as a field of strategic competition (Abels, [Citation2025](#)). While the US government has ensured the alignment of SubCom through diplomatic and regulatory efforts in its favour, France deemed nationalization to be expedient.

The behaviour of supply firms, in turn, reflects a mix of commercial interests – capitalizing on a booming market – and political-institutional ties to their home governments. In this complex environment, where profit considerations and geoeconomic interventions intersect, investors do not choose their supplier firms freely but are being pulled in the direction of specific providers by state sanctions, security considerations, and technological dependencies.

## 6\. Conclusion

A core characteristic of media and academic debates about the geoeconomic age is the notion that the US and China ‘divide’ global economic networks between them, from supply chains and production networks to digital and material infrastructures. Our analysis of the political economy of submarine cables reveals a more nuanced picture, highlighting that the commercial logic of the submarine cable sector serves as an obstacle to the ‘cable wars’. While geoeconomic competition between the US and China is indeed *influencing* these networks, its effects remain partial and fragmented. The global submarine cable network represents a market-grown infrastructure that is still largely owned and operated by private corporations. Corporate power consolidated during the era of neoliberal globalization, and it does not simply make way for state interests in an age of geoeconomic competition. States influence these networks to their advantage, yet market structures serve as a mediating factor to these efforts. What is more, the overarching trends we observe in the network reflect that private corporations act as partially autonomous network builders, pursuing infrastructure expansion according to their commercial interests.

We illustrated how the global submarine cable network has expanded in three phases: one driven by recently privatized telecommunication giants in the 1990s, a second by upcoming players from emerging digital economies in the Global South, and a recent one by the immense data demands of hyperscalers, primarily US tech firms. Our analysis shows that these groups of actors remain central to the ownership of the core cable network. Efforts by the US and China to exert influence over the network have not fundamentally altered these structures, although Chinese strategy has introduced SOEs as central players. Both the US and China have focused their strategic efforts on the supply side. China’s attempt to establish HMN Tech on a global scale has so far been mostly unsuccessful, particularly due to US countermeasures confining the firm’s operations to the Global South. In contrast, the US has positioned SubCom as a preferred contractor for large cable projects, particularly for US tech firms. We view this transformation of the cable supply market as generally supporting our argument that alignment between central firms and government strategy constitutes a key factor mediating the impact of geoeconomic competition on global networks.

We also outlined that geoeconomic competition has reshaped network topography, but that these effects again remain fragmented. The US has also taken steps to separate its cable network from direct links to mainland China, using sanctions to reshape cable routes. Despite their initial resistance, US tech firms have begun bypassing China in some of their more recent projects, but not in others.

Based on our analysis, what are possible future scenarios for the development of this global infrastructure? Chinese projects increasingly focus on connecting South-East Asia and forging links to Europe via the Arabian Sea and the Middle East. If this trend continues, one pathway for the global submarine cable network is a decrease in links between the two rivaling states and an intensification of building efforts centered around already established connections. This might amount to the infrastructural equivalent to economic decoupling. However, our analysis demonstrates that the submarine cable network as a market-grown, privately-owned structure is open to expansion, yet relatively resilient to rapid, fundamental change. We see signs of an *expansion* of the network in the form of a ‘doubling’ of existing connections. In a respective scenario, the US and China would provide incentives for investors to build parallel cables along the same or similar routes, involving US and Chinese supplier firms and investors respectively. We already observe instances of this. SubCom is currently laying the SeaMeWe-6 cable along a route from France to Singapore, mirroring the path of the Chinese-owned PEACE cable supplied by HMN Tech. Hyperscalers, first and foremost Google and Meta, are building their own proprietary networks, adding novel connections to the cable topography. This trend supports the notion that the global network could expand rather than fragment in the future. Which of those scenarios is going to materialize will be decided by the strategic choices not only of the states involved, but crucially also the firms that own, operate and build the submarine cable network.

We provided a first empirical basis to analyze the political economy of the submarine cable network by introducing our comprehensive dataset and using it to map the changing network topography. On the basis of this data, the article also developed some overarching claims about the mutual effects of geoeconomic competition and market-grown networks. It is beyond the scope of the article to examine causal mechanisms, for instance on how and when corporations escape state control, the strategic motives of hyperscalers and telecommunication firms, or how other powers respond to US-Chinese rivalry. We hope that our findings can serve as a foundation for future inquiries into these issues through more granular, significant case studies.

The submarine cable network should also not be viewed in isolation but as part of a larger architecture of global communication infrastructures where other technologies play a role. For instance, internet satellite constellations in Low Earth Orbit are developing rapidly, offering more secure communication, although they currently lack the capacity to replace submarine cables. In contrast to the more diversified cable sector, satellite communications as a business field are strongly dominated by US tech firms (Abels, [Citation2024](#)). More and broader empirical research into different types of actors and infrastructures involved in geoeconomic competition is key to better understand our day and age. Our study contributes one item to such an agenda by emphasizing both the centrality of the submarine cable network for geoeconomic competition; as well as the role played by private actors such as telecom giants, tech companies, and investors. These private entities remain the primary driving force behind the network’s expansion and should receive greater attention in future inquiries and theory-building.

[^1]: ## 1\. Introduction

Once regarded a seemingly ‘neutral’ foundation for an integrating global economy, transnational infrastructures have become contested terrains of geoeconomic competition for power, control, and profit (Abels & Bieling, [Citation2024](#); Bernards & Campbell-Verduyn, [Citation2019](#); Schindler et al., [Citation2024](#)). Especially submarine fiber-optic cables have recently attracted greater attention as a strategic arena for state competition (Bueger & Liebetrau, [Citation2021](#); Burdette, [Citation2021](#); Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#); Abels, [Citation2025](#)). This heightened interest is hardly surprising: submarine cables are the defining communication infrastructure of the digital age, carrying more than 97 percent of global internet traffic and enabling a tremendous $10 trillion in financial transactions daily (Rossiter, [Citation2023](#); Stronge, [Citation2023](#)). But this is only the beginning. Over the past two decades, the world has seen a renewed boom in submarine cable construction. The global mushrooming of data centers to fuel advances in capacity-hungry Artificial Intelligence (AI) and cloud applications multiplies the demand for bandwidth. The relentless digitalization of our wallets, workplaces, and private lives threatens to put even more pressure on energy and digital infrastructure systems. Contrary to popular imagination, the rising demand will, for the foreseeable future, not be met by communication satellites in the sky, but by the hard-wired infrastructures buried beneath the seabed (Starosielski, [Citation2015](#)). This is also discernible from projections that expect the submarine cable market to grow from roughly $13 billion in 2022 to over $30 billion in 2030, promising annual growth rates of nearly 12% (Insight Partners, [Citation2023](#)).

Despite their critical role, our knowledge about submarine cable networks remains relatively meagre and scattered. Some studies have provided us with insights into their geographical unevenness (Malecki & Wei, [Citation2009](#)); cultural and environmental aspects (Starosielski, [Citation2015](#)); technological developments (Thomas et al., [Citation2015](#)); security aspects (Bueger & Liebetrau, [Citation2021](#)); and strategic dimensions (McGeachy, [Citation2022](#)). Our limited knowledge notwithstanding, the material relevance of submarine cable systems is growing and it intersects with intensifying geoeconomic rivalries (Babić et al., [Citation2022](#)). Submarine cable systems have become a battleground for the rivalry of Chinese and US interests as well as other regional players. An amalgamation of security and geoeconomic motives plays a role. The Snowden revelations have demonstrated that submarine cables can be (and factually are being) tapped for surveillance purposes (Khazan, [Citation2013](#)). Cables have also become the target of sabotage, prominently in the Baltic Sea (Goldenziel, [Citation2025](#)), and the possibility exists that such attacks could extend to cable laying ships or landing points (Bueger & Liebetrau, [Citation2021](#)). Yet, states have not just shown an interest in weaponizing or manipulating the cable network, but also in influencing its further expansion. China has relied on public subsidies to increase its cable connections and gain a stronger footing in the cable-laying industry. The US has used diplomatic pressure and economic statecraft to prevent this (Brock, [Citation2023a](#)), even blocking several major cable projects from landing in Hong Kong. Still, state strategies – the ‘cable wars’ – unfold within a political-economic context where private corporations dominate nearly every commercial aspect of the network, from cable planning and laying to managing capacity. Hence, we ask how geoeconomic competition impacts on the patterns of ownership and supply in the submarine cable network – and vice versa. To address this, we pick up on concepts brought forward in this Special Issue viewing geoeconomics as ‘the study of global struggles for economic expansion and control’ (introduction to this Special Issue). Most significantly – and in contrast to perspectives focused more strongly on the competition of nation states, like great power rivalry, imperialism, or techno-nationalism – we conceptualize corporations as key actors in this struggle. We argue that corporations do not constitute ‘instruments‘ that states can, more or less successfully, employ for their goals. In large parts of the literature, like weaponized interdependence, such a view of geoeconomic competition is dominant: states may have a harder time manipulating networks in some cases, but the power hierarchy remains clear (see, e.g. Farrell & Newman, [Citation2019](#); Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#)). Against this backdrop, we show empirically that, in the case of submarine cables, it is global corporations that own and control large parts of the infrastructure networks which become subject to geoeconomic contestation. By implication, prevailing corporate control poses obstacles to state efforts to manipulate the network. Geoeconomic rivalry is decisively shaped by the juxtaposition of states and corporations, not merely by the instrumentalization of one by the other. State and corporate interests may align or diverge. Depending on their position in the network, corporations will have vested interests in state interventions either distorting competition in their favour and edging out foreign rivals; or leaving their sector largely to their own strategic activity. In short: corporate agency matters for the future of the submarine cable network.

To make our claims plausible, we provide the first empirical mapping of the political economy of the submarine fiber cable network. In debates about submarine cables, the actual ownership and control patterns over time are either not discussed, only indirectly addressed through (regional) case studies, or anecdotally described (see, e.g. Bueger & Liebetrau, [Citation2021](#); Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#); Malecki & Wei, [Citation2009](#)). One central contribution of our article is to fill this gap by introducing a novel open-access dataset through which we can explore the political economy of the submarine cable network amid intensifying geoeconomic competition. The dataset combines ownership and supplier data on over 600 submarine cable systems from provider TeleGeography with manually collected capacity information on these systems. Through analyzing the data, amended with qualitative background information from media reports, we show that the expansion and ownership of the submarine cable network is still overwhelmingly driven by private corporate actors. In line with previous studies, we find that hyperscalers – large tech companies like Google and Meta that offer cloud services at a global scale – indeed play an increasingly prominent role in building high-capacity connections (Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#); Winseck, [Citation2017](#)). However, far from being the only or even dominant type of owner, major telecom carriers retain a central position in the overall network. Likewise, we observe an increased share of state-owned enterprises (SOEs) in the ownership of high-capacity cables. These findings nuance and challenge the prominent arguments about the fundamental change of the global submarine cable network due to the rise of hyperscalers (Gjesvik, [Citation2023](#)). Beyond ownership patterns, we also map and analyze the cable supply market, which gets often neglected by existing research. We show that cable supply remains highly oligopolistic, yet geoeconomic competition is starting to change its composition as Chinese player HMN Tech, after quickly gaining ground into the market, is currently being pushed out of large-scale contracts.

This study responds to recent calls within International Political Economy (IPE) to empirically scrutinize the ‘geoeconomic turn’ (Babić et al., [Citation2024](#)). It expands our understanding about the relevance – not only influence – of global firms as juxtaposed actors and the infrastructures they control, which is an increasingly crucial, yet still underexplored, area in a field long dominated by state-centric analyses (Abels, [Citation2024](#)). By introducing the dataset, it provides our analysis, and follow-up research, with the empirical means to do so. On a theoretical level, it outlines that the era of geoeconomic competition has introduced new layers of conflict into a domain largely shaped by private commercial interests. Governments intervene in networks and adjacent markets to further their geoeconomic and security interests. Yet, they cannot simply steer or instrumentalize them as central non-state network players frequently act according to a different logic. In cases where state and business interests align more clearly, like cable supply, geoeconomic competition can be expected to transform the network more profoundly.

The article is structured as follows: the first section conceptualizes the effects of private business activity on the geoeconomic-infrastructure nexus. The second section depicts the historical dynamics and market forces behind the emergence, expansion, and change of the global fiber-optic network. It also examines how geoeconomic competition, especially between the US and China, has limited effects on this overwhelmingly privately owned network. The third section details the methodology used to map the political-economic features of the submarine cable network and presents our findings on the ownership structures (section [4](#S004).[1](#S001)) and supply market (section [4](#S004).[2](#S002)) of submarine cables. Section four discusses these findings and outlines the strategic orientations of investors, tech firms, and suppliers. Finally, the conclusion sketches out possible scenarios for the future of this vital infrastructure and proposes a research agenda on the political economy of global communication systems.

[^2]: ## 2\. Private infrastructure development and geoeconomic competition

Transnational infrastructures like submarine cables are large technical systems that function as a ‘meso geography’ (Mayer & Acuto, [Citation2015](#), p. 672), mediating between the practices of societal actors and the broader structures of the geoeconomic order. They are not just tools of connectivity but historical artifacts that embody past power relations, projecting them into the present and shaping today’s actions (de Goede & Westermeier, [Citation2022](#)). Many cables have been laid in the 1990s, a period marked by the financialization of infrastructure and speculative investment in the ICT sector. While early cable systems cannot compete with newer ones in terms of capacity, the investment decisions made during that era continue to shape the geographical contours of the global network. This concerns the prominence of infrastructure hubs, regional disparities in internet access, and the vulnerabilities and underdevelopment of less connected areas. In return, today’s decisions, both deliberate and unintentional, reconfigure the global network, with far-reaching implications for patterns of ownership and dependency. Amid rising geoeconomic competition, which this Special Issue understands as global struggles for economic expansion and control, infrastructures are at the core of global contentions. As actors are faced with exclusionary practices on the part of competitors, they tend to react with their own strategic measures seeking to reshape networks to their advantage (introduction to this Special Issue). Prominent contributions to the debate frame these dynamics primarily in terms of intentional state-led interventions that instrumentalize networks for strategic purposes (Baldwin, [Citation2020](#); Farrell & Newman, [Citation2019](#); Gertz & Evers, [Citation2020](#)). US and Chinese foreign economic policies have been the main empirical focus for perspectives that view major powers as driving network development (Beeson, [Citation2018](#); Ho, [Citation2020](#); Weiss & Thurbon, [Citation2018](#)). Yet, recent contributions have warned not to underestimate the strategic agency of private corporations that often stand at the center of modern infrastructure networks and adjacent tech sectors. Scholars have outlined that these corporations have amassed the capital and influence to pursue independent agendas (Abels, [Citation2024](#); Rikap, [Citation2024](#)).

In this article, we address the question of how this complex interplay of state and corporate strategizing affects the geoeconomic competition over global networks. As an essential infrastructure of the modern global economy, submarine cables are a case worthy of investigation in this regard. During the era of neoliberal globalization, the development of economic and material interlinkages – at the core of which stand global infrastructure networks – followed the whims of private investment rather than that of states. Cost considerations led to the concentration of cable centers in select regions, insufficient protection of landing zones, and supplier contracts awarded according to the lowest bidder principle (Starosielski, [Citation2015](#)). Available funds were channeled into speculative projects promising high returns on investment, rather than aligning with long-term state objectives. While recent shifts in the global order have been rapid and far-reaching, more inert and immobile infrastructure networks have responded only with significant delay. Subsea fiber-optic cables are fixed, material infrastructures, made of steel, glass fiber, and plastic, that reflect past decisions and constrain present possibilities.

We contend that private corporations as developers and owners of infrastructure networks play a central role in deciding how strongly interstate competition is able to reshape the control and topography of these networks. Our article contributes to a growing body of literature, mostly from political economy, that revaluates the influence of transnational corporations by highlighting their structural power (Braun, [Citation2022](#); Rafi Atal, [Citation2021](#)) and technological capabilities (Abels, [Citation2024](#); Rikap, [Citation2024](#)). We stress the importance to consider corporations’ roles in the evolution of global networks and the underlying patterns of ownership and control. Corporations continue to shape geoeconomically relevant infrastructures in ways that escape direct state governance. This applies in particular to infrastructures whose ties and nodes are located in different regions, in international waters, or even outer space. While such corporations are not ‘non-territorial’ (Lee et al., [Citation2018](#)), their cross-border operations limit the ability of individual states to govern or regulate their infrastructures. Privatization and fiscal constraints pressure governments to rely on private capital for infrastructure development, in the process ceding substantial decisions over the design and operation of said infrastructures to private actors (Wigger, [Citation2024](#)). Given the tendency toward concentration in global (especially digital) markets, this has also led to consolidation of power in the hands of a few dominant players.

In the case of submarine cables, international, mostly private consortia have been central to the historical emergence and initial structuring of the network. As our analysis shows, this continues to be the case even under conditions of intensifying geoeconomic competition. Companies like Google and Meta, alongside major telecommunication companies, are leading a new wave of submarine cable network expansion, transforming the very logic by which these cables are financed and laid. These firms are capable of fully funding cable projects, which they integrate into their proprietary networks to support cloud-based services. Instead of sharing capacity, for example within consortia, these actors seek to control large parts of the material architecture of the global internet.

Geoeconomic competition is now entering the equation. States, in particular the great powers US and China, have displayed an interest in reshaping the network in accordance with their security and geoeconomic interests. One aspect is that they seek to manipulate cable connections to ready them for weaponization – surveillance and sabotage being the main factors here – or to increase their resilience to weaponization attempts by global rivals. As our empirical analysis illustrates, this has changed some central parameters of network evolution. First, it propels the activity of SOEs within these networks as way of foreign political intervention (Stone et al., [Citation2021](#)). Second, states seek to leverage corporations’ reliance on favourable regulatory and financial conditions to manipulate their business decisions. At the same time, while the US and China manage to change the political economy and topography of the subsea network, considerable obstacles keep them from achieving this more holistically. As stated before, this has to do with the material inertia of global networks. Another major factor to consider is the influence of central network actors that prefer different outcomes. For instance, our analysis underlines that the US government has been able to intervene in tech firms’ plans to build cables that connect to China in some instances, but not in others. It has been a recurring theme in scholarship on transnational corporations that they pursue a profit-seeking logic – as some argue, even amended with broader political objectives (Abels, [Citation2024](#)) – that differ from the geostrategic logic of states (Rafi Atal, [Citation2021](#); Rikap, [Citation2024](#)). At the core of this article stands the argument that corporations should not just be considered intermediaries of state action, but at least partially autonomous builders of networks.

As a second key argument, we stress that the success of state action within these networks depends on its alignment with the strategic orientation of the corporations it centers on. This is particularly true in an era of transnational corporations that are ‘domestic’ in terms of being headquartered in a country, but whose production networks, business operations, and assets are diversified and spread around the globe (Babić et al., [Citation2017](#)). These firms adopt strategies that either hedge their geopolitical risks or align with a certain party (Choer Moraes & Wigell, [Citation2022](#); Schindler & Rolf, [Citation2025](#)). Depending on their position in the network, corporations can have vested interests in aligning their strategic decisions with state interventions that distort competition in their favour and edge out foreign rivals. In these cases, the impact of geoeconomic competition on the network can be expected to be more substantial. Out article illustrates this using the example of the cable supply sector: Chinese market intervention has established cable laying company HMN Tech as a new sectoral player, but the alignment of US supplier SubCom with its government’s foreign policy kept HMN Tech from further increasing its market share. Therefore, geoeconomic forces are reshaping the supply sector more substantially than ownership structures. Generally, US and Chinese financial, political, and regulatory pull will increase chances for corporations to align their strategies with them. In other instances, states’ strategic interests threaten the profitability and growth strategies of structurally powerful private firms. This will offer incentives for these firms to circumvent state intervention by expanding network links in ways that serve their own interest more. Hence, the alignment of central corporate actors indeed acts as a precondition for a successful shaping and instrumentalization of the network through state actors.

Our analysis of the submarine cable dataset serves the purpose of mapping the network’s political economy in terms of patterns of growth, ownership, and supply. It is, to that extent, exploratory. However, our analysis has theoretical purchase insofar as it underlines that the observed dynamics in submarine cable markets largely correspond with the arguments presented in this section about corporations’ role in global networks. It hence also paves the way to more case-based and fine-grained investigations that might allow for a refinement of the proposed mechanisms.

[^3]: ## 4\. Mapping the global submarine cable network

In the following, we map the submarine cable network to outline how it has been developing under conditions of geoeconomic competition. We do this on the basis of a dataset that amends data from TeleGeography, a provider of telecom-related information, with manually coded information. Specifically, we scraped data from TeleGeography’s regularly updated resource submarinecablemap.com, which contains all active and planned cables, 622 in total as of October 2024.[Footnote <sup>1</sup>](#) The dataset reaches as far back as 1989, which is the ready-for-service (RFS) year of the ‘oldest’ cable in our data. Our dataset includes the following variables for each cable: name, RFS year, cable length, owners, suppliers, and landing points (city and country). Detailed ownership shares are not included as they are often not publicly available. Likewise, the capacity of the cable system – how much data can be carried in a certain amount of time – was not part of the base data set, despite being a crucial metric for assessing strategic significance. To add this important information, we manually researched cable capacities and added verified values to the dataset. In total, we were able to obtain capacity data for 61% of our cables.[Footnote <sup>2</sup>](#) Figure 2 shows how the compiled data looks like in a Python dataframe.

### 4.1. Ownership and control in the submarine cable network

Examining the distribution of cable lengths, we observe a strong right-skew in the data (Figure 4). Around 80% of all cables are shorter than 5,000 km, while a few outliers such as the 2Africa cable, which circles the entire African continent, reach massive lengths of up to 45,000 km. To focus our analysis on the core of the submarine cable network, we apply a cutoff and take only into account cables that are 5,000 km or longer. Submarine cables are not directly connected to each other but landing stations serve as connective nodes. The 5,000 km threshold filters out minor, more regional systems and focuses on long-haul cables that are more vital to the global economy, such as transcontinental cables.[Footnote <sup>3</sup>](#) In contrast, longer submarine cables typically connect continents or major regions and are thus more likely to become the focus of geoeconomic competition. Hence, we take cable length and the total kilometers of cables owned by companies as one key metric to assess the geoeconomic relevance of specific connections and individual actors respectively.

Figure 4. Scatterplot (left) and Cumulative Distribution Function (right) of the cable length data in kilometers (ten longest cables marked in scatterplot).Display full size

Since precise ownership shares are mostly unobtainable [Footnote <sup>4</sup>](#), we use several alternative metrics to account for the owner and supplier rankings within the network. For the ownership analysis, we first consider the whole sample and identify the largest owners based on the following metrics: how often an owner appears in total among all owners; in how many distinct cable systems an owner appears; how many kilometers of cable they own based on an assumed equal share in consortia; as well as how many systems they wholly own (Figure 5).

Figure 5. Largest owners in the global submarine cable network.Display full size

Across these indicators, Google emerges as the most prominent player, appearing most frequently as general *and* single owner, holding the largest share of total cable length, and participating in more than five percent of all systems. This highlights its role as *primus inter pares* among global hyperscalers. It also points to significant shifts in favour of global corporate giants that occurred within the submarine cable market over the last decades. However, previous market structures did not disappear overnight: telecom carriers such as Orange, Telefonica, Vodafone, and Tata Communications still remain central to the network. Most owners featured in Figure 5 represent telecom firms from diverse regions, with an emphasis on European providers.

In a next step, we focus on the core of the network, defined above as systems that are at least of 5,000 km length.

Within this core subset, we observe both similarities and differences compared to the full network (Figure 6). Google continues to dominate, reflecting in an even higher presence across systems (16% of core cables). Firms like Global Cloud Xchange and Tata remain key players, retaining significant roles in these long-distance connections. A striking difference is the prominent presence of both Chinese SOEs, China Unicom and China Telecom, and Meta, another major hyperscaler. This might reflect the strategic importance of long-haul cable systems for these types of actors. Yet, even at the core of the global network, ownership is still primarily held by non-state and non-hyperscaler firms, as previously observed.

Looking at ownership of cable capacity, we again observe both differences and similarities to the previous findings. On the one hand, many top capacity holders own only one or two cables. In fact, seven of the ten largest capacity owners fall into this category (Figure 9). Moreover, high-capacity cables tend to be quite short: the top three systems are under 200 km, and only three out of the top ten are longer than 5,000 km. Owners like EllaLink or Crosslake Fibre are not global players but own a few systems in their respective world regions. A final difference is that most large-capacity cables are *not* built by consortia – only five of the twenty cable systems with the highest capacity have more than one owner – which is an important distinction from the collaborative models of the 1990s and 2000s. One obvious similarity to the cable length perspective is that hyperscalers (Meta and Google) own high capacities while also being present in a double-digit number of systems.

Among cables with a capacity of 200 Tbps or more, Google is involved in thirteen out of 50, including sole ownership of ten systems – all of which have been starting service recently or are nearing operability. This underscores Google’s ongoing central role in the expansion of the global cable network. Meta also plays a significant role. It is the single owner of Anjana, the highest capacity transatlantic cable to date, and has become involved in several smaller consortia. Notably, Meta and Google frequently collaborate on major infrastructure projects connecting to East and South East Asia (Apricot, Echo, Pacific Light Cable Network) and Europe (Havfrue/AEC-2). Generally, this reflects their shared interest in increasing connectivity between the US and other economic centers. However, this should not obscure the fact that the expansion of advanced fiber-optic networks in the Global South is also driven by regional investors. Two standout projects are Medusa and Asia Connect Cable-1 (ACC-1). Medusa is currently under construction in the Mediterranean and will connect Northern Africa with several European cable hubs, providing a capacity of 480 Tbps. Medusa is fully owned by African internet service provider AFR-IX Telecom. ACC-1, a 19,000 km trans-Pacific system with 256 Tbps capacity, will link the US to South East Asia and Australia and is financed by Singaporean investor Inlingo Networks.

Another striking observation is that Chinese players (China Mobile, China Telecom, China Unicom) have a more limited presence in high-capacity networks. They are involved in a few major projects, such as India Asia Xpress (IAX) and India Europe Xpress (IEX), both in partnership with India’s Reliance Jio Infocomm. The Pakistan & East Africa Connecting Europe (PEACE) cable, linking France to Singapore via connections *inter alia* in Egypt, Saudi-Arabia, Kenya, and Pakistan, constitutes the most significant Chinese presence in the transregional cable sector. In sum, the high-capacity segment of the global cable network reveals three key dynamics: the growing influence of US hyperscalers, particularly Google; the increasing concentration of capacity in the hands of a small number of telecom ‘champions’; and the limits to SOEs’ development of high-capacity networks.

### 4.2. Construction and supply of the submarine cable network

We now turn to supplier firms as the second key category of actors. The manufacturing of tens of thousands of kilometers of cables and their deployment in the depths of the oceans requires immense operational capabilities. Hence, cable construction and laying is the most oligopolistic segment of the submarine cable sector. As of 2022, only 60 cable ships existed worldwide (International Cable Protection Committee, [Citation2022](#)). These ships are costly to maintain, and demand for them is cyclical, spiking during major global buildouts and dropping in less active phases. Consequently, just four global suppliers possess the in-house capability to both manufacture and lay submarine cables: SubCom, a US subsea telecommunications company; Alcatel Submarine Networks (ASN), a former subsidiary of French telecommunications firm Alcatel; NEC, a Japanese IT and electronics firm; and HMN Tech, previously a subsidiary of Chinese tech giant Huawei under the name of Huawei Marine Networks. As McGeachy ([Citation2022](#), p. 162) aptly notes, ‘in short, cables are essential, expensive, and not many companies produce them’. The recent boom in cable deployment has led experts to warn of a ‘cable ship capacity crunch’ due to rising demand against a small and aging fleet (Swinhoe, [Citation2022](#)).

SubCom’s history can be retraced to AT&T’s marine unit, which was involved in building the first transatlantic telephone cable (TAT-1) between Scotland and Newfoundland in 1955. From its early days, the company maintained close ties with the US military. During the Cold War, it was contracted to construct the Navy’s Sound Surveillance System (SOSUS), an underwater network of hydrophones to detect Soviet submarines. In 1997, AT&T sold its cable-laying operations and fleet to Tyco International, a security systems firm. In 2018, New York-based Cerberus Capital Management acquired the company, now branded SubCom. The cooperation has continued since, with SubCom remaining the preferred contractor for US military projects (Brock, [Citation2023b](#)).

ASN has a legacy dating back to the nineteenth century through the Société Générale des Télégraphes Sous-Marins, one of the pioneering companies in undersea telegraphy. French telecommunications company Alcatel merged with several early telegraph companies and acquired cable manufacturing sites, eventually formalizing ASN as a subsidiary in 1994. ASN began operating its own cable-laying fleet in the 2000s, quickly establishing itself as a market leader. In 2015, its parent company Alcatel-Lucent was bought up by Finnish firm Nokia. ASN is a particularly relevant case as it has recently become state-owned (Abels, [Citation2025](#)). In June 2024, the French government announced that its Agence des participations de l’État (APE) would acquire ASN from Nokia (Ministère de l’Économie, [Citation2024](#)). The deal, worth €350 million, was finalized in November 2024.

Founded in 2008 as Huawei Marine Networks, HMN Tech was initially a joint venture between Huawei and Global Marine Systems. It focused on underserved markets in South East Asia and South America, quickly gaining ground. In 2020, the company rebranded as HMN Tech after Chinese cable operator Hengtong had acquired a majority stake. In light of geoeconomic tensions, the US warned against working with HMN Tech, citing data security concerns. In 2019, both Huawei and HMN Tech were blacklisted under the US Clean Network initiative, imposing investment and export restrictions for the companies (Alper & Psaledakis, [Citation2021](#)).

We identify a total of 19 firms, yet our analysis confirms that the market is highly concentrated. The ‘big four’ suppliers account for nearly 85% of all global submarine cables currently active or under construction (Figure 10).

From a supplier perspective, the global high-capacity fiber-optic network is currently built with minimal Chinese involvement.
