**Global Financial Integrity (2026), *Trade-Related Illicit Financial Flows in Africa,
2013–2022*.** Value-gap estimates for every Sub-Saharan African nation, built on the
**UN Comtrade** dataset via GFI's **"mirror trade" gap methodology** — comparing a country's
reported exports/imports against its trading partners' mirror figures at the HS6 product level
(with CIF→FOB adjustment and removal of "orphaned"/"ghost" transactions) and treating persistent,
large discrepancies as a proxy for **trade misinvoicing**. GFI notes this is a conservative
goods-only proxy (it excludes services, cash smuggling and other IFF channels), and that not
every gap is illicit — some reflect statistical reporting or timing differences.

**Headline findings (as of the 2013–2022 study period):**

- Total SSA trade value gaps reached a **series high of $152.9bn in 2022** (up from ~$90–115bn/yr
  pre-2020 and $139.8bn in 2021); the region averaged **$112.97bn/yr** over the decade, with **no
  country showing sustained progress** in narrowing gaps.
- **South Africa** had the largest cumulative gap — **$478.0bn** with all partners, **≈42% of the
  SSA total** — reflecting its weight as the region's largest commodity exporter.
- Second tier (cumulative, all partners): **Nigeria ~$77.7bn, Ghana ~$54.1bn, Côte d'Ivoire
  ~$47.7bn, Kenya ~$47.5bn**, then Zambia ~$35.8bn, Tanzania ~$35.5bn, Angola ~$35.4bn, Senegal
  ~$25.5bn, Ethiopia ~$24.6bn. Resource/extractives exporters dominate; **the Gambia** had the
  highest gap **as a share of total trade (44%)**.
- Context cited: UNCTAD (2020) estimated **~$88.6bn/yr** illicit capital leaving Africa (**3.7% of
  GDP**), on par with combined ODA+FDI inflows; the AU/Mbeki High-Level Panel (2015) earlier put
  it at **~$50bn/yr**. Tax-revenue loss ~**$17bn/yr**; high-IFF countries spend on average **25%
  less on health and 58% less on education** than lower-IFF peers.

**Why it's in this wiki:** the value gaps are, at root, a **data-integrity / statistical-quality**
signal in official trade data — a national-statistics ([[data.statistics]]) and open-data
([[data.open]]) governance question, and a cross-border trade-transparency ([[gov.regional]])
one — even though the report's primary subject is illicit finance. Full report:
