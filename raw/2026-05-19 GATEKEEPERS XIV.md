There is a particular type of Kenyan parliamentarian who surfaces reliably whenever a technology bill needs a sponsor. They are often nominated, placed on a party list as a loyalty reward, assigned to committees that make decisions about things they have never studied and may not understand. They make headlines for spectacle, public drama, social media, and tabloid coverage that follows them into international airports and foreign countries, rarely for the kind of legislation that would require what genuine lawmaking demands; deep study, sustained scrutiny of Kenyan conditions, and an honest reckoning with whose interests a bill is actually designed to serve. And on the rare occasion that legislation does arrive, it must genuflect borrowed from Brussels or Washington.

They are genuinely committed to certain things; self protection, political survival, the agenda of whoever secured their nomination, being addressed as muheshimiwa, mukubwa, balozi, daktari, etc the honorifics that function as tributes, extracted daily from Kenyans who cannot afford the cost of withholding them.

The same figure who demands this worship who makes a poor Kenyan kneel for a packet of maize flour at a harambee, will in the presence of tech bros, politicians, ‘investors’ from the west, become suddenly cartoonishly tender grinning ear to ear full set of teeth in display, proudly posing for photographs, shoulders soft, voices warm. when posted abroad and have to present their credentials, dressed up in western garb they bow so grotesquely low they are nearly kissing boots, extending both hands to receive a single handshake cradling it with deference the cupped palms, tilted head, the smile stretched so wide to say see, I am good, I behave like you, I speak like you so will you please let me be close to you, please let me be like you. Fanon diagnosed this as the colonised subject who has internalised the coloniser’s gaze as the only gaze that confers worth, one cannot perform dignity in that presence only audition for it. the full tragic comedy choreography of a person who truly believes they have finally arrived somewhere that counts. Their brutality and contempt is reserved for home, the beaming is for anyone who did not grow up, look or sound African. It is a culture that did not arise freely, it was forged in colonialism, slavery, religion, patriarchy, racial capitalism; the savage humiliating and dehumanising systems that continue to be brutally enforced to punish African dignity and reward African deference. after generations of this enforcement, what has remained is a people who have learned in their bodies and minds, that the bow is safer than the backbone. That lesson is then reproduced through every institution education, religion, colonial administration, the post-independence state, international development complex and much more. It runs so deep that even Kenyan media performs it in newsrooms, podcasts, online shows. anchors and hosts who have never left Kenya reading news, hosting programmes, events and conversations in voices borrowed from CNN or the BBC, as though Kenya requires an American or English accent to be taken seriously.

For the technical demands of what they are asked to sponsor, they are often entirely unequipped and they are not the exception in the legislature, they are the fabric of colonial psychology, still in daily use.

The Kenya AI Bill was introduced by a nominated senator placed on the ruling party’s list. a close ally of the administration, a former member of the Nairobi county assembly, former director of the Nairobi water and sewerage company, with no chapter on technology, data systems, or artificial intelligence.

According to multiple Kenyan outlets including Business Daily, TechWeez and the senator’s public statements, she allegedly introduced the bill after experiencing the circulation of AI-generated manipulated images of herself. This is a real and serious harm that affects women. Citron’s research on technology facilitated abuse, documents how synthetic intimate imagery functions not merely to manipulate, humiliate but to silence. Noble traces how Black women’s bodies are hyper sexualised by the algorithmic systems that produce and distribute this content, the targeting is structural, built into the training data long before the images are generated. Crenshaw names what often follows, gender based violence compounded by racial dehumanisation, experienced simultaneously producing harms that a single framework cannot fully account for. While the personal motivation is legitimate what arrived from it is not a bill about protecting women from synthetic media harassment, it is a regulatory framework for AI in Kenya, built around a powerful new presidential appointment, borrowing its entire structural logic from the European Union.

This piece is about the distance between a woman protecting herself and a national AI governance framework, it is also about the silicon Savanah ecosystem that was supposed to prevent this but was replaced by the philanthropic industrial complex and the governance tradition that taught an entire intermediary class to borrow what it should have built.

The question the Kenya AI Bill demands is who wrote it, who benefits from its architecture, who will pay for its failures, who gains the patronage appointments, who gets the compliance market, who bears the algorithmic harm with no legal standing to complain. Which communities are named in the bill and which are invisible to it. which platforms are disciplined and which are given a fee schedule they can absorb without changing behaviour. Which languages does this Kenyan bill protect and which remain outside the frame of what counts as harm, who decides what harm means and whose understanding of risk is now by legislative transplant Kenyan law. what happens to the systems already running, extracting and causing harm before this bill existed because a commissioner with no retroactive authority cannot protect anyone from yesterday.

**What Did Kenya Build?**

Kenya has never manufactured a semiconductor, trained a foundational AI model, produced a programming language, designed an operating system, nor manufactured the hardware for a data centre. It hosts data centres but does not build what goes inside them. the Kenya National Artificial Intelligence Strategy 2025–2030, lists establishing foundational AI models as a priority action within that strategy period, it remains an aspiration, not an achievement. The 2026 bill aims to regulate a technology stack Kenya did not build, cannot audit, does not control but calls this governance. This is the starting point for any honest analysis of what Kenya can realistically regulate.

What Kenya has is M-Pesa, which is the single genuine infrastructural innovation in the country’s tech history, and even this requires examination. According to MIT’s Centre for Information Systems Research, M-Pesa was conceived by Vodafone’s corporate social responsibility division, built by Sagentia, a UK technology firm, with funding from the UK Department for International Development (DFID), and deployed on GSM infrastructure that Kenya did not design, on a server platform owned and managed by Vodafone, not Safaricom a Cambridge Judge Business School case study corroborates this origin. Kenya provided the market, unbanked population, distribution network, regulatory accommodation, and the political will to let a foreign company operate in what was then an unregulated sector based on a Central Bank of Kenya letter of no objection, while the Communications Commission of Kenya, the Central Bank of Kenya, and the Ministry of Finance negotiated jurisdiction amongst themselves. The National Payment Systems Act that gave formal legal shape to what M-Pesa was already doing came in 2011, four years after launch.

The debate over whether M-Pesa is genuinely Kenyan has circulated in Kenyan media and the blogosphere for over a decade. Foster concludes that the intellectual property right was held outside Kenya from the beginning and that this produced significant South-to-North financial transfers and bottlenecks. That no African or Kenyan scholar has produced equivalent empirical work to refute this history is itself, a comment on the research ecosystem the silicon Savanah produced. Jina ni Kiswahili the architecture is not.

According to African Business, Capital FM Kenya, TechIsh Kenya, People Daily, Mwango Capital and others, Safaricom, which owns M-Pesa, is no longer even nominally Kenyan controlled. In December 2025, Vodacom a South African telecoms group, that is 65.1 percent owned by UK’s Vodafone, increased its direct stake in Safaricom from 40 percent to 55 percent by acquiring shares from both Vodafone and the Kenyan government, which sold down from 35 percent to 20 percent. The Cabinet Secretary for National Treasury and Economic Planning described the transaction as one of the first steps in the President’s stated agenda of innovatively unlocking capital, without increasing taxes or the country’s debt burden, to allow additional investment in critical infrastructure to support future growth. Kenya’s government sold its controlling share of the one technology it could credibly call its own, and called it a growth strategy. Couldry and Mejias describe how this arrangement works at scale; the platform receives the credit, the brand, and the profit while the population that made it possible, whose behaviour, density and needs created the conditions for the product to exist receives the service, fees, and the dependency. And Kwet names the endpoint; once a population’s financial architecture is built on foreign owned infrastructure, the cost of exit becomes prohibitive, and that captivity is the business model.

This was also the period when consequential regulatory decisions were being made at the Communications Commission of Kenya. licensing frameworks written, spectrum allocated, Universal Service Fund created, and three undersea fibre optic cables landing in rapid succession; TEAMS in June 2009, SEACOM shortly after, EASSy in 2010. These cables brought bandwidth but the structural terms of their arrival, which companies would control which layers, which incumbencies would be protected and what competitive possibilities would be foreclosed were shaped by forces larger than any regulatory body and accommodated in ways still producing consequences. The cables covered the whole region and a particular cohort descended, a group of western men in dashiki and a few Kiswahili words. they built their international reputations and careers mapping, narrating, and became the named authorities on African cable infrastructure and African digital connectivity, on ecosystems they did not build and bandwidth they did not finance confidently describing a region they could not possibly begin to comprehend and never will. The celebration of connectivity became the platform for their expertise and their income, funded by development agencies and tech companies. The distressing dimension of this arrangement was that the gatekeepers were mostly African women who sat on development agency boards, inside tech companies, on several funding agencies and committees, who controlled significant grant flows and held the institutional keys to which work got resourced and which did not, they said nothing to interrupt it. When asked why a certain non African demographic was receiving all the funding to map African broadband, one of them, without a hint of irony, said but he is the only one doing it. That sentence is worth sitting with. The condition of possibility for that sentence, the reason it could be said sincerely by a powerful African woman without irony is the same condition of possibility for this bill. The question none of us pressed hard enough was why were they the only ones? who decided that? and what did it mean that the people best positioned to ask had already accepted the answer? Birhane traces how the West’s algorithmic expansion simultaneously impoverishes the development of local products while leaving the continent dependent on Western software and infrastructure. Kenya is the proof of concept. What we produced, decade after decade, was not technology, we produced interfaces between foreign technology and Kenyans, and we gave those interfaces Kiswahili names. What came after M-Pesa and fibre optic cables was not a silicon Savanah but the performance of one.

The iHub, founded in 2010 was exciting for a season. it was a convergence point for engineers, designers, researchers, and many others who wanted to build something real. It was also, from the beginning, founded by an American who grew up in Kenya with missionary parents. born in Oregon, as he has described in his own interviews, website and personal blog called, White African. In the circles that mattered this was not a contradiction, it was a credential with so many scrambling to be associated with it, to be photographed alongside, mentioned in the same breath. it was identity and to be seen in that orbit was to answer, temporarily, the question of whether you belonged to the world that counted a question the ecosystem has never stopped asking, and that many of us have never stopped trying to answer.

The international legitimacy, name and presence conferred created its own sub economy. According to Village Capital, 90 percent of capital invested in East Africa in 2015 and 2016 went to a very small group of ‘expatriate’ founded businesses. Viktoria Ventures found that in 2019 only 6 percent of Kenyan startups securing more than $1 million were led by local founders, a figure corroborated by TechCabal’s analysis showing that of 17 Kenyan companies raising $1 million or more that year,11 were founded by ‘expats’ only and just one by locals. Mkalama and Ouma mapped the race, nationality, class and gender of every funded founder team in Kenya between 2019 and 2022 and found structural bias against Kenyan entrepreneurs, particularly women who lacked the social networks that determined who got funded and who did not. Business Daily Africa documented the same pattern in a 2023 piece titled; Why Kenyan Startups Have a White Face. the practice was open enough for a newspaper headline so it was not surprising when French Robin Reecht raised $1 million in pre-seed funding for Kune, a Nairobi food startup after spending only a few days in Kenya, according to TechCrunch. he claimed that affordable ready to eat food was impossible to find in Nairobi. The pre-seed round was described as one of the largest in East Africa and one of the few non fintechs to raise a seven figure pre-seed on the continent. Kune shut down less than a year after launching.

The media coverage that followed inspired German WordPress trainer based in Nairobi to build a website called; Hire A Muzungu. Quartz Africa, observed about the site’s proposition that Africa is not very widely recognised as an investment opportunity until another white nose comes into the picture. the minute an African startup is co-founded by a muzungu, money pours in. It was a structural reality documented across peer-reviewed research, newspaper investigations, and venture capital data, a bias so consistent it became the business model and the practice became so normalised it has its own informal market rate.

The iHub replicated this underlying logic in every direction; awards created and distributed, fellowships announced, ted talk stages allocated, recognition bestowed overwhelmingly upon those who moved comfortably within the sub culture and approved frameworks, who spoke the language and did not ask questions that would complicate the narrative. The silicon Savanah needed ambassadors, compradors, courtiers, validators more than it needed engineers, thinkers, artists, philosophers, mathematicians and it found so many of the former. The builders existed, we knew them, but the ones the ecosystem chose to fund, platform, recognise, and remember were rarely those who understood that technology was the smallest part of the solution, that the real problems were political, social, structural, epistemological, spiritual, ontological and that solving them would make the dependency, saviour model collapse. That kind of builder is a threat and the systems need problems to persist, not to be solved.

That ecosystem is now being rebuilt for AI. The Kenyan AI die hard has already arrived, fluent in the vocabulary of innovation, comfortable on the panels in tech festivals, summits, conferences, eager for the award and the recognition and the institutions that will validate them have already formed. The global north development sector consultants who spent the last decade on digital rights, internet health and internet governance have updated their slides and are now on the AI governance, AI Ethics, open source public AI circuit. same donors, same frameworks, new branding. So have the doctors, those in public health, lawyers, economists, agronomists, psychologists, the gender specialists every field seems to have produced its AI cohort, scrambling to attach themselves to the moment, to be the ones who arrive in Africa with a new vocabulary and the same old instinct; to help, convene, position, and extract.

The Gulf funded consultancies are here too sovereign wealth adjacent, moving through the region with partnership proposals and regulatory sandbox enthusiasm. The East African Asian business community that has always moved quietly through Kenya is now moving through AI investment and compliance markets less quietly. The American accelerator chapters arrived with their cohorts and curricula, importing the silicon valley formation wholesale and calling it local capacity building. The Kenyan diaspora has returned, credentialed in the West, appointed by the same international institutions that trained them as the authoritative African voice on African AI, cited in Brussels and Washington, unknown in Kibera. Kenya appointed Africa’s first special envoy for technology according to a Business Daily Africa interview published in 2025 accumulating awards, recognition lists, and UN advisory board seats while the Social Health Authority’s algorithm was quietly overcharging the poorest Kenyans. Some Kenyan technocrats spent the years including during the drafting of the Kenya AI Bill in Brussels, some physically inside the jurisdiction whose regulatory framework Kenya has now transplanted wholesale. The distance between the EU AI Act and the Kenya AI Bill is apparently a filing cabinet. Some of the silicon Savanah veterans who was on every fintech panel for example, are now on every AI panel same keynote architecture, same Q&A performance, slides updated.

The proof of concept is already running. Daily Nation and The Standard investigations revealed that the Social Health Authority (SHA) technology system, Kenya’s most consequential AI deployment already extracting from the poorest Kenyans, was awarded without competitive tender to a consortium whose majority shareholder, 59.5 percent, is Apeiro Ltd an Abu Dhab registered firm with documented links to India’s Adani Group, run by directors of Indian origin, through a web of holding companies deliberately structured to obscure beneficial ownership. When Apeiro failed to deliver a functional claims portal, a fourth company was quietly added, at further cost to Kenyan taxpayers already being overcharged by the algorithm. The muzungu’s, Indians and the returning diaspora are not waiting for the AI bill to pass, they have been here a while now and are already running the systems. The bill, with its commissioner, sandboxes and its compliance certificates is the paperwork that will legitimise what is already in operation. Kwet describes what happens next; the integration of big tech products at scale creates lock in that shapes what local talent builds and for whom. the infrastructure investments that follow are designed to deepen dependency, rather than displace it. Kenya built on foreign stacks, trained on foreign curricula, and continues to call the resulting dependency an innovation ecosystem.

The silicon savanah failed because the conditions required to build original technology not only structural but psychological, cultural, and epistemic were never assembled. Fanon’s diagnosis gets to the root of it, the colonised subject who measures worth through the external gaze cannot build for themselves because they cannot yet fully believe in themselves as builders. The awards, conferences, summits, tech festivals and ted talk circuits felt like arrival, the press releases and press coverage felt like proof. The regional headquarters of a silicon valley company in Kenya and working for it felt like belonging to the world that counted. We chose all of it and called it progress. This pattern repeats because it was never interrupted and we do not seem to have learned much from it. And now, having built nothing that requires AI regulation to protect Kenyan intellectual property, industrial capacity, indigenous knowledge, oral traditions, languages, the epistemic sovereignty of a people who have always known more than any system trained elsewhere will ever recognise, we have written a bill to regulate the sky.

**The Philanthropic Stack**

The AI bill does not arrive in a vacuum it lands inside an ecosystem already arranged and documented in official press releases, grant databases, and the Gates Foundation’s own financial disclosures with ‘philanthropic’ investment totalling $346 million to Kenya in 2024 alone by global cloud partnerships, and the World Economic Forum convening power. Understanding this shape requires following the mechanism; how African led institutions, funded by the same philanthropic architecture, are positioned as governors of African AI, to become the delivery vehicles for decisions made elsewhere. Among the most prominent actors in the continental AI governance space is a pattern that repeats with reliable consistency. an African led organisation, founded and financed within the philanthropic industrial complex, is positioned as the convener of African AI governance while remaining structurally accountable to the foundations, cloud providers, and development sector intermediaries that finance it.

The April 2025 Global AI Summit in Kigali produced a declaration signed by 54 African governments, including the African Union, and announced a $60 billion Africa AI Fund with the Gates Foundation named as the largest supporter. Tue summit was co-convened by the World Economic Forum and a US tech company co-organised the digital trade and regulatory sandbox workshop that will continue to shape the regulatory environment for its own products. During the summit, the Gates Foundation’s Africa Director said that Africa’s digital transformation must be shaped by African priorities, talent, and leadership. This sentence is accurate in its words but empty in its meaning. A declaration about African AI sovereignty, facilitated by a Gates funded consultancy, launched with Gates Foundation public endorsement, co-convened with the World Economic Forum, featuring a workshop co-organised with Google is not African leadership of Africa’s digital future, this is performed sovereignty, a form of self determination without the conditions that would make it real. McGoey’s documents how funding at this scale deprives governments of tax revenues, sets global agendas without democratic mandate, and substitutes private wealth for public accountability while calling itself charity.

The accountability gap is not abstract, public records show that in 2024, the Kenya government gazetted granted the Gates Foundation diplomatic protections according to legal notice 157 under the Privileges and Immunities Act, privileges typically reserved for foreign embassies and UN agencies. It was challenged by the Law Society of Kenya and the High Court suspended the immunity. The Gates Foundation subsequently withdrew its host country agreement in April 2025 but not before the episode had revealed, in formal legal terms, what the accountability gap actually looks like when a private foundation operates with state level protection and no democratic obligation to the population it claims to serve. the Foundation’s investments in food systems through the Alliance for a Green Revolution in Africa have been documented and contested by the Institute for Agriculture and Trade Policy and the Alliance for Food Sovereignty in Africa, which has demanded that donors stop telling Africans what kind of agriculture Africans need. Kenyan food sovereignty researchers and agroecologists have characterised these interventions as a second phase of colonisation; decisions designed elsewhere, implemented on Kenyans, accountable to no one. Its reach into health data infrastructure through GAVI and the global health architecture follows the same pattern, with the Gates Foundation becoming the WHO’s largest single donor. And now, through a December 2025 partnership with Abu Dhabi’s ADQ sovereign wealth fund the foundation announced in its press release that digital learning platforms are part of a nation’s essential infrastructure and has now moved into education AI across the region. Kwet observes that this mechanism of foreign owned product infrastructure is how dependency is institutionalised before any accountability is imaginable. Njoya’s sustained analysis of Kenya’s education system argues that curricula and systems designed elsewhere produce populations oriented toward consumption rather than creation.

Land, food, water, seeds, culture, reproduction, history, health, education, media, financial systems, digital identity, AI governance, soon the air we breath and so much more, much more than we can fathom; underneath all of it, values. A specific conception of human progress of what a good life is, who is worthy of life, what knowledge counts and what does not; now arriving with every programme, platform, partnership agreement signed in a hotel conference room with a view of a city someone flew into a few days ago.

The conditions of Kenyan‘s life, from birth to death, from what you eat and drink and breathe, to what your children are taught and what your communities learn to believe about themselves and their future, are now shaped and administered by foreign capitalist organisations that cannot carry what they do not have; the generational memory, the ancestral knowledge, the relational accountability through which African communities have always understood what is sacred, what is sustainable, and what must never be touched. This is not a gap in capacity that more funding or more technology will close, it is the terrifying architecture of a looming catastrophe not only in Kenya but everywhere that global systems of capital have substituted foreign private power for the living knowledge of a people.

Mbembe traces how colonial rationality continues to organise Africa’s relationship with systems of capital and knowledge recognising this as the continuation of a structure in which the conditions of African life are governed by decisions made elsewhere, while African institutions perform the theatre of participation. Birhane demonstrates how this functions at the level of AI ethics frameworks, designed by those with structural interests in the outcomes end up producing the illusion that problems have been solved while masking underlying power imbalances and further marginalising those already affected by structural oppression. The Africa AI Declaration 2025, operates in exactly this way, a document that frames African inclusion in AI as a question of participation in a system already built elsewhere, rather than whether Africans have any ownership in it or control it. Mhlambi‘s Ubuntu grounded framework for AI ethics proposes relational accountability as an alternative to Western frameworks of individual legal liability, and identifies the structural problem with transplanted governance. communities in the region typically experience technology harm collectively and structurally through systems that discriminate across a population, extract data without meaningful consent architecture, and reshape labour markets before any individual can claim standing to complain. The Africa AI Declaration says nothing about this, neither does the Kenya bill it helped create the political conditions for.

Then there is the faith based AI flowing through networks connected to silicon valley right wing christianity, which adds another layer. According to Religion News Service 2026, Peter Thiel Palantir co-founder and one of the most influential funders in the conservative tech ecosystem has publicly argued, using christian theological framing, against what he calls the “one world totalitarian state” of AI regulation. His network, organised through Founders Fund partners and faith organisations including the ACTS 17 Collective in San Francisco, has built documented bridges between silicon valley capital and evangelical christianity. Religion News Service and New York Times have both documented substantial congregation growth in San Francisco tech circles since 2023, as the existential questions raised by AI have made the religious community newly legible to people who build the technology and where congregations have grown substantially as AI ethics has now become a religious question.

The ideology that frames AI governance as a threat to freedom travels through the same funding networks that arrive in Nairobi as innovation partnerships, capacity building grants, and continental declarations about Africa’s digital future. What presents itself as African AI self determination is, in part, the downstream of a well funded silicon valley conviction that AI regulation is an existential threat, a conviction that travels, rebranded, into nearly every partnership agreement and capacity building grant that lands in Nairobi.

**What the Ecosystem Is Saying**

It is worth mapping the commentary the Kenya AI Bill 2026 has generated because the commentary is data about who the ecosystem serves.

The bill has produced distinct responses. The uncritical celebration, which posits that the bill is bold, visionary, Kenya’s moment, a signal to investors. The concern trolling from innovation groups that claim that the bill will stifle startups, create regulatory burden and impose compliance requirements that small teams cannot meet. The structural diagnosis which says the bill creates jurisdiction over domestic AI deployment but has no mechanism to compel foreign platforms, which represent most of the actual risk to Kenyans to comply with anything.

The celebration is the most revealing. Several commentators, firms and organisations have welcomed the bill while simultaneously marketing their own tools as the compliance infrastructure it requires. Others have offered themselves, their networks, convening power, willingness to provide technical input, participate in public hearings, and support the legislative process as the indispensable partner without whom the borrowed architecture cannot claim local legitimacy. One Nairobi based startup used a public analysis of the bill’s weaknesses as a vehicle to position its product as solving each one; the bill demands auditability here is our audit layer, the bill requires explainability, here is our reasoning trail, the bill creates regulatory sandboxes here is our evidence engine for inside the sandbox. This product placement using flawed public legislation as free marketing collateral reveals the actual mechanism by which captured regulatory environments reproduce themselves, the bill creates a compliance requirement, the compliance requirement creates a market, the market rewards whoever positions earliest. the question of whether any of this protects a single Kenyan never makes it to the conversation.

The concern trolling from the innovation groups has a different structure but the same interest. the argument that the bill will burden small startups is not wrong, it will. but the policy conclusion drawn that regulation should therefore be lighter serves the interests of the platforms whose harmful deployments at scale the bill is supposedly meant to address. Birhane demonstrates that solutions focused on technical fixes and reduced regulatory burden, consistently fail to centre the communities disproportionately impacted by algorithmic harm. The innovation concerns for Kenyan startups conveniently coincides with the structural conditions that allow foreign platforms to operate without accountability.

The structural diagnosis falls short of naming the patronage architecture underneath. Kenya indeed has clear jurisdiction over AI deployment. But a bill structured around a presidentially appointed commissioner with KES 5 million maximum fines with no extraterritorial reach and no enforcement authority against the actors who need enforcing. The structural critics identify the gap but fail to ask who benefits from its existence.

The commentary stays at the surface because when a pattern is named, especially when tied to harm, or one that would implicate the people doing the commenting something predictable happens, those who feel implicated, even indirectly, do not engage with the substance, they defend, deflect, individualise, reach for exceptions, because exceptions feel safer than patterns.

There is one genuine merit in the bill; the right to explanation and human review provisions. Any Kenyan whose loan application, employment screening, medical recommendation, or government service is determined by an automated system would, under this bill, have the right to a plain language account of how the decision was reached and to request human review. Benjamin traces how automated decision systems embed discrimination while presenting themselves as objective so the right to contest those decisions is a much needed protection, if the enforcement mechanism behind it is functional. The problem is that the enforcement mechanism is the commissioner, a presidential appointee with no independent accountability structure, the remedy is captured before it can operate.

There is silence in the commentary that is the most telling. According to DataReportal’s Digital 2026 Report, 97.5 percent of Kenyans online report using at least one AI tool within the past month, placing Kenya‘s consumption first globally. The UAE comes second at 94.2 percent and the global average is 81.2 percent. Kenyans are roughly 20 percentage ahead of the world and more than 25 ahead of the United States. Rather than building AI, Kenya is consuming it at world historic rates, overwhelmingly by a population with a median age of 20 in educational settings, healthcare, in daily life. Yet the predominant public commentary on the AI bill contains almost nothing about what this actually means for a generation of Kenyans. Kos’myna found that participants who wrote essays using ChatGPT across multiple sessions accumulated cognitive debt, consistently underperforming at neural, linguistic, and behavioural levels compared to those who wrote unaided, with cognitive activity scaling down in direct relation to AI tool use. they said their urgency to release before peer review came from fear that a policymaker would decide to roll out a GPT kindergarten and that developing brains were at the highest risk. Recently Pope Leo XIII created an Interdicasterial Commission on Artificial Intelligence and told teenagers to use AI in such a way that if it disappeared tomorrow, you would still know how to think. Kenya’s bill says nothing about AI in classrooms, the commentary around it says nothing about cognitive effects, homogenisation of written expression, hallucination, sycophancy, psychosis or the epistemic dependency that comes from outsourcing reasoning to a system trained on someone else’s knowledge of the world. None of the commentary welcoming this bill has asked what it means for a generation of Kenyan learners to outsource their thinking to a system trained on someone else’s knowledge of the world at world-historic rates, before anyone has measured what is being lost. Yet, Kenya celebrates being ranked first in the world for consuming a technology whose effects on learning and cognition are only beginning to be documented and the bill designed to govern this technology does not address any of it.

**Performed Regulation**

The Kenya Artificial Intelligence Bill 2026 is, structurally, the EU AI Act with a Kenyan patronage chassis installed underneath. Page 73, Memorandum of Objects notes that the bill was designed to align with global standards including the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act the transplant is self declared. The risk classification framework, conformity audits, regulatory sandboxes, public register of high risk systems, all these are EU regulatory concepts, lightly paraphrased, lifted into a Kenyan legislative draft. The transplant imports more than it declares, the epistemological assumptions, enforcement architecture, and the institutional design of a system built for conditions that do not exist in Kenya. It also imports what the EU framework does not contain; any acknowledgment of Kenya’s languages, cultures, social architectures, or the specific ways Kenyans actually encounter algorithmic harm. There is nothing Kenyan in this bill except the flag on the cover page.

**The Ledger Framework developed across this series asks several questions of any arrangement that claims to support or serve while extracting.**

**Who extracts value from this arrangement?**

The commissioner role is a high value patronage position with a five year renewable term, broad enforcement powers, sole authority to classify systems as presenting unacceptable risk making them prohibited in Kenya but with no visible judicial review and no specified appeals process.

Osei-Bonsu & Amoah observe that Kenya’s record with nominally independent regulatory offices is documented and consistent. The Office of the Data Protection Commissioner has operated since 2019 with consistent enforcement against small actors, a school, a phone manufacturer, a hospital and documented silence against the largest platforms processing Kenyan data at scale. Faturoti and Juma, describe Kenya’s data protection enforcement as hindered by limited capacity, fragmented regulation, and low public awareness. Those of us who observed the Communications Authority operate across two decades know what a nominally independent Kenyan regulatory office does with its mandate when the political conditions do not support independence. The new commissioner will be appointed into this tradition.

**Who bears the risk?**

Over Fifty million Kenyans whose interactions with AI systems in healthcare, finance, education, employment, policing and more will be shaped by a regulatory architecture with no mechanism for class complaints, no pre-deployment impact assessment requirements, nor extraterritorial reach over foreign built systems operating at scale, no whistleblower protections, and no provisions for Kenya’s linguistic diversity. The bill contains nothing about how AI systems will handle Kiswahili, or any of the forty plus languages through which Kenyan life is actually lived. Mhlambi notes that systems that classify, sort, and make decisions about communities whose relational identities, communal data practices, and cultural contexts that are entirely absent from their training data are not neutral tools, they are instruments of epistemic erasure and this bill gives them a compliance certificate.

The Social Health Authority (SHA) is the proof of concept. Kenya’s AI-driven SHA was sold as a revolution in healthcare financing. Faith Odhiambo, former Law Society of Kenya president, called out what the algorithm actually does; it overcharges the poorest Kenyans while undercharging the wealthy. A single mother earning KES 3,500 monthly is billed KES 1,030 for health cover. She names this digital poverty extraction, noting that a mother struggling to put food on the table is reduced to an affordability score by an opaque algorithm that cannot measure exhaustion, caregiving, vulnerability, or survival.

A pre-deployment assessment by data consultancy IDinsight reported by Africa Uncensored, Lighthouse Reports, and The Guardian, warned that the system was inequitable, particularly for low-income households. The National Assembly Health Committee chair formally warned the government against the rollout four days before launch, citing corruption risks, single sourcing concerns, and an unready ICT system, as reported by the Daily Nation. The SHA transition committee warned in writing following a pilot in Marsabit, that the new system was not ready and recommended the rollout to be suspended, this and the committee findings were reported by Business Daily Africa and Daily Nation. the Kenya government postponed to October then proceeded anyway. Today, of the 29 million Kenyans registered with SHA, only 5 million pay regularly, a collapse documented by the Daily Nation and confirmed by the National Assembly’s health committee they also documented that Kenyans are being turned away from facilities they cannot afford to access. This is an operating AI system already extracting from the poorest Kenyans not a future risk and the bill as designed cannot address this because it has no pre-deployment impact assessment requirement, nor community standing for collective harm claims and a commissioner with no retroactive authority over systems already in operation. The SHA calls its algorithm neutral, Birhane’s names what neutrality means in practice; the illusion that a problem has been solved while the harm compounds.

section 11(1)(g) requires the commissioner to establish an appeals mechanism by their own appeals process against their own decisions, a structure that gives the appearance of redress while capturing it at the point of design.

Section 26(1)(a) and (b) require both a risk assessment and a human rights impact assessment before deployment but only for high risk systems, and only prospectively. no pre-deployment impact assessment requirements for systems already in operation, like SHA which is already running no retroactive authority over harms already occurring, and no community standing for collective harm claims.

**What does compliance cost, and for whom?**

A maximum fine of KES 5 million, approximately $40,000 is to any international platform operating in Kenya just a fee. There is no revenue based penalty scaling or market suspension authority. The fine regime does not scale to the actor’s capacity to pay and does not function as enforcement but as a cost of doing business. For comparison, the EU’s AI Act scales penalties for high risk system non compliance to three percent of global annual turnover and up to seven percent for prohibited practices. The gap between these two approaches is a structural choice about who the law is actually designed to discipline.

**What is the exit condition?**

No specific sunset clause, the mandatory three year review is conducted by the Cabinet Secretary in consultation with the commissioner, the captured architecture reviewing its own capture.

**Who was not in the room?**

The Advisory Committee has six government seats, civil society and private sector one seat each. Communities most exposed to AI harm, informal sector workers subject to platform algorithmic management, women subjected to biometric surveillance, rural populations enrolled in AI-assisted health or agricultural programmes, pastoralist communities for whom no AI training data exists at all have no representation. The bill also creates three new institutional bodies layered on top of the existing Data Protection Commissioner and Communications Authority, whose mandates already extend to many of the harms this bill targets. The result is bureaucratic proliferation that will consume whatever enforcement capacity exists before it reaches the populations it claims to protect. Sambuli establishes the baseline condition that Kenyan institutions consistently violate; for digital governance to be people centric, it must safeguard users’ rights through design, not through complaint processes that lead to captured regulators. The bill’s architecture moves in the complete opposite direction.

The grass has written a bill about the bulls.

**Copy Paste as Governance**

There is a phrase many of us who have worked in African tech and policy circles have repeated for too long; we are building on what works. It is the rationalisation for every adopted framework, borrowed model, every governance structure imported from Brussels or Washington and given a Kenyan cover page. It was said in good faith, often by people who understood the constraints we were working within. It was also, structurally, a form of accommodation choosing the achievable over the necessary, dressing dependency in the language of pragmatism.

Farida Nabourema, Togolese writer and pan-Africanist, names the structural mechanism that polite corners of African civil society tend to avoid; the NGO-isation of African activism is the biggest fraud ever perpetrated against the continent’s liberation movements. When political struggle is converted into project cycles and donor reporting requirements, organisations that began as genuine movements for structural transformation gradually become service providers, managing the symptoms of oppressive systems rather than challenging the systems themselves. They learn to translate radical political demands into language acceptable to funders in Washington, Brussels, Berlin, Paris and in that translation, the demand for genuine sovereignty becomes a workshop on transparency, and the demand to dismantle extraction becomes a report on corruption indicators.

This is what happened to the so called multistakeholder model as practised in the internet governance space. It was, structurally, a participation framework built for a specific kind of actor: western educated, English fluent, conference mobile, comfortable in expensive western cities, and most critically, acceptable. Acceptable to the cliques that determined who got the travel grant, who got the speaking slot, who got invited into the working groups, committee‘s, boards and to join the next organisation. a small and remarkably consistent group of western men and women circulating through every position of formal leadership, from one organisation to another, one working group/committe to another, one board to another, constituency to another, in a system so self referential, someone inside once described it as incestuous. Those who arrived from Africa arrived on terms that had already been set. The cliques were real too and those who were accepted quickly learned what acceptance required; a willingness to translate African concerns into language the framework could absorb, to perform legitimacy within a system that had already decided what legitimacy looked like and underneath all of that, a slow erosion of the self. A willingness to depreciate your own people, your own culture, your own language, in order to remain in the room. The incidents were not rare, in a meeting in Nairobi someone asked, without shame, why Africans smell strange, question was asked openly and most of us smiled, because the system had trained us, over years of managed inclusion, to absorb that kind of violence quietly and keep the meetings moving. in its most extreme form, Africans fight other Africans, openly humiliating each other against their own collective interests in these governance spaces, policy forums, conferences summit, etc just one recent example is the battles that nearly destroyed AFRINIC, Africa’s only regional internet registry. That smile is what the multistakeholder model produced; Africans at the table, learning to swallow what is said and call it participation. so it should surprise no one that the same formation, decades of managed inclusion, eroded self-trust, and trained deference produces Africans who draft, convene, and lend their names to AI frameworks, bills, and governance processes that go against African interests. My baby used to ask, at every internet governance meeting, what does multistakeholder mean? they were never given an adequate answer and I am still not certain we fully understand what multistakeholder means in practice because it never means what it claims. It often means at face value that everyone is welcome at the table, but only if the table does not move, your presence legitimises decisions you had no part in making. the table was never where the decisions were made, decisions about infrastructure investment, platform regulation, data sovereignty, and who would ultimately control the digital conditions of African life were made in shareholder meetings, bilateral agreements between states and platforms, acquisition decisions in silicon valley, Shenzhen, Washington, Geneva, Brussels, New York, Paris. We debated the outcomes at the margins and called this multi stakeholder governance. Those of us who served in regulatory bodies during the formative years of Kenya’s digital ecosystem, the period between roughly 2003 and 2011 when M-Pesa was licensed, three undersea cables landed on the Kenyan coast, when unified licensing frameworks were being written that would shape the market for a generation were present at decisions that compounded in ways we understood partially and described optimistically. The connectivity was real but the structural terms on which it arrived were accommodated in ways that are still producing consequences now.

One of Kenya’s oldest digital policy networks founded in the early 2000s as a multistakeholder platform for Kenya’s ICT development recently submitted a memorandum welcoming the Kenya AI Bill as a timely and necessary recognition. calling the commissioner framework, advisory committee, risk classification system, and regulatory sandboxes, solid foundations. They offered to provide technical input, participate in public hearings, and support the legislative process. The memorandum’s references include the OECD, the EU AI Act, California Senate Bills, New York City local law, a White House executive order, and an article from International Affairs, no African scholar is cited. One of Kenya’s most consequential technology bills and the response from one of its oldest established digital voices cites Western regulatory frameworks and zero African intellectual traditions. It does not mention the SHA nor the 97.5 percent AI consumption figure. does not mention a single classroom or a single language. Not one question whether a commissioner appointed by the administration that benefits from the ecosystem’s current arrangement can regulate that arrangement. The network learned the form of the foundational work but did not inherit the forensic instinct that made the form worth anything. And it cannot because that instinct, the willingness to name what the funding depends on not naming, is what the funding structure selects against. This is the endpoint of a trajectory, Kwet’s concept of infrastructure as debt applies to institutions as much as to technology; when organisations integrate foreign frameworks at scale, the resulting dependency shapes what governance is even imaginable. An institution built on donor funding and international engagement will, over time, produce exactly the kind of analysis those structures reward. It will welcome the bill as a foundation because foundations are improvable, and improvement is fundable, and the work of challenging the entire architecture naming the capture, the transplant, naming the patronage is not a project cycle it is a reckoning and those are different things.

The Kenyan tech policy community that emerged from that period produced people who could engage brilliantly with governance frameworks designed elsewhere but it did not produce the conditions for building frameworks of our own. This is a failure of structural conditions many of us accepted, not a failure of individuals. It the donor dependency that shaped agendas, from the capacity building funding that produced conference speakers rather than thinkers and institution builders, to the international recognition that rewarded African policy voices who could translate African concerns into terms legible in the West, and not those who built indigenous governance infrastructure from the ground.

Nabourema names the condition this bill embodies, Africa is not only going through a political crisis but a profound epistemological collapse. When a political class abandons the discipline of thinking and the intellectual foundations that give purpose to action, everything that follows becomes an empty performance. Slogans replace ideas, convenience replaces conviction, and improvisation masquerades as strategy. A movement deprived of intellectual grounding behaves like a body without a spine, flailing in every direction while convincing itself that chaos is a form of adaptability. She is writing about African political parties but it also describes the current generation of Kenyan tech/AI policy intermediaries, their affiliations, behaviours, borrowed frameworks, panel confidence, their willingness to convene endlessly and build nothing, to circulate the same ideas, activities, and programmes in the same rooms with the same people and call it an ecosystem. The bill arrived from this cohort that has substituted performance for thinking that it can welcome a European regulatory transplant as a sovereign Kenyan achievement, and mean it. The bill before the Senate is what we get when we build policy, technology, advocacy ecosystems without building an intellectual sovereignty ecosystem. It is borrowed regulation for borrowed technology, welcomed by an institution now fluent in the language of the frameworks it was built to interrogate.

**What would Regulation Require?**

The Kenya AI Bill should be read for what it reveals about what we believe governance is for. A bill designed to protect Kenyans from AI harm would look different.

\-It would require pre-deployment algorithmic impact assessments for any system operating at scale in Kenya conducted by independent technical bodies with public reporting obligations and before deployment, not after harm.

\-would set fines on a revenue percentage basis, scaled to the operator’s global earnings.

\-create extraterritorial jurisdiction over foreign systems processing Kenyan data, following the GDPR’s territorial reach and would mandate procurement rules that require AI risk assessment before any state agency adopts AI systems in healthcare, education, policing, or any public service.

\-establish community standing for collective AI harm claims, so that the next SHA style extraction can be challenged as a class, not an individual complaint

\-would require that AI systems operating in Kenya demonstrate linguistic and cultural competence in Kenyan languages as a baseline condition of deployment not just as a feature

\-it would protect whistleblowers who disclose AI harms from inside institutions

\-it would say something, anything about what AI does to the cognitive development of children in a country where 97.5 percent Kenyans are already consuming it monthly

\-it would also require technical capacity to audit AI systems, engineering talent to inspect models, institutional knowledge to distinguish a genuinely high risk system from a vendor’s marketing brochure. Kenya has very few people who could serve competently as an AI commissioner in the technical sense the bill imagines, and the bill’s design suggests no awareness of this constraint. But the technical deficit is the smallest part of what is missing, the deepest deficit is epistemic, ontological, metaphysical and more that cannot be addressed by creating an authority, appointing a commissioner, or aligning with European standards.

\-it would require a reorientation, a deliberate, generational decision to rebuild the conditions under which Kenyan knowledge systems, Kenyan languages, social architectures, spiritual and cosmological traditions that are not the subject of governance but the foundation of it.

\-An education system rebuilt from the ground not reformed, rebuilt. one that produces mathematicians who think in Kenyan languages, philosophers who theorise in our languages, engineers whose first instinct is to ask what the community needs rather than what the investor will fund.

\-research ecosystem with the intellectual freedom and public financing to produce knowledge that does not need to be legible to Brussels or Washington to be considered valid.

\-It would require something most systems cannot deliver and no bill can mandate: a society that has developed the collective discernment to know when technology serves the people and when it serves the people who profit from it and, most importantly, whether Kenya’s most urgent problems require technology at all. The erosion of communal land tenure, collapse of intergenerational knowledge transmission, slow deliberate destruction of indigenous food systems, the spiritual displacement of communities whose cosmological frameworks have been systematically delegitimised by centuries of brutally violent colonial and missionary intervention, now being compounded by algorithmic monoculture. These are problems that no algorithm however locally built, ethically governed, open sourced, or Ubuntu aligned will resolve them. One of the most important questions Kenya could ask about AI is whether, for the problems that actually matter, it is the right tool at all. While Kenya borrows a European regulatory framework and calls it sovereignty. One of the bill’s stated objectives, is to align with international standards on artificial intelligence not to protect Kenyan‘s populations or govern Kenyan conditions.

\-It would require political leadership willing to tell foreign tech that their presence in Kenya is a privilege governed by Kenyan terms not a favour for which Kenyans should be grateful. not a partnership whose conditions are set elsewhere, nor a philanthropic intervention whose accountability does not benefit the people it claims to serve. And it would require the institutional capacity to enforce that position knowing that the same political leadership has, for so many years, been funded, convened, trained, credentialed, and flattered by the very organisations it would need to confront.

\-It would require governance rooted in African philosophical traditions of accountability; social, legal, spiritual, and cosmological and more, not borrowed from a Brussels framework designed for a different legal culture, political economy, different history of what states owe their people, and a fundamentally different understanding of what a person is and what connects them to the living, the ancestors, and the unborn. The question worth asking is what an AI governance framework would look like if it began with African conceptions of personhood, relationality, the sacred and built outward from there. That framework does not yet exist, building it is the work. This bill is not that work, it is the avoidance of it.

\-It would require those who govern to understand that a pastoralist community in Turkana whose land use patterns are being algorithmically mapped for a carbon credit scheme is facing a sovereignty question, not compliance, that touches land, ancestry, and the sacred relationship between a people and the earth they have tended across generations. That a mother in Kibera whose SHA affordability score was calculated by an opaque foreign model is not a data subject she is a citizen whose constitutional rights to dignity and fair administrative action have been violated, and a human being whose dignity cannot be reduced to an affordability variable without committing a kind of violence that no regulatory sandbox was designed to detect. That student who has outsourced their thinking to a large language model trained entirely on western knowledge is not a technology user, they are a mind being shaped by someone else’s epistemology, someone else’s cosmology, at someone else’s profit, with no Kenyan framework to name what is being taken nor mourn what is being lost. Mhlambi names what Western AI governance cannot reach; personhood in African philosophical traditions is not individual, nor transactional, and not separable from relationship to community, to land, to ancestors, to the unborn. Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu. A person is a person through other persons. An algorithm that processes an individual as a data point has already committed a categorical error so fundamental that no explainability clause can correct it. It has mistaken the unit of analysis and has regulated the shadow and left the person untouched.

What is actually needed is the courage to name which problems in Kenya are technology problems and which are political, social, and spiritual problems that technology will never solve and will even worsen. It is the collective discernment, which no regulatory framework can manufacture to know when a tool serves the people and when it serves the few who profit from extracting. It is the long, unglamorous, unfunded work of rebuilding the intellectual sovereignty that was the first thing taken and the last thing returned. You cannot regulate what you cannot see. And you cannot see if the entire conceptual apparatus you are using to look was built by the people doing the extracting.

None of this is in the bill, the bill creates a commissioner.

**The Sky We Did Not Build**

Ngũgĩ wa Thiong’o wrote that independence had moved Kenya from a colony serving British interests to a neo-colony serving imperial interests from around the world, with capitalism replacing social justice as the organising principle of the post-colonial state. He was describing 1963 when Kenya inherited a colonial administrative apparatus built to violently extract. The question of the so called independence generation was whether those institutions could be transformed from the inside or required dismantling from below. Sixty years later, the digital equivalent of that question sits in the senate, and we are answering it the same way we answered it then, by putting a Kenyan name on a foreign structure and calling it sovereignty.

The Africa AI Declaration says that AI is “an African arrow that, when thrown with the right ethical frameworks and inclusive policies, can pierce the way to African digital prosperity.” What a beautiful metaphor, the arrow. But you cannot throw an arrow you did not make, from a bow you do not own, at a target set by someone else’s hand, and call the flight yours.

Kenya can and must regulate AI. What it cannot do with this bill, in this ecosystem, at this moment of institutional design is protect the people it claims to govern. Those are different things.
