# A smart national ID powers Somalia's Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) vision

> ## Excerpt
> Feature on the biometric national ID as the foundation of Somalia's DPI: 11-digit
> lifetime number, physical + mobile ID, 15m target before 2029; NADRA technical
> partnership fuels a data-security/sovereignty debate; election-timing concerns.

---

Somalia is rolling out biometric national identity cards to replace passports as the country's primary form of identification, a move officials say will underpin its digital transformation and expand access to essential services. NIRA began issuing the IDs in September 2023, but demand has surged as the cards became mandatory for obtaining passports from Sept. 1, 2025, and will be required for domestic travel from Jan. 1, 2026. The biometric IDs will also be essential for opening bank accounts, accessing education, licensing businesses and cars, and using government and private services.

According to NIRA, 85% of Somalia's estimated 19.7 million people lack any form of legal identity. The government aims to distribute 15 million IDs before 2029. Each card carries a unique, lifetime 11-digit number, available as both a physical card and a digital mobile ID. "Without an ID, citizens cannot open a bank account, get a loan, travel inside the country or even register for school," said NIRA Director General Abdiwali Ali Abdulle. "This card will... serve as the foundation for Somalia's Digital Public Infrastructure."

The rollout has seen long queues outside registration centers in Mogadishu and regional states. The program is free of charge. Some Somalis question the timing, noting the rollout coincides with preparations for one-person-one-vote elections expected in 2026, and worry the national ID and electoral card may be conflated without adequate public awareness. Abdulle dismissed concerns that the program is election-driven, saying "the ID is a lifetime document."

The system is supported by the World Bank, UNDP and others, with technical infrastructure developed in partnership with Pakistan's NADRA — fueling debate over data security, privacy, and legal safeguards. Abdulle insists Somali citizens' data is stored securely within Somalia. Digital rights advocates warn that without clear legal frameworks, the program risks exclusion or misuse. Somalia has nearly 4 million internally displaced people and large nomadic populations who face greater registration challenges. Observers note lessons from Kenya's Maisha Namba, which faced constitutional challenges and exclusion concerns.
