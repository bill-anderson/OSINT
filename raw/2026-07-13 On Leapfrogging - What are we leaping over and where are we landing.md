# On Leapfrogging: What are we leaping over, and where are we landing?

Analytical essay by **Uche Ezejiofor** (Nigerian researcher/strategist; Pan-African studies) for the **Global
Center on AI Governance**'s *Algorithmic Review*.

## Thesis

A drone-logistics start-up serving disconnected smallholder farmers in rural **Rwanda** (a basic phone + USSD
code summons a drone to collect harvested crops) is genuinely innovative but is **"a privatized solution to a
large-scale public failure"** — it patches an infrastructural gap the state was meant to fill, and in doing so
**reassigns who is responsible** rather than solving the underlying problem. So, "in the name of innovation …
what are we leaping over? And where are we landing?"

## Argument

- Leapfrogging (per the Institute for Security Studies' African Futures) = skipping intermediate stages of
  technological development. Real successes exist — **M-Pesa** (Kenya) banked millions without branches; MTN
  delivered mobile connectivity without legacy telecoms — but concerns arise when such solutions are treated as
  **a substitute for the state** (and M-Pesa still rests on Global-North digital infrastructure).
- The **AU Digital Transformation Strategy** frames the lack of legacy infrastructure as a leapfrogging
  opportunity; the **World Bank** counsels going "back to basics." Ezejiofor argues both, and international
  institutions generally, **position governance failure as a market opportunity** — producing public servants
  trained to manage investor contracts and PPPs rather than to deliver public services or build institutional
  capacity, so **the state only gets weaker**.
- Reads this as a **contemporary manifestation of neoliberalism** — the logic of 1980s **Structural Adjustment
  Programs** rebranded as "innovation ecosystems." Over **$4bn** of VC went into African tech in 2025 alone
  (Partech), but VC deployment does not build governing capacity. Draws on **Samir Amin's *Unequal
  Development***: peripheral formations are structurally shaped to serve external capital, not internal
  development.

## Framing (for the wiki)

"When we leapfrog foundational governance infrastructure, we are not skipping a phase. We are skipping the
mechanism through which a state becomes capable of serving its own people." The drone company is not the
problem; **the absence of a government mandate for rural connectivity is**. A sharp
**sovereignty/colonialism-lens** critique of the private-innovation-as-development consensus.
