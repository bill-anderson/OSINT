*DIAL's own primary research — the missing spine of Uganda's interoperability story: the UGA hub lists dpi.exchange as an active topic while holding no UGHub material at all.*

As countries worldwide implement national data exchange systems, DIAL argues we can learn from Uganda's model. Good data exchange can improve government operational efficiency, drive economic growth, enable access to essential services and build trust. The factors that make up good data exchange — effective laws and regulations, technology architecture, and accountability/oversight mechanisms — are well established, but far less research has been done on how these function in the real world.

DIAL researched the unique drivers and considerations behind integrated national data exchange systems and their implications for governments, the private sector and people. There is no one-size-fits-all approach; each country's progression and timeline are unique and seldom linear.

Uganda's approach centres on its UGHub system. Today, UGHub is utilized by over 100 government ministries, departments and agencies as well as private entities — including banks, insurance providers and fintech firms. The system has dramatically increased the efficiency and convenience of several essential services, reducing wait times from days to hours in many instances, particularly for passport applications, work permit applications and vehicle registrations. Yet challenges remain, including financial sustainability, slow uptake and connectivity gaps.

Key learnings documented:
1. Prioritizing and nurturing trust among government stakeholders is crucial to ensure buy-in and, ultimately, adoption of the platform.
2. Financial sustainability remains a concern for UGHub, as for national data-exchange platforms in general; governments should explore "business models," particularly those built on open-source software.
3. Increasing investment in policy and institutional changes to build trust and ensure buy-in could significantly increase UGHub's value to government, citizens and other sectors.
