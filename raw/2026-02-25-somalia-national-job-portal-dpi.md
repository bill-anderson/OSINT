# Somalia's National Job Portal becomes a test case for Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI)

By Hassan Istiila — Hiiraan Online, Wednesday February 25, 2026

On a warm afternoon in Mogadishu, 23-year-old Abdirahim Ali Mohamud Shuriye refreshed a webpage that had not existed a year earlier. For months after graduating from SIMAD University, he had searched for work the way many Somali graduates do — scrolling through LinkedIn, Telegram channels, Facebook groups and WhatsApp forwards for vacancies that might already be outdated.

Then he came across the Somali National Job Portal for Employment Opportunities. That decision to trust a centralized digital platform placed him among ten winners of a national business idea competition and connected him to a $2,500 grant.

His story is about a country attempting to replace a patchwork hiring culture with unified digital infrastructure — a shift that reveals deeper questions about governance, transparency, trust and power in Somalia's evolving state institutions. For decades, applying for public and private-sector jobs meant navigating silos: each ministry handled recruitment independently, vacancies posted on office gates or radio, applicants printing CVs and delivering documents in person. The result was duplication and opacity, with informal networks, clan affiliations and diaspora contacts filling the gaps.

The Somali National Job Portal, launched on March 15, 2025, was designed to consolidate this fractured recruitment culture into a single entry point for public and private employment. Policymakers frame it as part of Somalia's emerging Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI). The National Data Agency under the Ministry of Posts and Telecommunications hosts the portal, which serves as a centralized database where employers post vacancies and applicants upload CVs. Officials say only the job seeker's CV is used, employers must verify licenses before posting, and servers are hosted domestically by the National Data Agency.

The competition was supported by the International Organization for Migration (IOM), which provided financial and technical assistance to Somalia's National Employment Service Center under the Ministry of Labor and Social Affairs (MoLSA); IOM supported the design, development and rollout of the portal. Each of the ten finalists was awarded $2,500. Centralized databases raise governance questions — cybersecurity and data protection frameworks in Somalia are still evolving. In fragile states where institutions are still building legitimacy, the architecture itself remains visible, encoding institutional rules into systems that shape how decisions are made, recorded and audited.
