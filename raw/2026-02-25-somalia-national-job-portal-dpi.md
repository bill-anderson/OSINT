Somalia’s National Job Portal becomes a test case for Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) - Hiiraan Online Jobs | Twitter | Somali Links | RSS Dark Mode

Hassan Istiila Wednesday February 25, 2026

A young girl works at a computer inside the National Employment Service Center office

On a warm afternoon in Mogadishu, 23-year-old Abdirahim Ali Mohamud Shuriye refreshed a webpage that had not existed a year earlier.

For months after graduating from SIMAD University, he had searched for work the way many Somali graduates do, scrolling through LinkedIn, following Telegram channels, scanning Facebook groups and WhatsApp forwards for vacancies that might already be outdated by the time they reached him.

Opportunities surfaced in fragments: a ministry announcement here, a private-sector posting there, a vacancy shared through personal networks or briefly uploaded to an institutional website that few people regularly checked.

advertisements “It was confusing,” Abdirahim said. “You could hear that an entity was hiring, but you didn’t know where to apply, or if the deadline had already passed.”

Then he came across something new: the Somali National Job Portal for Employment Opportunities. Its design was plain, almost austere. A search bar. A list of openings. Counters display the number of registered job seekers and employers. No banners, and no slogans.

“I saw the Somali National Job Portal website and didn’t hesitate,” he said. “It is a simple website with many opportunities. I had a business idea and was looking for funding while unemployed.”

That decision to trust a centralized digital platform would place him among ten winners of a national business idea competition and connect him to a $2,500 grant.

Abdirahim Ali Mohamud receiving confirmation of his $2,500 award

But his story is not only about a grant. It is about a country attempting to replace a patchwork hiring culture with unified digital infrastructure, a shift that reveals deeper questions about governance, transparency, trust, and power in Somalia’s evolving state institutions.

For decades, applying for public and private-sector jobs in Somalia meant navigating silos.

Each ministry handled recruitment independently. Vacancies were posted on office gates, shared on social media, uploaded briefly to websites, or announced over the radio. Applicants printed CVs, photocopied certificates, delivered documents in person, or sent them via email. Files were stored in offices. Shortlists were rarely published. Rejections were rarely explained.

International organizations and private companies ran parallel processes on separate platforms. Despite the existence of some private job-advertising websites, there was no single location where a graduate could survey the labor market comprehensively.

The result was duplication and opacity. Applicants often submitted identical documents to multiple institutions. Data was not shared. There was no centralized record of how many people were seeking work, or which sectors faced shortages.

For young graduates without connections inside institutions, the system often felt impenetrable. Informal networks, clan affiliations, personal referrals, diaspora contacts, frequently filled the gaps left by administrative disorder.

The Somali National Job Portal, launched on March 15, 2025, was designed to consolidate this fractured recruitment culture. Instead of separate ministry announcements, duplicated databases, paper submissions, and opaque shortlists, the portal aimed to create a single entry point for public and private employment.

Policymakers frame it as part of Somalia’s emerging Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI).

Last year, the National Employment Service Center advertised a business grant competition through the portal. Hundreds applied. Weeks after submitting his application online, Mr. Shuriye received confirmation that he had been selected among the top ten finalists.

The competition required participants to pitch their business ideas before a panel of judges drawn from the private sector and outside government. Finalists underwent five days of training and received up to four hours of individualized coaching from mentors to refine their proposals.

Each of the ten finalists was awarded $2,500 to implement their ideas.

“I was told that the money I received was not a loan but a grant,” he said. “I was happy that I found that opportunity through the Somali National Job Portal.”

For him, the significance went beyond the money. It was about the clarity of the process, the fact that the opportunity was publicly posted, that applications were submitted digitally, and that private-sector judges made the final decisions.

The competition was supported by the International Organization for Migration (IOM), which has provided financial and technical assistance to Somalia’s National Employment Service Center under the Ministry of Labor and Social Affairs (MoLSA). IOM supported the design, development, and rollout of the portal, working alongside a specialized implementation committee composed of IT experts from MoLSA and IOM.

Mr. Shuriye’s business idea, AgriSoko Digital Platform, is still under construction. The dashboard remains incomplete, and the software is nearing completion but not yet launched.

The concept emerged from conversations with women farmers who described recurring obstacles: limited access to reliable markets, knowledge and skills gaps, lack of access to finance, high post-harvest losses, and systemic gender barriers restricting participation in formal supply chains.

AgriSoko aims to address six core services including a knowledge base with expert access; an agricultural inputs marketplace; real-time market price information; buyer–seller linkages; facilitation of access to finance; and digital tools to reduce post-harvest inefficiencies.

Its core beneficiaries would be women agripreneurs and youth farmers, along with buyers of agricultural produce.

“I want the application to benefit the people it is intended for to simplify work and add real value,” he said.

His story offers a human entry point into an extensive institutional transition because of Somalia’s shift from fragmented hiring practices to a centralized digital recruitment system rooted in the logic of Digital Public Infrastructure.

Director of the Department of Employment and Job Creation, leads a session on CV preparation and job search skills.

Zakariye Abdi Hashi, Director of the Department of Employment and Job Creation at MoLSA, describes the portal as a response to structural gaps in the labor market.

“It came from a need to connect talent with opportunity,” he said in an interview at his office. “With youth unemployment stands at a hight percent, we needed a centralized employment service.”

The National Data Agency under the Ministry of Posts and Telecommunications hosts the portal, which serves as a centralized database where employers post vacancies and applicants upload CVs.

As of February 12, 2026, 1,976 job seekers were registered. On that day, two vacancies were listed and 15 employers were active, figures publicly visible on the homepage.

“Only the job seeker’s CV is used to search for a job. No other sensitive information is collected,” Hashi said. Employers must verify their licenses before posting vacancies, a safeguard intended to prevent fraud. The service is free for both job seekers and employers.

The portal forms part of Somalia’s gradual investment in Digital Public Infrastructure, foundational digital systems designed to function as public utilities. In practice, DPI includes digital identity frameworks, interoperable databases, and secure data-hosting environments. Rather than building isolated platforms for each ministry, the approach emphasizes shared infrastructure.

“IOM is helping us from start to finish,” Hashi added.

Hashi emphasized that the portal’s design prioritizes transparency alongside efficiency. Government agencies now advertise a significant portion of vacancies through the platform, followed by international and private employers.

The ministry conducts workshops to train applicants on using the portal and strengthening their CVs. Special sessions are held for women and persons with disabilities, both in person and virtually.

Yet the numbers on the homepage tell a quieter story: nearly 2,000 registered job seekers. Two vacancies listed that day.

The imbalance reflects the structural constraints of Somalia’s formal labor market. Centralization does not create jobs; it organizes access to them. In a country where most economic activity remains informal and graduate unemployment is widespread, the portal exposes scarcity even as it attempts to manage it.

Centralized databases raise several governance questions. Cybersecurity and data protection frameworks in Somalia are still evolving. Hashi insists that strict legal safeguards protect citizen data and that servers are hosted domestically by the National Data Agency.

But as more public services move online, the risks of digital security increase.

Digital Public Infrastructure is not simply about websites. It encodes institutional rules into systems that shape how decisions are made, recorded, and audited. In countries with mature bureaucracies, such systems often fade into the background. In Somalia, where institutions are still building legitimacy, the architecture itself remains visible.

The portal project was implemented through a public-private collaboration led by MoLSA, with input from the Federation of Somali Employers and Trade Unions, the Chamber of Commerce, and the National Technical Advisory Committee on Labor Migration.

A specialized implementation committee composed of IT experts from MoLSA and IOM oversaw system development and deployment. IOM Somalia provided technical support to ensure alignment with labor market needs.

The initiative aligns with Somalia’s National Development Plan, which prioritizes job creation, economic recovery, and social stability. It also forms part of the Better Regional Migration Management programme, funded by the United Kingdom’s Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (UK FCDO), to promote economic recovery and human capital development across the region.

However, the portal has evolved into more than a recruitment platform.

In December 2025, the National Employment Service Center concluded a three-month training program benefiting more than 200 young people, with 25 participants trained each week. Participants gained communication skills, CV writing techniques, and interview preparation.

The center reports that more than 1,000 youth have been empowered through business skills development programs. The Ministry has pledged that plans are underway to create job opportunities for more than 40,000 youth in the coming months, as part of broader efforts to reduce unemployment and poverty.
