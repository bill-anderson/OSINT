# Home Affairs struggles to unblock identity numbers due to unserviceable addresses

Mayibongwe Maqhina Mayibongwe Maqhina|Published 41 minutes ago

Image: Stock Images

The Home Affairs Department has blamed unserviceable addresses for its failure to trace nearly 400,000 people with blocked IDs since the Gauteng High Court found that the placing of markers against identity numbers without a fair administrative due process was unconstitutional and invalid.

The High Court had granted the department a window period of 12 months, from January 16, 2024, to March 1, 2025, to correctly reflect the particulars of the person to whom the identity number was assigned after determining the identity numbers against which markers had been placed.

The order had prompted the department to embark on a media campaign to provide the affected members of the public with an opportunity to make representations.

The move sought to address the long-standing issue of wrongfully blocked IDs while curtailing the prevalence of fraudulent documents in circulation.

Responding to parliamentary questions from IFP MP Busaphi Machi, Minister Leon Schreiber blamed unserviceable addresses submitted by affected persons.

“Locating them on the National Population Register did not yield any desired results and compelled the department to look for alternative approaches for better reach,” he said.

Schreiber also said the department had applied to the court for substituted service and was granted court approval to implement the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act notices in the form of substituted service to reach out to rural and under-resourced communities.

“Substituted services use various communication platforms and mediums for far wider reach in all nine provinces of the Republic.”

He also said the court has granted the department 24 months, until March 2027, to resolve all of the remaining cases.

Schreiber further said the department has a total of 377,060 identity numbers that remain blocked on the National Population Register.

He described this as a “ very significant progress over the past 18 months”.

In December 2025, Schreiber told Parliament that a total of 384,189 ID numbers were blocked, down from the 702,267 recorded when the court delivered its judgment on the matter.

Schreiber also said: “Out of the total identity numbers, which remain blocked, 198,336 (52,6%) are under the category of Duplicate Identity Records and 7,963 (2%) cases are under South West Africa category and these still require citizenship/status determination.”

He stated that the department is using approved Standard Operating Procedures for purposes of resolving blocked IDs and compliance with the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act.

There were step-by-step guidelines for the public and in-person representation by clients to assist with determining the citizenship and origins of the individual.

Schreiber said affected people are required and urged to visit department offices in-person for representation for an interview with immigration officers and the recording of biometrics.

“Thorough investigations and verification processes ensure that lawful South African citizens are not incorrectly deprived of access to a valid identity number,” he said.

Last month, the department launched a nationwide campaign to assist individuals whose IDs were blocked prior to November 2022.

Department spokesperson Thulani Mavuso said affected individuals were invited to submit representations, explaining why their identity numbers should be unblocked.

“The department is committed to ensuring that all affected individuals are afforded a fair opportunity to present their case,” he said, adding that affected individuals should visit any department office, excluding refugee reception centres and banks.

Mavuso said the number of the unblocked IDs, including those classified as duplicates, stands at 2.2 million to date.

The department has stated that as part of its investigation, it may cancel ID or records where it is found that they were issued to individuals who do not qualify for inclusion in the National Population Register or contained incorrect personal particulars.
