# Can Cape Verde challenge Nigeria and Ghana as West Africa's tech powerhouses?

TechCabal — 29th May, 2025 — By Adonijah Ndege

Cape Verde is placing a bold bet that a small, stable island nation can punch far above its weight in the digital economy. At the heart of this ambition is the $45 million TechPark CV on the outskirts of Praia, which the government hopes will become a launchpad for startups, an investment magnet for global tech firms, and a symbol of how agility can outpace scale.

The investment is audacious for a country of just over half a million people, whose total national budget is less than $1 billion. But for Pedro Lopes, Cape Verde's Secretary of State for the Digital Economy, this is precisely the point. "We don't aim to follow the tech narrative in West Africa," he says. "We want to reshape it."

Cape Verde's pitch rests on its ambition to become a regional hub for digital innovation, remote work, and agile tech policy. The government has earmarked over $100 million for digital infrastructure in the past two years, including state-of-the-art submarine cable and 14,530 km island-wide fibre connectivity. Beyond infrastructure, the state is also investing in people — through coding academies, English-language tech training, and diaspora engagement.

Lopes, a former TEDx organiser and Mandela Washington Fellow, was appointed at 31 with no formal party affiliation, one of Africa's youngest digital economy ministers. Cape Verde's ambitions come when Nigeria and Ghana have solidified their positions as West Africa's dominant tech markets — Nigeria's fintech sector alone attracted over $180 million in funding in 2024. By contrast, Cape Verde has received only a fraction of that investment; its startup ecosystem is nascent, with just over 100 registered companies, including Vale.cv, CV Innovation, Muska, and Prisma Videos — mostly early-stage ventures.

Yet Cape Verde sees an opportunity in its size. "We can move faster. Our stability and governance make us a safe sandbox for innovation," Lopes says. The island nation ranks 35th globally in the World Bank's 2023 Government Effectiveness Index, ahead of regional peers like Ghana, Nigeria, and Cote d'Ivoire. Central to the vision is TechPark CV, positioned as a free zone for digital firms offering fiscal incentives and streamlined regulation. One anchor tenant, Portuguese cybersecurity firm VisionWare, has already hired more than 50 local engineers. The government is also creating an Economic Technology Zone (ZET), a regulatory sandbox combining tax incentives with flexible innovation rules — a model inspired by similar zones in Estonia and Rwanda.

Cape Verde's strategy includes reversing the brain drain by building remote work infrastructure and increasing tech-sector incentives, and integrating second- and third-generation Cape Verdeans raised abroad into its workforce. Much of the long-term play hinges on education, with investment in STEM and mandatory skills-transfer obligations for park tenants. With a median age of 25 and one of the highest literacy rates in sub-Saharan Africa, Cape Verde sees its young population as a competitive advantage — though challenges remain: high youth unemployment, limited access to risk capital, and a small domestic market.
