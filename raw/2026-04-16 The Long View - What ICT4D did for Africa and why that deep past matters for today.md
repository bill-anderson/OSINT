*(NB. This post will mainly refer to ICT4D – ICT 4 development – as this was how this area was generally described before transitioning to becoming digital development)*

Nothing of great significance digitally seemed to have happened in Sub-Saharan Africa over the last two decades or more without the fingerprints of development actors being on it somewhere. Africa is not a private sector market like any other. In matters digital, important things happened because it got by with a little help from its friends.

This post is the first of two that look at ICT4D and its impact on Sub-Saharan Africa between 2000 to around 2008/9, when mobile data and apps on smartphones began to shift consumer markets. The second post will look at digital development from the latter date forward to the present day and teases out what might happen in the future. I’ll also look at how recent alterations in patterns of development funding might change how things are done. The quotes are all from people I’ve been in conversation with for this post (see end of post for acknowledgements).

Why take a whole post to look at those early decades again? Because as one person put it when I asked about how ICT4D had been altered by more recent developments: “I don’t think it’s changed that much. There’s still the same issues around power relations and claims made about its potential.” Put simply, there remain concerns around the equity of access to digital advantages and whether technology changes behaviour (or vice-versa) and if so, positively and/or negatively.

The development sector does not have a single viewpoint. But at the beginning of what become the digital changes, in the early 2000s, mainstream development voices painted investment in digital as a distraction from continuing to invest in classrooms, water supply or whatever seemed most important to them in their argument. Techies from outside development came bearing gifts and making enormous promises, often wrapped up in parables where fishing-folk were saved from storms or farmers got better prices for their crops. Nowadays, the idea that digital is useful in development work is more widely accepted. However, tech, through things like AI, continues to pose new challenges that reflect much of the same ‘side-taking’.

What is always hard with the development sector in all its forms is that it likes to have debates that are painted in primary colours (largely black and white), where looking to be on the highest point of the moral high ground is much to be admired. But as one person observed about the shift to digital platforms: “It provides challenges for digital development. It’s really hard to theorise or celebrate Google, Meta or Microsoft separate to their good and bad intentions in general. You end up depending on those platforms. There are women using WhatsApp for their livelihoods. Nobody built WhatsApp to do this. It’s harder to understand what they do. Your X is different from mine. Since it’s part of the economy and the culture, how could it not be part of digital development?”

And as another person told me with a laugh: “Sanctimonious-ness is in NGOs’ DNS. They often have boards with rich people who want to save the world.” I wrote something in 2002, a couple of years after launching Balancing Act, that describes what I felt I was doing: “If wealth is created by those using digital technologies, then Africa has to figure out how either to attract wealth to invest in its countries or to create ideas and products that will make wealth for them. Without successful wealth creation, how can the enormous costs of Africa’s social needs be met or indeed wealth be redistributed in any way?” I was also interested in cultures and behaviours and online content, the latter being something that really didn’t happen to any great extent until a decade later.

**The Starting point**

Before 2000, many of the countries in Africa seemed stagnant. The idealism of independence had long evaporated in cold war battles. Apartheid South Africa had tied down the Frontline States. Long-term rulers provided no sense of wanting to change things. One egregious example, Togo’s Gnassingbe Eyadema, seized power when I was still at school and didn’t die until 2005. He was succeeded by his son, who rules to this day. The slow, steady grind of extractive economic growth was not producing enough rewards for growing populations. Many governments were elite rent-seeking machines whose greed was balanced only by the patronage they dispensed.

It is hard to understate the importance of the liberalisation of communications markets. Part of the ‘African problem’ at the beginning of this period was the inability of Governments to get stuff done: an absence of vision and very little desire to confront self-interested parties. The failure of state-owned incumbent telcos was a microcosm of this broader problem, shot through with inefficiencies, patronage and corruption. (This changed and by the end of the period under discussion, for example, Kenya built the TEAMs fibre link in a globally competitive timeframe.)

Liberalisation (and accompanying regulation) bought in investment and competition. It was an uneven process but the latter drove down prices and fostered innovation. In the early years, the ability to get your money back from a cell site in months rather than years created considerable incentives. Mobile operators (MNOs) were taken by surprise at the level of demand. They built networks in countries where civil wars were still going on. This success spurred a greater level of partnerships between the private sector, civil society, development actors and government. Aside from sponsorship of events, at a level of principle the private sector and civil society could argue that the high calling prices of the monopoly rent-seekers were not good for citizens, and a country’s development.

This was the ‘idealistic’ period of private sector development before monopolies returned. Regionally, competition often solidified into de-facto MNO monopolies or duopolies. Wind forward to the present day and there is the same problem writ large for digital platforms. Successful money transfer platforms like M-Pesa and Momo are very often near-monopolies. Network effects create monopolies on a preferred platform: “The internet is a monopoly making machine.”

Into the world of African governance of the early 2000s came people who believed technology would transform people’s lives: “ICT4D people were like fabulists. They would spin a yarn and people would eat it up. In many cases, there was no result.” ICT4D was (and continues to remain, as digital development), the ‘fashion business’ of the deeply unglamorous development sector.

It provided (and continues to provide) the latest hot tech or tech-influenced thing: open source, M4D, drones, 3D printing, bitcoin and blockchain, start-ups, impact investing and challenge funds to name but a few. The technology is always the answer to almost everything, providing the intellectual ‘leapfrogging’ for difficult and sometimes seemingly impossible challenges like basic infrastructure, literacy and changes in culture. It sounds cruel to say it but it’s almost the worst of the TED Talk approach to life, where one charismatic person’s answer to a particular problem will solve everything.

I believe in miracles…

The challenge has always been distinguishing between the rhetoric and the likely result because some things actually have made a difference: “The mobile phone was (and is) the most improving thing for development ever. There were more ideas faster than they can walk, and this is (now) the norm, not the exception.” Digital provided challenges to the established way of doing things and new energies and ideas. Those coming from outside development did not always share its assumptions and wanted things to happen fast and this was the ‘grit in the oyster.”

**What has changed (2000-2026)**

This is a Substack post, not an academic article, so I’m going to take a lot of short-cuts. The available data about what has changed is much stronger on inputs (eg investment) and outputs (eg network coverage, number of subscribers, jobs) than outcomes, particularly behavioural outcomes. However, many things have changed since 2000 and what follows tries to name the major changes. Below are four things that I think are unarguable:

• ***Investment:*** The GSMA has estimated that between 2000-2007 mobile operators (MNOs) invested over US$35 billion and they have continued to invest in the billions up to the present day. The overall success of this investment drew in further tech investment later in other areas like start-ups, network infrastructure and more recently, data centres, all in the billions of dollars. Spending by MNOs drove the growth of advertising, sponsorship (of music and sports) and broadcasting and played a key role in the development of things like digital marketing. Taken as a whole, the communications sector contributes in the low hundreds of billions to Sub-Saharan Africa’s GDP. You could make a counter-factual that Government telcos could have done all this but the moment you say it, the idea dies.

• ***Means of communication and transaction:*** All that investment in networks has created a system of communications and transactions that can be used in most countries by the majority of the population. By speaking to a range of people, you create your own opportunities and social capital. You can send a message to your relatives asking for money in an emergency. You can exchange views rather than simply receiving Government news on the radio and television. You can stream your favourite African music artist and follow them. For development actors, you can gather data more easily and use this to better match resources to need. Does this mean that this is an unproblematic nirvana? Of course not, but it did not exist in 2000 and two and a half decades later, for all its problems, it now does.

• ***Industry jobs and skills:*** By 2008/9, according to Professor Richard Heeks, over 100,000 worked in mobile companies and related industries. Coming up to the present, the more partisan estimate by the GSMA is 1.5 million direct jobs and 2.2 million indirect jobs. Even if you put a discount on those numbers, name another industry in Africa that has generated as much new employment over the same period. It has bought skills to both its lowest employees and its managers. At its best, it has been an academy for people who have later launched their own businesses. As a service industry, it has produced a very different dynamic in economies previously dominated by largely unimaginative providers. Remember what African banking was like in the early 2000s.

• ***Serendipity, the haphazard and unintended consequences:*** A combination of early innovation by mobile operators and African ingenuity produced both long-lasting products and workarounds including: Prepaid calling, sending credits leading to M-Pesa and mobile money, dual SIM phones, Facebook and WhatsApp selling, new African online media and so on.

However, all this has not really changed the fundamentals of African economies. Digital has not shifted the needle on producing new goods and services or higher levels of processing and assembly. There are examples - Dangote’s refinery, Cote d’Ivoire becoming a major cashew nuts processor, a bit of mobile assembly here and there - but these are few and far between.

Over 50 years, the internet in Sub-Saharan Africa has produced wealth but probably not yet the breadth of wealth I thought it would create: “There is no evidence that internet access is shifting patterns of prosperity.” As tellingly: “None of the communities I’ve been involved with have seen a digital dividend. There are lots of places that look like they did 20 years ago.” Discuss.

The work of Research ICT Africa and the Review of the WSIS process (See Further Resources below) provide ample evidence for these judgements. Did ICT contribute to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)? Probably but it’s hard to say. Is 5G coverage a good measure for the Millenium Development Goals? (MDGs). Probably not. Supply is not demand.

One of the big challenges is African exceptionalism. In other words, Africa and its many countries are not the same as anywhere else. Therefore, tech does not follow the same pattern as elsewhere. Hence the allure of the mobile-only narrative that makes the continent distinct.

Two things are at play here, the first is economic and the second is cultural. Take any African country, let’s say Nigeria or DRC. They are both rich countries with lots of poor people without the money or education to discover the utility of services like the internet and devices like computers. The second factor is that the history and cultures of the continent are very different from say North America or European countries, one aspect of which I’ll highlight below. However, for all these differences, it is, in other ways, the same as anywhere else. Those with money and education want the same things digitally. The content and behaviours used (particularly hybrid uses of different technologies, platforms and analogue) may differ but the intent is almost identical.

Kenyans found a trust in M-Pesa that allowed it to be used for cash transfer at scale. Other countries, for example, in West Africa, were much slower to place trust in mobile cash transfer. Overall, key aspects of mobile and online systems are trust-based and this trust has taken time to build. For example, Jumia, once dubbed the ‘Amazon of Africa’ started with 95% of its transactions in cash and it still had 72% cash or non-Jumia Pay in Q1, 2025. Discuss.

Another example is pertinent. A senior ICT adviser in an East African country told me that his parents disapproved of social media when it first launched. They saw it as showing off but there are now more WhatsApp family groups in that country than you ‘can shake a stick at’. Things can and do change. It also turned out that Facebook was well-suited to African culture and although its adoption has not been unproblematic – especially for women (more on gender in part 2) – it did not require capacity-building workshops to explain it. However, those that use social media like TikTok are not usually well-represented in poor, rural areas. The shiny surface is not the same as what is hidden below it.

**ICT4D successes and failures (2000-2008)**

In this period, the key challenges were the lack of most kinds of communications infrastructure, getting permission to build it (in the face of Governments and incumbent telcos) and providing prices that would enfranchise as many people as possible into the market. Early digital activists (myself included) and the programmes they created focused initially on using the internet. It took a number of years to realise that the tools we were selling the ‘digital dream’ with would be voice and SMS for some time. Those early years were all about limitations. The internet was expensive and like trying to access global knowledge by sucking on the digital equivalent of a straw. SMS meant you had only 160 characters to say something and would the person receiving the message, be able to read it, let alone do anything about it?

The strongest idea, oft-repeated, was that African countries were leapfrogging the need for network infrastructure and many other things besides. Microwave cell sites meant you didn’t need fixed infrastructure. VSAT satellites would give you internet anywhere. Except, of course, that satellite bandwidth was hugely expensive and had very limited throughput. As we have discovered from 2008 onwards, there was no long-term way of leapfrogging the need for reliable, ubiquitous fibre and voice networks and harder things to deliver like household internet. The physical and the digital go hand in hand. The cloud sounds all ethereal but needs physical data centres and power supplies.

Modern versions of ‘leapfrogging’ continue to plague the debate. No ‘bricks and mortar’ retail? E-commerce will get you what you want faster. No roads? Zipline will deliver those much-needed drugs to a health clinic. No coverage for poorer, rural areas? Starlink will beam you down an answer to a smartphone. Maybe but more likely not.

The table below is my personal assessment of the successes and failure of that period. Three things were absolutely foundational: the financing of international fibre and the setting of the terms of access; creating a money transfer system (through M-Pesa); and the use of regional National Research and Education Networks (NRENs) to provide cheaper, better internet access for African students. The fourth item was that with things like disaster relief and refugee crisis work, improved communications was always going to be advantageous. Discuss.

The first international fibre cable – SAT3/WASC – was launched in 2001 by a consortium of Government-owned telco incumbents (except Orange-owned Sonatel) led by the privatised former South African incumbent telco Telkom. The consortium agreement provided monopolies to those who built the landing stations and all adopted rent-seeking behaviour, mirroring wider Government behaviour: they charged high prices, ensuring low volumes of use but ensuring very fast commercial returns.

There was no fibre down the east coast of the continent and so a debate took place – supported by development funders – about how a new fibre route could be done on the basis of a ‘low prices, high volumes’ strategy. This was resisted by all the telco incumbents but as organisations that had no capital, they were eventually ‘arm-twisted’ into accepting this approach by those with finance. Privately-owned fibre cable Seacom did a ground-breaking deal with the South African NREN, TENET, to deliver low-cost bandwidth to universities and their students. However, the future of the internet in Sub-Saharan Africa might have looked very different if the Government-owned telcos had adopted prices that would grow the market in the public interest. That is a counter-factual worth pondering.

The arrival of these cables on this basis laid the foundations for cheaper, more widely distributed retail internet. However, it still needed the building of national fibre networks that could also be accessed on a competitive basis.

Some of these publicly-owned telcos acted as price-gatekeepers to their landlocked neighbours (see second table below). The attitude was very clear, as one of Tanzania’s Private Secretaries told an international cable policy advisor in 2006: “We’re going to have the landlocked countries pay for our terrestrial networks. Tanzania is a big country and its six neighbours will all to connect to us.” Luckily, most were able to go round it and use the much more liberalised route via Kenya. But Tanzania and its attitude, still remains something of an obstacle.

The story of M-Pesa does need repeating at length. In terms of development funding, it was probably the best value project ever. A tiny amount of DFID funding and a private sector partnership unlocked another foundational service. However, it has proved difficult to repeat the trick in other areas. It was harder to find a similar ‘single’, systemic change for other development challenges.

Again, the table below is my assessment of the countries that benefitted most and least in the early period. These are all examples, not the full list of all African countries. In the early 2000s, the reach of mobile and the internet was largely an urban capital phenomenon but it began to move out across larger parts of the population as time passed.

In terms of liberalisation, the francophone and lusophone countries were slower to adopt open, competition-based frameworks. The landlocked countries suffered a double-whammy. It cost more to get physical goods inland to them (often incurring taxes in transit) and over and over again they were held to ransom by those countries on the coast with the landing stations. To this day, Camtel has held back several Central African countries from getting reliable, cheaper internet. A now very small group of countries has resisted any kind of liberalisation. The worst of these is Eritrea that still has no international fibre connection. Some allowed private MNOs but continued to protect parts of the Government’s incumbent monopolies, most significantly wholesale monopolies.

**My view on 2000-2026**

I’ll hold off my conclusions for part two of this post. Even as things have undoubtedly got better in terms of digital access, the gap between the haves and have-nots has increased. At its starkest, nearly all African countries have two-speed markets: those with and without smartphones and therefore those using USSD and those making digital interactions. But as one person told me: “ICT served to raise the issue of equitable access that wouldn’t have been raised without it.” To conclude: “In 2008 you saw the ‘Twitterification’ of the space. Governments fell, people were dragged from their houses. We moved from a focus on phones to apps.” See you for part 2 in May.
