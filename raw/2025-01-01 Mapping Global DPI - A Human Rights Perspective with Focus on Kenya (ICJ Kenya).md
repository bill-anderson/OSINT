**ICJ Kenya (2025), *Mapping Global Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI): A Human Rights
Perspective with Focus on Kenya*.** A report evaluating DPI — digital identity, digital
payment and secure data-exchange systems — through an international human-rights framework,
with **Kenya** as the focus country.

Key points:

- Uses a human-rights lens (accessibility, accuracy, equity, privacy, ownership) to weigh
  DPI as both a catalyst for socio-economic transformation and a potential threat to
  fundamental rights, especially in developing states with resource/governance constraints.
- Global case studies: Estonia (X-Road / e-Residency), India (Aadhaar / UPI), Ukraine
  (Diia), Brazil (Cadastro Único), the EU digital-ID wallet, the US NIST federal digital-ID
  framework, South Korea, China. African cases: Tanzania, Nigeria, South Africa, Ghana,
  Ethiopia, Uganda — and **Kenya** in depth (**M-Pesa, eCitizen, Huduma Namba**).
- Devotes particular attention to the **Worldcoin** project in Kenya (from early 2022) as a
  cautionary case on **biometric-data collection** and what constitutes voluntary informed
  consent.
- Core human-rights concerns: weak **privacy/data-protection** safeguards (esp. biometric
  data); **equity and accessibility** risks that can deepen socio-economic divides absent
  intentional design; **transparency and accountability** gaps enabling surveillance or
  discrimination.
- Recommends universal human-rights-based DPI design principles, multilateral cooperation on
  global data-protection standards, and transparency/informed-consent/accountability
  safeguards; a dedicated African-context set of regional recommendations.
