# DRC connectivity improves but usage, power supply gaps linger

*By Tawanda Karombo, Special Correspondent, Connecting Africa. May 13, 2026.*

**NOTE FOR INGEST — contradiction flag:** this piece describes an "$8.7 billion National Digital Plan 2025-2030". The wiki's REV-COD-001 (resolved 2026-07-16) found the official PNN2 envelope to be **$1.5bn** ($1bn treasury + $500m secured partners) and treated the circulating "$8.7bn"/"$10.4bn" totals as uncorroborated. This item is a fresh trade-press re-assertion of the $8.7bn figure (and dates the plan 2025-2030, not 2026-2030). It does not overturn REV-COD-001 but shows the disputed figure still circulating.

The Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) is attracting a steady flow of investment into its telecom and IT sector although high taxes, device affordability and power supply constraints remain major hold-ups.

Increased investment inflows into the IT and telecom sectors in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) are helping to close connectivity deficits, although the populous African country still struggles with network usage gaps due to device affordability and electricity supply constraints.

Tower companies, development financiers and other IT sector companies have been investing in the DRC to boost connectivity through laying fiber, constructing base stations and linking to satellites to connect rural and remote areas.

For example, Helios Towers plans to invest US$110 million in the DRC while a partnership between Orange and Vodacom has committed to construct 2,000 new solar-powered basestations in the country by 2031.

"Mobile operators in the DRC are expanding access through a mix of network upgrades and service innovation," Caroline Mbugua, senior director for public policy at the GSMA said in an exclusive interview with Connecting Africa.

As operators upgrade networks, 3G coverage in the country has risen to 68% of the population while 4G has expanded to 57%, data from the GSMA shows.

Competition among key operators – including Vodacom, Orange, Airtel and Africell – has helped drive up the country's total mobile subscriber base to almost 65 million, according to DataReportal.

Analysts at GlobalData have projected that total revenues for telecom operators in the DRC will increase at a five-year compound annual growth rate of 6.5% over the 2024-2029 period.

GlobalData attributes this to revenue growth prospects in mobile data, mobile voice, and fixed broadband service segments.

## Supply-side shortfalls

It is not just private operators that are bumping up connectivity; the DRC government too realizes the importance of playing its part.

DRC President Felix Tshisekedi's administration recently announced an $8.7 billion National Digital Plan 2025-2030 that aims to connect communities and modernize public services.

Officials believe the digital sector can contribute $4.1 billion to GDP by 2029.

This will be achieved through the laying of 40,000 to 50,000 kilometers of fiber-optic cables, building Tier III data centers, and the establishment of more fintech platforms.

Investment inflows into the DRC telecom and IT sector are currently heavily focused on addressing "significant supply-side shortfall" according to Esther Chan, an investment director with asset management company Ninety One.

Ninety One manages the Emerging Africa Infrastructure Fund (EAIF), which has committed $30 million to tower company Eastcastle, enabling the company to purchase and develop telecom towers across the DRC.

## Additional constraints to connectivity

Mbugua emphasized that the DRC's major connectivity constraint is no longer coverage but rather gaps in usage, especially with the country's smartphone penetration rate below 50%.

"Smartphone adoption is projected to have reached around 40% of connections by 2025, but affordability remains the biggest barrier. Ultimately, connectivity is improving, but closing the usage gap will be the defining challenge for the DRC," said Mbugua.

The GSMA notes that many Congolese cite affordability as the most important factor when choosing a smartphone.

This underlines that cost is a primary barrier to adoption of smartphones, with the median price for an entry-level smartphone in the country standing at around US$305.

The impact of the cost of devices in the DRC is clear: despite having nearly 35 million Internet users, the DRC's Internet penetration rate is only 30.5% and there are significant regional disparities in access to the Internet.

The United States International Trade Administration recognizes the immense opportunities of investing in the DRC's ICT sector.

It however noted that licensing fees remain high, and there is uncertainty in terms of regulatory enforcement, which can be inconsistent, while import duties on ICT equipment remain high.

"[The DRC] faces substantial gaps in infrastructure and rural connectivity," said Nairobi-based Eva Mwasho, digital economist at the European Investment Bank, which has invested €135 million ($158 million) in support of the Congo Digital Transformation program.

The program supports e-government, cybersecurity and personal data protection frameworks as well as digital capacity building for public and private sector IT professionals.

Despite growing interest from investors to sink money into connectivity in the DRC, the Central African country's telecom sector is still nascent, presenting immense opportunities for investors.
