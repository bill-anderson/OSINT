# Project Appraisal Document — Digital Eswatini Project (P508948)

World Bank Group appraisal document for the Digital Eswatini Project, a **US$65.0m**
operation comprising a **US$19.3m IBRD loan** to the Kingdom of Eswatini, a **US$19.7m IDA
credit** to the Kingdom, and a **US$26m IBRD loan to the Eswatini Post and
Telecommunications Corporation (EPTC)**. Document dated **8 June 2026**, disclosed 19 June
2026; approved **15 June 2026** by the Regional Vice President for Eastern and Southern
Africa under delegated authority, as a subsequent phase of the Inclusive Digitalization in
Eastern and Southern Africa (IDEA) multi-phase programmatic approach. Components: Affordable
Broadband US$36.0m, Government in Your Hand US$25.0m, Project Management and Capacity
Building US$4.0m. Closing 31 July 2031.

**Note on the phase number:** this document says *Phase III*; the disclosed Stakeholder
Engagement Plan and the World Bank regional practice director both say *Phase 2*. The
inconsistency is in the World Bank's own materials and is recorded rather than resolved.

**Note on the cover date:** the cover's "APPROVED BY THE BOARD ON JUNE 27, 2024" refers to
the IDEA programme, not to this project.

I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

1. This Project Appraisal Document (PAD) covers phase 3 of the Inclusive Digitalization in Eastern and Southern Africa (IDEA) Multi-phase Programmatic Approach (MPA) Project approved by the World Bank’s Executive Directors on June 27, 2024, with the first phase (P502032) covering operations in the Republic of Angola, Democratic Republic of Congo, Republic of Malawi and the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa (COMESA).

A. Country Context 2. The Kingdom of Eswatini 1(Eswatini) is a small lower middle-income economy, largely driven by agro-trade and public sector investments. With a land area of 17,364 km 2, population of 1.2 million, and Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita of approximately US$3,928 2 in 2024 – Eswatini is a small economy. The economy is driven by agriculture, concentrated manufacturing, agro-processing industries (e.g., sugar industry at 15 percent of GDP), and the public sector. After a post-covid-19 recovery, real GDP growth slowed to 2.8 percent in 2024, before rebounding to an estimated 4.2 percent in 2025. Growth is expected to average above 3 percent over the medium term, with binary risks. GDP remains largely consumption-led, but with a boost from infrastructure investments 3, including digitalization.

3. The country heavily relies on its neighbors, and benefits from regional integration; single currency within the common market area (CMA), revenue sharing with the Southern Africa Customs Union (SACU). Eswatini relies on South Africa for ~85 percent of imports and 60 percent of exports. The Lilangeni (SZL) 4 is pegged 1:1 to the South African Rand, which is also legal tender via the Common Monetary Area (with SA, Lesotho, Namibia). Eswatini is in SACU (common external tariff, free internal trade, and revenue sharing that has supported fiscal consolidation 5) and in the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa (COMESA), benefiting from wider regional trade and payments systems.

4. Two thirds of the population live in rural areas; poverty and inequality remain the most stubborn primary development challenges. Poverty levels have remained elevated over the last five years, with approximately 42 percent of the population estimated to be living under the international US$3 poverty line. Women, especially in rural areas, face higher vulnerability to poverty due to factors like limited access to education, employment opportunities, and land ownership. 6 Income inequality is also high, with an estimated Gini coefficient of 54.6 percent in 2016, amongst the highest in the world.

5. The country is vulnerable to climate events. Eswatini ranks 65th in vulnerability and 147th in readiness according to the Notre Dame Global Adaptation Initiative's Index. Temperatures are projected to rise by up to 1.5°C by 2040,

B. Sectoral and Institutional Context 6. Eswatini’s digital sector has seen significant development in the last decade, but gaps remain that could constrain its ambitions to use digitalization as a tool for service delivery, economic development and job creation. The population coverage of mobile internet (4G) is high, with nearly 95 percent coverage. However, the subscription rate is only 57 percent and there are significant rural-urban and gender disparities in the use of internet services. As of 2024, only 70 percent of urban inhabitants use the internet. The number falls to 63 percent in rural areas. Similarly, fewer females use the internet as compared to males (a 100-to-97 ratio). 8 Moreover, Eswatini does not have sufficient coverage of fixed broadband networks, critical for a competitive and future proof digital economy; fixed-broadband subscriptions are only at 3 percent of the population.

Digital Infrastructure

7. The major reason for these gaps is the under-development of broadband infrastructure. Gaps in the middle mile infrastructure, connecting communities to the national backbone network, have left public institutions, schools, health centers, and Tinkhundla Centers (community centers) with limited or no connectivity. Furthermore, much of the existing network is made of copper lines, which are both outdated but have also led to significant theft. This implies that investment will be needed to both build new, future-proof networks and to replace existing copper with fiber-optic cable.

8. The existing market structure in the middle-mile segment directly contributes to under-investment. At present, Eswatini Post and Telecommunications Corporation (EPTC), a state-owned enterprise (SOE) established through the Communications Act in 1986, has the de jure monopoly through an exclusive license to provide middle-mile infrastructure. EPTC currently owns and operates over 2,500 km of fiber backbone in the country with an exclusive license and retains a dominant market position in wholesale 9. In this context, EPTC is expected to build and lease infrastructure to last mile service providers at reasonable quality and price. However, given EPTC’s own structure, including managing telecom services and the mandate of running postal services that are unprofitable, and other legacy financial challenges related to pension liabilities (see Box 1), EPTC has been unable to make necessary investments in building critical infrastructure.

9. In the small market of Eswatini, there is limited interest from the private sector in the long-term investments needed for national-scale connectivity improvements. Supported by legal reforms and regulatory action by the sector regulator, Eswatini Communications Commission (ESCCOM), the retail market has become increasingly competitive (there are now 21 licensed internet service providers (ISPs), a satellite ISP, and a competitive mobile services market). However, given the small market size with high fixed costs of building broadband infrastructure and need for economies of scale, there has been limited interest from the existing providers to scale middle-mile connectivity investments. 10. The Government of Eswatini (GoKE) has attempted to restructure EPTC in the past, albeit unsuccessfully, with renewed momentum today. Two bills were drafted in 2017 11 to remove control of the backbone infrastructure from EPTC, and enable EPTC, as a telecommunications corporation, to establish a third competing mobile network (two existing operators MTN, and Eswatini Mobile currently hold ~90 percent of market share, see figure 1). In January 2025, the GoKE sought World Bank financing and technical assistance to support reform for EPTC. Since, leadership changes to EPTC have been enacted with a call for appointment of a new CEO underway although the position currently remains vacant, and the replacement of 5 (of 8) Board positions to improve governance in August 2025. 11. The second reason for low internet use is the high price of data. Eswatini’s ICT price basket for access to fixed broadband internet averaged 19.06 percent of GNI per capita per month, while Eswatini residents paid on average 3.4 percent of GNI per capita per month to buy the cheapest 2GB of mobile broadband data (above the global benchmark at 2 percent of individual’s average monthly income). 12. EPTC has faced legacy issues that led to high operating costs, resulting in the downstream impact of high retail prices. Legacy financial issues primarily consist of i) unpaid and accumulating pension liabilities and ii) a large payroll. These costs are passed on to retail operators and onwards to consumers in the form of higher prices. Since 2022, EPTC has been implementing measures to address these issues, such as an ongoing staff downsizing round, albeit under parliamentary review, 14 and the preparation of a turnaround business plan. However, stabilizing EPTC finances hinges greatly on the ability to address its historical pension liabilities (see Box 1).

13. Regulatory reforms are needed to increase and sustain the competitive pressure on EPTC’s performance and improve pricing. ESCCOM, established in 2013 as an independent agency, has a history of successfully implementing key reforms (e.g., to regulate wholesale broadband and leased line rates). ESCCOM now seeks to introduce further good practice regulatory safeguards and oversight measures, 15 including introducing guidelines on infrastructure sharing and open access at cost-based pricing, and a price glide path directive. Defining this next round of reforms would help ensure that improvements in EPTC’s performance will also translate into reduced prices in downstream markets. The combination of a better-functioning EPTC and further regulatory reforms will help improve the state of the broadband market and set the stage for additional private sector participation, a more inclusive digital economy, and improved digital services. The downstream impact of a digitally connected market is expected to be on greater number and type of digital service offerings, resulting in more digitally enabled jobs.

Box 1: EPTC’s Legacy Pension Liabilities

The pension liabilities deficit results from a legacy defined benefit pension fund, which was closed to new members in 2011 and replaced with a more modern contribution scheme. EPTC’s legacy defined benefit scheme (DBS), comprising 155 active members and 511 pensioners, has fallen into a significant deficit amounting to a total of US$26 million as of November 2025. The fund's local administrator and a quarterly management committee were in charge of its performance review, and its liquidity crisis stems from a combination of i) poor historical investment performance; and (ii) a reduction over time in contributions as active staff in the plan retired.

To address the pension liabilities, a defined contribution scheme (DCS) has been put in its place since 2011, but financial support is required to complete the transition through clearing existing liabilities relating to the legacy fund. To do this, both the costs of transitioning active staff who are still working but members of the legacy pension scheme, as well as the costs of existing retirees who are drawing a pension under the legacy scheme, will need to be resolved.

An external actuarial assessment was carried out in Sep’25 16 which has recommended adopting a two-prong approach (i) For active staff members who are still working, will be given an option to be paid a lump sum equivalent to the actuarial value of their funds due to them and transitioned into the DCS. The lumpsum dues are estimated to be US$10million, including for inflation adjusted salaries, as well as budgeting for prospective increase in pensions due (ii) For existing pensioners under the DBS, the obligation for the payment of their pension costs (estimated to be US$16million) will be transferred to a well-qualified and reputed insurance company, which would then pay the pension obligations for the remainder of the time required. This is an established mechanism internationally for the resolution of legacy defined benefit schemes and comes with a fee to the insurance company to take on the pension scheme. Before these two actions happen, liquidation of the DBS must be approved by the Financial Services Regulatory Authority (FSRA) and an estimated US$26 million will be required to cover the cost of transition to the insurance company, and ongoing management costs during the transition.

Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) and Digital Services

14. The lack of digital services such as digital social security transfers, payments, financial services, and e-commerce is limiting wide-scale internet usage and resulting in inefficiencies from duplication of systems. Eswatini’s e-gov portal (online.gov.sz) offers few services (tax, utility services), whereas some services (e.g., health) are provided independently via platforms run by separate ministries, and other remain offline (education, immigration). The majority of public services are still delivered offline via Government offices and Tinkhundla Centers, which often see long waiting lines up to several weeks and still carry out paper-based transactions causing inefficiencies and security risks. Gender differences are also observed in the usage of digital services: in 2022, only 14 percent of women made digital in-store payments and just 6 percentshopped online, compared to 17 percent and 12 percent of men, respectively. 17

15. Digitalization of services at scale would greatly benefit from the development of digital public infrastructure (DPI). 18 National ID coverage is high (85–90 percent) 19, but back-end integration is weak. There is no systematic verification against the National Population Register (NPR), and a whole-of-government, MICT-led interoperability/enterprise architecture with modern IT and trusted data flows is missing. The ID system needs to add stronger verification and an authentication (biometrics, verifiable credentials) system and connect the NPR to other government DPIs and services to shift from paper-based processes to a user-centric, integrated digital services platform.

16. Increasing ubiquity of data flows and digital usage is likely to increase risks pertaining to digital safeguards, which the country has only started preparing for. The 2022 Data Protection Act, Computer Crime and Cybercrime Act, and the National Cybersecurity Strategy set a legal roadmap, but operationalization of this is now required. Key operational frameworks and functions are required following the legislation, including a Critical Information Infrastructure (CII) framework, a national Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT), a Data Protection Office and privacy safeguards (e.g., digital certificates, cryptographic tools). 29. Rationale for PBCs: The performance improvements targeted under PBCs are aimed towards both financial stabilization of the wholesale provider (EPTC), and a resulting wider spillover reform of improving pricing and competition in the telecom market. Delivering universal connectivity in Eswatini requires addressing the systemic issues undermining the financial stability of the only wholesale provider. Furthermore, given the small market size and limited interest from private capital in the form of a new entrant coming in, regulatory tools can be effective mechanisms to improve pricing and competition in the telecom sector. In this vein, 50 percent of project funding for affordable broadband (component 1) will hence be linked to achievement of select PBCs targeting both financial stabilization of EPTC and performance improvement -- allowing EPTC to deliver on lower pricing in the market -- and incentivesforthe regulator (ESCCOM) to ensure due price reduction directives are followed through. Regulatory interventions on open access and infrastructure sharing further aid in building competitive pressure. The chosen PBCs i) remain reasonably within the entities’ (EPTC, ESCCOM) control; ii) represent quantities and actions that can be easily monitored and verified by an Independent Verification Agent (IVA); and iii) do not introduce excessive complexity that could risk delaying project implementation.

D. Rationale for Bank Involvement and Role of Partners 39. A World Bank Group-wide approach will be leveraged to address market failures and stimulate and crowd in the private sector. Research across deployment of broadband infrastructure has shown 32 how certain environments may exhibit a market failure due to scarcity of capital, small market with limited ARPU, crowding out by SOEs, and undervaluation of public goods benefits. This is characteristic of the market in Eswatini where all these conditions hold. In this case, public sector investment (via World Bank) is required to stimulate demand and de-risk investments to crowd in additional capital from the private sector. In this vein, this project has been designed to strengthen the backbone (World Bank financing) with the intention of mobilizing private capital in the last mile from the private sector. The project is also leveraging upstream support from IFC transaction advisory team through standby technical advisory (as needed during project lifecycle) for restructuring the SOE and furthering policy dialogue to crowd in greater private capital.

52. Assessment and reduction of climate mitigation and adaptation risks: The project’s activities are universally aligned and hence have low mitigation risks. Through Component 1, the project will deploy energy efficient fiber optics digital connectivity infrastructure to lower mitigation risks to a low level. On the adaptation side, extreme rainfall, floods, and heat stress can adversely impact infrastructure so the project will conduct climate risk assessment and integrate climate resiliency measures to lower the adaptation risks to an acceptable level. Under Component 2, the project will strengthen the climate resilience of institutions and communities. Currently, paper based records—such as birth and death registrations—are stored locally across the country without centralized backups, making them vulnerable to damage or loss from climate disasters like floods 43. This project will mitigate that risk by digitizing these critical records and securely backing them up electronically. Additionally, the digitalization of several public services through the e-services portal will enhance the climate resilience of both the system and its beneficiaries by providing a robust digital platform that ensures continuous, uninterrupted service delivery. Unlike manual systems requiring physical visits to public offices, the e-services portal under subcomponent 2.2 supports service and business continuity during climate-related disasters 44. This is a key activity that directly contributes to climate adaptation by supporting Eswatini’s Digital Transformation Strategy 2024– 2028 (“Government in Your Hand”), which envisions “moving services online—including e-health, digital IDs, licensing, tax, and utilities—to reduce dependence on physical offices and ensure essential services continue during physical disruptions” such as floods and storms. The introduction of electronic civil registry services (which record life events like births, deaths, marriages, etc.) and large-scale digitization of services under component 2 will substantially cut down on travel and paper use, resulting in lower net GHG emissions in the long run. The Ministry of ICT has confirmed that the public services to be digitalized through this project are not currently active in the country making this initiative the first of its kind. This Bank-financed project supports an unprecedented and large-scale digital transformation especially for minority groups, especially women and remote underserved populations, who lack access to critical public services. Without this large-scale digital transformation of public services in Eswatini, at least 500K individuals (target for digital ID)45 would remain dependent on paper-based systems and need to travel frequently to public service offices.

B. Fiduciary 53. Financial Management (FM). FM arrangements for the project meet the minimum World Bank FM requirements, and several mitigation measures have been put in place to strengthen the FM arrangements further. Residual FM risk is assessed as Substantial. The 2024 Public Finance Review highlights some weaknesses in the PFM system which amongst others includes persistent cashflow shortfalls, regular bank reconciliations are not conducted, accounting records are unreliable, with discrepancies in government bank balances, financial liabilities, and revenue reporting. In as much as the above weaknesses are identified, there are key actions in progress which include implementing the new IFMIS, enhancing budget preparation and execution, and clearing expenditure arrears. An FM assessment was conducted at MICT and EPTC, the project implementing agencies, to evaluate whether the proposed project FM arrangements will provide reasonable assurance that World Bank provided funds are used for the intended purposes. The FM system and arrangements at MICT have been assessed to be capable of producing periodic reports to monitor the financial aspects of the project. EPTC uses Sage accounting system and will also be able to produce the required reports. During the review, key risks were identified that could impact the project, including non-compliance with fiduciary requirements due to limited FM capacity in the MICT. There will be a PIU situated in MICT which will be responsible for management, implementation, and coordination of the project. In the PIU, there will be finance personnel to manage the finances of the project and ensure that reporting is completed timeously and accurately. The auditing arrangements are considered adequate. EPTC has received an unqualified audit opinion for the past three financial years (2023, 2022, and 2021) while the government of Eswatini (consolidated) received a qualified audit opinion in the financial year 2023/24.

54. Ensuring that World Bank-provided funds will be used for their intended purposes can be achieved by using the same public financial management (PFM) systems a country uses for the management of its overall budget, provided these are assessed as being sound overall. MICT PIU will oversee the implementation of projects in the implementing agencies; therefore, assurance of compliance with FM processes and management of the budget will be monitored. The Ministry of Finance will authorize the opening and use of a Designated Accounts, denominated in United States Dollars, at the Central Bank of Eswatini to receive the World Bank funds.
