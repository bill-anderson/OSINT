Paratus launches Namibia's first private mobile network

Paratus Group has launched the first mobile network in Namibia that is owned by a private company, offering both LTE and 5G capabilities.

2 Min ReadBarney Harmse, Paratus executive chairman; Emma Inamutila Theofelus, minister of information and communication technology of Namibia; and Andrew Hall, Paratus Namibia MD. (Source: Paratus Group)

Pan-African telecommunications and network services provider Paratus Group has launched the first private mobile network in Namibia with both LTE and 5G technologies.

The company said the launch challenges the "the dominant state-owned companies in that country" and coincides with Paratus Namibia's 20th anniversary of operations.

In Namibia, Paratus has traditionally operated as an Internet service provider (ISP) and runs a large fiber network in the country and also offers connectivity via satellite.

Paratus is coming in as a mobile challenger to state-owned telecom operators MTC Namibia and Telecom Namibia.

MTC is the country's biggest operator with more than 2 million mobile subscribers by mid-2025, in a country with a population of just over 3 million.

Meanwhile, Telecom Namibia had around 336,000 mobile subscribers by the end of June 2025, according to statistics from market research company Omdia.

Paratus Group CEO Schalk Erasmus said the mobile network going live across Namibia marked a defining moment in the company's journey.

"Connectivity is the backbone of economic growth. Launching Namibia's first private mobile network is a deliberate step in our mission to transform Africa's digital landscape," Erasmus added.

Paratus said its Namibian operation is "the group's power base" and it has two decades of investment in the country.

The group has invested 1.42 billion Namibian dollars (US$79.8 million) in network infrastructure since 2018.

In the last year, a further N$600 million (US$33.7 million) was invested in the new Namibian mobile network, one-third of which was allocated to build a digital technology stack that integrates all Paratus services into a single platform.

The new mobile network was developed with Cerillion as the digital technology partner and Nokia as its mobile network partner.

Paratus claimed that the new mobile network will offer affordable, transparent data and voice packages, and includes both LTE network and 5G capabilities.

In Africa, Paratus Group has operations in 15 countries and runs an intercontinental fiber network and satellite infrastructure, including the landing of the Equiano subsea cable. It also operates data centers and offers network services and low-Earth orbit (LEO) satellite services.

"As we expand across the continent, every investment we make is designed to solve real challenges and open new opportunities. The new mobile service in Namibia is yet another example of that," added Erasmus.
