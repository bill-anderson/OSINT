Guinea and Sierra Leone Link National Fibre Networks for Regional Digital Integration - Telecom Review Africa My Custom Home Title

In a pivotal step toward regional digital integration, the Guinean Backbone Management Society (SOGEB) and Sierra Leone’s Leoncom have signed an Interconnection Agreement to link their national fibre optic networks. The agreement, formalized in Conakry, builds on a broader telecommunications cooperation framework between the two governments.

The partnership is designed to enhance cross-border data exchange, cut internet and transit costs for telecom providers and end users, and strengthen the resilience of national networks through shared infrastructure and redundancy. It also aligns with regional initiatives under ECOWAS and the African Union that promote a unified digital market across Africa.

The interconnection point is located in Pamelap, a key border town between Guinea and Sierra Leone. The initial transmission capacity is 42.5 Gbps, with expansion plans to reach 100 Gbps by 2026. The infrastructure leverages fibre optic technology and secure IP/MPLS protocols to deliver high-speed, reliable connectivity.

“This agreement is a major milestone for Guinea, reflecting our commitment to building an open and interconnected national backbone that benefits operators, enterprises, and citizens alike,” said Mohamed Kourouma, Director of SOGEB.

Foday Rado Yokie, CEO of Leoncom, emphasized the bilateral value: “Our collaboration with SOGEB will improve broadband access in Sierra Leone and stimulate economic interaction with Guinea.”

Nouhan Traoré, Secretary General of Guinea’s Ministry of Posts, Telecommunications and the Digital Economy, highlighted the initiative’s strategic significance: “This project is a key component of the Guinea–Sierra Leone Digital Pact 2024, aimed at unifying digital markets and services.”

The interconnection is expected to deliver widespread benefits. Telecom operators like Orange and MTN stand to gain from lower data transit costs, while enterprises and public sector institutions will experience improved latency and greater network stability—facilitating services such as cloud computing, remote collaboration, and VoIP. Crucially, communities along the border will gain better access to essential digital tools, including e-health and e-learning platforms.

Next steps in the technical rollout include establishing redundant infrastructure to ensure continuous, high-performance service. Meanwhile, discussions are underway to extend the fibre network toward Liberia, reinforcing regional digital infrastructure and fostering deeper economic ties.
