By Matshepo Sehloho, Associate Editor, Connecting Africa, May 29, 2025

The Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) has announced its intention to take stringent action against the unauthorized use of Starlink, a satellite Internet service operated by SpaceX.

ICASA said it has noted with "serious concern" reports alleging that the Internet service provider may be offering its services without the requisite authorization, and has formally engaged with SpaceX and is awaiting a response.

The Authority has also published a general notice in the Government Gazette, No. 49777, reiterating that all entities offering electronic communications services within South Africa are required to comply fully with the applicable legislative and regulatory frameworks.

"ICASA has instituted a formal investigation to ascertain the veracity of the allegations and to gather relevant facts and evidence. Members of the public and industry stakeholders who are in possession of any supporting evidence are encouraged to submit such information to: [email protected], to assist with the investigation. In the interim, ICASA has deployed inspection teams to conduct physical verifications in the affected areas," ICASA said in a statement.

"Should the investigation yield any breach with regulatory and legislative frameworks, the Authority will explore the applicable enforcement actions within its disposal, which may include, amongst others, lodging a formal complaint with the International Telecommunication Union (ITU)," said ICASA Chairperson, Mothibi Ramusi.

ICASA reiterated its uncompromising position against any form of non-compliance within the South African regulatory environment.

ICASA crackdown on Starlink

The clampdown comes at a time when Starlink owner Elon Musk and the South African government are at loggerheads over licensing rules requiring 30% local equity for telecommunications licensees.

The tensions between the country and Starlink were in full view during President Cyril Ramaphosa's working visit to Washington, D.C., last week, where he and his delegation sought to revitalize trade relations with the US, South Africa's second-largest trading partner.

During a post-meeting media briefing, Ramaphosa unveiled that a new trade and investment framework had been submitted to the US.

When asked about Starlink, he emphasized South Africa's commitment to fostering a favorable environment for foreign direct investment.

A couple of days after the press briefing, South Africa's Minister for Communications and Digital Technologies, Solly Malatsi, released a draft policy directive for public comment that, if passed, could open the door for Starlink to operate in the country.

The policy directive could significantly alter the way South Africa regulates empowerment obligations in the information and communication technology (ICT) sector.

During an appearance in Parliament this week, Malatsi said the recent publication of a policy directive was not giving Starlink special treatment.

"We are not attempting to open a special dispensation for Starlink or any other company or an individual," Malatsi told the parliamentary committee.

Malatsi's directive requests ICASA to look at the role of Equity Equivalent Investment Programmes (EEIPs) in the ICT sector as a mechanism to accelerate broadband access.

Current regulations under the Electronic Communications Act (ECA) do not permit companies that can advance South Africa's transformation goals through non-traditional ownership methods to qualify for individual licenses.

EEIPs have been established for multinationals whose global practices or policies prevent them from meeting the ownership requirements of broad-based black economic empowerment (B-BBEE) through traditional equity or share sales to black South Africans.

Instead these multinationals can find alternative ways to invest in the local economy or make an impact on South Africa's socio-economic development.

These multinationals must demonstrate that they have not engaged in ownership partnerships in other countries.

EEIPs are designed to foster investment in the development of small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) and to encourage broad-based economic participation.

Industry body weighs in on Malatsi's directive

The Association of Comms and Technology (ACT) said it noted with great interest Malatsi's policy directive on aligning ICASA's empowerment regulations with the B-BBEE ICT Sector Code, and the proposed inquiry into the need for Individual Electronic Communications Network Service (I-ECNS) license applications.

"These developments will shape competition, transformation, and the regulatory framework and provided it is implemented transparently, consistently, and with an equal application of the law, could usher in a new era for the sector," Nomvuyiso Batyi, CEO of ACT, said in a statement.

The Association said it supported efforts to resolve regulatory ambiguity between ICASA's ownership rules and the B-BBEE ICT Sector Code, whilst being considerate of South Africa's transformation agenda.

It advocated for smarter, scalable approaches that deliver meaningful impact and restore policy clarity, consistency, and investor confidence.

"We have consistently been on the pulse of this issue. We've supported the rollout of satellite technology in South Africa, within the same rules that everyone else follows," Batyi explained.

Starlink still illegal in SA

ICASA has always maintained that Starlink is illegal in South Africa unless it obtains a license to operate locally.

In November 2023, the regulator warned companies that were illegally importing and selling Starlink satellite Internet terminals in South Africa, calling the practice "illegal" and warning that fines could be imposed.

However, in August 2024, ICASA initiated a consultation process on a proposed new licensing framework for satellite services.

This led to SpaceX announcing that it planned to participate in public hearings held by the ICASA to plead its case. However, it withdrew from the hearings at the eleventh hour.

Starlink may eventually be granted access to operate in South Africa. If it does, it will join a long list of African nations that offer the company's services.

Starlink is available in many countries on the continent including: Nigeria, Burundi, Botswana, Ghana, Zimbabwe, Benin, Rwanda, Malawi, Mozambique as well as Zambia, Kenya, Madagascar, Sierra Leone, South Sudan, Eswatini, Niger, Liberia, Benin, Sierra Leone and the island nation of Cape Verde.

Starlink received a license in Somalia in April 2025 and has also reached an agreement with authorities in Chad and Guinea-Bissau.
