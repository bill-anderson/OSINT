Niger has become the latest African country to begin rolling out biometric identity documents to its citizens.

Biometric IDs are now compulsory for all citizens in Niger aged 15 and above.

Last December, Niger's government resolved to approve the introduction of biometric identity cards (IDs) and electronic passports (e-passports).

Following the resolution, the government in March 2026 officially launched the enrollment process for biometric IDs and e-passports and this month several members of the government were enrolled on the system.

According to the government, the use of biometric IDs and e-passports will replace six decades worth of the use of conventional identity documents and aims to simplify access to services in the public and private sector.

## Importance of biometric documents in Niger

The biometric IDs, which are now compulsory for all citizens aged 15 and above, have a 10-year validity period.

According to the government, the nationwide rollout of biometric documents is part of an agreement of the Alliance of Sahel States (AES) – comprising of Mali, Niger and Burkina Faso.

The biometric IDs feature multifunctional electronic chips that store biometric data, enabling secure verification for government services, banking and cross-border operations within the AES.

According to political commentator Garang Wal, Niger's transition from conventional identity documents to biometric is crucial in maintaining regional integration.

"Niger has become the second AES country after Burkina Faso to launch a biometric identity card system, General Abdourahamane Tiani [transitional President of Niger] set an example by personally enrolling for the new biometric national ID, marking the project's official start laying the foundation for regional adoption within AES," said Wal.

Furthermore, Sani Suleiman, a researcher and advocate, said biometrics have become deeply embedded in Africa's political, social and economic landscape.

"The rapid adoption of biometric and digital identification systems is transforming governance and public administration across Africa," he said.

"Promoted as tools to modernize service delivery, enhance electoral integrity, and strengthen state capacity, these systems are becoming central to how identity and citizenship are managed," Sulieman added.

## Implementation challenges for new biometric documents

At the end of 2025, Internet penetration in Niger stood at just 23.2%, which might result in it taking several years before every citizen obtains a biometric ID.

The costs of the new IDs is reportedly set at around US$6, an amount that could be beyond the reach of many Nigerien people – particularly those that reside in the rural areas.

Suleiman also noted that this technological expansion comes with profound risks due to weak legal frameworks, limited oversight and a growing reliance on foreign vendors which have over the years created an ecosystem vulnerable to privacy breaches, state surveillance and systemic exclusion.

"Biometric systems increasingly integrate electoral and civil identity data, giving governments vast surveillance capabilities while disenfranchising marginalized groups such as rural communities, migrants, and individuals without foundational IDs," said Suleiman.

Niger has become the second AES country after Burkina Faso to launch a biometric identity card system.

In addition, Suleiman said while biometric systems are often introduced to improve electoral processes and service delivery, their fragmented rollout forces citizens to repeatedly submit sensitive data across multiple platforms, increasing the costs and risk of fraud.

Nevertheless, the government can make use of banks, mobile money operators, and mobile network operators to enhance the nationwide rollout of biometric IDs.

## Biometric IDs foster regional stability

Over the past decades, AES countries have been plagued by frequent terrorist attacks particularly from organizations such as ISGS and JNIM which have caused immense instability in the region.

The lack of biometric IDs has made it easier for terrorist organizations to infiltrate the AES, however, this new initiative by the Niger government could help to abate this.

In addition, AES is likely to see economic growth via the growing digital sector due to the ease of doing business and seamless movement of goods and people amongst the three countries.

A 2024 pact between AES and the Russian Space Agency, Roscosmos, aimed at jointly developing telecommunication and remote sensing satellites will also see a country like Niger benefitting.

Once fully developed, these satellites are set to help strengthen national security, manage natural disasters and provide high-speed Internet access.
