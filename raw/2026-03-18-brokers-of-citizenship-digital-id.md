Biometric Update reports on an academic study by Laura Lambert (writing in The Conversation) on the "invisible yet inevitable" human intermediation in Sierra Leone's digital ID system. The study highlights "brokers of citizenship" — chiefs, legal personnel, local authorities, teachers, document brokers, family and friends — who help enroll, update and certify identities, particularly in the last mile where underserved communities reside.

Lambert conducted ethnographic research within a transnational research project on digital identification. She notes the new digital ID card for citizens costs 165 Leones (about US$8), seven times more than the old paper card.

Those lacking documents can turn to a justice of the peace — mostly retired men and some women appointed to administer oaths — who for a small informal fee record a person's name, birth date and birthplace on an affidavit. Citizens present these affidavits to the National Civil Registration Authority (NCRA) for biometric enrolment; NCRA staff told Lambert they generally trust the information ("What you put on it is what we now believe in").

Justices of the peace are described as a colonial-era institution that can reinforce discriminatory ideas about belonging, yet remain vital in helping underdocumented people gain recognition and avoid statelessness. "In contrast to their promise, digital identities do not abolish intermediaries," Lambert summarizes — the state relies on their work for identifying people (relevant to SDG 16.9).
