Sublime Seas to Storage Sphere: Mauritius Expands Hyperscale Data Hub - Telecom Review Africa My Custom Home Title

Traditionally celebrated for its tourism and natural beauty, Mauritius is positioning itself as a major data and connectivity hub in the Indian Ocean.

Through investments in hyperscale data centers and advanced connectivity, the country is reshaping its economic landscape, aiming to become a digital bridge between Africa, Asia, and beyond.

This development aligns with Mauritius‘s vision to diversify its economy, foster innovation, and attract global tech enterprises by offering secure, scalable, and efficient digital infrastructure.

Data Hubs Powering the Digital Mauritius Vision

At the heart of this transformation is the country’s Digital Mauritius 2030 strategy. The plan focuses on accelerating digital infrastructure development, including the rollout of state-of-the-art data centers.

Several hyperscale data centers have already been launched or are in development, built to Tier III and Tier IV standards to ensure reliability and uptime. These facilities are capable of supporting high-volume data processing and offer a strong foundation for cloud service delivery, catering to the needs of both domestic industries and multinational corporations operating in the region.

There are 10 data centers currently operating in Mauritius, spanning five key locations: Port Louis, Reduit, Arsenal, Quatre Bornes, and Rose Belle.

The capital, Port Louis, is home to Aphelion DC3, which features multiple dedicated, redundant backbone networks that connect them to the public IP network, ensuring that the risk of single-point failure is virtually eliminated; Rogers Capital Port Louis Data Center, which caters to single-server co-location and complex managed enterprise infrastructure; Harel Mallac Technologies Data Center, a Tier III data center that bolsters connectivity via fiber optic and radio wireless access; Rogers Capital La Tour Koenig Data Center, which focuses on providing disaster recovery solutions; and Rogers Capital Les Cascades Data Center, which is a high-availability (HA) site that has its own infrastructure and allows for a 50-50 load balanced configuration.

There are two data centers located in Reduit. The Bhumishq Data Center is a Tier IV‑designed carrier-neutral facility, offering fault-tolerant infrastructure, redundant power/connectivity, and advanced physical and logical security. The Rogers Capital Ebene Data Center houses the extended Mauritius Internet Exchange Point (MIXP) and is well positioned to host content servers, content delivery networks (CDNs), and international carrier infrastructures.

The Emtel Data Center was strategically erected in Arsenal as it is a flood-free area situated far from major business hubs, offering maximum resiliency.

The BIRGER Candos Recovery Center in Quatre Bornes has been fitted with non-ozone depleting, redundant precision cooling units to ensure continuous network availability.

The Rose Belle Data Center, located in Rose Belle, boasts 1,500 square meters of secured rack space and is supported by a stable 3-MW power supply, ensuring operational continuity for all hosted assets.

Strategic Location and Connectivity Strength

Mauritius is situated at the intersection of key digital corridors connecting Africa, Asia, and Europe. The island is linked to the South Africa Far East (SAFE) cable, the Lower Indian Ocean Network (LION/LION2), and the Meltingpot Indianoceanic Submarine System (METISS), providing high-speed, low-latency international bandwidth essential for hyperscale operations.

New undersea cable investments and planned upgrades promise to further enhance Mauritius’s global digital reach. A new undersea cable, named T4, will replace the South Africa-Far East (SAFE) cable that’s coming to the end of its life in 2027. T4 will have a thousand times more capacity than SAFE, according to Kapil Reesaul, CEO of Mauritius Telecom.

Domestically, broadband penetration and fiber optic infrastructure are expanding rapidly, providing businesses and institutions with dependable access to cloud platforms and digital services. Total internet subscriptions reached 1.01 million internet users in January 2025, while fixed broadband subscriptions are expected to hit 432,680 in 2026. These developments have made Mauritius not only a destination for data storage and management but also an ideal location for transit traffic and content delivery networks (CDNs), serving broader African and Asian markets.

Meeting Global Demand with Hyperscale Capacity

The global adoption of cloud computing, artificial intelligence (AI), big data analytics, and digital services is driving demand for hyperscale infrastructure.

Unlike traditional data centers, hyperscale facilities offer greater flexibility, energy efficiency, and cost savings. Mauritius is tapping into this trend by positioning itself as a low-risk, regulation-aligned host for regional and international cloud service providers (CSPs). Its bilingual workforce, favorable tax policies, and commitment to data protection laws, such as its Data Protection Act, modeled after the EU’s GDPR, further enhance its appeal.

Additionally, Mauritius has implemented a National Cybersecurity Strategy, ensuring digital resilience and bolstering its reputation as a secure base for data-intensive operations.

Mauritius’s progress in the digital sector is fueled by strong collaboration between the public sector and private stakeholders. Key local telecom operators—Mauritius Telecom, Emtel, and others—have partnered with global technology firms to build future-ready digital infrastructure.

The country is also actively participating in regional initiatives like the Smart Africa Alliance, promoting cross-border data flow and harmonized digital policies that support long-term digital transformation across the continent.

Overcoming Hurdles and Harnessing Potential

While Mauritius has made notable progress, it must continue addressing certain challenges, such as ensuring digital inclusivity, enhancing digital skills across the population, and securing sustainable energy sources to support its data center ecosystem.

Nevertheless, the opportunities are immense. The global data center market continues to grow rapidly, and Mauritius is well-positioned to capture a significant share by focusing on green infrastructure, partnerships with hyperscale cloud providers, and nurturing local tech innovation.

Looking ahead, integrating renewable energy into data operations and expanding edge computing capabilities could further strengthen the country’s data hub and digital value proposition.

With strategic investments, supportive policies, and a vision for a digital future, Mauritius is charting a path that not only benefits its own economy but also offers a model for other nations seeking to join the global digital economy.

As demand for reliable, secure, and efficient digital infrastructure grows, Mauritius is well on its way to becoming one of the leading digital gateways to Africa and beyond.
