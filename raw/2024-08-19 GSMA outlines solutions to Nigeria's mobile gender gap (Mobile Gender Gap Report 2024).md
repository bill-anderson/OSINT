GSMA, the global body for telecommunication firms, has outlined steps Nigeria can adopt to close its mobile gender gap, arguing stakeholders should prioritise gender equality and improve women's access to mobile internet and technology.

In its "Mobile Gender Gap Report 2024," GSMA identified key barriers — affordability, knowledge and digital skills, safety and security, and access — and recommended: mobile network operators lower the cost of internet-enabled phones and offer transparent, creative pricing; policymakers adopt affordability and subsidy policies; operators and internet companies invest in women's digital literacy and tailored skills training; and stakeholders develop apps and services enhancing women's safety and strengthen protections against online abuse. GSMA also stressed better gender-disaggregated data.

On the figures: two in three Nigerian adult women lack mobile internet access, a 38 percent gender gap in smartphone ownership. While 52 percent of adult Nigerian men use mobile internet, only 33 percent of women do. As of March 2024, Nigeria had 163.89 million mobile internet subscriptions. The most underserved women include those with low literacy, low incomes, in rural areas, or with disabilities.
