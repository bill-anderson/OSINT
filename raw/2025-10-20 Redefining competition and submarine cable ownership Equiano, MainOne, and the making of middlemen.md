## Abstract

With their growing power and ownership of the top layer of the internet, Big Tech are investing in submarine cables and are redefining the cable industry and, by extension, the digital economy. By exploring two submarine cables – Equiano Cable, a cable independently built and owned by Google in Africa; and MainOne Cable, a cable originally built by a Nigeria company in West Africa and later acquired by a US-based company – this article examines the complexities of critical infrastructure ownership. Through these case studies, this article, in the context of the Next Billion User discourse, highlights the ongoing redefinition of competition through obsolesce and re-making of middlemen through co-location technologies. It notes the evolution of Google from a content provider to a telecommunication, media, and tech company – the convergence of different sectors under Google’s control. Finally, it opens opportunity for further research on cables, centralization, convergence, coloniality, digital risks, and other critical issues that may arise from Big Tech’s growing ownership across the pillars of the digital economy. It also highlights the landscape of connectivity infrastructure in West Africa (with a specific focus on Nigeria).

## Introduction

Submarine cables are a critical infrastructure that enable international communication – ergo, they facilitate data traffic and economic, social, and cultural exchanges across borders. Due to their burial in the ocean, submarine cables are invisible and outside scholarly and popular imagination (Starosielski [Citation2015](#)) – although scholarship in this area is growing (Bueger and Liebetrau [Citation2021](#); McCabe and Flynn [Citation2024](#); McGeachy [Citation2022](#); Starosielski [Citation2015](#); Winseck [Citation2017](#)). Given their strategic role in security, communication, and global economy, the ownership and control of submarine cables needs to be studied and understood.

In particular it is important to study ownership and control of cables given the current changes taking place across the communication value chain. Currently, content providers take up the largest portion of international bandwidth. Netflix and YouTube alone take up more than a quarter of the global downstream traffic on the internet (Kranjec [Citation2023](#)). Further, Big Tech are building new submarine cables either as a consortium or independently (TeleGeography [Citation2022a](#); Winseck [Citation2017](#)), becoming both content providers and telecommunication companies, a historic development that will redefine the internet, political economy, and the business of technology.

By exploring the ownership and control of two submarine cables – Equiano Cable, independently built and owned by US-based technology company, Google, across the Atlantic coast of West Africa; and MainOne Cable, a cable originally built by a Nigeria company in West Africa and later acquired by a US-based company – this article examines the complexities of critical infrastructure ownership. In sketching out these case studies, it relied on a variety of sources to highlight the landscape of connectivity infrastructure in West Africa with a specific focus on Nigeria and the evolution of the global cable market. Thereby, it contributes to critical infrastructure studies and digital political economy. It also contributes to African studies by bringing the political economy of digital infrastructure in Africa, which is an understudied area, into the conversation.

## Landscape of connectivity infrastructure in Nigeria: An overview

There are different types of submarine cable operators (Winseck [Citation2017](#)): traditional telecoms operators, telecommunication companies that emerged within the last two decades, and US-based content companies – the last two being later entrants into the market. Cable operators adopt one of three approaches to accessing connectivity from or building submarine cables: purchasing or leasing capacity cable built or owed by someone else, through a consortium, or building one independently (Stowell [Citation2018](#)). The consortium and leasing approach continues to be employed by traditional telecommunication operators. At the same time, content providers such as Google, Amazon, and Meta, have become major investors in new cables since the late noughties and are building cables either independently or as part of consortia with traditional telecoms operators. Regardless of approach, building a submarine cable is a significant investment – both financially and otherwise with the cost of construction being a barrier to entrance into the cable sector.

The SAT-2 cable, which became operational in 1993 and is now obsolete, and SAT-3, operational in 2001, were the first fiber optic internet submarine cable built to Africa (Afullo [Citation2000](#); Hayes [Citation2013](#)). The earliest internet cable built to Nigeria was completed in 2002 and it was followed by nearly a decade of inactivity till 2010 when MainOne cable – and, later, Glo-1. Currently, Nigeria and the West Africa region are served by eight privately-owned cables: MainOne, Glo-1, SAT3 Cable, Africa Coast to Europe (ACE), Nigeria Cameroon Submarine Cable System (NCSCS), West Africa Cable System (WACS), each supplying international bandwidth for local use within Nigeria and onward delivery to neighboring countries *via* terrestrial cables (Google, Africa Practice, and and Genesis Analytics 2022; Shapshak [Citation2019](#)). Notably, the two latest (and biggest in terms of capacity) cables are Google’s Equiano and 2Africa (co-owned by Meta, formerly Facebook) cables. The capacities of Equiano and 2Africa, the two cables with either whole or part ownership by US-based Big Tech, far surpasses all other cables in Africa. In fact, the total combined capacity of all submarine cables to Africa is dwarfed by the individual capacities of Equiano (144Tps) and 2Africa (180Tbps) by approximately 20 times (22Africa 2024; Francois and George [Citation2019](#)). Meanwhile, one may argue, as a Google-sponsored report on the Equiano Cable notes, that Nigeria has a “relatively strong first mile infrastructure” (Google, Africa Practice, and and Genesis Analytics 2022, 6; Urama and Ogbu [Citation2018](#)) with about eight submarine cables serving the country. What becomes apparent, when contrasted with the availability of first mile infrastructure, is that Nigeria’s – and, indeed, the region’s – low bandwidth capacity is a function of the shortfalls in middle mile infrastructure (Google, Africa Practice, and and Genesis Analytics 2022).

The submarine cables operating or being built to Nigeria are owned by a diverse cast of players ranging from technology companies to traditional telecommunication companies. Of the eight submarine cables, four are wholly owned or independently built – they include, Google’s Equiano, MainOne, GLO-1, and NCSCS. This article focuses on two of this wholly owned and independently built cables: Google’s Equiano and Equnix’s MainOne.

## Case study 1: The Equiano cables and Google at sea

To reach the next generation of internet users who are currently un(der)connected – i.e., the Next Billion Users (Arora [Citation2019](#); Google [Citation2023](#); Oyedemi [Citation2021](#)) – and improve its global internet business, Google is building its own connectivity infrastructures (Fanou et al. [Citation2017](#)). However, unlike Equinix which has investments in the first and middle miles, Google’s focus is on building international internet connectivity *via* submarine cables. To achieve this, Google is employing all three approaches (Stowell [Citation2018](#); Winseck [Citation2017](#)) to attaining connectivity: purchasing capacity, forming a consortium, or building a cable independently (Stowell [Citation2018](#)). At first, the company adopted the first two approaches; however, its strategy has evolved with more investments being made toward independently building submarine cable systems across the world – including the Equiano Cable that will connect countries across the Atlantic Coasts of Africa to Europe. Furthermore, much like Meta (Facebook), Google appears to be employing disintermediation in the deployment of the Equiano Cable. Given the historical business model of cable owners, its adoption of disintermediation strategy suggests that Google is seeking independence from existing carriers. It also suggests that profiting directly from the cable isn’t its goal but rather acquisition of customers and their data (Atkinson and Oshadami [Citation2022](#); Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#)). In addition, Google’s independent strategy, which this article further argues later, suggests that it is working toward gaining a competitive advantage in the cable market and to facilitate better access to its cloud services (Guilfoyle, Paige, and McLaughlin [Citation2022](#)). Therefore, to understand the import of the Equiano Cables, we need to examine Google’s history as an influential global business shaping global commerce, culture, and economics.

As is well known, Google was founded in 1998 by Larry Page and Sergey Brin with the lofty goal of arranging the world’s information (Brin and Page [Citation1998](#); Google [Citationn.d.](#); Stross [Citation2008](#)). Google has now expanded its services and product beyond search – although search remains Google’s core and most profitable business, as it continued to evolve and adapt to changing times or respond to present and/or future market dynamics to “stay relevant” (Page [Citation2015](#)). Indeed, as far back as 2008, for instance, Google clarified, in a regulatory filing, its ambition that, although it began as a technology company, it has “evolved into a software, technology, internet, advertising and media company all rolled into one” (quoted in Lee [Citation2010](#), 914). This evolution, powered by the company’s surveillance and advertising-driven business model, has profoundly influenced the internet and digital economy (Lee [Citation2010](#); Smyrnaios [Citation2019](#); West [Citation2019](#)).

Despite its significant achievements in the decades since founding, Google recognizes that there is opportunity for more growth in userbase, information control and, ergo, revenue (Conti [Citation2009](#)). To meet growth demands, Google has trained its sight on the Next Billion Users as the market in developed economies saturates or plateaus. To achieve these goals, it is investing in international connectivity infrastructure, specifically submarine cables. Google has explored several unconventional approaches to build connectivity infrastructure in developing countries. For example, Google launched Google Station to provide public Wi-fi in select locations across developing countries (Oyedemi [Citation2021](#); Pisa and Polcari [Citation2019](#)). The company also introduced Project Loon, Project Link – later rebranded to CSquared – and Project Taara to build internet networks in Africa’s urban centers (Cassidy [Citation2013](#); Davies [Citation2019](#); ITU [Citation2022](#); Lawler [Citation2021](#); Pisa and Polcari [Citation2019](#); Westgarth [Citation2020](#); x [Citation2023](#)).

Google’s foray into traditional connectivity – specifically, undersea cables – began in 2008 when it took part in a consortium to build Unity, a trans-pacific submarine cable connecting California to Japan (Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#); Hansell [Citation2007](#); Winseck [Citation2017](#)). The company continued to build out its international broadband backbone *via* partnerships with existing telecommunication players until 2018 when it changed strategy by announcing its first wholly owned and self-financed intercontinental cable: Curie Cable connecting Chile to Los Angeles (Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#); Stowell [Citation2018](#)). That same year, Google disclosed that it had spent $30 billion in three years expanding its range of telecommunication infrastructure – including submarine cables and data centers – with plans for more investments (Sloss [Citation2018](#)). The goal, according to Google, is to connect the world further entrenching its self-acclaimed role as the “world’s largest” network delivering “25% of worldwide internet traffic” (Sloss [Citation2018](#)). The scale of Google’s ambition in building its own internet is apparent its nomenclature for its new wholly owned and built cables: they are named alphabetically after historic personalities: Curie, named after the Nobel Prize winning scientist, Marie Curie, runs from California to Chile; Dunant, named after Red Cross co-founder, Henry Dunant, connects Virginia and France; Equiano, named after Nigerian-born author and abolitionist Olaudah Equiano, connects Portugal to South Africa; Firmina, named after Brazilian author and abolitionist Maria Firmina dos Reis, connects eastern US with Argentina; Grace Hopper, named after computer science pioneer Grace Hopper, connects the US, UK, and Spain. This suggests that Google intends to build as many cables as there are letters of the alphabet, in keeping with its parent company’s name, Alphabet (Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#)).

Equiano cable connects sub-Saharan Africa *via* the Atlantic coastline to Europe *via* Portugal, further extending the company’s global cloud and telecommunications infrastructure (Francois and George [Citation2019](#)). When Google introduced Equiano in 2019, it was its third intercontinental cable and 14th submarine cable investment (Francois and George [Citation2019](#)). Google’s announcement said that it will extend connectivity in Africa using a space-division multiplexing (SDM) technology to increase network capacity by 20 times – a rate that is reportedly faster than previous cables built in Africa (Francois and George [Citation2019](#); Google Africa [Citation2022](#)). In March 2022, it was reported that the Equiano cable had landed in Lome, Togo – the first landing in the region (Al Jazeera [Citation2022](#)). A month later, Google followed this up with the announcement of a second landing in Lagos, Nigeria (Ehimuan [Citation2022](#)). In Nigeria, Google’s landing partner for Equiano, the West Indian Ocean Cable Company (WIOCC), landed the cables at the OADC Lagos data center – also a subsidiary of WIOCC – from where the cables were to feed to other data centers (Qiu [Citation2022](#)). Additional landings were in Swakopmund, Namibia, Cape Town, South Africa, and finally, Sesimbra, Portugal later in 2022.

Google, unlike other Big Tech currently pursuing goals of connecting the next billion users by building international connectivity (including Microsoft, Facebook, Amazon, etc.), has chosen to build its new cable ecosystem independently and has full control of its cables by fully owning it. It appears that the decision to independently build its own cables, while indicative of Google’s broader global ambitions, is also aimed at reducing the company’s dependency on third party cables and, thus, reducing expenses involved in transmissions (The Daily Upside [Citation2021](#)). This, in a way, follows similar development by other tech companies who choose to build their own physical infrastructure for better service delivery and growth. Amazon, for example, has successfully developed its own logistics system and, in so doing, reduced dependence on third party delivery, enabling further growth, and acquisition of more customers. In explaining this approach to cable, Ben Treynor Sloss, Google’s Vice President of Engineering, pointed out that: “Since we \[Google\] control the design and construction process, we can fully define the cable’s technical specifications, streamline deployment and deliver service to users and customers faster” (Sloss [Citation2018](#)).

However, independently building a cable, unlike a consortium approach, means that “a single company often had to negotiate landings in many nations where they were unfamiliar with established practices” (Starosielski [Citation2015](#)). To land its intercontinental cable, Google would have had to negotiate with stakeholders of each country where the cable lands – and consent to local regulations. The company, instead, partners with other companies in landing its cables at specific countries but retains ownership and control of the underwater segment of the cable therefore securing total autonomy over its cables (Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#)). In other words, Google builds and maintains the international segments and works with landing partners at branching points (i.e., where the ocean connections docks and meets ground connections) in specific countries to make terrestrial connections that *via* a cable landing station and other intermediaries reach the middle and final mile across the African continent.

This decision to build its own cables represents a new iteration of the company’s heady ambition for as a 2007 *New York Times* report notes:

Google may be the ultimate do-it-yourself company. From the start, Google’s sense of its own engineering superiority, combined with a tightwad sensibility, led it to build its own servers. It writes its own operating systems (Hansell [Citation2007](#)).

Google’s decision to wholly own their subsea cable rather than form a consortium begins to make sense as the company, in full control of a powerful international cable, can chose partners to land its cable in specific countries. This allows Google to avoid some of the encumbrance of country-specific regulations associated with being the landing party and operate its cable infrastructure in international waters with minimal regulations. For as Guilfoyle, Paige, and McLaughlin ([Citation2022](#), 658) note, submarine cables are “not (for the large part) located within sovereign territorial jurisdiction” – meaning that major section of cables located in the sea are outside of the scope of country regulations till it lands in a specific country. Thus, without owning actual physical submarine cable assets in specific countries and by partnering with local landing partners who deal with country-specific regulations, Google avoids the regulatory complications and other costs surrounding ownership of physical infrastructure in multiple countries (Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#)).

Google has framed the Equiano cable as a developmental imperative: an altruistic project that has immense socioeconomic benefits with primary goal to “interconnect humanity” (Google Africa [Citation2022](#)). According to a Google sponsored report, the Equiano cable will have significant impact on the Nigerian economy by cutting the retail price of internet by 21%, improve download speed by six times, indirectly create over 1.6 million new jobs and adding $10.1 billion to the Nigerian GDP by 2025 (Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#); Ehimuan [Citation2022](#); Google, Africa Practice, and and Genesis Analytics 2022; Njanja [Citation2021](#)). At 144 Tbps, Equiano has several times the capacity of MainOne at 8 Tbps – in fact, Equiano’s capacity outstrips the combined capacity of all the current cables services (Google Africa [Citation2022](#); Google, Africa Practice, and and Genesis Analytics 2022); or, as a report puts it “with gargantuan capacity enabled by the latest technology, Google’s Equiano and Meta’s 2Africa will make those older cables functionally obsolete – old minibuses in a fleet of shiny coaches” (Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#)). This higher capacity confers on Google – in addition to the company’s dominant position across other areas of the digital economy – a superior position allowing Equiano to dominate the regional market for international internet communication while securing for Google a near dominance in broadband, software, and hardware. Thus, Google’s monopoly in submarine cables opens up a pathway for further monopoly or, at least, a controlling interest in cloud technologies, search and other emerging areas.

## Case study 2: MainOne cable, the Equinix connection and the local within the international

MainOne, a telecommunication company founded by Funke Opeke in 2008, played a cornerstone role in galvanizing West Africa’s digital economy and, more specifically, Nigeria’s nascent tech ecosystem (Carlen [Citation2016](#)). Apparently, the company was borne of the desire to extend internet connectivity in Nigeria. This followed the failed attempts to privatize Nigeria’s then under-performing and now defunct national carrier, the Nigerian Telecommunications Limited (NITEL) (Onukwe [Citation2021](#)). Over the years, MainOne grew to become a provider of connectivity and data center solutions, and owns digital infrastructures including four data centers; 1,200 kilometers of terrestrial fiber network across three states in southern Nigeria: Edo, Ogun, and Lagos; and an eponymous submarine cable, MainOne Cable, the first wholly owned Nigerian submarine cable, connecting Portugal to West Africa with branching units in Lagos, Nigeria, Accra, Ghana, and Cote d’Ivoire (Equinix 2021). MainOne, the company, by virtue of its ownership of communication infrastructures, also serves as a key internet exchange point and provider of data services to local and international companies (Carlen [Citation2016](#)) – including Big Tech companies such as Google. Consequently, MainOne enables international and domestic connectivity for West African users and provides internet services in over 10 countries (Kene-Okafor [Citation2021a](#)). The company’s network of internet infrastructure – particularly, the MainOne submarine cable – is often credited as the enabling factor behind the growth of Nigeria’s tech economy (Kazeem [Citation2017](#)) as it provided the additional first mile and second mile infrastructure that spurred internet connectivity and technological innovations.

In 2008, MainOne began building its 7,000km submarine cable which runs between Nigeria and Portugal, and interconnects with other cable systems running to key cities in Europe, North America, and Asia (MainOne [Citation2022](#); Okonji [Citation2009](#); Urama and Ogbu [Citation2018](#)). At the time of its launch announcement in the late noughties, the MainOne cable was expected to improve the broadband capacity in West Africa by 15 times (Okonji [Citation2009](#)). The company’s goal of building a submarine cable was initially meet with skepticism; however, to build the cable, MainOne sought for and raised $240 million in debt and equity investment from the Africa Finance Corporation, Africa Development Bank (AfDB), and other funders (Carlen [Citation2016](#); Onukwe [Citation2021](#); Opeke [Citation2017](#)). Building of the MainOne submarine cable was contracted to TE SubCom/Ciena (then known as Tyco Telecommunications) (MainOne [Citation2023](#)). MainOne submarine cable is reportedly West Africa’s first privately owned, open access subsea cable (MainOne [Citation2022](#); Okonji [Citation2009](#); Onukwe [Citation2021](#); Opeke [Citation2017](#)). In 2013, it was extended to Cameroun with further extension to Cote D’Ivoire and Senegal in 2018 (MainOne n.d.) with possibility of further extending to Morocco, Senegal, and Canary Islands. To complement its submarine cable system service, MainOne has continued to grow out its suite of digital and telecommunication services, including expanding its digital infrastructure business to include data centers that enable data connectivity in 10 African countries (MainOne [Citation2023](#); Onukwe [Citation2021](#); Opeke [Citation2017](#)). MainOne, by its own admission, owns the largest digital ecosystem in West Africa through which it provides public internet and private interconnection across West Africa – including to landlocked countries such as Burkina Faso and Chad (Atkinson and Oshadami [Citation2022](#)).

In 2021, Equinix, a US-based multinational internet company, announced that it will acquire MainOne at $320 million. This development followed similar expansion by Equinix across the world including a US$161 million purchase of GPX India in 2020 and, in 2022, US$160M data center investment in South Africa (Equinix [Citation2020](#), 2021, [Citation2022](#); Kene-Okafor [Citation2021a](#), [Citation2021b](#)). MainOne’s acquisition by Equinix, which was closed in 2022, is reportedly the largest tech deal in Nigeria (Kene-Okafor [Citation2021a](#)) that includes the MainOne submarine cable and data centers. It was Equinix’s first in-road into Africa as part of its “long-term strategy to become a leading African carrier neutral digital infrastructure company” ([CitationSEC 2021](#), 6).

Equinix, a Silicon Valley company founded in 1998 by Al Avery and Jay Adelson as a data center services provider, bills itself as the digital infrastructure company providing “sustainable” digital and telecommunication services for organizations and governments across the world (Equinix [Citation2022](#); [CitationSEC 2021](#)). The company began operations as an internet exchange point that enabled service providers and organizations to interconnect ([CitationSEC 2021](#)). According to the company, the name Equinix – a combination of equality, neutrality, and internet exchange – reflects its vision of facilitating the growth of the internet. The founders reportedly believing that “they not only had the opportunity but also the responsibility to create a company that would be the steward of some of the most important digital infrastructure assets in the world” ([CitationSEC 2021](#), 5). In many ways, this espousal of a responsibility to create and spread the internet as a self-appointed steward of the internet is hardly surprising given the long history of the leaders of Silicon Valley companies envisioning themselves as messiahs whilst employing the language of techno-optimism in describing their companies (see: Daub [Citation2020](#); Geiger [Citation2020](#)).

Equinix’s mission to be a company that manages global digital infrastructure assets feeds directly into the company’s goal to power the world’s “digital leaders” by providing the infrastructure needed for digital operation (Equnix [Citation2021](#); [CitationSEC 2021](#)). Equinix reportedly owns about 240 data centers across 32 countries on six continents, counts about half of Fortune 500 companies as customers, and enables over 419,000 interconnections globally (Equnix [Citation2021](#); Kene-Okafor [Citation2021a](#)). Mergers and acquisition are central to Equinix’s growth plans as the company has pursued expansion by acquiring or merging with other companies operating within its areas of interest.

Network effect bestows on Equinix a valuable position as a widely used infrastructure service provider, an advantage Equinix seeks to maintain by continuously acquiring more customers to keep expanding its network. This, in other words, is both a value proposition for new customers and an imperative for Equinix to keep acquiring more customers, as the company puts it: Equinix offers a “global platform” spanning 27 countries and a large customer base that creates a network effect, “a significant source of competitive advantage for Equinix” ([CitationSEC 2021](#), 10).

Equinix’s investments in digital infrastructure, therefore, appear to be strategic for maximal network effect. However, it is one thing to invest in expansion and another to realize returns on the investment for as Equinix points out in its SEC filing: the company may not have sufficient customer demands in certain markets where it is building data centers despite the substantial operational and financial resources committed to them ([CitationSEC 2021](#)).

From the foregoing dive into Equinix it is apparent that the company is more invested in and clearly has more interest in the middle mile market where it is positioning itself as a market leader and “steward” of the world’s digital infrastructure through its ownership and control of middle mile infrastructures and solutions like data centers. However, according to its SEC filing, the company is acquiring not just data center but also business solution and technologies that are complementary to its core data center solution – including submarine cables. With Equinix’s middle mile infrastructure, according to Alex Vaxmonsky, Equinix’s Director of Business Development for Global Service Providers, the company has the capability to interconnect to submarine cable systems. This capability forms a part of its value-added service offerings to customers (Vaxmonsky [Citation2021](#)). Furthermore, about 40 of Equinix’s data centers are submarine cable enabled where they “help offload cable landing system (CLS) overhead costs and the management of landing points” (Vaxmonsky [Citation2021](#)). In other words, with recent innovations in the sector, Equinix can now terminate submarine cables directly inside its data centers rather than use the previous approach of landing cables at a facility stationed at a beach (Vaxmonsky [Citation2021](#)). This new interconnection approach – directly to a data center rather than a traditional landing station – allows Equinix to cheaply connect networks and removes delays “by eliminating the backhaul between the CLS and a data center or another interconnection hub” (Vaxmonsky [Citation2020](#), [Citation2021](#)).

Following on from this, one may reasonably conclude that the MainOne submarine cable is a complementary infrastructure for Equinix. However, MainOne’s middle infrastructure and network, on ground connection to local governments and businesses, and knowledge of the West Africa are the key payoffs of its Equinix acquisition. This is clear from Equinix ongoing efforts to consolidate at the middle mile even as it invests a small amount in first mile infrastructure. Furthermore, given the changing dynamics of the industry with content providers like Google becoming submarine cable owners, Equinix strategy appears to be to position itself firmly at the middle mile by “developing the next generation of submarine system landing station infrastructures to accommodate this shift by placing cable landing stations in its global data centers” (Vaxmonsky [Citation2019](#)).

## Redefining competition: Equiano, MainOne, and the making of middlemen

Cable owners, typically telecommunications companies, historically, made profits by leasing out capacity and charging to connect the ocean trunk of the cable to the terrestrial network. In other words, they were both submarine cable operators and, in some cases, the landing party, charging exorbitant fees to facilitate terrestrial connection to first mile services – due, in large part, to their monopoly on submarine cables and the cost of owning and maintaining cables. Or, as Malecki and Wei ([Citation2009](#), 368) write, “traditionally, the terrestrial ‘backhaul’ link that connects a cable landing point to an urban demand center was the monopoly of the incumbent national telecommunications provider, as is still the case in much of Africa.” This tradition mostly continued even with the neoliberal reforms of the late twentieth century and the cable market crash of 2000s which saw the emergence of new cable owners from BRICS nations (Winseck [Citation2017](#)). MainOne Company, for example, is the owner and appears to be the landing party of the MainOne submarine cable in all the countries where it is present often choosing external partners to build its landing station – such as Vertiv, a provider who built MainOne’s co-locating cable landing station and data center in Ivory Coast (Vertiv [Citation2020](#)).

As noted earlier, Equinix, MainOne’s parent company, has the capacity to interconnect its data centers directly with submarine cables, i.e., it can directly terminate cables in its data centers, as opposed to the traditional practice of landing them on a facility located on a beach. The foregoing suggests disintermediation – i.e., the removal of middlemen – in the telecommunication value chain with new technologies and business models to facilitate faster and cheaper internet connectivity. However, it also leads to the consolidation of the position of incumbents. With its growing ownership of co-located data centers and cable landing stations and increasing elimination of traditional landing stations, MainOne is increasing its control of the middlemen market as a dominant intermediary with a unique selling point to attract more clients: data center where cables can terminate thereby reducing the costs and delays associated with traditional cable landings and interconnection (Vaxmonsky [Citation2021](#)). Admittedly, MainOne’s physical properties in Nigeria do not have these capabilities yet. However, Equinix is expanding MainOne’s ecosystem by making new investments in data centers to attract more international cloud providers and companies, and localize content (Atkinson and Oshadami [Citation2022](#)).

On the other hand, Google, with its long history of upsetting traditional industries, appears poised to redefine telecommunications, from the ocean trunk to landing (Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#)). Google’s ad-focused business model means that it does not have to contend with some of the financial and business challenges faced by traditional telecommunication companies. This, in other words, is to argue that Google does not face the capital constraint that a traditional telecommunication operator may face as its entry into the telecommunication market appears to be subsidized and offset by the profit generated from its ad business. The business of submarine telecommunication is a costly enterprise that demands significant capital investment in building, maintenance, and upgrades. Thus, as telecommunications market expands with more users coming online and demanding more bandwidth, submarine technology operators face more pressure to meet market demands by upgrading their technology – a development that may chips away at profit margins. Equiano’s entrance into the market will, unsurprisingly, disrupt the status quo due to Google’s business and cable model: the company is building its cable independently, restricting its connectivity presence to the ocean trunk, deploying partnership as tool to reach the last mile, and its cable has a higher capacity than all the other cables combined.

With Google’s immense financial and technical capabilities, brought on by ad-powered global business empire, the company, as some analysts suggests, can afford to forgo profitability *a la* the traditional telecommunication model even as their advanced cable technology with superior capacity will out-compete existing cables (Atkinson and Oshadami [Citation2022](#); Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#)). Thus, preexisting cables and their owners are faced with forced obsolescence if they decide to compete at the submarine side or pivot to the retail side where they function as middlemen providing middle mile services that facilitate access for other second mile operators as landing partners or enabling access for the last mile and markets that are further inland (Blum and Baraka [Citation2022](#)). In this way, terrestrial operators become partners, in a mutually beneficial relationship with Google who needs their capacity to reach the end user and, conversely, they rely on Google to access superior international bandwidth. Putting into perspective Google’s investment across telecommunication, media, and technology, it becomes clear that the Equiano cable is but one strand in a multifaceted strategy designed to maintain control over information consumption and production, securing for Google the capacity to shape the present and future of the internet.

Asked in an interview if Google’s Equiano (and Meta’s 2Africa) represent a challenge to MainOne, Funke Opeke, founder and CEO of MainOne Company, answered thus:

Not at all. One of the studies Google did on the impact of their submarine cables that they had built in other parts of the world is actually when they start building in a region, they actually buy more than they’re able to provide themselves. So we see the entrance of global platforms and other players with their own direct investments into our market not a sign of competition but as a sign of the growing size and significance of our market and the need for greater participation by these players. From the inside of the business, these companies are not building enough infrastructure for them not to need to buy from others, not to need to extend the services to different locations, and not need diversity from their networks. So yes, there is more interest in our market because our market has become more significant. Yes, some of the interests and the investments will compete with us. But I think it would also largely accelerate the growth of our market. That means a bigger business opportunity or addressable market for MainOne (Kene-Okafor [Citation2021b](#)).

Cables follow financial capital and capital follows profit. That is to say, cables are built to serve markets where there is already or, at least, the possibility of profiteering or generating value that feeds the bottom-line. Therefore, as Opeke notes, there is more interest in Africa because the “market has become more significant” (Kene-Okafor [Citation2021b](#)). Indeed, while capacity is growing globally, Africa is the world’s fastest growing international bandwidth market at a compound annual rate of 44% between 2019 and 2023 (TeleGeography [Citation2023](#)). Africa’s fast growing but under-connected population that not only makes it the future of the internet, i.e., the Next Billion Users. Indeed, as an Equinix blog posts, in the context of Google’s Equiano and Meta’s 2Africa cables, notes: “the predicted drop in Africa’s international connectivity costs will be substantial because the ultimate prize for both companies is not direct profit from the cable systems, but acquisition of hundreds of millions of new subscribers and their valuable data” (Atkinson and Oshadami [Citation2022](#)).

Interpreting the market significance of Big Tech’s investment in cables solely from the perspective of profit is insufficient when, in fact, the reality may be much more complicated for different reasons. Therefore, to understand the context and dynamics of Equiano’s relationship with MainOne – and, by extension, their parent companies, Google and Equinix – it is imperative to note that:

- Google’s connectivity infrastructure is largely focused on the high seas as opposed to Equinix’s significant investment at the middle mile. With the aid of co-locating facilities that serve as both a landing station and data center, in the words of Opeke, “ensures accessibility of the data center content to local and international nodes,” e.g., Google’s Equiano (Vertiv [Citation2020](#)). Thus, MainOne market is significantly expanded by Google’s higher capacity submarine cable, Equiano, which will undeniably drive more data traffic and demand as the market is expanded, leading to more business for MainOne (Equinix) as a strategic middle mile player offering interconnection and data services.
- Equiano will, unsurprisingly, disrupt the telecommunication status quo but it does not appear to be focused on profiting from the cable. Google’s intentional focus on the high sea highlights their conspicuous absence at the middle mile (where connectivity investments is most needed) and suggests that Google may require partnerships to execute its ambition of reaching the Next Billion Users.
- Finally, the need for diversity of cables is a security imperative (see Starosielski [Citation2015](#)).

Therefore, it can reasonably be said that Equiano may not be a direct competitor of MainOne at the submarine level but rather a partner and facilitator of Google services at the middle mile. For one thing, Google is a client of MainOne in Nigeria – using the company’s middle mile infrastructure for its data traffic needs. Furthermore, Equinix, MainOne’s parent company, and Google share a relation beyond Nigeria: Equinix provides landing station services supporting Google’s Curie cable in Los Angeles (Vaxmonsky [Citation2019](#)), and data center services in different countries. Therefore, while Google and Equinix may play in the same sector, they have different focus and, thus, serve different segments of the market even if they may interconnect and are interdependent in ways. However, a reasonable argument could be made within the context of imperialism and capitalism, that Google and Equinix’s efforts at disintermediation could be linked to the historical elimination of local middlemen in the production value chain. The local middlemen lack the financial wherewithal and political influence of global companies and are, therefore, unable to compete.

## Conclusion

Telecommunication infrastructure in Africa – particularly Nigeria as Africa’s biggest economy and a littoral country – has attracted significant investment over the last decade due to the high cost of data, low quality of service of local traffic, and growth in mobile telephone (Fanou et al. [Citation2017](#)) – these factors representing, at once, barriers to internet use for users but, for internet businesses, a market opportunity. In response to these market gaps, international and local companies have trained their sights on the lower-income and emerging markets of the Global South, positioning it as the place to, in the case of Big Tech, reach the Next Billion Users (Arora [Citation2019](#); Oyedemi [Citation2021](#); Pisa and Polcari [Citation2019](#)). Big Tech’s entrance into the cable market also highlights that the market is currently evolving, as this article shows.

Google’s Equiano is a dominant cable with outsized bandwidth capacity that dwarfs other cables on the continent, owned and controlled by one of the wealthiest and most powerful corporations the world has ever seen, which does not directly land in Nigeria. Google’s approach to cable ownership may, on the surface, appear to make no sense given that it runs contrary to the logic of the cable industry: undersea cables are landed on the shores by its owners who then sell capacity to other businesses at the middle and last miles. However, Google’s cable model, as the case study show, involves partnerships that effectively inoculates the company from getting too entangled with the local politics and regulatory regimes, as it relies on local partners to land and provide access to Equiano’s capacity. Placed in the context of the Next Billion Users and the company’s drive to acquire users fast, it seems that the point of the Equiano cable may not be to generate profit directly but serve as an ancillary service that supports Google’s vision of holding a dominant position as an information company. This perspective, too, may be applied to the MainOne cable, a local cable that was acquired by a global company, to understand and compare the changing dynamics in the submarine cable industry.
