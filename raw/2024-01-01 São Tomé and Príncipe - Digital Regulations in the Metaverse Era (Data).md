# São Tomé and Príncipe — Digital Regulations in the Metaverse Era (Data chapter)

> ## Excerpt
> IBA country chapter mapping STP's data-protection framework: the Personal Data Guarantee and Protection Law (Law 03/2016), modelled on the EU 1995 Directive; the National Framework for Interoperability (Law 1/2024); Malabo Convention signatory status; and the ANPDP as enforcement authority, with fines in STN and criminal sanctions.

IBA Intellectual Property, Communications and Technology Law Committee — "Digital Regulations in the Metaverse Era". São Tomé and Príncipe chapter, Data section.

## 1. Data policies, strategies or regulations applicable to the metaverse

Although São Tomé and Príncipe (STP) currently lacks specific data protection policies, strategies or regulations explicitly addressing the metaverse, the existing legal framework for data protection applies. The **Personal Data Guarantee and Protection Law (Law 03/2016)** in STP closely mirrors the European Data Protection Directive (Directive 95/46/EC) and aims to safeguard privacy and individual liberties concerning personal data by promoting transparent processing practices. The law has a wide scope, extending protection to all types of personal data of individuals, and applies to processing (paper-based or digital) where:

- a data controller is headquartered in STP;
- processing occurs within a controller's establishment in STP;
- STP law applies due to international public law, even if processing occurs outside the country; and
- equipment (automated or not) located in STP is used by a controller not based in the country, except equipment used solely for transit.

STP has recently tackled data sharing and governance in public institutions through the **National Framework for Interoperability (Law 1/2024)**. STP is also a signatory to the **African Union Convention on Cyber Security and Personal Data Protection** (Malabo Convention), which establishes a framework for cybersecurity, cybercrime and data protection across Africa. Non-personal data is not specifically addressed in the STP legal framework, and there are no policies or strategies addressing non-personal data in the metaverse context.

## 2. Protection of metaverse-associated data

Although it does not specifically refer to metaverse data or novel data categories, the Personal Data Guarantee and Protection Law applies to personal data processing in the metaverse — user or third-party data collected through devices and peripherals, inferred data, or avatars. Provisions on data-processing principles, data subject rights, and controller/processor responsibilities are fully applicable. Novel data types such as synthetic data raise new regulatory challenges the current framework does not adequately address. Intellectual property rights may apply to certain non-personal data.

## 3. Stakeholders in the data value chain

The metaverse data value chain can be complex, involving content creators, platform and service providers, data intermediaries, analysts, regulators, researchers and users. Depending on the nature of their activities they may be subject to different rules; because the same stakeholder may assume multiple roles, a case-by-case analysis and clear mapping of data flows is necessary.

## 4. Data protection principles and enforcement

The STP constitution enshrines the protection of private life; all citizens are entitled to protection of their personal identity and private and family life. The Personal Data Guarantee and Protection Law is the primary instrument embodying data protection principles: **lawfulness**, **transparency**, **proportionality**, **minimisation**, and **veracity**.

The law establishes fines and criminal sanctions for breaches, including violations of principles, obligations and rights, and unauthorised data communication or transfer. The **Data Protection Agency (Agência Nacional de Protecção de Dados Pessoais, ANPDP)** imposes fines and ancillary measures such as data cessation, blocking, deletion or destruction. Failure to properly notify, or provision of fraudulent information to the agency, can result in fines ranging from STN 50m to STN 120m for individuals and from STN 250m to STN 500m for legal entities. Most intentional data-protection violations can be punished with fines upwards of 120 days' salary and/or one year imprisonment.
