The Incorporated Trustees of the Digital Rights Lawyers Initiative (DRLI) have sued the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) for failing to publish a privacy policy on its official website.

The group filed the suit alongside Solomon Etisang, a digital rights lawyer, at the Federal High Court in Yenagoa, Bayelsa State.

The applicants argue that INEC has not told Nigerians how it collects, stores, uses, shares, retains or protects the personal data it processes through its website and electoral activities.

They say the omission violates the Nigeria Data Protection Act (NDPA) 2023 and the right to privacy guaranteed under Section 37 of the 1999 Constitution.

According to the court filings, Section 24(1)(a) of the NDPA requires data controllers to provide data subjects with clear and transparent information about how their personal data is processed.

The applicants also argue that INEC failed to meet its duty of care under Section 24(3) and did not comply with Section 27(1), which requires data controllers to make relevant privacy information available to data subjects.

Through their lawyer, Olumide Babalola, they are asking the court to declare that INEC breached these obligations by failing to publish a privacy policy.

They also want the court to order the commission to immediately publish one explaining how it collects, processes, stores, retains, discloses and safeguards the personal information of voters, website users and other data subjects. In addition, they seek an order directing INEC to file an affidavit showing it has complied with the court’s judgement.

#### FIJ HAS REPORTED THIS

The lawsuit comes about 10 months after FIJ [**reported**](https://fij.ng/article/npf-inec-jtb-nafdac-collect-personal-data-only-to-disregard-privacy-policies/) that INEC’s official website had no privacy policy in October 2025. The commission holds one of Nigeria’s largest databases of personal information.

FIJ found that while many public institutions had updated their websites to comply with the NDPA, INEC’s website did not explain what personal data it collected, why it collected the data, how long it retained it, who it shared it with or the rights available to data subjects.

On the subject of data protection, FIJ reported that Lere Olayinka, a media aide to Minister of the Federal Capital Territory Nyesom Wike, shared details from one citizen’s voter registration record on social media during a political dispute in May.

[**READ MORE: Wike’s Aide Under Fire for Leaking Voter’s Data From INEC’s Admin Webpage**](https://fij.ng/article/wikes-aide-under-fire-for-leaking-voters-data-from-inecs-admin-webpage/)

The disclosure prompted questions about how information stored in INEC’s voter database became publicly available. INEC denied suffering a data breach.

Instead, the commission said an authorised user misused valid administrative credentials to access the information. It maintained that its database had not been hacked.

At the time of filing this report, INEC had not publicly responded to the suit, and the court had not fixed a hearing date.
