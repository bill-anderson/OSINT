#### Summary

O

ver the past three years, the Central African Republic (CAR), a deeply unstable country in the grip of transnational organized crime and foreign state influence, has implemented a series of cryptocurrency ventures that endanger its economic future and create an open invitation to foreign criminal organizations to capture the country and its economy. Launched in the midst of a war waged by Russian mercenaries and national armed forces against civilian populations perceived to be affiliated with rebel groups, the CAR government claimed that its cryptocurrency programme was designed for the benefit of the people and would boost economic growth and infrastructure development across the country. 1 But that statement seems far from reasonable. An impoverished population, exposed to mass executions, torture and gang rape, with limited access to electricity, mobile phones and the internet, cannot engage in crypto investments in any meaningful way. Instead, the cryptocurrency programme launched by the CAR government appears to be tailored more to the interests of foreign investors than to the needs of its own population. 2

Cryptocurrencies are known to be exploited by transnational criminal networks for money laundering, 
drug trafficking, fraud, ransomware, sanctions evasion and the financing of terrorism, and the CAR’s 
cryptocurrency initiatives lack the regulation and oversight needed to protect the system from such 
illicit financial flows. The CAR’s two main crypto initiatives, first Sango Coin and then the $CAR meme 
coin, were launched hastily and appear to have performed poorly. 
The country’s crypto policies – particularly the decision to make cryptocurrency legal tender – have 
been criticized by international financial institutions, regional banking regulators and the country’s 
own constitutional court. 3 In particular, a July 2023 law that facilitates the tokenization of national 
resources – allowing digital tokens issued on a blockchain to represent ownership rights, usage rights or 
revenue streams linked to oil, gold, timber or land – lacks critical governance safeguards and threatens 
the country’s sovereignty. The involvement of foreign private individuals who have been charged with 
or implicated in fraud and other criminal acts in the development of the country’s crypto programme 
further undermines its already dubious credibility. 4 

Ultimately, the CAR’s cryptocurrency initiatives – incongruously imagined, poorly executed and riddled with red flags – in effect favour elite, and possibly criminal, interests at the expense of the wider population. Rather than being a means for monetary innovation, they are yet another avenue through which the country can be captured and exploited so that those in power can maintain their status. In recent years, the space for independent research and civil society in the CAR has significantly shrunk. Access to information has become increasingly limited. A climate of fear prevails among those who possess sensitive information, leaving very few willing to speak out for fear of reprisals. For these reasons, this paper relies on publicly available information and data obtained through open-source research techniques. It is the product of three complementary research approaches: an in-depth literature review, open-source investigation and cryptocurrency analysis. 5

#### Introduction

S

ince 2022, the president of the CAR, Faustin-Archange Touadéra, has championed a series of cryptocurrency ventures that threaten the CAR’s economic sovereignty and invite further capture by foreign actors and criminal networks. These initiatives reflect a long-standing pattern of fragmenting state authority and outsourcing national wealth to private foreign interests. 6

Since his election in 2016, Touadéra has leaned heavily on external security, as well as political and transnational criminal networks, to consolidate power. 7 Chief among these is Russia’s paramilitary Wagner Group, a designated transnational criminal organization that played a decisive role securing Touadéra’s hold on power during the disputed 2020–2021 election campaign. 8 For three years, Wagner mercenaries, alongside national armed forces and local militias, conducted brutal operations marked by mass killings and atrocities that the United Nations warned might constitute war crimes and crimes against humanity. 9 In return for protecting Touadéra, Wagner, like other foreign allies, was granted privileged access to the CAR’s natural wealth, notably in the mining and logging sectors. 10

Backed by Russia, Rwanda and foreign business interests – including organized criminal networks – 
Touadéra has further entrenched his rule. 11 A controversial 2023 constitutional overhaul that removed 
the limitation on presidential terms, among other changes, consolidated what observers describe as the 
‘Touadéra system’. 12 This system rests on the support of his political party, the United Heart Movement 
(Mouvement Coeurs Unis, MCU), and a 
network of shadow advisers, mobilized as 
needed to advance the president’s shifting 
political and economic objectives. 13 

President Faustin-Archange Touadéra in January 2025 at a meeting with Vladimir Putin in Russia. © Evgenia Novozhenina/ POOL/AFP via Getty Images

With Western donors withdrawing, state finances collapsing and resources being drained by armed violence and humanitarian crises in rural regions, the CAR government turned to cryptocurrency. 14 In 2022, in the lead-up to the constitutional change, the CAR became the second country in the world to legalize cryptocurrency as official tender. In the three years that followed, the country launched two separate cryptocurrencies and authorized the sale of mineral resources, forests, agricultural land and passports in exchange for cryptocurrency. These successive initiatives have been fraught with issues and have lacked meaningful regulatory safeguards, enabling financial fraud and opening the door to transnational organized crime. In building a platform that primarily serves powerful networks operating through opaque methods, the CAR regime is effectively trading away the country’s sovereignty at the expense of the wider population.

#### Digital currency in the CAR:

#### Grand promises, serious risks

I

n April 2022, the CAR established a legal framework for cryptocurrencies and recognized bitcoin as legal tender alongside the CFA franc. 18 This initiative bypassed domestic institutions and regional financial authorities, and drew immediate opposition from the Banque des États de l’Afrique Centrale and the regional banking commission, which warned that the law violated treaty obligations and threatened regional financial stability. 19 Undeterred, Touadéra presented the initiative as a path to prosperity and modernization. His chief of staff told the press: ‘The president supports this bill because it will improve the conditions of Central African citizens’. 20

In this, the initiative was fundamentally unrealistic. With just 15.7% of the population connected to electricity, 21 fewer than 40% holding mobile subscriptions and GDP per capita at only US$467, 22 most citizens lacked the infrastructure and resources to trade in digital currency. In practice, this decision was primarily intended to attract foreign investors and capital. Yet no legitimate corporation is likely to invest in a country mired in conflict and marked by acute state fragility and rampant corruption, and so the initiative instead left the field open to predatory and criminal actors. 23

Monetary innovation or risky business?

When the 2022 law recognizing bitcoin as legal currency was adopted, the CAR stood at the centre of an intense geopolitical contest. Five years earlier, the Touadéra regime had forged a strategic alliance with Russia, and, as a result, the Wagner Group had established a presence in the CAR, providing military training and security, and advancing Moscow’s interests in Africa. In parallel, the CAR distanced itself from its former Western partners, notably France and the European Union, and – together with

Touadéra presented the initiative as a path to prosperity and modernization.

Wagner – sponsored disinformation campaigns targeting Western interests. 24 This situation led to power struggles between the permanent members of the United Nations Security Council. The CAR–Russia alliance, however, soon tested the limits of the CAR’s financial system. In October 2017, a CAR delegation travelled to Sochi to meet Russian Foreign Affairs Minister Sergey Lavrov. 25 While the discussions reportedly covered mining, security, politics and communications, it was banking that emerged as a critical obstacle. According to minutes of the meeting revealed by independent media outlet Mondafrique, ‘all banks in the Central African Republic carry out transfers through correspondent accounts in France, which prevents the Russian side from making bank transfers’. To resolve this issue, the parties agreed that ‘the Russian side ha[d] the right to make payments in cash’, to be delivered by private jet. 26 The CAR delegation reportedly expressed willingness to explore other cash-transfer mechanisms with its finance ministry, 27 and in 2022, Touadéra had found a solution, announcing, ‘The alternative to cash is cryptocurrency.’ 28

By introducing a national cryptocurrency, the Touadéra regime could have perceived several potential 
benefits: 
■ Monetary autonomy, by reducing dependence on the French-controlled CFA franc 
■ Foreign investment, by leveraging bitcoin’s global profile to attract investors in high-margin sectors 
such as mining, energy and digital finance 
■ Digital integration, by positioning the CAR as a frontier actor within the global fintech ecosystem 
■ Alternative financing, by diversifying financial flows in a country excluded from international credit 
markets 
■ Fiscal gains, by capturing revenue through transaction fees, taxation or licensing in a context of low 
banking penetration 
Although no exit from the CFA franc had been formally announced, the decision to legalize crypto 
currency signalled an attempt to bypass structural financial constraints. More broadly, it reflected an 
effort to entice non-traditional foreign investors operating outside of conventional banking channels – for 
instance, the Wagner Group, a designated transnational criminal organization. 29 
Upon the law’s adoption, regional banking regulators warned that legalizing cryptocurrency carried 
severe risks: extreme price volatility, crypto crime, money laundering, terrorist financing and tax 
evasion. 30 The UN Conference on Trade and Development later echoed these concerns, cautioning that 
cryptocurrencies create new channels for illicit financial flows in developing countries. 31 

A member of the CAR military wearing the symbol of the Wagner Group, which has provided military training and security to President Touadéra’s regime. © Patrick Meinhardt/AFP via Getty Images

These warnings were not without merit. In El Salvador, the only other country to have adopted cryptocurrency as legal tender, the government ultimately failed to deliver its promised benefits. There, the initiative, adopted in September 2021, was rolled back in January 2025. 32 The International Monetary Fund (IMF) had long warned that such policies entailed ‘large risks for financial and market integrity, financial stability, and consumer protection’. 33 Since then, the IMF has emphasized that the most important safeguard for any country is to avoid granting crypto assets the status of official currency or legal tender, stressing that without comprehensive regulatory and policy frameworks, the consequences could be ‘dire’. 34

The Sango sell-off of sovereignty

Despite these warnings, in July 2022, just a few months after the law recognizing cryptocurrency as legal tender was adopted, the CAR began issuing its own national cryptocurrency, Sango Coin, 35 on the bitcoin blockchain. 36 Called ‘the most progressive economic initiative in Africa and beyond’, the project’s goal was to create a ‘new monetary system’ for the country, 37 including a digital national bank and a national bitcoin treasury. 38

From the start, the Sango Coin project was marketed to attract foreign investors and capital. It offered foreigners Central African passports in exchange for US$60 000 in cryptocurrency, provided that the buyer held equivalent Sango Coin for at least five years; it offered e-residency for US$6 000 in Sango Coin held for at least three years, as well as plots of land for US$10 000 in Sango Coin held for ten years. 39 The scheme also enabled foreign investors to acquire shares in the mining and forestry sectors at low cost. 40

The project further envisaged that Sango would be ‘the catalyst of the tokenization of the country’s natural resources’, essentially letting the government raise funds from investors in exchange for digital tokens, or bonds, backed by state-owned resources. 41 The prospectus also promised the creation of a ‘crypto city’ and a ‘crypto island’ in Bangui, the capital, which would be developed into ‘a futuristic city in the heart of Africa’. 42 It stated that improving the country’s physical infrastructure was ‘one of the main pillars of the Sango project’. 43

In August 2022, however, the Constitutional Court ruled that the purchase of citizenship, e-residency or land using cryptocurrency was unconstitutional. 44 Whether citizenship was granted under the initiative, before or after the court’s ruling, remains unclear. However, an elite circle close to Touadéra has repeatedly been implicated in the sale of diplomatic passports to foreign nationals – including notorious criminals and drug dealers wanted by international justice. 45And, despite the Constitutional Court’s ruling, Touadéra’s government continued to support the scheme, stating that the decision does not call into question Law no. 22.004 of 22 April 2022, which governs cryptocurrency in the CAR. 46

That position shifted slightly in March 2023. Under pressure from the Economic and Monetary Community of Central Africa, the IMF and the World Bank, among others, the parliament amended the April 2022 law governing cryptocurrency on 23 March 2023, revoking bitcoin’s status as legal tender and softening the ‘obligation’ to accept cryptocurrency as payment to the ‘freedom’ to do so. 47

The most important safeguard for any country is to avoid granting crypto assets the status of official currency.

These changes did not deter the CAR’s cryptocurrency initiatives, however, and in July 2023, the parliament adopted a new law on the tokenization of land and natural resources – in apparent contravention of the constitutional court’s ruling. 48 Intended to complement the April 2022 cryptocurrency law, it set the stage for a sweeping strategy to digitize and commoditize the country’s natural wealth – including minerals, petroleum, forests and agricultural land – and to explicitly authorize the use of virtual currency, Sango Coin in particular, in the investment process. 49

This new law envisaged the publication of mining titles on the Sango platform, to be auctioned to locally registered companies. 50 Crucially, it also allowed foreign investors to register companies directly online and to acquire mining titles, land and forestry concessions. It even enabled them to apply for business visas through the system. 51 The legislation made available ‘at least 5 000 hectares of pre-demarcated land’ for concessions ranging from 49 to 99 years. 52 It further authorized the tokenization of agricultural land from 10 to 200 000 hectares and the parcelling of forested land from 10 000 to 500 000 hectares under renewable 30-year licences. All plots were required to be developed within five to seven years, or risk withdrawal – a condition that was unlikely to be enforced, given the weakness of state institutions. 53

Framed as a push for innovation, the promotion of cryptocurrency and the tokenization of the CAR’s resources – without any apparent contribution to state treasury or budget forecasts – heightened concerns about the exploitation of these new systems, the erosion of national sovereignty and the lack of safeguards for public interest and local communities. The initiative also opened the door to foreign speculation and opaque transactions. In late 2023, the Action Group Against Money Laundering in Central Africa (Groupe d’action contre le blanchiment d’argent en Afrique Centrale, GABAC) warned that government authorities had neither conducted an assessment nor demonstrated an understanding of the risks ‘inherent in the misuse of virtual assets’. 54

In the end, the Sango Coin initiative failed to meet its ambitious objectives. While the goal was to sell 210 million tokens within a year, only 10% were purchased, representing less than €2 million in total. 55 Within two years, the initiative had largely fizzled, and its website was taken offline until 29 April 2025, when the project’s X account announced it would not continue in its original form and that a complete transformation was on its way. 56 The status of the funds already invested remains unclear, and the ambitious plans to develop a crypto city and a crypto island have not materialized.

Cryptocurrency enabling transnational crime

C

ryptocurrencies are widely recognized as being vulnerable to exploitation by transnational criminal networks for money laundering and other illicit activities. Criminal actors exploit pseudonymity, 57 regulatory loopholes and the transnational nature of these assets. 58 The Financial Action Task Force, Europol and blockchain analytics firm Chainalysis have documented the use of crypto transactions in crimes including the trafficking of drugs, humans, weapons and wildlife, as well as fraud, extortion, cyberattacks, sanctions evasion, money laundering and terrorism financing. 59

Recent cases around the world demonstrate how highly sophisticated networks use cryptocurrency to conduct criminal operations and launder hundreds of millions of dollars. 60 In early 2025, for example, the United States accused North Korea-linked hackers of conducting one of the largest known cryptocurrency thefts, seizing approximately US$1.5 billion worth of ethereum, a blockchain-based platform similar to bitcoin, from a Dubai-based firm. 61 That same year, INTERPOL conducted a cybercrime operation across

Africa that led to more than 1 200 arrests and identified 88 000 victims: in Angola, authorities dismantled 25 cryptocurrency mining centres run by Chinese nationals and, in Zambia, they broke up a crypto investment scam that had seen victims defrauded of an estimated US$300 million. 62

In the CAR itself in 2023, hundreds of people who had given their money to La Clé du Succès (Key of Success), a CAR-registered financial agency promoting cryptocurrency investments, discovered that they had been swindled and that management had fled with up to US$1.6 million in cryptocurrency and fiat money. 63

Although traditional banking channels continue to facilitate the criminal activities of transnational organized criminal networks and terrorist groups, virtual assets are playing an increasingly prominent role. 64 Cryptocurrencies such as bitcoin, ethereum and monero, along with stablecoins like tether, are increasingly used by these networks and terrorist groups to raise, store and transfer funds. 65 This phenomenon is accelerating, and the African continent faces a growing risk of becoming fertile ground for cryptocurrency scams, transnational financial fraud and money laundering. ■

$CAR meme coin

In the context of the controversial Sango Coin initiative having failed to gain traction, 66 Touadéra announced on 9 February 2025, via his X account, the launch of a meme coin named $CAR. 67 Meme coins are highly speculative digital tokens with little genuine financial utility, typically launched with great online hype. They are sometimes linked to public figures, who, if involved, can leverage them for promotion and profit. In the United States, the creation of a $TRUMP meme coin, operating on the Solana blockchain network, 68 coincided with Donald Trump’s second presidential inauguration in January 2025. It was introduced by two entities reportedly affiliated with the Trump Organization, and, according to Reuters, made nearly US$100 million in trading fees in the first two weeks of its launch. 69

Touadéra framed the $CAR coin, also hosted by Solana on the Pump.fun platform, 70 as ‘an experiment 
designed to show how something as simple as a meme can unite people, support national development 
and put the Central African Republic on the world stage in a unique way’. 71 Although his presentation was 
different from Trump’s, the initiative was equally speculative and designed as political spin. 
The $CAR meme coin initiative was implemented through the state-owned Comptoir des Minéraux et 
des Gemmes (COMIGEM), a company originally created in 2009 to oversee diamond sales, 72 alongside 
an obscure private entity called Barn$ley. 73 
The launch of $CAR was marked by chaos, fuelling widespread concerns that the project was a scam. 74 
The internet domain was registered only three days prior to the launch, and within hours, the site’s 
domain registration service said that it had ‘suspended the abusive service’. 75 The project’s official 
account on X was also temporarily taken down. 76 Further undermining credibility, when Touadéra’s X 
account posted a video of him ‘officially launching’ the project, deepfake detection tools flagged the 
footage as AI-generated. 77 

Since its release, the $CAR meme coin has shown extreme market volatility. Unlike Sango Coins, which had a lock-up period of one year, $CAR coins can be sold immediately on any scale, making them an unpredictable asset. 78 Upon its launch, $CAR briefly surged to a market capitalization of more than US$900 million over a single weekend before rapidly losing over 75% of its value. 79 The coin saw a slight

bump, however, after Touadéra posted in May 2025 that he had signed a presidential decree to tokenize 1 700 hectares of land in the Lobaye prefecture, about 50 kilometres from the capital, Bangui, 80 under 99-year concessions, payable in $CAR directly through the Solana blockchain. 81 By November 2025, 122 plots of varying sizes had been sold at a fixed price of about US$314 per plot, with the sales totalling approximately US$38 308. 82

In an effort to stimulate interest, Touadéra announced his own purchase of tokenized land, posting a digital land ownership certificate on social media. 83 Analysis shows that the certificate was likely generated using artificial intelligence, however, raising concerns about the programme’s credibility and the risks it poses. 84

The Global Initiative Against Transnational Organized Crime was unable to locate evidence showing that revenues from the sale of $CAR tokens were declared as public income or incorporated into the national budget, or any information that they directly benefited COMIGEM. 85

#### Digital currency as criminal

#### capture?

B

ehind the rhetoric of a new monetary system, sovereignty and modernization, the CAR’s cryptocurrency adoption is an attempt to bypass financial regulatory constraints, as Touadéra plainly stated upon the launch of the country’s cryptocurrency ventures when he announced, ‘For us, the formal economy is no longer an option’. 86 Yet, as GABAC noted early on: the CAR has not demonstrated that the actors using virtual assets within its territory possess a developed understanding of the money laundering/terrorism financing risks specific to this sub-sector […]. This situation exposes the CAR […] to the risk of becoming a magnet for all kinds of capital, including toxic funds and those of dubious origin. 87

Indeed, several practices and trends occurring in the context of the CAR’s cryptocurrency initiatives make clear that the country has already opened the door to opaque financial flows and potential criminal capture. They also raise questions as to what other benefits those in power might be receiving from these ventures.

The crypto circle: Shadow networks behind the schemes

When Touadéra launched the CAR’s cryptocurrency venture, he was surrounded by crypto enthusiasts, pro-Russian businesspeople and controversial business magnates, several of whom had histories of international fraud or other criminal allegations and charges. 88 Their involvement with Touadéra further undermines the credibility of an already questionable programme and highlights the profound risk of state capture by criminal networks. At worst, it suggests that the CAR’s crypto initiative was designed to enrich a narrow circle of insiders while opening new channels for foreign influence and transnational organized crime at the expense of the wider population. Among this circle are Swiss-Romanian businessperson Nicolae Bogdan Buzaianu and Émile Parfait Simb, a self-styled crypto guru from Cameroon. In November 2021, Mauritian businessperson Sandra Boodhun reportedly introduced Touadéra to Buzaianu, 89 who was to become an architect of the Sango Coin

initiative. 90 According to Africa Intelligence, Buzaianu intended to invest in gold in the CAR. 91 In June 2019, Buzaianu had been reported to the Swiss public prosecutor’s office by TRIAL International, a Geneva based non-profit organization. TRIAL International presented information about the alleged involvement of Buzaianu – referred to as an associate of former Gambian president Yahya Jammeh – in the trafficking of conflict timber between Senegal and The Gambia. 92 Buzaianu is suspected of having participated in the sale of more than 300 000 tonnes of illegally cut precious wood from Senegal to Chinese markets between 2014 and 2017. 93 In 2022, the Swiss Attorney General’s office announced that it had opened an investigation into Buzaianu’s activities in the Gambia. 94 Research Initiative, an investigative journalism network, wrote in 2024 that Buzaianu ‘continues to operate a vast network of interconnected businesses across Europe and Africa, strategically structured to conceal his involvement. Recent reports reveal his efforts to extend influence into Africa, further solidifying his reputation as a resource exploiter and manipulator of financial systems.’ 95 As of December 2025, Buzaianu has not been formally charged. Simb was the architect of several cryptocurrency schemes in his home country and has been accused of defrauding nearly 300 000 people of billions of dollars in total. 96 His business dealings led to arrests in Burkina Faso and Cameroon on allegations of fraud and money laundering; 97 a criminal conviction in Benin for internet fraud; 98 and hundreds of fraud claims from participants in his crypto schemes in the US, Canada, Cameroon and France. 99 His notoriety earned him the nickname the ‘African Madoff’, 100 after New Yorker Bernie Madoff, who, in 2009, was convicted for running the biggest Ponzi scheme in history. 101 But Simb has largely been able to evade accountability, which lawyer Dominique Fousse, who represents complainants in Cameroon, attributes to the businessman’s ability to court those with influence and power. Simb himself said, in a WhatsApp note shared by Afrique XXI, ‘In Cameroon, I kept a network of people who believe in me. It is enough for me to give them an instruction so that they obey.’ 102

Such is the opacity around the CAR’s crypto schemes that Simb’s precise role is hard to pin down, but he was undoubtedly influential in their development. 103 In 2022, when the CAR embarked on its crypto journey, he had a government position, a CAR diplomatic passport and a close relationship with Touadéra. 104

Simb is also reportedly connected to the Russian regime. His television channel, Global TV, was managed by a Russian deputy and carried pro-Russian, anti-French content. 105 In August 2022, he took over the African Organization of Russophonie, allegedly a shell company for Russian interests. 106 Simb has lived in Moscow since September 2023. 107

Crypto minting: Public losses, private gains

The minting process, in which new cryptocurrency coins are generated, can be highly lucrative for token developers, especially in the context of meme coins like $CAR and $TRUMP. 108 Developers can acquire large portions of the token supply early on at negligible costs and place these holdings into escrow wallets that release tokens gradually. While this can give the impression of fairness and stability, it leaves ordinary buyers purchasing coins at inflated prices driven by online hype, political endorsements or coordinated social-media campaigns, thus bearing the consequences of price volatility and market manipulation. In practice, minting transforms speculative digital assets into tools for wealth extraction, enabling a small group of actors to profit while exposing the wider population to unregulated risk. In fragile states like the CAR, such practices undermine economic sovereignty and erode public trust in government-backed digital initiatives. Citizens are left vulnerable to exploitation while foreign actors position themselves to capture value from national resources and capital flows.

Recent academic research reinforces these concerns. Large-scale on-chain studies find that most newly issued meme tokens lose almost all value within days, 109 and that only a small fraction remain actively traded after a week. 110 Widespread artificial growth strategies combined with concentrated ownership are recurring features of high-return meme coins. By creating a misleading appearance of market interest, these tactics enable early insiders – only those who buy at the earliest and lowest-priced stages – to capture substantial profits while later buyers are exposed to rapid collapse. 111

The $CAR meme coin illustrates these dynamics. Minutes before its launch, a wallet linked to the token’s anonymous developer purchased 793.1 million tokens – 79.3% of the total supply – using 85.9 SOL (Solana’s native cryptocurrency), worth approximately US$16 600 at the time. 112 This granted the developer near-complete control over the market and allowed them to dictate timing, distribution and liquidity. 113 Within thirty minutes of the purchase, the developer transferred large token allocations – 350 million, 100 million, 250 million and 93.1 million tokens, respectively – into four different escrow wallets (see below). 114 These transfers coincided with Touadéra’s launch announcement on X just before midnight on 9 February 2025. 115

Opaque lock-up mechanisms in $CAR allowed the developer to control token supplies and influence release timing, amplifying perceived demand. This concentration of assets heightens volatility and the risk of market manipulation, endangering ordinary investors and creating opportunities for speculative or illicit exploitation.

Corroborated on-chain reporting shows that a small group of traders realized large profits almost 
immediately after Touadéra shared the $CAR contract address on X. 116 Publicly available data is insufficient 
to establish whether the developer or the traders who profited from the rapid price surge were connected 
to Touadéra’s entourage. This uncertainty highlights a structural paradox at the heart of cryptocurrency: 
while blockchain makes all transactions publicly traceable, the identities behind wallet addresses remain 
concealed as long as funds circulate within the crypto ecosystem, creating an opaque system that can 
be exploited by unscrupulous actors and organized crime groups. 
In parallel to the creation of $CAR, its developer held 31 additional crypto coins, all loosely linked to 
the CAR and indirectly to $CAR (see the annex). 117 This practice raises multiple red flags. By controlling 
numerous tokens on a similar theme, the developer could fabricate the appearance of widespread crypto 
adoption and national momentum. Some coins even embedded metadata linking to Touadéra’s posts or 
images, likely to lend them a veneer of credibility. 118 None of these tokens were officially sanctioned by the 
CAR crypto programme, and none were launched with a white paper, the standard disclosure document 
that explains a cryptocurrency’s objectives, technical structure, economic parameters and practical 
use cases to investors. Some coins were later traded back on swapping platforms to access more liquid 
virtual assets, and others were ‘burned’, reducing the token supply to inflate the value of remaining 
coins. 119 This concentration of control and cross-token orchestration closely mirrors documented risks 
in crypto markets, whereby large holders, or ‘whales’, coordinate supply and governance across multiple 
assets, thereby amplifying informational asymmetries, facilitating market manipulation and undermining 
decentralization. 
Taken together, these practices set the scene for ‘pump-and-dump’ schemes that expose investors to 
significant losses while allowing the developer and knowledgeable early actors to benefit. 120 The proliferation 
of such tokens underscores the opacity and high-risk nature of CAR’s cryptocurrency ventures. 

Speculative land sales

The opacity of CAR’s cryptocurrency initiatives becomes even more concerning when considered 
alongside the government’s broader agenda to tokenize land and natural resources. Historically, the CAR 
state has monetized its natural resources – diamonds, gold and timber – to foreign political and military 
actors, as well as to organized crime networks, in exchange for political support, military protection or 
financial gain. These arrangements entrenched corruption, weakened governance and exposed the 
country’s strategic assets to illicit exploitation while impoverishing the population by depriving citizens 
of the benefits of their own natural resources and lands. 
The current proposal to tokenize land sales through the $CAR meme token represents a further escalation. 
By converting national land into instantly tradable digital assets on largely unregulated blockchain 
platforms, the government has created a mechanism that is not only opaque but also accessible to 
virtually anyone with cryptocurrency. 
Unlike traditional resource deals, which at least rely on established intermediaries and contractual 
obligations, tokenized land transfers are executed without identity verification, anti-money laundering 
safeguards or enforceable legal oversight. Buyers only need an email address and cryptocurrency, and 
transaction records remain large inaccessible. The online tokenization platform advertises the process 
as a ‘near-instant’ method to acquire land in the CAR, with ‘no paperwork’ and ‘no delays’. 121 As a result, 
virtually anyone – regardless of origin, status or intent – can purchase land. 

Compounding this risk, the $CAR platform prohibits the resale of parcels until all 5 900 plots are sold, meaning that investors must rely on collective belief in the project’s bankability – an arrangement that amplifies both liquidity and legal risk. 122 The legal validity and enforceability of the digital land certificates issued under this scheme remain highly questionable as well. In many jurisdictions, real-world asset tokens do not enjoy clear or consistent legal recognition, placing them in a grey zone of property law where smart contract ownership may not be equivalent to a traditional title deed. 123 Furthermore, without robust regulatory frameworks and integration of tokenized real estate into official land registries, disputes over ownership and transfer could default to conventional title systems. 124

This approach effectively constitutes an open invitation to transnational organized crime networks, 
which can exploit the system to launder illicit funds, acquire control over strategic land and resources 
or gain political leverage. The combination of the CAR’s historical patterns of resource monetization, 
the opacity of the blockchain ecosystem and the absence of regulatory oversight creates a unique high 
risk environment. The tokenization scheme not only threatens investors and national finances but also 
risks reproducing and amplifying past forms of corruption, now with the added anonymity and speed 
offered by cryptocurrency. 
In October 2025, Touadéra publicly announced that the CAR’s next phase of tokenization would extend 
beyond land to include mining rights, effectively promising to tokenize mineral concessions. 125 This 
expansion into mining is even more concerning. In addition to further impoverishing the population, it 
exposes the country to increased risk of illicit exploitation by transnational actors and a loss of control 
over its strategic resources. In the absence of anti-money laundering and countering the financing 
of terrorism safeguards – and given the already opaque structure of $CAR-based land tokenization – 
mineral-rights tokens could be used to launder illicit proceeds or secure de facto control over resource 
rich territories. In addition, as with tokenized land, it is unclear whether tokenized mining rights will 
confer legally binding property or concession rights under the CAR’s national mining code, or whether 
they will remain purely digital constructs without enforceable guarantees. 
Given the lack of state oversight or a robust legal framework, these digitalized mining rights are unlikely 
to benefit the population and carry a high risk of exploitation by criminal actors. 

#### Conclusion

T

he CAR remains one of the most challenging environments for business and investment in the world, ranking near the bottom of the World Bank’s Business Ready index. 126 Weak institutions, entrenched corruption, a crime-prone environment, poor infrastructure, chronic insecurity and a highly undiversified economy deter serious investment and make the country vulnerable to elite capture. Against this backdrop, President Touadéra’s repeated efforts to introduce cryptocurrency – the Sango Coin in 2022 and the $CAR meme coin in 2025 – and the development of an opaque and unpredictable crypto-based infrastructure are deeply concerning. While presented as a strategy to attract foreign capital, speculative tokens appeal more to risk-takers and potentially criminal networks than to legitimate, long-term investors. The design and distribution of the $CAR token, in particular, suggests that elite enrichment may be an end in itself, enabling insiders to profit rapidly while maintaining a veneer of legitimacy. The broader adoption of bitcoin compounds these risks, as its liquidity and global acceptance provide a ready channel for illicit capital outflows. Meanwhile, the CAR government has already monetized mining sites in exchange for regime protection and has begun tokenizing land, with plans to extend the tokenization to mining rights, further accelerating the commodification of strategic national assets. 127 Actions such as these create avenues for foreign actors to exert outsized influence and potentially seize control over key functions of the state. 128

While Sango Coin and the $CAR meme coin have thus far produced limited economic impact, these developments signal a dangerous trend: cryptocurrency in the CAR is primarily serving the interests of entrenched elites and transnational criminal networks. Without robust regulatory frameworks, institutional capacity and transparency, these initiatives are unlikely to advance sustainable development. Instead, they may become vehicles for rent-seeking, elite enrichment, money laundering and terrorism financing, further entrenching the CAR’s position as a hub for transnational organized crime.
