# NESS 2026 charts new course for leading, funding and equipping Nigerian schools

By Fancy Goodman for partner (Partner, Press Room) — 18th Jul, 2026

L-R: Mr Tayo Osiyemi (C.E.O SKLD Integrated Services Ltd); Dr Sulaimon Ogunmuyiwa (Director-General Office of Education Quality Assurance (OEQA), Lagos State Ministry of Basic & Secondary Education); Mrs Temilola Adepetun (Founder & Executive Vice Chairman SKLD Integrated Services Ltd); Mr Jamiu Tolani Alli-Balogun Honourable Commissioner Lagos State Ministry of Basic & Secondary Education; Dr Adetola Salau Special Adviser to the Minister of Education on STEMM and Corporate Sector Engagement; Mr Oluwakoyejo Oluwatosin Founder & C.E.O, Chronicles Software Development Company Limited; Mr Olubiyi Ogundepo Head of Treasury, Lotus Bank

School leaders, education executives, policymakers and industry innovators gathered at the Civic Center, Victoria Island, Lagos, on Thursday for the Nigeria Education Supplies Summit (NESS) 2026.

Organised by SKLD Integrated Services Limited with the theme "Resilience in Motion, Schools Leading Change", the summit set out to reimagine how Nigerian schools are led, funded, equipped and sustained.

Tayo Osiyemi, Chief Executive Officer (CEO) of SKLD Integrated Services Limited, noted that the summit was a deliberate departure from one-off conversations about the education sector's problems.

"NESS is our contribution to creating an annual platform where regulators, school leaders, policymakers, financiers, technology providers, and education suppliers can come together not merely to discuss challenges but to build practical solutions together," Osiyemi said.

Goodness Morakinyo, Proprietor of Valencia Schools and Greenwood British School Ibadan, delivered a speech titled "Leading Your School in a VUCA World: Strategies for Sustainability and Growth."

He explained that Volatility, Uncertainty, Complexity and Ambiguity (VUCA) was "a major word that was used in the U.S [army] back then, but looks more like what we are facing in this [Nigerian] economy."

Morakinyo proposed a corrective framework for school leaders, urging them to answer volatility with vision, uncertainty with understanding, complexity with clarity, and ambiguity with agility.

On the second and third of these, he said, "Getting clarity or understanding from data, and listening much to parents [of the pupils in your school] will replace uncertainty. Being able to simplify systems will replace complexity."

Morakinyo closed with practical direction for school owners looking to build resilience.

"Invest in technology for vision. Train your staff to be flexible. You are allowed to fail fast, but you should learn faster. Try new things," Morakinyo said.

Mr Oluwakoyejo Oluwatosin, Founder and CEO of Chronicles Software Development Company Limited, spoke on "Learning & Development in the Age of AI: A practical guide for Nigerian school owners and school leaders on adopting artificial intelligence responsibly and staying ahead of the curve."

He urged school leaders to resist the temptation to reinvent their operations around Artificial Intelligence (AI) and instead use it to sharpen what already works.

"Don't try to do anything new with AI. Learn the skills and enable your current landscape, because AI will confuse the directionless, but AI will enable those with clarity of vision," Oluwatosin said.

He extended the point to classroom practice, arguing that teachers who ignore AI risk being left behind.

"If you are a teacher and you are not engaging AI, then you are going to be extinct," Oluwatosin said.

Oluwatosin also framed data literacy as the defining skill of the current moment, distinguishing it from the broader AI conversation.

"Your ability to make sense of data gives you an edge in your industry and an edge in your day and time," he said. "We are now in an intelligence age. So the data you gather must provide intelligence, and must give you the capacity to predict what is going to happen tomorrow".

He cautioned, however, that adoption without caution is a liability.

Ms Tinuke Bosun-Okusaga, Managing Consultant and founder of People Plus Consulting, turned attention to talent management as the structural support beneath any school's vision.

"At the top of the vision of the school, of the business, are the people. And you need to attract, onboard, train, retain and manage [them]," she said. "Putting all of these together gives you a good footing in terms of managing, retaining talent, and ensuring the sustainability of the business".

Mr Olubiyi Ogundepo, Head of Treasury, Lotus Bank, shared perspectives on the role of innovative financial solutions in supporting sustainable growth within the education ecosystem.

He described Nigeria's operating environment in blunt terms, noting that his own institution has spent five years building resilience within it.

"Nigeria is a very murky business environment," Ogundepo said. "To thrive in it, you need resilience."

Turning to schools specifically, Ogundepo identified cash flow as one of the sector's central challenges, pointing to widespread late or unpaid school fees even among well-established institutions.

To address this, Ogundepo said Lotus Bank's Edupartner solution to extend financing beyond schools to parents themselves.

"Apart from providing financing to schools, we're also looking to [support] the parents to pay the school fees," he said.

Professor Ndubueze Mbah, Commissioner for Education in Enugu State, addressed the summit on systemic reform, naming the sector's core problems directly.

"We get a lot of the experiential learning system of innovation. It is one that understands that at the fundamental level, the problems that we have are foundational learning, quality of learning access for children in schools, the gap in digital interest and the skills gap," Mbah said.

He linked this to the wider labour market distortion facing graduates, noting Enugu state's reform agenda was designed to be "gender inclusive [and] class inclusive."

Prof. Mbah detailed Enugu State's flagship infrastructure programme as a practical case of this reform in motion.

"[There are] 267 smart green schools across Enugu State. We want to ensure access to every child, whether they are in the urban area or rural area, access ensured by energy that is clean, renewable and reliable," Mbah said.

This access, he explained, is powered by clean, renewable and reliable solar energy, optimised through a combination of satellite internet and local internet infrastructure, including supply routers and access points. The goal, Mbah said, is to make sure "that no matter where every child is, they have access to the standardised instruction and tuition."

"Education sector transformation at scale and of this ambition becomes an avenue for catalysing inclusive prosperity that is transglobal," Mbah noted.

Across sessions throughout the summit, speakers repeatedly returned to a shared conviction: Nigerian schools cannot outsource resilience to circumstance. Clarity, deliberate systems and sustained investment in talent and technology are what separate schools that merely survive disruption from those that lead through it.
