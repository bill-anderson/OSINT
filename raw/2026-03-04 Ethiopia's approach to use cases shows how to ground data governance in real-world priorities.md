How do national strategies translate into tangible improvements in people’s lives? Our work in Ethiopia offers a timely lens into this question as the country navigates its first National Data Governance Strategy.

In partnership with the Government of Ethiopia, we have worked closely with local institutions and partners to surface examples rooted in local realities that could anchor the country’s strategy as it takes shape.

What we found is both encouraging and instructive: while priorities differ across sectors and consensus can be an arduous process, starting with concrete use cases offers a pragmatic approach for moving from strategy to action.

At the same time, Ethiopia’s experience reminds us that moving from strategy to implementation is rarely linear. Priorities can shift, and alignment takes time. In this context, identifying and piloting real-world needs can help ground abstract governance decisions in service delivery, surfacing realities early, before policies or systems are locked down.

## The use case approach is a pragmatic entry point.

Ethiopia’s forthcoming National Data Governance Strategy establishes the essential foundation for managing data as a strategic national asset, setting out the policies, standards, and institutional roles needed to enable secure, interoperable, and value-driven data use across government and the broader ecosystem. However, strategy alone is not sufficient. Its real impact will depend on how effectively it is operationalized within sector systems, embedded into service delivery workflows, and translated into practical tools, capabilities, and accountability mechanisms that improve outcomes for people.

In many public sector contexts, implementation is understood as a linear progression. Typically, this takes the form of a sequential chain: from a national strategy to sector-specific frameworks, to an implementation guide, with use cases treated as a downstream output, referenced but not fully integrated into the design process itself.

While effective, this standardized process presents a risk of frameworks being developed in the abstract – well-intentioned, but insufficiently grounded in how data is generated, shared and used in practice.

Piloting use cases can help mitigate this risk by adding a connective step of translating high-level priorities into concrete, testable pathways for action. A use case approach forces alignment by asking:

- What data needs to flow to enable the service?
- Between which institutions or systems?
- Under what rules, safeguards, and incentives?
- And with what capacity in place to use the data responsibly?

In Ethiopia, applying this lens has allowed for open discussions on coordination, ownership, interoperability, trust, and institutional readiness.

## We listened for priorities to surface use cases.

Through interviews, virtual roundtables, and in-person workshops convening ministries, partners, and ecosystem actors, we explored three core questions:

- What are your institution’s top priorities?
- Given these priorities, what pain points do you face for data-sharing?
- What tangible impact would you want to see in the next three years?

Alongside sector-specific needs, the process explicitly factored in broader societal realities that shape what is feasible and responsible in practice. These included persistent gender gaps in digital access, particularly in rural areas; uneven institutional capacity to manage and use data; and community trust shaped by repeated exposure to pilots that never fully delivered value.

Stakeholders in health, agriculture, and public financing helped surface more than 20 potential priorities. Working with a smaller set of stakeholders, this long list was refined by weighing both impact and feasibility, not as abstract criteria but as practical questions: would this improve outcomes that matter, and could it realistically be delivered using today’s systems, capacity, and governance arrangements?

Three insights emerged, each highlighting a different aspect of what it takes to translate a national data strategy into practice.

**1\. Break down broad priorities.**

Priorities need to be defined at a more precise level before data governance questions can meaningfully be asked. In Ethiopia, this was illustrated most clearly in discussions on agriculture, where several high-impact priorities were identified but remained too broad to function as use cases in practice.

Two priorities were shortlisted: AI-enabled advisory services and improved access to credit for farmers. Both speak to real and pressing needs, yet at their current level of aggregation, neither cannot be implemented as a pilot use case. “Credit for farmers,” for example, could refer to financing for agricultural inputs, post-harvest activities, or working capital; it could target smallholder farmers, cooperatives, or other producer groups; and it could be delivered through fully digital, assisted, or hybrid models. Each of these choices implies different data requirements, risks, and stakeholder involvement.

The same applies to AI advisory services, which could range from generalized information dissemination to highly personalized recommendations based on farm-level data. Without clarity on who the service is for, what decisions it is meant to inform, and how farmers will realistically access it; governance frameworks have nothing concrete to attach to.

In Ethiopia, the [rural mobile internet gender gap (81%) is more than double the urban gap (29%)](https://www.gsma.com/solutions-and-impact/connectivity-for-good/mobile-for-development/blog/mobile-has-the-potential-to-empower-rural-women-but-persistent-gender-gaps-must-be-addressed/), and rural access challenges, particularly for women, require sensitivity. On the other hand, there is also a key opportunity in the country: FAYDA, the national digital ID, emerged as a potential enabler across agricultural priorities. Until priorities are sufficiently disaggregated, it is difficult to determine what data is needed, how consent should be obtained, or how safeguards should be applied to include and protect populations.

***Key insight:** Use case implementation depends on working at the right level of granularity. Ethiopia’s experience shows that broad priorities must first be broken into governable components before alignment, technical design, or piloting can responsibly move forward.*

**2.** **Prioritize integration.**

Data governance delivers the greatest value when it reduces fragmentation, rather than introducing new systems. In Ethiopia, this clearest example of this appeared in the health sector, where stakeholders consistently pointed to fragmentation as the primary constraint.

Ethiopia’s digital health ecosystem includes more than 70 siloed platforms and over 200 pilots, many of which address similar problems without the foundational interoperability required for seamless service delivery. Discussions surfaced several pressing priorities, including antenatal care workflows, master facility tracking, early outbreak detection, and unified electronic medical records.

For this reason, stakeholders converged on a multi-tenant electronic medical records pilot as the most critical next step to test interoperability. Rather than launching another standalone solution, the focus is on improving the foundation that can enable multiple service priorities while testing data standards, safeguards, and institutional coordination in practice.

***Key insight:** The most effective way to operationalize data governance is to focus on integration of siloed platforms. In Ethiopia, prioritizing interoperable electronic medical records creates a practical pathway to test governance choices across real workflows, while delivering tangible improvements to continuity of care and health outcomes.*

**3\. Build institutional alignment.**

Interoperable systems are necessary but insufficient to translate data strategies into real-world impact. Arguably, institutional alignment is an important enabler without which data flows risk reinforcing silos rather than enabling coordinated action.

In Ethiopia, this was particularly apparent in public financial management, where progress depends as much on institutional coordination and accountability as on technical integration.

Public financial management has been identified by the Government of Ethiopia as a priority area for use case development. This is a great domain to test the national data strategy as it spans multiple functions, such as budget formulation, procurement, execution, and oversight. Each of these functions may be housed in different institutions with distinct mandates and incentives and requires robust alignment and technical integration. Progress here can strengthen greater transparency, which in turn leads to improved trust.

***Key insight:** Effective data governance requires institutions to work together in ways that extend beyond technical interoperability. Ethiopia’s focus on public financial management highlights the importance of clarifying roles, responsibilities, and accountability mechanisms to ensure that data sharing leads to coordinated action and improved public outcomes.*

## Centering real-world needs can accelerate a practical way forward.

Ethiopia’s experience shows that the question of how national data strategies translate into real improvements in people’s lives is best answered through practical approaches, which are often rooted in sectoral realities.

The path has not been linear. Some sectors have moved faster than others, and not every priority is ready to advance at the same time. Working through real use cases made these differences visible early, surfacing where alignment exists, where gaps remain, and where governance questions need to be resolved before implementation is possible.

Use cases do not replace strategy or frameworks, but they help ensure that both are grounded in reality and oriented toward impact. As Ethiopia continues this work, its experience offers useful signals for other countries navigating the same journey from ambition to implementation.
