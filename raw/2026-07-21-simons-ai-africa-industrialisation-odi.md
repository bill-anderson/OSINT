## Some scenarios of an African future

In Lagos, a junior associate at a commercial law firm breezes through a sale-and-purchase agreement in 20 minutes with the help of AI. Two years ago, it would have taken her an entire afternoon.

In Kumasi, a car mechanic diagnoses a stubborn fault by ear, replaces a worn solenoid he fabricated the day before, and scribbles some illegible work notes in the full knowledge that no one, and certainly no compliance officer, will ever review them.

In Tete, a Mozambican mining engineer watches her predictive-maintenance dashboard go dark for the third time in a fortnight. The field sensors meant to feed it have lost calibration. She lacks the staff to keep them aligned, and the local supplier isn’t much help.

Three radically different scenarios. The first describes a step-change in productivity made possible by AI. The second demonstrates persistent AI deserts. And the third highlights someone worse off because she has invested in an AI tool and been defeated by weak infrastructure all the same.

## AI is several things at once

Popular discourse treats artificial intelligence as a single technology that automates tasks. The reality is more complex. **Different AI architectures automate different modes of human reasoning, and those modes correspond to very different parts of the African economy.**

Large Language Models (LLMs), the mainstay of the generative AI revolution, are supreme verbal-deductive emulators. They thrive in law, banking, customer service and policy drafting.

The logical-inductive mechanism connects the textual artefacts generated by the verbal-deductive nodes (outputs such as contracts and manuals) with changes in the physical world. In current AI parlance, this is usually referred to as ‘agentic workflows’ – routines that are analytically generalisable to a vast permutation of scenarios. Consider, for instance, how advanced automated facilities handle the massive number of edge cases that show up in technical support functions.

World Models, the emerging frontier signalled by [LeCun’s Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=BZ5a1r-kVsf) and successive [V-JEPA releases](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.09985), target spatial-abductive reasoning. They offer the algorithmic foundation for transforming sectors like mining, manufacturing, infrastructure, precision agriculture and clinical diagnostics.

## The AI prize for Africa

The generative-AI prize for Africa has been estimated at between [$61 billion and $103 billion in additional annual value](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/leading-not-lagging-africas-gen-ai-opportunity).

But there is a bias here. The early wins are concentrated in retail, telecommunications, banking and customer service. Mining, heavy industry, agriculture and energy register comparable headline value but vastly lower readiness, because the valuation is so infrastructure-sensitive (see [the annex](https://brightsimons.com/2026/07/ai-may-not-be-a-friend-of-africas-industrialisation/) for more insight into this contrast). Capturing the value from these sectors using GenAI tools requires considerable infrastructure complements that are often too expensive and complex to operate in many African contexts. An easy example is the low adoption and maintenance rate of Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) systems for electricity grids and drainage-basin sensors for water management, which combine both logical-inductive routine automation to handle edge-cases and spatial tools like geolocation sensors to optimise electricity distribution.

Africa’s colonial inheritance reinforces these patterns. Take, for instance, the 2023 [South African National Benchmark Tests](https://www.nbt.ac.za/sites/default/files/NBT%20National%20Report%202023%20Intake%2007032024.pdf), which classify 23% of university applicants as proficient in academic literacy, but only 10% in quantitative literacy. The [2019 PASEC results](https://www.cgdev.org/blog/new-pasec-results-show-modest-improvements-student-learning-francophone-africa) across 14 francophone African countries largely tell the same story: graduates are being produced primed for LLM-augmented work and unprepared for the spatial-abductive demands of the engine room of structural economic transformation.

In simple terms, a much larger proportion of the incoming African workforce have the skills and _trained aptitude_ to handle verbal interactions with LLM-powered chatbots and similar tools than those capable of specifying logical routines, especially in agentic workflows, that require quantitative thresholds such as would be necessary to integrate SCADA or drainage basin sensor readings into industrial or precision-agriculture processes. The verbal-deductive bias favours certain applications, whilst the logical-inductive gap undermines others.

## Paper bridges

LLMs enable under-resourced operations to bridge the gap at relatively low cost between local reality and the formal demands of the global market. A small Lagos enterprise can produce world-class policy documents, contracts and grant applications in hours. A government department can publish a fluent national AI strategy authored largely by a chatbot. We may call this _facading._

**But a bridge made of paper cannot carry heavy loads.** If the underlying business lacks logistics networks, inventory systems and maintenance regimes, it crumbles under the weight of real economic activity. LLMs can make a Lagos trading firm look like a global enterprise on paper. They cannot fix the generator when it fails. They cannot walk the enterprise through a site audit by an ISO inspector. And they certainly cannot reduce post-harvest losses in rural Kano.

This has been called [isomorphic mimicry](https://www.hks.harvard.edu/sites/default/files/centers/cid/files/publications/faculty-working-papers/239_PritchettWoolcockAndrews_Looking_like_a_state_final.pdf): institutions adopt the outward forms of functional states without the underlying functionality. LLMs are extraordinarily effective tools for this. The risk is not that they are thus inherently problematic. Not really. The risk is that, without deliberate counter-design, they make the mimicry cheaper to maintain, and thus more likely to persist.

## It is easier to use AI to make shiny powerpoints than to track gritty powerplants

Capital flows where it is rewarded fastest. This means verbal-deductive sectors with low integration barriers. Integration cycles tend to be short, and the proof of value is verbal, and so legible to investors at a glance. The market for verbal-AI start-ups is plausible and growing.

Spatial-abductive AI obeys different logics. A predictive-maintenance algorithm in a mid-tier copper mine on the Zambian Copperbelt can cut unplanned downtime by, say, 10%, but only after millions of dollars of sensor capex, broadband investment, a calibration regime running at a million dollars annually, and a team of engineers fluent in mining, networks and data engineering. Without the infrastructural complement, the AI software is software-as-shelfware.

Restating the point in macroeconomic terms leads to the formulation that capital, talent and policy attention concentrate in the verbal layer of the GDP stack. The spatial layer, where the transformation of the industrial and primary sectors takes its purest form, is starved. For example, services productivity rises gently while the manufacturing share stagnates. Call this _premature deindustrialisation 2.0_: the same trap [Dani Rodrik](https://academic.oup.com/cje/article/47/4/725/7216522) diagnosed two decades ago, but this time accelerated by AI.

## This ‘honeypot’ trap has antecedents

Mobile telephony offers a precedent. The technology reached Africa in three waves, and each one automated something different. 2G was a verbal technology: voice and SMS sped up the flow of simple commercial information. Adoption happened at historic speed because it accelerated familiar transactions. 3G and 4G opened up apps, data and platforms. For a while the start-up boom this created seemed destined to transform the continent. However, adoption ultimately proved uneven and persistent gaps have capped the internet economy. 5G and upcoming 6G are industrial IoT, machine-to-machine control, real-time systems integration across factories, grids and mines. That, of course, is the natural habitat of spatial-abductive work.

The first (2G) wave was met with triumphant predictions of ‘leapfrogging’; subsequent waves, not so much. As recently as 2024, [2G and 3G together still made up over half of Africa’s mobile connections](https://www.gsmaintelligence.com/research/the-mobile-economy-africa-2025), with 5G at 2%; in North America today, [nearly four in five subscriptions are 5G](https://www.ericsson.com/en/reports-and-papers/mobility-report/dataforecasts/regional-subscriptions-outlook). Even the 5G-capable handsets now filtering through African cities are deployed in what is essentially 3G fashion – more streaming and messaging at higher volume – to the point where [operators openly struggle to monetise the technology](https://techcentral.co.za/south-africa-telcos-monetise-5g-4g/266815/). The ‘grids and sensors’ layer that 5G was engineered for remains nearly untouched. This is a honeypot in the strict sense: the first taste was genuinely nourishing, and precisely because it was, the continent lingered at the rim of the jar while the technology stack migrated increasingly beyond its reach.

Verbal-deductive AI is the 2G of the present cycle: sweet, real and sticky. The open question is whether the continent will sip past that honeypot towards the abductive realm.

## The latent genius problem

Africa’s vast informal economy is, paradoxically, a reservoir of exactly the abductive talent structural economic transformation demands. The auto-garage complex Suame Magazine in Kumasi, Ghana, covers an estimated 20 square miles, hosts roughly 12,000 enterprises and supports 200,000 workers. The [Kamukunji jua kali](https://erepository.uonbi.ac.ke/bitstream/handle/11295/39363/Kamukunji_cluster.pdf?sequence=1) cluster in Nairobi fabricates jikos, sufurias and folding machines for an aftermarket spanning East Africa. The continent’s most under-utilised asset is hardly raw cognitive potential. It is abductive judgement: the skill and insight to operate and manoeuvre physical economic units. Africa’s informal sector has these skills in abundance; what its workers lack is the ability to codify, package and present these real and serious skills for the right value to be placed on them.

The same AI that threatens to deepen mimicry can, if deployed differently, dismantle exactly this trap. Imagine a system that listens to a master mechanic’s spoken diagnosis – _‘the sound is like grinding metal at idle, the pattern is consistent with a worn solenoid’_ – sees a photograph of the worn part, and converts the input into a formal diagnostic note, parts requisition, maintenance log entry and trained signal for next time. Far from being a façade, this is a real bridge between informal genius and formal economic recognition.

## How to push beyond the façade with AI

Diagnosis without instruments is, sadly, therapy at best. Four operational levers, deployed together, can prevent African AI deployment from being stuck at ‘façading’.

A _productivity-tithe rule_ would bind large public agencies and state-owned enterprises to reinvest 15–25% of measured verbal-AI productivity gains in physical instrumentation, sensor capex or TVET endowment. A _process-trace procurement standard_ would refuse AI software contracts in physical sectors that lack bundled sensor capex and lifetime calibration services. A class of _abductive interpreters_ – such as a senior _cross-thinking_ technician (similar to what we have elsewhere called ‘[transmediaries](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Chapter-Eleven_Breakthrough.pdf)’), recognised, credentialled and supported to bridge informal expertise and formal documentation – would translate the mechanic’s diagnosis into the corporate procurement system. A _continental micro-credentialling scheme_ aligned with the [African Continental Qualifications Framework](https://acqf.africa/) would issue stackable digital badges in abduction-intensive areas such as Digital Twin Management, Drone Diagnostics, IoT Calibration and Solar Grid Balancing.

## Convex leadership will be critical

In an era when average reasoning is being commoditised, the value of human judgement migrates to the tails. Sherwin Rosen’s [economics of superstars](https://www.jstor.org/stable/1803469) is a useful guide here. A _convexity_ arises whereby such competences in the tails generate outsized payoffs, amplified in African contexts by three structural features. The capability middle is thin, so the tail is, by construction, longer and more disproportionate. Institutional checks are weaker, so individual leader effects are larger. Frontier infrastructure is scarce ([Africa hosts under 2% of global data-centre capacity](https://www.undp.org/speeches/time-africa-lead-global-ai-revolution), for instance), and so leaders who can mobilise capital and partnerships face few realistic competitors.

Convex leaders distinguish the verbal-deductive layer from the spatial-abductive layer and refuse to substitute easy gains in the first for hard gains in the second. They orchestrate AI deployment with a clear theory of which architecture solves which problem.

They invest deliberately in the under-rewarded, capex-heavy complements that make AI [supermodular](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/j.2325-8012.2005.tb00664.x): the sensors, calibration routines, control systems, field devices, maintenance data and technicians without which a predictive-maintenance model is nothing more than software stuck on a shelf. In other words, the payoff does not come from the model alone, but from assembling the mutually reinforcing stack around it.

Convex leaders also cultivate the [abductive interpreters or transmediaries who translate informal expertise into formal recognition](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5wRgavN5LBY). In high-stakes environments (such as mining and large-scale manufacturing), where the cost of AI-deployment error is sharply convex in the sense that small errors have outsized consequences, the human override premium becomes decisive.

Africa’s AI future will not be shaped by the pace at which ChatGPT is adopted in government offices. Rather, it turns on whether the gains are tithed back to the engine room, whether the Suame Magazines and Kamukunjis of this continent are productively bridged to the formal economy, and whether the small cadre of leaders capable of making those connections is recognised, supported and held accountable.
