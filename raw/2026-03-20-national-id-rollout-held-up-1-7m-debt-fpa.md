# Liberia: National ID Card Rollout Continues to Be Held Up by $1.7 Million Unpaid Government Debt

By Joyclyn Wea and Tetee Gebro with New Narratives — FrontPage Africa, March 20, 2026

Summary:
- Liberia's national digital identity system is still stalled because the Registry says it cannot fully access the biometric database until the government pays a US$1.7 million debt to the company that built it.
- Millions of people are still outside the system, while many cards have expired, and even earlier registrants were locked out of the ID system, which is the key to many public services.
- A committee set up to restart the rollout has one more month to report. Civil society groups say the longer the delay continues, the more public trust will drop.

Seven months after President Joseph Boakai halted Liberia's national digital identity card rollout to "fix the system," the National Identification Registry says Liberia still cannot run the system because it is locked by the Kenyan company that built it. The Registry says the company will only release access after the government clears a US$1.7 million debt owed to it.

Millions of Liberians are still not enrolled, and expired ID cards are piling up. Even the 640,000 Liberians who were signed up under a previous version of the digital database, which electronically stores biometric data (photographs and fingerprints), cannot access it. A $2 million tranche of World Bank funding for the expansion of the centers is on hold.

"This is a security risk. Liberia cannot sacrifice its national identification card," said Eddie Jarwolo, executive director of Naymote Partners for Democratic Development, a leading accountability group in Liberia.

The program was part of component one of the US$30 million World Bank-funded Governance Reform and Accountability Transformation Project, designed to "enhance access to selected digitally provided public services, raise tax revenues, and improve the openness of accountability institutions." A national digital identity card was meant to store digital information of all Liberians, linked to SIM cards, to allow easy and transparent access to the electoral system, taxation, banking, and other public services. The government had committed to registering another two million Liberian adults on the system before 2030. But that has ground to a halt.

Last April, President Boakai unveiled the expanded rollout and gave all Liberians until August to register. But it soon became clear that the government had not put enough staff and offices in place to process all citizens. By June, thousands of citizens were angry at being forced to wait in long lines only to be rejected from service for days on end. FrontPage Africa/New Narratives reported that, with 15 centers in Monrovia processing about 85 people a day, it would have taken more than three years to process Monrovia's population of more than a million adults, and six years to process the two million people the government had committed to enroll. Many people, including elderly and other vulnerable citizens, were being locked out of banking, government, and communications services now mandated to demand ID cards. President Boakai suspended the rollout a week later.

New Narratives/FrontPage Africa found that the rollout chaos came because the Registry did not have access to the new system and did not have the World Bank funds to expand the number of centers, because it owed $1.7 million to Techno Brain, the Nairobi-based firm that built the Registry's system for $5.9 million. Seven months later, that debt has still not been paid. Deputy director Sylvester Nyema said the $1.7 million is not in the 2026 approved budget for the Registry by the Legislature.

A committee put in place by President Boakai — drawn from Internal Affairs, Foreign Affairs, Post and Telecommunications, the Central Bank, the National Elections Commission, the senior economic advisor, and the President's Delivery Unit — was told to deliver a new rollout plan by April 13, 2026. Samuel Kofi Woods, national security adviser and committee head, declined to comment. NIR executive director Andrew Peters also declined, referring reporters to Nyema, who took the job in September after the suspension. Nyema said Woods' committee has not shared any report with Registry management, and he could not say when it would report to the president. The Registry has been pushing for a solution and is in talks to raise money, including potentially borrowing it. Anyone registered before the halt can go to the Registry headquarters in Congo Town and receive their card, though there is no information campaign to notify the public.

Jarwolo said low enrollment is a national risk, warning that weak identity systems can expose Liberia to fraud and political manipulation. He criticized a push for private actors to carry costs upfront in a country where repayment is uncertain, and questioned how serious the steering committee process has been. "The political will is lacking," he said.
