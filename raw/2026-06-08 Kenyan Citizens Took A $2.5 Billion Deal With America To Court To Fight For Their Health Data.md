On December 4, 2025 Kenya signed a $2.5 billion health cooperation deal with the United States.

Of course you know there was a big ceremony with lots of handshakes and smiles.

Eight days later Kenyan citizens were in court.

The Consumer Federation of Kenya filed a petition arguing the deal violated Kenya’s Data Protection Act and Digital Health Act.

Their argument was simple.

***“Once Kenya’s medical and epidemiological data is transferred abroad, the harm becomes permanent and irreversible. Neither this court nor Kenyan regulators would have the power to recall, restrict, or oversee how such data is used overseas.”***

The High Court agreed.

And suspended the data transfer component of a $2.5 billion agreement with the most powerful country on earth.

Now this in itself, needs a handclap.

Pause, breath and take this in.

They used domestic law, filed by ordinary citizens and the courts suspended an agreement with the most powerful nation on earth.

Now, the Court of Appeal later reversed that suspension while the case continues to be heard.

But the moment had already happened.

Kenya looked at a $2.5 billion deal and said not without protecting our people’s data first.

That is data sovereignty working in real time.

Let me show you what’s actually being built for Africa

#### The African Union

The AU Data Policy Framework adopted in 2022 positions data as a strategic asset and makes sovereignty a core guiding principle.

It calls for governance mechanisms that ensure the value derived from African data is retained on the continent.

This is a true foundational idea.

That data generated by African people about African lives should benefit African people.

Quite revolutionary for us as a people if you asked me.

#### Kenya

Kenya’s Cloud Policy 2025 came into force in May 2025.

It requires government data classified as sensitive, top secret, secret, restricted to be stored within Kenya’s borders.

Not in a server farm in Virginia.

Not in a data centre in Dublin.

In Kenya.

It is a legal requirement and not just policy.

#### Ghana

Ghana’s Draft Data Protection Bill 2025 proposes mandatory domestic storage for some of the most sensitive categories of data.

Biometric data. Health records. Genetic information. Children’s data.

The argument is straightforward.

If this data is about Ghanaians, it should be protected under Ghanaian law.

Not subject to whatever terms a foreign cloud provider decides to update on a Tuesday afternoon.

**And the infrastructure is following.**

Africa’s data centre market is projected to reach $9.15 billion by 2029.

Microsoft and G42 are investing $1 billion in a geothermal powered data centre in Kenya right now. (Great but I have a whole other thing about data centres, but that’s just me.)

Our continent is not just writing laws about data sovereignty.

It is building the physical infrastructure to make it real.
