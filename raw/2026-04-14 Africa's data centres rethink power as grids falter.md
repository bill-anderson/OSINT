Across Africa’s fast-expanding digital economy, a central assumption underpinning modern infrastructure planning is increasingly being challenged, that national electricity grids can reliably power data centres. Industry executives say the continent’s fragile power system is now the decisive bottleneck shaping the pace and structure of investment in data centres, cloud computing, and emerging artificial intelligence workload

At the GITEX Africa conference in Marrakech, executives from across the energy and digital infrastructure sectors argued that the continent’s power systems are structurally misaligned with the demands of cloud computing, artificial intelligence and high-density data processing. Rather than acting as the backbone of digital growth, the grid is often an intermittent and constrained input that operators must design around rather than depend on.

The issue is not simply one of capacity but of reliability, frequency stability, and delivery. Even in relatively advanced markets, electricity supply is often characterised by outages, voltage fluctuations, and transmission bottlenecks that make continuous uptime difficult to guarantee. For data centres, where downtime is measured in milliseconds and service-level agreements are non-negotiable, these constraints are decisive.

“Put bluntly, a grid-first approach cannot be Africa’s strategy,” Vice President for Secure Power in the Middle East and Africa at Schneider Electric, Hady Stephan, stated. “It is impossible, actually a liability.”

Nowhere is this tension more visible than in Africa’s largest economies, where digital infrastructure investment is accelerating, but power systems remain under strain. In Nigeria, one of the continent’s most active data centre markets, operators say grid electricity is rarely sufficient to meet baseline operational requirements.

Industry estimates suggest the country has a little over 20 operational data centres, most of which are concentrated in Lagos, the commercial capital, and landing point for multiple subsea cables. Installed capacity is growing steadily as new entrants target enterprise demand and cloud migration, but the sector’s expansion has become increasingly dependent on alternative power systems.

Executives say the gap between installed generation capacity and actual delivered electricity is the core challenge. Although Nigeria’s nominal generation capacity is significantly higher, available supply often falls far below demand due to gas constraints, transmission losses, and system inefficiencies. For operators, this translates into unpredictable grid access and heavy reliance on self-generation.

Stephan argued that data centre operators across the continent have already internalised this reality, designing facilities that treat the grid as a supplementary rather than primary source. Diesel generators, battery storage systems, and onsite renewable energy are now standard components of infrastructure planning rather than backup contingencies.

He added that the challenge is structural rather than cyclical, meaning incremental improvements in grid performance are unlikely to fundamentally alter investment behaviour. Instead, the executive said, Africa’s digital infrastructure must be designed around energy constraints from the outset.

That view is echoed by operators already active in the region. Companies such as Liquid Telecom, Teraco, and Africa Data Centres have increasingly adopted hybrid energy models combining grid electricity with solar installations, battery systems, and diesel generation. These configurations allow facilities to switch dynamically between power sources depending on availability and cost.

For many operators, the grid functions less as a stable input and more as one variable in a broader energy optimisation system. The objective is no longer full reliance on public electricity but rather resilience through diversification.

“Africa cannot rely on the grid alone,” the Managing Director for Africa at Vertiv, Wojtek Piorko, noted. “The technologies already exist; the question is how to scale them to meet the demands of modern data centres.”

Piorko said hybridisation is rapidly becoming the default architecture for new facilities, particularly those designed to support artificial intelligence workloads, which require significantly higher and more volatile power densities than traditional cloud services. In some cases, rack power demands have risen sharply, placing additional pressure on already constrained energy systems.

The implications extend beyond operational design to investment geography. Developers are increasingly considering proximity to power generation assets when selecting sites for new facilities rather than prioritising connectivity alone. In some cases, this has led to the exploration of collocating compute infrastructure directly with energy producers.

The Chief Executive of Backbone Connectivity Nigeria Limited, Ibrahim Dikko, said this shift represents a potential structural rethinking of infrastructure development in Africa. The executive likened it to the liberalisation of the telecommunications sector, when the continent bypassed fixed-line infrastructure constraints and moved directly to mobile networks.

“Now we have the opportunity to leapfrog energy,” he said, pointing to early-stage projects that aim to integrate computing infrastructure with generation assets.

One such initiative involves collaboration with Mainstream Energy at a hydropower site, where data processing facilities are being developed alongside electricity generation infrastructure. The model is designed to reduce exposure to grid instability while improving cost predictability for large-scale workloads.

The concept of data centres as anchor consumers of electricity is gaining traction among developers and investors. In this model, digital infrastructure is not merely a passive user of power but a stabilising demand base that can justify investment in dedicated generation capacity. This is particularly relevant in markets where grid expansion is slow and capital-intensive.

Despite growing interest in decentralised solutions, cost remains a significant constraint. Diesel generation, still widely used as backup power, is expensive and exposes operators to fuel price volatility and supply chain disruptions. Renewable alternatives, while increasingly viable, require high upfront capital expenditure and are often dependent on complementary storage systems to ensure uptime.

At the same time, Africa’s broader digital transformation is accelerating. Demand for cloud services, local data storage, and artificial intelligence processing is rising rapidly, pushing operators to expand capacity despite infrastructure constraints. This has created a paradox in which demand is growing fastest in markets where foundational utilities are the least reliable.

Industry participants say the result is a hybrid energy future rather than a transition to a single dominant source. Solar photovoltaic systems paired with battery storage are expected to play a larger role in baseload supply over time, while diesel and grid electricity remain part of a multi-input framework.

The challenge, executives argue, is no longer whether Africa can build data centres but how efficiently it can power them. In that sense, energy management is becoming as critical as connectivity in determining competitiveness.
