# Agencies defy Ruto's e-Citizen order, 1,695 services yet to be migrated

Only 1,695 government services have been migrated to the e-Citizen platform in the financial year ending this month, as ministries, departments and agencies (MDAs) continue to flout a presidential directive issued three years ago.
...
The Controller of Budget has highlighted the slow pace of migration, pointing to widespread non-compliance with the government’s target of transitioning at least 11,000 services annually to the platform.
...
President William Ruto directed MDAs in 2023 to move their services to e-Citizen, a reform intended to improve access to public services and plug revenue leakages.
...
An estimated 20,000 government services are now available on the platform.
...
The slow pace of migrating government services to the e-Citizen platform points to continued challenges in curbing revenue leakages, often attributed to corruption involving officials who solicit bribes or undercut official fees to bypass approved payment systems. “As of 31 March 2026, only 1,695 government services had been onboarded onto the e-Citizen platform against the annual target of 11,000 services,” Dr Nyakang’o said.
...
“This was a dismal performance, given the Executive Order issued in 2023 directing that all government services be made accessible through the e-Citizen portal, with a requirement for full compliance and strict timelines for transitioning from legacy systems to the centralised paybill 222222.”
...
The e-Citizen platform enables citizens and residents to apply for, pay for, and track public services — including business registration, immigration services, company searches, land queries, birth registration and passport applications — through a single account, eliminating physical queues and reducing bureaucratic processes.
...
The current administration has aggressively promoted the shift to the e-Citizen platform, primarily aimed at enhancing revenue transparency, curbing corruption and improving access to government services remotely. Kenyans and foreign nationals seeking government services through e-Citizen are required to pay a Sh50 convenience fee per transaction, a charge that has sparked public concern, particularly where it is perceived to be nearly equivalent to the cost of some services.
...
President William Ruto has on several occasions lauded the platform, saying it has eliminated bottlenecks that previously hampered access to thousands of government services. The government is now proposing to revise the fee structure, which could see the Sh50 charge increased and pegged on the value of each transaction. The proposal is contained in the Public Finance Management (e-Citizen System Management) Regulations, 2026, which are currently before the public for consideration.
...
The regulations are part of the government’s effort to close legal gaps identified by the High Court late last year when it declared the Sh50 convenience fee illegal.
...
The court found the charge discriminatory, constituting a double levy, and introduced without public participation. A consortium of three companies operates the e-Citizen platform. One provides technical support and onboarding services, another aggregates payments and processes revenue on behalf of the State, while the third handles communications, including bulk SMS services.
...
The firms are Pesaflow Limited, Webmasters Kenya and Olivetree Limited. The government assumed full ownership of e-Citizen in 2023 but continues to contract the three companies to maintain and operate the platform in return for a fee.
