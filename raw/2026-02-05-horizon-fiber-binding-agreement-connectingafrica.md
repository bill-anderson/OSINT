# East African telcos ink cross-border fiber network deal

*Matshepo Sehloho, Associate Editor, Connecting Africa — February 5, 2026*

Ethio Telecom, Djibouti Telecom and the Sudatel Group have turned a previous cross-border optical fiber memorandum of understanding into a binding agreement.

The agreement is aimed at strengthening regional connectivity and network resilience.

According to a joint statement, the agreement establishes a resilient terrestrial fiber corridor connecting the international submarine cable landing stations in Djibouti, traversing Ethiopia, and extending onward to Sudan's landing stations.

The companies said that the route creates a scalable, secure, and diversified regional connectivity pathway linking East Africa to global digital networks.

In December 2024, the telcos signed the original Horizon Fiber initiative MoU. The initiative is intended to expand international bandwidth capacity and strengthen network resilience and redundancy.

It also aims to support rising demand for data, cloud services, hyperscale connectivity, digital platforms, and cross-border data flows across the region.

The partners said the Horizon Fiber Initiative responds directly to Africa's accelerating digital economy driven by cloud adoption, fintech, e-commerce, AI, content delivery, and enterprise digitalization.

Ethio Telecom CEO Frehiwot Tamru said Horizon Fiber strengthens Ethiopia's position as a strategic digital transit hub, and it supports international expansion.

"Horizon Fiber underpins advanced digital services including cloud, data centers, enterprise solutions, and cross-border digital trade," she explained.

Djibouti Telecom CEO Mohamed Assoweh Bouh said the project showcases the operators' collective capacity to develop strategic infrastructure, strengthen digital sovereignty, and elevate the region's global standing.

Sudatel CEO Magdi Abdalla Taha described the initiative as a replicable benchmark for the rest of the continent.

## African cross-border agreements

The cross-border binding agreement among the three telcos comes at a time when regulators and telecom operators in other countries are also signing agreements.

In September 2025, Ghana's National Communications Authority (NCA) and the Togolese telecom regulator Autorité de Régulation des Communications Électroniques et des Postes (ARCEP) held a meeting aimed at strengthening cross-border telecommunications cooperations between the two countries.

In August 2025, Ghana held bilateral meetings with Burkina Faso to finalize a frequency coordination agreement to help mitigate interference challenges along their common borders.

The same month, Congo-Brazzaville's Electronic Communications Regulatory Agency (ARPCE) and the Angolan Institute of Communications (INACOM) signed a frequency coordination agreement on the common border, which is 231km long.

In July 2025, Tanzania Telecommunications Corporation (TTLC) and Kenya's Information and Communication Technology Authority (ICTA) launched and implemented a terrestrial fiber link connecting Dar es Salaam in Tanzania to Mombasa in Kenya, to enhance digital connectivity across the East African region.

In May 2025, digital connectivity solutions company Bayobab completed a fiber optic network linking Kenya's east coast city of Mombasa to Kampala in Uganda.
