There is no shortage of ambition when it comes to data governance in Africa. The African Union has its [Data Policy Framework](https://au.int/en/documents/20220728/au-data-policy-framework), which attempts to unify data governance strategies across the continent, while the [Malabo Convention](https://au.int/en/treaties/african-union-convention-cyber-security-and-personal-data-protection) creates a legal framework for digital commerce, data protection, and cybercrime. Various regional economic bodies have their own strategies, frameworks, and mandates such as the East Africa Community (EAC) Data Governance Framework, the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) Regional Open Data Legal Framework and Interoperability Framework and the Southern African Development Community (SADC) Model Data Strategy. And many African countries have data protection laws and authorities to govern their jurisdictions. These speak to how data should flow, be protected, and be governed across the continent.

One particularly ambitious concept is the African Union’s vision for the African Single Digital Market. The vision represents a comprehensive transformation of Africa’s economic landscape through cross-border digital trade and data flows. Perhaps no milestone has moved the continent firmly in that direction more than the [AFCFTA Digital Trade Protocol (DTP).](https://au.int/sites/default/files/treaties/45079-treaty-EN_AfCFTA_Protocol_on_Digital_Trade.pdf) The DTP has the potential to create a seamless market—where businesses trade across borders with trusted systems and recognized credentials, all while preserving national sovereignty.

If successful, this would support a small business owner in Lagos to trade confidently with a supplier in Nairobi. Or allow a professional to have their business visa qualifications recognized across multiple African countries. The result is stronger markets, greater inclusion, and shared regional success.

Despite strong political momentum, individual countries face real challenges in aligning policy, governance, and technical systems to enable the trusted cross-border data sharing, digital identity, and trade systems that such a vision requires.

This is the paradox that defines the current moment – and demands a new approach to the fragmentation that has long plagued digital government initiatives.

## Fragmentation takes different form across various levels of society.

Both within and across borders, there are many dimensions by which fragmentation can be observed.

- **Fragmentation between policy and practice.**Even where data policies exist, enforcement often falters because organizations lack the capacity to comply, and oversight bodies like Data Protection Offices are under-resourced and understaffed. Compounding this, laws rarely reach across borders, widening the gap between what is written on paper and what actually happens in practice.
- **Fragmentation between systems and departments.**  Institutions operate in silos, and attempts at collaboration are frequently undermined by incompatible systems, inconsistent data standards, and restrictive centralized controls. This reveals a deeper truth: technology is usually the easy part, while power dynamics and social realities shape whether interoperability is ever actually achieved.
- **Fragmentation between rights and recourse.**  Uneven digital literacy and a rapidly evolving data ecosystem leave many citizens with limited understanding of their data rights, and even where awareness is high, the focus tends to be on knowing rights rather than exercising them. Without deliberate design to bridge that gap — pointing people to the right institution, process, or remedy — data governance frameworks risk leaving the most excluded further behind.

## Governance can be a key enabler for integration.

Taken together, these forms of fragmentation point to a deeper challenge. The issue is not simply that systems are disconnected, policies unevenly implemented, or rights inconsistently understood. It is that the mechanisms needed to coordinate these pieces across institutions and borders remain weak. Integration therefore becomes less a technical exercise and more a governance challenge: creating the rules, incentives, and institutional arrangements that allow fragmented systems to operate as part of a coherent regional whole.

For the African Single Digital Market, this matters because integration will not be built on technology alone. It will depend on trust at a continental scale — confidence that systems, institutions, and rules can work together across borders. Building that trust requires governance that is both structurally sound and operationally real.

The DTP forms a critical part of this vision, turning the ambitious idea of a unified continental digital market into something real and workable. One of the most promising parts of the DTP is the Digital Identity Annex, adopted by the African Union in February 2025, which calls upon state parties to:

- Adopt digital identity regimes
- Harmonize technical regulations and standards for the facilitation of digital trade
- Conduct joint assessments of each other’s digital identity systems
- Promote interoperability in digital identity technologies by adopting principles or common technical specifications

This opens the door to testing digital public infrastructure (DPI) solutions that could overcome the barriers to cross-border trade, even as the complex process of harmonizing legal and regulatory frameworks proceeds.

## This moment demands an action-oriented approach. Enter the Africa Data Leadership Initiative (ADLI).

Tackling this opportunity head-on will be the focus on this year’s ADLI cohort.

ADLI, a peer-to-peer learning network on data governance, provides a unique space for senior government practitioners to collaborate across technical, regulatory, and policy functions within and between governments. The ADLI curriculum and peer exchange are designed to strengthen country readiness, build trust across systems, and create actionable pathways for implementing interoperable, secure, and scalable digital trade solutions across Africa. In partnership with the AfCFTA, the 2026 cohort will take a bold test-and-learn approach to strengthening countries’ abilities to comply with the Digital Identity Annex. Rather than treating implementation as a theoretical exercise, the cohort will explore how governance frameworks and DPI can work together to enable trusted and secure verification of people, businesses, and goods.

The cohort will bring together representatives from six countries across three trade corridors, with participants spanning ministries, departments, and agencies that shape digital governance ecosystems both within and across borders. This structure recognizes that integrated digital trade cannot be enabled by isolated systems or institutions; it requires coordination across governments and between countries.

ADLI is specifically designed to break down barriers, facilitate policy harmonization, and tackle fragmentation. Here’s how ADLI does it at every level:

- **Harmonizing policy and practice:** ADLI moves beyond policy design toward implementation. By mapping and operationalizing the provisions of the DTP, the program supports countries in developing practical implementation roadmaps that translate policy commitments into pilots, institutional processes, and sustained practice.
- **Integrating systems and departments:** ADLI convenes the institutions needed for implementation across government — including revenue authorities, digital identity agencies, ICT ministries, and other key actors. By creating space for collaboration across functions and jurisdictions, the program strengthens the institutional coordination required for interoperable digital trade systems.
- **Reconciling rights and recourse:** ADLI embeds a people-centered and inclusive approach to digital governance within its curriculum, exploring mechanisms for redress, strategies for meaningful societal participation, and approaches to strengthening practical application of digital rights.

This year’s ADLI curriculum is grounded in a practical question: what does it take to make Africa’s data economy function across borders? By building trusted systems for digital credentials and data sharing, ADLI strengthens the conditions for trust to become operational, not just aspirational.
