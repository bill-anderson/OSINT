Her name is Grace.

She runs a wholesale supply business in Accra.

Has been running it for nine years.

- *She knows which suppliers deliver on time and which ones need chasing.*
- *She knows that the first week of the month is slow and the last week is electric.*
- *She knows her customers by name, by business, by what they need before they call.*
- *She knows her business the way she knows her own face in the mirror.*

Last year, she applied for a loan to expand her warehouse space.

The bank’s AI scoring system reviewed her application in seconds.

Rejected.

Grace asked why.

The loan officer looked at his screen. Shrugged. Said the system flagged her as high risk.

#### High risk!

A woman with;

- *9 years of consistent business.*
- *Zero defaults.*
- *A savings group that has never missed a cycle.*
- *A business that survived a pandemic, a fuel crisis, and two floods.*

#### High risk.

She never got a clear explanation.

Because nobody at the bank could give her one.

Because the system was trained on financial behaviour from markets where people have formal salaries, credit card histories, and bank accounts they’ve held for decades.

It looked at Grace’s mobile money patterns, her rotating savings group, her seasonal income flows.

And it saw something it didn’t recognise.

So it said no.

And moved on to the next decision.

Grace is not a single random case.

She is the majority.

---

Grace’s story is what a true governance gap looks like, don’t mistage this to be about a bad algorithm.

The tool that rejected her was doing exactly what it was built to do and it wasn’t broken.

###### The real problem is what it was built to do and

###### who was in the room when they built it.

Nobody who understood mobile money.

Nobody who understood chama savings.

Nobody who had ever sat across from a market trader and tried to explain how her financial life actually works.

And that absence, that gap between who built the tool and whose life it was making decisions about is the most important governance problem on the African continent right now.

The good news though?

**Africa is starting to notice.**

And what comes next depends entirely on who shapes the answer.

---

Let me be clear about something before we go further.

**Africa is not starting from zero.**

**In July 2024,** the African Union endorsed the **Continental AI Strategy** at its Executive Council session in Accra, Ghana. All 55 member states now have a shared continental framework for AI, something Europe’s equivalent regional body took years to achieve. (FYI, we are not in competition, just an observation)

**The strategy calls for a “Local First” principle:**

AI production, development, and assessment should prioritise African challenges with African solutions.

That sentence alone, in an official continental document is extraordinary.

**Then in April 2025**, 49 African countries gathered in Kigali, Rwanda and signed the **Africa Declaration on Artificial Intelligence.** They established the Africa AI Council, co-chaired by the African Union Commission and the International Telecommunication Union. They committed $60 billion; yes, $60 billion to an **Africa AI Fund.**

And they named something that had never been named so clearly before at a continental level: African datasets represent only 1% of global data despite Africa being 17% of the world’s population.

Say that again slowly.

## 17% of the world’s people.

## 1% of the data.

The people who signed that declaration looked at that number and said: **NO MORE!**

And then there are the countries building real structures on the ground.

**Rwanda**, has a fully approved national AI policy and they even built an institution. The **Responsible AI Office** sits inside Rwanda’s Ministry of ICT and Innovation. The Rwanda Information Society Authority is the named implementing body. There is an annual consultation forum. Ethical guidelines are reviewed every three years. Rwanda benchmarks itself through an annual AI Readiness Index. Now that is true governance infrastructure.

**Nigeria** went even further in one specific direction. The Central Bank of Nigeria’s regulatory sandbox expanded in 2024-2025; now requires AI powered fintech tools to demonstrate explainability, fairness, and consumer transparency before receiving regulatory approval. An AI tool cannot get a licence to operate in Nigeria’s financial sector if it cannot explain its own decisions. The CBN also embedded this requirement in its anti-money laundering framework: AI systems must provide explainable insights into why an alert was triggered. A human investigator must understand the factors.

That is true operational governance.

And **Kenya’s** National AI Strategy, launched in March 2025, put one sentence in writing that deserves to be in every governance framework on the continent: Kenya’s data should be governed under Kenyan law. Not foreign legal systems. Ours.

**Now let me tell you what’s still missing.**

And I say this with deep respect for everything that has been built, because pointing at what’s missing is the next step of the work not criticism.

Every single one of these frameworks; **the AU Strategy, the Kigali Declaration,**

**Rwanda, Nigeria, Kenya;** has one shared blind spot.

#### Not one of them meaningfully addresses the informal economy.

#### Not one.

The AU Continental AI Strategy talks about African contexts. But it says;

- Nothing specific about mobile money as a primary financial system.
- Nothing about rotating savings groups.
- Nothing about seasonal agricultural income.
- Nothing about the 2000 languages through which most Africans actually live their lives.

Kenya’s strategy, which is quite sophisticated in comparison to what we have on the continent, was reviewed by the University of Cape Town’s IP Chair.

The review found that the strategy treats local and indigenous communities as “data generators, system validators, and passive consumers” not as creators and custodians of knowledge.

Not as people whose communal governance structures should inform how AI decisions are made and contested in their communities.

That is not a small gap. It is the majority of the continent.

- ***The market trader in Lagos.***
- ***The smallholder farmer in Uganda.***
- ***The boda boda rider in Nairobi.***
- ***The chama in East Africa.***
- ***The community savings group in Accra.***
- ***The informal trader in Johannesburg.***

They are invisible in every framework currently in existence.

And they form the majority of our gdp and are the people AI is most actively making decisions about.

That is the governance gap that Africa needs to close.

We will not be copying Brussels or adapting Washington.

We will start with Grace.

Because she represents most of us on the continent.

---

Here is what all of this looks like on the ground.

Right now, across East Africa, AI credit scoring tools are making decisions about who gets loans.

These tools were built with data from markets where financial behaviour runs through formal banking systems.

- They see a chama and label it as irregular income.
- They see mobile money and call it unstructured.
- They see seasonal farming earnings and classify it as instability.

Then Grace gets rejected.

And just FYI, she is not high risk. She is simply invisible to the model that we **imported** to make crucial decisions for our economy.

And the governance framework that should protect her, that should require the tool to explain itself, to demonstrate fairness, to be tested on people like her before it is deployed on her, doesn’t exist yet in most African markets.

***Rwanda has built the infrastructure.***

***Nigeria has built the fintech gate.***

***Kenya has named the data sovereignty principle.***

But none of them have answered the question that matters most for **Grace.**

***What happens when AI is deployed on communities it was***

***never designed to understand and it fails them?***

Here is the opportunity.

#### Africa is the only continent in the world that can build an AI governance framework from inside the informal economy.

Not adapting someone else’s risk categories. Not importing someone else’s definition of creditworthiness.

**Starting from the chama.**

**From mobile money.**

**From the market woman.**

**From the 2000 languages.**

**From the communal decision-making traditions that are not just cultural but legal infrastructure in many communities.**

That framework would not just protect African people.

It would be the **most innovative governance contribution to global AI policy** in a generation.

---

Get this; Africa is not behind on AI governance.

Africa is early.

And being early, with full knowledge of what happens when you get it wrong is the most powerful position in any room.

Here is what African AI governance built from our reality could actually look like.

It redefines high-risk AI. Not just by sector; hiring, healthcare, credit etc but by consequence.

If an AI tool is making decisions that affect someone’s access to capital, food, health, or opportunity in a context where there is no specialist to catch the error and no legal framework to provide recourse, that is high risk. **Full stop**.

Regardless of what sector it sits in.

1. **It names accountability in the informal economy.** When an AI credit scoring tool denies a loan to a market trader in Lagos, someone specific must be responsible for that decision.
	The vendor who built the tool. The bank that deployed it. One of them.
	**Named. Accountable. Contactable by Grace.**
2. **It requires language testing.** Before an AI tool is deployed in any African community, it must be tested for accuracy in the languages of that community.
	If it cannot understand Luganda, it should not be making healthcare decisions in Buganda. That is simple basic accountability.
3. **It centres communal governance.** Africa has legal traditions that are not about individual rights alone. They are about community accountability, the elder, the council, the group. An African AI governance framework that builds in communal redress mechanisms, not just individual appeal rights, would be unprecedented globally.

The AU Continental AI Strategy. The Kigali Declaration. Rwanda’s institutional model. Nigeria’s fintech accountability gate. Kenya’s data sovereignty principle.

The building blocks are here.

Now someone needs to put them together in a way that starts from Grace.

---

Now, there is something I want to say that I don’t see written anywhere in any of these frameworks.

The reason Africa has been absent from so many of these conversations is a **frequency problem,** we don’t lack the knowledge.

For a long time and I am not judging, I lived inside this too, African policymakers, African professionals, African business leaders arrived at governance conversations in a particular posture.

Listening. Adapting. Translating someone else’s framework for our context.

Politely, looking at everyone else as our master and we just had to follow rules.

***And that posture; that energetic position of arriving***

***after the decisions are made and finding our place inside them***

***has shaped what our governance frameworks look like.***

They are good. They are earnest. They are often better than people realise.

The frequency you’re building from determines what you build.

And building from borrowed frameworks produces borrowed outcomes.

The manifesto I’ve laid out in this Wave, the one that starts from the chama, from mobile money, from 2000 languages, from communal accountability cannot be built from a borrowed frequency.

**It can only be built by someone who decides that Africa’s governance framework should look like Africa.**

Not as a concession or regional adaptation.

But as a contribution.

### And that person is YOU and I.

The world is watching what Africa builds with AI.

And the frameworks we build here, if we build them from our reality will be the ones the world learns from next.

Build from that frequency.

Not from a place of catching up.

From a place of knowing we are exactly where we need to be.
