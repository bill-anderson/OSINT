Tanzania spends 131bn/- to speed up production of national identity cards

February 13, 20263 Mins Read Facebook Twitter LinkedIn Email Telegram WhatsApp Copy Link

The Tanzanian government has invested 131 billion shillings to modernize the national ID card production system, aiming to expedite the issuance process and improve service delivery to citizens. This significant technological upgrade comes as part of broader efforts to strengthen identification infrastructure essential for public service access and economic participation.

Tanzania’s ambitious push to modernize its national identification system reached a milestone with a substantial investment in cutting-edge technology. Home Affairs Minister Patrobas Katambi announced the 131 billion shilling (approximately $56.3 million) allocation during an inspection visit to the National Identification Authority (NIDA) headquarters in Dar es Salaam on Tuesday.

The investment targets critical technological infrastructure designed to streamline the production of national identity cards, addressing long-standing bottlenecks in the issuance process that have frustrated citizens for years.

“This investment demonstrates our government’s commitment to ensuring every Tanzanian can easily access their constitutional right to identification,” Minister Katambi stated while touring NIDA’s production facilities.

The modernization initiative comes at a crucial time as Tanzania intensifies efforts to achieve universal identification coverage. National ID cards are increasingly essential for accessing banking services, social welfare programs, and mobile communications in the East African nation of approximately 65 million people.

During his inspection, Minister Katambi examined the complete production workflow, from data collection to card manufacturing and distribution. The new technology is expected to significantly reduce processing times, which have historically stretched to several months in some regions.

Industry analysts note that Tanzania joins several African nations prioritizing digital identity systems as foundational infrastructure for economic development. The continent-wide push toward robust identification systems aligns with the African Union’s Digital Transformation Strategy, which emphasizes digital identification as a cornerstone for inclusive growth.

In a remarkable financial achievement, Minister Katambi also acknowledged NIDA’s contribution of 38 billion shillings in dividends to the central government treasury. “This dividend payment is evidence of sound management and operational efficiency at NIDA,” the minister remarked, highlighting how strategic investments in public institutions can yield financial returns while improving service delivery.

The dividend payment represents a significant shift in the authority’s financial performance, transforming it from a purely cost-center institution to a revenue-generating entity that contributes to government coffers.

Tanzania’s digital identity evolution mirrors similar efforts across East Africa, with neighboring Kenya and Rwanda also making substantial investments in their respective systems. Regional experts suggest these parallel developments could eventually facilitate greater cross-border mobility and economic integration through interoperable identification systems.

The ID card modernization project also creates opportunities for local technology firms and could generate specialized employment in Tanzania’s growing digital economy. Technology sector stakeholders have welcomed the investment as potentially catalytic for developing local expertise in secure document production and identity management systems.

For ordinary Tanzanians, the anticipated improvement in ID card production efficiency could eliminate a significant barrier to formal economic participation. Without national identification, citizens often face challenges accessing formal banking, registering businesses, and participating in government programs.

As NIDA implements the technological upgrades throughout 2026, officials expect to dramatically increase daily production capacity and establish more service centers in underserved regions, particularly rural areas where access has historically been limited.
