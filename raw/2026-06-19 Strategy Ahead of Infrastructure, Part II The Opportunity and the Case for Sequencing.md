This is the second piece in a two-part series on the Central Bank of Nigeria’s June 2026 payments circular. [Part I set out the circular’s four pillars (market structure, data localisation, ultimate beneficial ownership disclosure, and systemic oversight) and argued that the strategic logic behind them is sound, even as it flagged four unresolved implementation risks: data centre capacity, consumer cost passthrough, grid constraints, and cloud provider ambiguity.](https://anjolaadebowale.substack.com/p/strategy-ahead-of-infrastructure-8ef?r=ks2i2)

This piece picks up from there. It looks at the commercial and architectural opportunity the circular creates, then makes the case, using India’s 2018 experience and Rwanda’s phased approach as contrasting precedents, that a sequenced rollout would have served the CBN’s own goals better than the deadline it chose.

## V. The Opportunity

The CBN circular is generating demand for specific kinds of architectural, AI, and governance work that Nigerian institutions had collectively been postponing.

A balanced assessment of the circular must acknowledge that the same provisions which create implementation pressure also create commercial and architectural opportunity. Viewed from an implementation perspective, three opportunities stand out, each tied directly to a specific pillar of the circular and each creating demand for a category of work that Nigerian institutions had collectively been deferring.

The first opportunity flows from the data localisation provision in Pillar 2, which forces an architectural decision that institutions deploying AI had previously been able to defer. The requirement that payment data be stored and managed in Nigeria by January 2027 means that AI workloads in fraud detection, credit scoring, risk modelling, and customer intelligence can no longer be designed against cross-border cloud infrastructure as a matter of convenience. They must be designed against domestic infrastructure as a matter of regulation. For institutions that treat this as a compliance task, the outcome is AI infrastructure that is geographically compliant but operationally constrained. For institutions that treat it as an architectural opportunity, the outcome is AI-ready data foundations that compound across every workload deployed afterwards. The most visible supply-side response to this specific question is MTN’s [$235 million Sifiso Dabengwa Data Centre](https://techcabal.com/2025/07/01/mtn-nigeria-completes-first-phase-of-235-million-data-centre/), launched in July 2025, which paired a 4.5-megawatt Tier III facility with a $20 million domestic cloud service explicitly positioned for compute-intensive workloads. MTN’s chief executive described the second phase as readiness for the processing power that artificial intelligence requires. Pricing in naira rather than US dollars removes the foreign-currency exposure that has historically made GPU-intensive AI workloads prohibitively expensive for Nigerian institutions. The opportunity extends beyond additional hosting capacity. It includes the emergence of a domestic compute environment capable of supporting more advanced AI workloads at scale, in a market that previously had to rent that capability from abroad.

The second opportunity flows from the market structure provision in Pillar 1, and most public commentary has missed its technical dimension. Restructuring an institution to comply with the 25%/15% cap is not primarily a legal exercise in corporate reorganisation. It is data engineering work. Affected institutions must disentangle shared platforms across card issuing and merchant acquiring, separate previously-integrated data flows, build cleaner APIs between newly-separated services, and rebuild identity federation across entities that used to operate as one. This is the kind of architectural work for which Nigeria has a thin specialist base, particularly in regulated-industry data architecture at scale. The institutions and individuals positioned to do this work, especially indigenous data and integration specialists, gain a structural advantage during the 2026 implementation window. The work itself does not appear in colocation revenue numbers or in cloud market sizing, but it determines whether the affected institutions emerge from restructuring with cleaner architectures or with technical debt that constrains them for the next decade.

The third opportunity flows from the combined effect of the UBO disclosure requirement in Pillar 3 and the monthly market-share returns required by Pillar 4. Together, these create a continuous data governance obligation that Nigerian regulated institutions have not historically maintained at the required standard. Compliance requires accurate, audited, structured data about ownership and market position, current enough to support monthly submission and defensible enough to survive regulatory scrutiny. The work this generates is master data management, ownership-tracking systems that resolve through corporate structure to natural persons, real-time market-share calculation, and audit trails designed for regulatory inspection. This is a category of work that is likely to expand significantly in scale as a result of the circular. The firms that build credibility in regulated-industry data governance during the implementation window position themselves as the trusted operators of that capability for years afterwards. If the CBN extends similar reporting and transparency obligations to adjacent sectors, which is the consistent pattern observed in other jurisdictions that began with payments, the same capability becomes increasingly valuable.

None of this resolves the implementation concerns raised in Part I. Aggressive timelines, cost passthrough to consumers, structural grid constraints, and the cloud provider ambiguity all remain real. The opportunity side of the ledger matters because it reframes the strategic question. The CBN circular is generating demand for specific kinds of architectural, AI, and governance work that Nigerian institutions had collectively been postponing. The firms and specialists positioned to do that work are beginning to move. Whether the regulation succeeds in its stated goals depends partly on whether the architectural capacity to absorb it can scale at the pace the deadlines require.

## VI. Sequencing and Implementation

Regulations that depend on infrastructure tend to work when the infrastructure already exists, and tend to fail when the infrastructure is being built in parallel with the rule.

This is the question that matters most for a practitioner audience, and the available evidence suggests the answer is yes: this should have been a plan rather than a deadline. Regulations of this magnitude are not new, and the countries that have implemented them successfully, alongside those that have stumbled, leave a useful evidence base.

The most instructive cautionary precedent is India in 2018. The Reserve Bank of India issued its [payment data localisation directive in April of that year](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/NotificationUser.aspx?Id=11244), giving payment system providers six months to comply. The deadline was missed. Industry concerns about implementation feasibility forced the RBI to issue an extensive FAQ document clarifying the rule. In 2019, the RBI relaxed the original provision to permit data processing abroad, provided the data was deleted from foreign systems within twenty-four hours and stored only in India. Further clarifications followed in September 2021. As recently as the present period, payment system operators in India are still working through implementation guidance, with major banks publishing phased migration plans across different channels and products as they continue to align with the rule.

The Indian experience is not a failure. It is, more accurately, a regulation that landed in strategically correct territory but operationally premature condition, and which required years of phased rollback, clarification, and industry consultation to become workable. The Nigerian circular is structurally similar to the 2018 RBI directive, with one significant difference: India had considerably larger domestic data centre capacity and a substantially more stable electrical grid when its directive landed than Nigeria does today. If the Indian rollout required FAQs, formal relaxations, and seven years of phased implementation guidance to function reliably, the foreseeable trajectory for Nigeria’s circular is likely to follow a similar path.

The most instructive positive precedent is [Rwanda](https://govinsider.asia/intl-en/article/how-rwanda-built-a-credible-execution-ready-digital-public-infrastructure-dpi). Three features of the Rwandan approach distinguish it from the CBN circular. First, regulation is tied to a multi-year national strategy rather than announced through standalone circulars with short compliance windows, giving industry visibility into the regulatory trajectory years in advance. Second, the infrastructure was built before the regulation required its use, rather than in parallel, so operators could plan capacity expansion against a known and stable roadmap. Third, a [regulatory sandbox established in 2022](https://www.bnr.rw/regulatorysandbox) allowed regulators and industry to test compliance in operational conditions before it became binding, surfacing implementation problems early enough to be addressed through rule design rather than retroactive clarification.

The underlying principle is straightforward. Regulations that depend on infrastructure tend to work when the infrastructure already exists, and tend to fail when the infrastructure is being built in parallel with the rule. India in 2018 demonstrated the second pattern. Rwanda has demonstrated the first.

A more strategic version of the Nigerian circular would have anchored its provisions to a multi-year national milestone rather than to an eighteen-month deadline. One plausible sequencing would have introduced the components in stages, beginning with the provisions that require no new infrastructure and ending with the provisions that depend most heavily on capacity that does not yet exist at the required scale.

In such a sequenced approach, UBO disclosure and the monthly reporting framework could have been made operational immediately, since both require regulatory and procedural changes rather than physical infrastructure. The market structure caps could have been phased in across 2027 and 2028, with formal industry consultation on transition plans and clear runways for affected institutions to restructure or divest where required. Data localisation could have been phased in by data category, beginning with the most sensitive categories such as cardholder data and authentication credentials, and expanding outward in subsequent phases. The migration timeline could have been tied explicitly to documented growth in domestic data centre capacity and grid reliability, with the CBN publishing benchmarks against which the regulatory timeline is measured. Throughout the rollout, a regulatory sandbox could have allowed institutions to test compliance approaches in controlled conditions and the CBN to refine its guidance before final enforcement. The entire programme could have been anchored to a published national digital infrastructure strategy with measurable milestones for power generation, data centre capacity, and skills development.

This approach is not less sovereign than the circular as currently drafted. It is arguably more sovereign in practice, because it produces a rule that holds reliably from the date of enforcement rather than a rule that softens through quiet supervisory discretion in response to implementation pressure. The Indian 2018 directive looks more aggressive on paper than the operational outcome that eventually emerged through years of relaxation. A phased Nigerian version would look less aggressive on paper, but it would bind from the first day of enforcement, which is a stronger position for the regulator to hold than the alternative.

## VII. Conclusion

Strategic direction without sequenced execution tends to produce aggressive deadlines that quietly soften under operational pressure. Sequenced execution without strategic direction tends to produce capacity that no one uses.

The CBN circular addresses four real problems through a single coordinated instrument, and its strategic logic is coherent and consistent with what serious sovereignty looks like across the data, governance, and oversight layers of a modern payments system. On the strategic dimension, there is little to disagree with.

The implementation dimension is where the difficulty lies. The deadlines are aggressive relative to the underlying capacity. The infrastructure required to comply is being built in parallel with the regulation rather than in advance of it. The cloud provider ambiguity is likely to produce a period of regulatory uncertainty before the CBN is in a position to clarify its interpretation. Compliance costs will be significant, particularly in the early phase, and the question of who absorbs those costs remains unaddressed in the published document.

The most plausible outcomes over the next eighteen months fall into three broad scenarios. In the first, the CBN issues clarifying guidance that treats international cloud provider local zones as compliant infrastructure, foreign hyperscalers accelerate their Nigerian builds in response, institutions migrate workloads to those zones, and data sovereignty is achieved in geographic terms but not in terms of underlying dependency. This is the most likely short-term outcome, given the existing posture of major cloud providers in adjacent African markets. In the second, deadlines are quietly extended through supervisory discretion, as has happened in India and elsewhere when implementation realities have outpaced regulatory ambition. The circular would remain technically in force, but the binding deadline would drift, which is the path of least resistance and is consistent with the CBN’s historical approach to technical compliance challenges in the payments sector. In the third, Nigerian operators including MainOne, Rack Centre, Africa Data Centres, and Nxtra accelerate construction, supported by major energy investments alongside. This is the outcome that would constitute genuine sovereignty in infrastructure terms, but it cannot be achieved on an eighteen-month timeline regardless of policy ambition, which limits its viability as a near-term path.

What the circular reveals, more than anything, is that regulation and infrastructure need to be sequenced together rather than announced separately. Strategic direction without sequenced execution tends to produce aggressive deadlines that quietly soften under operational pressure. Sequenced execution without strategic direction tends to produce capacity that no one uses. The combination of the two is the harder regulatory work, and it is the work that the countries that have built durable digital sovereignty have, in different ways, all done. The next eighteen months will test whether the country can build the implementation timeline to match it.
