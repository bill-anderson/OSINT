21sGlobal payments giant Visa has partnered with Safaricom's M-Pesa and pan-African network Onafriq to launch a live stablecoin pilot in the Democratic Republic of Congo, directly challenging traditional correspondent banking rails.

The initiative, announced in early July 2026, utilizes U.S. dollar-pegged stablecoins on blockchain infrastructure to settle cross-border mobile transactions instantly. By bypassing traditional intermediaries that siphon an average of 8 percent in remittance fees across Sub-Saharan Africa, the consortium aims to radically compress the cost and time of moving capital in one of the continent's fastest-growing digital economies.

## Bypassing the Correspondent Banking Chokehold

Historically, sending money across African borders required a tortuous journey through correspondent banks, often routing through London or New York before returning to a neighboring African country. This legacy architecture is notoriously slow, frequently taking days to clear, and highly expensive due to multiple currency conversion spreads and intermediary fees.

The Visa-M-Pesa-Onafriq pilot attacks this inefficiency at the infrastructure level. Rather than using fiat currency ledgers, the backend settlements are executed using digital stablecoins whose value is mathematically pegged to the US Dollar.

- **Instant Settlement:** Blockchain protocols allow the transactions to clear in seconds, rather than days.
- **Cost Reduction:** By eliminating correspondent banking fees, the cost of moving capital drops to fractions of a cent on the backend.
- **Frictionless User Experience:** For the end consumer, the blockchain technology is entirely invisible. Merchants receive their payments in local currency, while users simply trigger a standard M-Pesa wallet transfer.

## The DRC as the Prime Testing Ground

The selection of the Democratic Republic of Congo for this watershed pilot is highly strategic. The DRC possesses a rapidly expanding mobile money ecosystem but remains severely underserved by formal banking infrastructure. The nation's vast geography and fragmented physical infrastructure make digital financial services the only viable mechanism for rapid economic inclusion.

Furthermore, the DRC pilot builds upon the Visa Pay platform, a Payments-as-a-Service system launched in the country in September 2025 through an API connection with Onafriq. This foundation allowed engineers to seamlessly integrate the new stablecoin settlement layer without disrupting existing merchant networks.

## M-Pesa's Blockchain Pivot

For Safaricom and the broader M-Pesa Africa joint venture, the pilot represents a monumental strategic pivot. After dominating domestic fiat mobile money for two decades, M-Pesa is aggressively moving into decentralized financial infrastructure.

Earlier in 2026, M-Pesa entered the stablecoin space through a partnership with a UAE-based firm, signaling its intent to capture a larger share of the lucrative Gulf-to-Africa remittance corridor. By partnering directly with Visa—a company that has rapidly expanded its own crypto-treasury operations—M-Pesa is ensuring it remains the primary consumer interface while upgrading its backend to Web3 standards.

## Regulatory Implications for African Central Banks

The success of this pilot could force a regulatory reckoning across the continent. Institutions like the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) and the Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) have historically viewed cryptocurrencies with deep suspicion, citing capital flight and monetary sovereignty risks.

However, stablecoins operating within closed, KYC-compliant networks managed by regulated entities like Visa and M-Pesa present a different paradigm. If the DRC pilot successfully demonstrates that stablecoin settlements can lower inflation by reducing import/export friction and drastically cheapen diaspora remittances, other regulators will face immense pressure to authorize similar frameworks.

The era of 8 percent fees for moving money across an invisible border is nearing its end. The plumbing of African finance is being rewritten, block by block.
