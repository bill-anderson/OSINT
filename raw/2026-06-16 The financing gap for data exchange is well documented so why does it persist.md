# The financing gap for data exchange is well documented, so why does it persist?

16 June 2026 |

7 mins read

 ![](https://dial.global/wp-content/themes/dial_unfold/images/heroes/article-hero.png)

A national data exchange platform goes live. Ministries are onboarded; the first cross-agency queries clear; the launch deck makes the rounds. Eighteen months later, the platform is fraying. The engineers who built it have moved on. Two custodian agencies have quietly stopped syncing because the relying-party support desk has no one to staff for it. There is a limited budget for new use cases, and none for keeping the existing ones alive. Financial sustainability has stopped being a planning question and turned into a reliability question – threatening hard-earn trusts among partners and users.

This scenario is an all-too-familiar reality.

Today, governments across Africa are seeking to strengthen data sharing as part of their [digital public infrastructure (DPI)](https://dial.global/good-dpi/) approach. But our research shows that even with the best intentions, this gap between the funded build (capex) and the unfunded running and maintenance (opex) persists as a structural challenge.

So, what is needed to effectively close the financing gap? We surface key insights on financing around data exchange systems and flag persistent questions that need further exploration.

## Most data exchange systems today are financed in one of six ways. Each one has a different blind spot.

Despite the variety of financing models available, no single approach reliably covers both the cost of building a system and the cost of keeping it running. Understanding where each model succeeds — and where it falls short — is essential for designing a more durable approach.

**Government-led financing** takes the form of direct budget allocations or statutory funds, as seen with Aadhaar in India and PIX in Brazil. This model is often strong on capex, providing the funding needed to build the system. But the recurring opex line is often the first to be cut when fiscal pressure mounts, and when political attention moves on, competing priorities can squeeze it further.

**Philanthropic and donor capital** arrives in the form of grants, pilots, and catalytic funding from foundations and bilateral donors such as the Rockefeller Foundation or Gates Foundation. This type of financing, direct to countries, is well suited for getting systems off the ground, testing new use cases, and derisking early-stage innovation. Philanthropic or donor capital can also fund digital public goods that multiple countries can draw on without individual financing commitments. The limitation is time horizons: funding cycles are short and innovation-focused, with funding rarely extending to long term operations.

**Multilateral-backed financing** comes in the form of concessional loans, technical assistance grants, and ecosystem programs from institutions like the World Bank and regional development banks – Fayda in Ethiopia and PhilSys in the Philippines are examples. These instruments typically offer medium to long term horizons, with co-funding from the recipient government. They work well during implementation, but the opex picture gets complicated once the initial capital runs out, and the system must be sustained without external support.

**Membership and cooperative models** involve countries, agencies, or institutions jointly owning, governing, and financing a shared infrastructure they all rely on. Operational costs are distributed across members through subscriptions, transaction-based contributions, or jointly funded core entities. The Nordic Institute for Interoperability Solutions – which governs X-Road’s core on behalf of Estonia, Finland, and Iceland – and the HISP network that supports DHIS2 across implementing countries are both examples. This model aligns incentives well: funders are the users, and opex is structurally shared rather than orphaned. The harder challenges lie elsewhere: reaching critical mass on membership, agreeing on equitable cost-sharing across members of different sizes and capacity, and the governance work of sustaining a multilateral entity over time.

**Blended and hybrid vehicles** are pooled capital combining public, donor, and private sources, often through risk-sharing instruments or guarantees. They can effectively de-risk the build, but the logic rarely extends to long term financial sustenance, and exit pathways are unclear.

**Self-sustaining, revenue-backed models** generate revenue through API fees, service charges, or B2B revenue – tiered access pricing or transaction fees, for example. In the context of data exchange systems, this could include micro-pricing small data units based on specific partnerships with private players. It is the only model where opex is structurally covered, and the rarest in practice. The underlying economics – what data should be priced, and at what level – are still being worked out, which is why this model has struggled to take hold.

## Beneath capex and opex sit very different cost structures. Opex is where the picture gets blurry.

Within DPI financing, capex tends to be well documented in the form of feasibility studies, institutional setup, procurement, hardware, server configuration, development and testing environments, security and compliance, and cloud infrastructure. These have established benchmarks and clear procurement pathways.

Opex is where the picture gets blurry: it needs to cover a range of on-going components including sandbox environments, digital service design and deployment, transaction volumes, infrastructure and service provider fees, and the pricing and revenue model. It also covers the parts of a DPI approach that look least like technology: onboarding relevant partners, running pilots, project monitoring, retaining specialist staff, and absorbing overheads. For DPI, lifetime operational cost will most likely exceed the initial capital outlay.

The opex gap is not theoretical. Ghana launched its Open Data Initiative in 2012 with support from the World Wide Web Foundation. By 2014, the portal could not be sustained due to a lack of funding and had to be revamped through a USD 900,000 allocation under a separate e-transform project. The pattern shows up in other regions and domains too. [Costa Rica’s open data portal](https://cepei.org/en/documents/datos-abiertos-latam-caribe-2/), once a regional reference point, was disabled after political transitions stripped the operational backing it needed.

These stories are not outliers. The 2025 donor funding shocks made the fragility legible at scale: donor-funded health management information [systems like DHIS2 were exposed to sudden pullbacks and weak domestic financing, while systems with stable domestic opex coverage and local capacity have weathered the same shocks](https://dhis2.org/report-highlights-need-to-sustain-health-data-systems/). Planning for continuous opex funding is what separates the systems that bend from the ones that break.

If the operational funding gap is well documented, and the consequences are visible – from Costa Rica to Ghana and DHIS2 – the obvious question is: why has the paradigm has not shifted? Part of the answer lies in the financing models themselves, which are weighted towards building, not sustaining. Part of it lies in the absence of a viable model that can cover operational expenses in the long run.

## Throughout our recent research, three questions surfaced repeatedly.

Across our consultations with DPG builders, funders, researchers, and policymakers, several key challenges emerged – each pointing to a question that the ecosystem has not resolved.

-   How should we value and price data in systems built for public benefit? Nano-pricing of data offers signals, and public-private partnerships for shared data point in one direction. The cost-recovery question for national-level data exchange is wide open. There are experiments underway that include nano-pricing of data and public-private partnerships, but these have not yet scaled. Without a clearer economic case, large-scale investment in data exchange will stay constrained.
-   How can value created across data exchange ecosystems be captured and redistributed? Platforms generate value across governments, researchers, and firms, but the mechanisms to channel that value back into operations, or back to data custodians and producers, are often missing or controlled by a few dominant actors. Weak demand signals, high onboarding costs, and unclear value propositions drag down participation, which caps the value the platform can generate.
-   What design choices, beyond the technical architecture, most affect operating costs and trust? Technical costs are rarely the binding constraint: tools like X-Road’s cost calculator already exist. The harder cost factors are governance, membership models, consent modules, redress mechanisms, and transparency portals. These are routinely not considered nor clarified during sustainability planning.

## The pathway forward requires concerted action.

Progress on these challenges will not come from any single actor. Each requires a different coalition — and a willingness to treat operational sustainability as a first-order design question, not an afterthought.

1.  **Building a shared evidence base to better understand the value of data in public-benefit systems.** Without a clearer economic case for what data is worth in different sharing contexts, pricing and cost-recovery conversations will keep stalling. This is primarily a research challenge, and the levers are largely there: empirical studies of micro-pricing experiments, value-flow mapping in operating data exchanges, and cost-benefit work on data-driven public services. A wide range of actors like academic institutions, regional research networks, development agencies, and country statistical offices need to feed into this work.
2.  **Looking beyond national data exchange systems for business models and governance innovations.** Through our research on [data trusts](https://dial.global/research/climate-action-data-trusts/), [open transaction networks](https://dial.global/research/data-for-climate-open-transaction-networks/), and [data spaces](https://dial.global/research/unlocking-data-for-climate-action-requires-trusted-marketplaces-that-promote-transparency-and-control-data-spaces-offer-a-promising-solution/), we have explored how different governance and financing models are helping to get data flowing across borders, sectors, and other silos. These entities often leverage hybrid public-private models that match donor funding with membership models or tiered pricing to incentivize sustainable data sharing. They understand that the non-technical layer is where most of the operating cost and most of the trust gets built or eroded. Countries can learn from these models, both in terms of exploring new ways to sustain their national data exchange platforms and how to support data sharing innovations to improve their country’s overall data ecosystem. Convenings and knowledge sharing through peer-learning networks between operating data exchanges, technical assistance partnerships, and cross-country secondments can help advance this. For example, the Africa Data Leadership Initiative (ADLI), which DIAL convenes, is a good structure to facilitate this.
3.  **Shifting funder priorities from launches to lifecycles.** No amount of research or model innovation will land if the underlying capital still flows mostly to builds. Bilateral donors, multilateral development banks, and philanthropic funders all need to make space for opex-eligible instruments, longer funding horizons, and joint-funding arrangements that share operational risk across funders rather than parking it with the recipient government. The [One Future Alliance](https://dial.global/good-dpi-more-than-tech-funding/) – flagged in the 2023 G20 Leaders’ Declaration as a vehicle for technical assistance and financing for DPI in lower- and middle-income countries – is one of the more visible venues for this conversation. None of these are yet specifically focused on the opex for data exchange questions, and that is the opening.
