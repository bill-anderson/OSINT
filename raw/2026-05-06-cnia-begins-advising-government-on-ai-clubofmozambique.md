# Commission begins advising Government on the use of Artificial Intelligence in Mozambique (CNIA)

The new National Commission for Artificial Intelligence (CNIA) of Mozambique will begin advising the Government on ethical, legal, security, and social issues involving these tools, as well as their use in the labor market.

According to the Council of Ministers’ decree creating the CNIA, which came into force in April and was consulted yesterday by Lusa, the new body aims to “guarantee the adoption and dissemination of best practices for the responsible and ethical use” of Artificial Intelligence (AI), “as well as the development of the regulatory framework, education, scientific research, cybersecurity, technological development, and innovation.”

The CNIA carries out its activities throughout the national territory and is headquartered in the city of Maputo, remaining subordinate to the Council of Ministers. Its competencies include “advising on the elaboration of strategic actions to promote the development” of these tools in Mozambique, while “taking into consideration the ethical and security aspects” associated with AI.

It must also “advise on initiatives that can elucidate the Government on the state of the art and on the actions for the development of science and technologies, products, and associated services” with AI initiatives “with potential impact on the various areas of economic and social development” of the country, and “prepare opinions on proposals for policies, strategies, action plans, and proposals for the establishment of scientific research and education institutions” in this field. It will further “advise on whether ethical, legal, security, and other appropriate social issues are adequately addressed by AI initiatives,” and “prepare opinions on opportunities for international cooperation with strategic allies in research matters and activities” involving these tools, as well as in the “development of standards and compatibility of international regulations.” It shall also “advise on issues related to AI and the workforce in Mozambique,” including those related to the potential of its use for workforce training and to prepare opinions on the establishment of institutions and development programs in this area, in addition to “issuing opinions on proposals for the establishment of specialized scientific research entities, including laboratories and centers of excellence.”

The CNIA will also prepare opinions on the funding of strategic AI programs and projects in Mozambique, ensure scientific and technical advice to the Government in the development of AI initiatives and action plans, and monitor and evaluate the implementation of international recommendations in this area.

Added to its competencies, among others, is the issuance of opinions on proposals for legal and regulatory framework instruments aimed at developing AI in Mozambique, “respecting ethical and security principles,” or even “recommending initiatives to promote investment in AI initiatives, programs, and projects.” The CNIA is chaired by the minister overseeing the Information and Communication Technologies (ICT) area and has as vice-presidents the ministers overseeing the areas of National Defense and Education. The commission, as defined by the same decree, will be composed of staff and specialists from the public and private sectors, academia, and civil society, “with relevant experience in matters of AI, Science, and Innovation.”
