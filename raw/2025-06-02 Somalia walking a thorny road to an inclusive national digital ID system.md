# Somalia walking a thorny road to an inclusive national digital ID system

> ## Excerpt
> ID4Africa 2025 interview with NIRA DG Abdiwali Ali Abdulle: ~85% of Somalis
> (pop. ~19.7m) have no legal ID; the World Bank is the biggest partner and
> Pakistan's NADRA the technology partner; target of 15m IDs by end-2029.

---

Since 2023, Somalia has an ambitious story of a country poised for progress in implementing an inclusive national digital ID system despite several challenges. In an interview on the sidelines of the ID4Africa 2025 AGM, the Director General of Somalia's National Identification and Registration Authority (NIRA), Abdiwali Ali Abdulle, described a country fully engaged in its identity development journey despite infrastructural, security, and awareness problems.

About 85 percent of citizens currently have no legal identity, many lacking feeder documents like birth certificates. Somalia's population is estimated at 19.7 million.

Road covered so far: for decades Somalia struggled with a fragmented ID system until 2019, when the government decided to put in place a unified legal ID architecture. "After several consultations, the cabinet and the two chambers of parliament approved the identification and data protection laws, which went into effect in 2023. This permitted us to launch the new national ID system," Abdulle said. NIRA recently launched the Hubiye and e-Aqoonsi platforms to bring services together and make verification easier.

Hurdles: lack of public awareness of digital ID concepts after three decades without a formal ID system; the siloed nature of government databases and lack of standardization in interoperability; insecurity in conflict-affected areas; and absence of physical infrastructure such as roads and electricity.

Reaching 15 million by 2029: Abdulle is bullish about issuing up to 15 million national digital IDs by the end of 2029, via registration offices and mobile teams, partnerships with government entities, banks and telecoms operators, and diaspora enrolment through embassies. The biggest partner is the World Bank (financial and technical support); Pakistan's NADRA is the technology partner providing the system and hardware; UNDP and IOM provide capacity-building.

For those without birth certificates, NIRA accepts passports and academic certificates, and uses community witnesses where no document exists. Digitizing civil registration and expanding ID adoption in line with SDG 16.9 are part of future plans.
