# Cyril Ramaphosa and Solly Malatsi meet Amazon to discuss Starlink competitor

By Luis Monzon, MyBroadband, 15 July 2026

On Tuesday, communications minister Solly Malatsi and President Cyril Ramaphosa held meetings with David Zapolsky, the chief of global affairs and top legal officer at Amazon.

Both discussions focused on Amazon's continued investment in South Africa's digital economy and took place ahead of the announcement that Amazon would launch LEO services locally through Herotel.

Amazon has been operating in South Africa since 2004, when its cloud arm, Amazon Web Services (AWS), established a presence in Cape Town.

The global e-commerce and technology giant has since expanded its businesses in the country, launching its online marketplace and is now embarking on satellite Internet services.

In late 2025, Amazon Leo, the former Amazon Kuiper, began allowing local users in South Africa and other countries to register their interest in using the Internet service.

The launch of the dedicated Leo registration page came just ahead of the company's opening public testing with select enterprise customers ahead of a planned commercial rollout in 2026.

Leo currently has a fleet of over 375 production satellites in orbit, which it aims to expand to 1,618 satellites by 30 July 2026. It began full-scale deployment of its constellation in April 2025.

While it is far from matching Starlink's over 10,700 satellites, Leo claims its top-end product will offer speeds of up to 1Gbps.

On Wednesday, local fibre network provider Herotel revealed that it was the first authorised Amazon Leo distributor in South Africa.

Van Zyl Botha, Herotel CEO, said that the company will be selling Amazon Leo services as part of a new product line called Evry.

Evry will aim to connect households and businesses in regions where terrestrial infrastructure is either impractical or unavailable, highlighting the power of satellite Internet services.

## Amazon Leo beats Starlink to South Africa

The question about Amazon's licensing remained unanswered. It is a major competitor to Starlink, which is still waiting for the introduction of equity-equivalent investment programmes (EEIPs).

The EEIPs would allow Starlink to obtain a licence in South Africa without its parent company, SpaceX, having to comply with demographic-based local ownership requirements.

Under the Electronic Communications Act, new and renewing telecommunications licensees must be 30% owned by historically disadvantaged groups.

Starlink's licensing prospects hit a major roadblock in May when South Africa's communications regulator said it would not change regulations on the ownership requirements for telecoms licences.

Regulatory experts have repeatedly said that Starlink's competitors could beat it to market in South Africa by complying with existing telecoms regulations rather than calling for their amendment.

On Tuesday, Malatsi said he met with a delegation from Amazon, led by David Zapolsky, the company's head of legal and foreign affairs.

The meeting was also joined by Department of Communications officials and ICASA representatives "to discuss Amazon's continued investment in and commitment to South Africa's digital economy."

"Present in South Africa since 2004, Amazon continues to make a significant contribution to our economy through its diverse business operations, supporting thousands of South African jobs," said Malatsi.

## Amazon meeting with top officials in South Africa

David Zapolsky (far left), chief of global affairs and legal officer at Amazon, meets with President Ramaphosa. Robert Koen (far right), Amazon's managing director for Sub-Saharan Africa, was also present.

It was one of a few meetings Zapolsky held with high-level South African politicians recently. He also posted on LinkedIn that he met with President Cyril Ramaphosa.

"Today I had the honour to meet with President Cyril Ramaphosa in Pretoria to discuss Amazon's investments in South Africa," Zapolsky said.

The pair also discussed the country's ambition to be a leading digital investment destination, which Ramaphosa alluded to in a speech at the recent Google Cloud Summit in Johannesburg.

"South Africa is Africa's digital investment powerhouse and Africa's largest cloud market," Ramaphosa said.

"Beyond housing approximately 70% of Africa's hyperscale data centre capacity, South Africa is also a major investment hub for tech start-ups."

Per Zapolsky, the meeting with Ramaphosa on Tuesday went deeper into how Amazon is helping advance South Africa's digital economy goals.

"Amazon has invested more than R54 billion in South Africa over the last decade, spanning cloud infrastructure, e-commerce, logistics, renewable energy, and skills development," he said.
