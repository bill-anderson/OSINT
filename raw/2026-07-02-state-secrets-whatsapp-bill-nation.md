Public officers will be prohibited from using personal email accounts or social media platforms such as WhatsApp to share official government documents if a new Bill before Parliament becomes law.

The proposed legislation also seeks to punish public officers who disclose or misuse classified information obtained through their employment, publish sensitive government information or use unofficial email addresses and telephone numbers to transmit or circulate classified documents.

The Access to Information (Amendment) Bill, sponsored by Deputy Majority Leader Owen Baya, seeks to establish a legal framework for the protection and classification of information held by State agencies.

Offenders will face a fine of up to Sh1 million, a jail term of up to six months, or both.

Appearing before the National Assembly's Committee on Communication, Information and Innovation, Mr Baya said the Bill proposes four categories of classified information: top secret, secret, confidential and restricted.

Under the proposal, information classified as top secret would include any material whose unauthorised disclosure could cause exceptionally grave damage to Kenya's national security, public safety or national interests.

Mr Baya told the committee, chaired by Dagoretti South MP John Kiarie, that although the Access to Information Act guarantees the right to information under Article 35 of the Constitution, it offers limited guidance on how State agencies should classify and safeguard information whose disclosure could prejudice national security or the country's interests.

“This Bill strikes an appropriate balance between the constitutional right of access to information and the need to protect information whose disclosure may undermine national security or the interests of Kenya .It introduces a structured legal framework to ensure that sensitive State information is protected while preserving transparency and accountability,” Mr Baya said.

The Bill further requires all classified information to be properly marked and entered into a register of classified information.

According to Mr Baya, the provision will promote accountability and proper records management within public institutions while ensuring that classification is not arbitrary but follows an established and reviewable process.

The Kilifi North MP said he was motivated to draft the Bill following what he described as an emerging trend in which current and former public servants share sensitive government documents through social media platforms.

He said some current and former public officers have been releasing privileged State information through social media, television and radio interviews despite having sworn an oath of secrecy.

“They obtained these documents because of the privileged positions they held in government, yet they continue to release them after leaving office,” he said.

“We have a situation where people use private emails, WhatsApp or X to transmit official government business and information. While in office, some stole sensitive documents while others continued receiving classified information through their personal email accounts.,” he said.

Mr Baya said some former public officers retain critical government documents and later use the information against the State.

He warned that advances in artificial intelligence have increased the risk of sensitive government information being hacked and misused.

He argued that personal email accounts and social media platforms such as WhatsApp are particularly vulnerable to cyberattacks, underscoring the need to strengthen the protection of government information.

According to Mr Baya, the proposed framework aligns public sector information management with internationally recognised security practices by providing clear levels of classification.

“A key safeguard in the Bill is the requirement that classified information be reviewed every 10 years, with reports submitted to the Commission on Administrative Justice. The Bill further allows declassification or downgrading where information no longer meets the threshold for protection. Importantly, before declassification, authorities must consider whether the public interest outweighs any potential harm to national security, thereby balancing transparency with legitimate security concerns ,” he said

He added that classified information would automatically be declassified after 30 years unless there are legal grounds to retain its protected status.

The provision, he said, promotes openness and historical accountability.

Mr Kiarie said the proposed amendments are timely given the increasing number of attempts to hack government data systems.

“This committee is working very hard to ensure that the Information and Communication Technology Authority (ICTA) ensures that every government official transacts business using .go.ke domains .We will then require officers to use those domains for all official government business,” Mr Kiarie said.

He said cyberattacks targeting government systems are becoming increasingly frequent and sophisticated, adding that the problem has been compounded by a growing number of highly trained but unemployed ICT professionals.

“If we don't get these highly trained young people jobs, they will certainly go to the dark web to earn a living. I assure you that this committee will examine this Bill and strengthen it because we cannot afford to leave the classification of sensitive government information unanchored in law,” he said.

Mr Kiarie supported Mr Baya's call for the speedy passage of the Bill after the Kilifi North legislator proposed that the committee adopt and champion the legislation to fast-track its enactment.
