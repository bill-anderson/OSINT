Cybersecurity has become a defining issue for the financial sector as institutions increasingly rely on digital platforms to deliver services, store customer data, and process transactions.

At its core, cybersecurity in finance involves safeguarding digital assets, sensitive customer information, and critical business operations from cyberattacks.

Because banks, insurers, fintechs, and mobile money operators handle large volumes of confidential data and money flows, the sector remains one of the most attractive targets for cybercriminals.

Experts warn that this growing exposure demands stronger security frameworks, advanced technologies, and continuous monitoring systems capable of detecting, preventing, and responding to cyber threats in real time.

Without these measures, financial institutions risk not only financial losses but also reputational damage and erosion of public trust.

Speaking after Milima Security, a Ugandan cybersecurity firm, secured ISO/IEC 27001 certification, its Chief Executive Officer and Founder, Emmanuel Chagara, pointed to skills shortages as one of the biggest challenges facing the country’s cybersecurity ecosystem.

“There is still a shortage of skilled labour, especially when it comes to dealing with aggressive and sophisticated cyber threats,” Chagara said.

“These gaps continue to exist, but notwithstanding that, there has been notable progress over the last five to six years.”

He noted that the COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital solutions across many sectors, including finance.

While this rapid digitisation expanded access to services and improved efficiency, it also opened new avenues for cyberattacks.

“The pandemic pushed many institutions to go digital. That led to a rise in cyberattacks, but it also created growth and awareness around cybersecurity,” Chagara added.

Globally, the scale of cybercrime is escalating at an alarming rate. In 2023, worldwide damages linked to cybercrime were estimated at USD 8 trillion, with projections indicating losses could reach USD 10.5 trillion annually by 2025.

This trend underscores the increasing vulnerability of financial systems, particularly in developing economies where digital adoption is growing faster than cybersecurity preparedness.

Uganda is not immune to these threats. According to the Uganda Police Crime Report 2024, a total of 446 cybercrime cases were processed during the year, resulting in losses amounting to Shs 72.1 billion (about USD 20 million).

Although this represented a slight decline from the 523 cases reported in 2023, the overall threat remains significant. Authorities are still grappling with a backlog of 887 unresolved cybercrime cases, highlighting the persistent and evolving nature of cyber risks.

These threats are especially worrying for users at the “last mile” of financial inclusion, many of whom rely on mobile money and digital platforms but lack adequate awareness or protection against cyber fraud and digital exploitation.

IT expert Richard Irumba told this publication that recognising the strategic importance of cybersecurity, the Ugandan government has adopted a more collaborative approach to strengthening the country’s financial ecosystem.

“The government is focusing on shared infrastructure, governance, and accountability to ensure a coordinated response to cyber threats across the financial sector,” he said. According to Irumba, this approach is crucial for building resilience and supporting sustainable growth in digital financial services.

Chagara also emphasised that Milima Security’s ISO/IEC 27001 certification—an internationally recognised standard for Information Security Management Systems positions the company to support financial institutions more effectively.

“This certification enables us to help strengthen the cybersecurity capabilities of institutions by aligning them with global best practices,” he said.

The financial cost of cyber incidents further illustrates the urgency of action. Data from Statista shows that the average global cost of a data breach in the financial sector rose to USD 6.08 million this year, up from USD 5.90 million in the previous year.

This sharp increase reflects not only the financial impact of breaches but also the growing sophistication of cyberattacks targeting financial institutions.

Experts agree that as cyber threats continue to evolve, security practices must evolve just as rapidly. Financial institutions are being urged to invest in skills development, adopt international standards, and embed cybersecurity into their core operations.

In an increasingly digital economy, robust cybersecurity is no longer optional it is essential for the stability, growth, and credibility of the financial sector.
