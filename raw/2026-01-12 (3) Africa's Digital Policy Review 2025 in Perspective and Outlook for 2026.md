As we look back on 2025, across the continent, ambitions are increasingly being translated into concrete regulatory and institutional reforms that support the emergence of a continental digital market. Amid economic recalibration and rapid technological change, policymakers across the continent played a decisive role in shaping how digital tools drive growth, inclusion, and accountable governance.

2025 also marked a growing momentum for pan-African digital cooperation, as the [African Union’s Digital Transformation Strategy (2020–2030)](https://au.int/sites/default/files/documents/38507-doc-dts-english.pdf) reached its midpoint. While progress toward a digital single market remained uneven due to infrastructure gaps, capacity constraints, and coordination challenges, the policy direction is increasingly clear that governments are investing in foundational systems, common standards, and governance frameworks intended to support an integrated African digital economy.

In this article, we share policy shifts that defined 2025 and outline what they signal for 2026.

### Fintech

Fintech policy in 2025 advanced significantly across Africa. Central banks in Kenya, Nigeria, Ghana, and South Africa enhanced licensing regimes, improved digital banking frameworks, and expanded regulatory sandboxes to support controlled experimentation and market entry, embedding fintech into broader national economic goals. The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) updated documentation requirements for [PAPSS](https://www.icirnigeria.org/afcfta-cbn-revises-requirements-for-pan-african-transactions/) transactions to simplify cross-border payments and urged commercial banks to adopt the platform to deepen trade and financial inclusion. PAPSS also expanded continent-wide, now connecting [16 countries](https://african.business/2025/06/partner-content/papss-afreximbank-led-payment-initiative-expands-to-16-countries) and over 150 banks in support of the African Continental Free Trade Area’s payment ecosystem. These developments signal a continued push from fragmented fintech rules toward inclusive digital financial systems across the continent.

### AI

A defining feature was the rapid maturation of AI policy across Africa, even as overall adoption remains limited. As of July 2025, only 16 African countries have launched national AI strategies, underscoring both the progress made and the scale of work still ahead. Countries including Côte d’Ivoire, [Nigeria,](https://ncair.nitda.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/National-Artificial-Intelligence-Strategy-19092025.pdf) [Kenya](https://ict.go.ke/sites/default/files/2025-03/Kenya%20AI%20Strategy%202025%20-%202030.pdf), Namibia, [Egypt](https://ai.gov.eg/SynchedFiles/en/Resources/AIstrategy%20English%2016-1-2025-1.pdf) and [Lesotho](https://www.gov.ls/download/draft-artificial-intelligence-policy_1-0/) developed or advanced national AI strategies, reflecting both opportunity and urgency. At the continental level, the Africa Declaration on Artificial Intelligence and the AU Continental AI Strategy signalled growing alignment around shared principles. In 2026, attention is expected to shift from strategy documents to implementation, including standards, sector-specific rules, and clearer accountability mechanisms as AI becomes embedded in public and commercial services.

### Startup and Innovation

Startup and innovation policy continued to evolve in 2025, though implementation remained uneven across countries. Senegal, Ethiopia, Tunisia, Nigeria, and Côte d’Ivoire progressed with operationalising their Startup Acts, while South Africa, Ghana, Zambia and Kenya remained largely in consultative or drafting phases of their Startup Acts. The overall reform direction points toward reducing regulatory friction, formalising incentives, and strengthening institutional support for innovation ecosystems. Looking ahead to 2026, a stronger emphasis is expected on implementation and cross-border coherence, particularly as countries seek to align startup regulation with AfCFTA digital trade objectives. We also anticipate increased use of blended-finance instruments to crowd in private capital for early-stage ventures and SMEs.

### Digital Trade

One of the most consequential policy developments of 2025 was progress on the AfCFTA Digital Trade Protocol, particularly the adoption of [eight technical annexes](https://au.int/sites/default/files/treaties/44963-ax-ENG_Circulation_Digital_Trade_Compiled_Certified_Legal_Instruments_38thAssembly_Feb_2025_27_May2025.pdf) that moved the framework closer to operational readiness. However, digital trade across Africa remains constrained by fragmented data governance, limited interoperability, and weak mutual recognition of digital documents and signatures. In 2026, we expect that the focus will shift from negotiation to domestication and enforcement. Countries are expected to align national laws with the Protocol and explore Mutual Recognition Agreements (MRAs) that allow digital invoices and e-signatures issued in one jurisdiction to be legally recognised in another. Equally transformative will be the scaling of the Pan-African Payment and Settlement System (PAPSS) across national switches, with the potential to make cross-border digital payments a practical reality for SMEs and informal traders.

### Data Protection, Privacy, and Cross-Border Data Flows

[46 of Africa’s 54 countries](https://iapp.org/news/a/evaluating-data-privacy-across-africa-toward-a-unified-gdpr-inspired-framework) have data protection laws. The Gambia passed its Personal Data Protection and Privacy Bill, Botswana brought its Data Protection Act (2024) into force, and Sierra Leone advanced reviews of its draft data protection legislation. Nigeria sharpened enforcement with the issuance of [GAID](https://ndpc.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/NDP-ACT-GAID-2025-MARCH-20TH.pdf) 2025. Ghana reopened data protection and cybersecurity reforms for public consultation, signalling renewed regulatory momentum. In Kenya, the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner’s [2025–2029 Strategic Plan](https://www.odpc.go.ke/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/ODPC-Strategic-Plan-2025-2029.pdf) prioritised guidance on cross-border data transfers and regulatory codes. Ghana inaugurated a new governing board for its Data Protection. Egypt adopted an Open Data Policy pending a comprehensive Data Governance Law, and Rwanda issued national data governance and classification standards to guide public-sector data use. These national efforts reflect a broader continental push to operationalise the [African Union Data Policy Framework](https://au.int/sites/default/files/documents/42078-doc-DATA-POLICY-FRAMEWORKS-2024-ENG-V2.pdf) (AUDPF) and enable secure, interoperable digital ecosystems. In 2026, we expect to see stronger data protection enforcement, alongside a heightened focus on cross-border data transfer frameworks aligned with AfCFTA objectives and the AU Data Policy Framework.

### Looking Ahead

With the AU Digital Transformation Strategy now at its midpoint, the next phase of reform will focus mainly on execution. 2026 will test the continent’s ability to operationalise continental frameworks in areas such as digital identity, payments, cross-border data flows, and digital trade. Success will depend on institutional coordination and a sustained commitment to building digital systems that are trusted, resilient, and accessible.

We will continue to closely track how countries are translating these commitments into practice, identifying emerging gaps, and keeping our readers informed of what is working, where progress is stalling, and what it means for the continent.
