(Originally published 11 March 2024; updated 18 November 2024. First appeared in Issue 2 of Southern Africa Digital Rights.)

Between June and July 2021, Eswatini experienced political upheaval, largely stemming from governance issues worsened by COVID-19. In Eswatini, two pivotal legislative measures impact internet governance: the Computer Crime and Cyber Crime Act of 2022 and the Data Protection Act of 2022. Enacted in 2022 following a consultative phase in which civil society organizations contributed submissions, concerns loom over potential implications, particularly a perceived threat to press freedom and the possible curtailment of social media criticism of the government.

The Eswatini Communications Commission is the regulatory authority overseeing the communication sector; its mandate is delineated by the Swaziland Communications Commission Act No. 10 of 2013. During the June-July unrest, the government directed service providers - the state-owned Eswatini Post and Telecommunications Corporation, plus private entities MTN Eswatini and Eswatini Mobile - to suspend internet services. The International Commission of Jurists wrote to the CEO of MTN South Africa urging resumption of services. Earlier, in October 2021, during protests, the government restricted access to various social media platforms.

Eswatini legislation on privacy and data protection: The Data Protection Act 2022 is a comprehensive piece of legislation providing for the collection, processing, and disclosure of personal data. The Eswatini Communications Commission is charged with regulating adherence and has powers to sanction data controllers. Section 6(3)(b) allows the commission to impose administrative fines of up to five million Emalangeni (about USD 268,000) for non-compliance by a public or private party.

The Act governs the handling of personal information (particularly in electronic formats) and cross-border movement of data. Section 9(2) emphasizes the necessity of explicit consent. Some sections, notably Section 36, raise concerns regarding potential encroachment on human rights (reasons provided for non-action on filed complaints). Section 44 addresses unsolicited electronic communications, granting citizens the right to halt direct marketing. The Act prohibits data collection from children without permission. Section 17(1) requires notification of security breaches: where there are reasonable grounds to believe personal information has been accessed or acquired by an unauthorised person, the data controller must notify the commission and the data subject.

The Computer Crime and Cyber Crime Act 2022 complements the Data Protection Act by criminalising actions intended to manipulate, modify, or destroy data. Section 6 addresses illegal data interference (a fine on conviction).
