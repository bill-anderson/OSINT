# Chad, Mali, Senegal pursue birth registration reforms to close gaps

Efforts by Chad, Mali and Senegal to reform and digitize their birth registration systems are said to be paying off, with an increasing number of undocumented children able to obtain a legal identity. These countries presented their birth registration stories during the ID4Africa 2025 AGM.

## Health sector integration advances inclusive civil registration in Chad

Speaking for Chad, the Coordinator of Cooperation and Civil Registration at the National Agency for Secure Titles (ANATS), Brahim Ali Seid, said the country has registered remarkable progress in civil and birth registration thanks to legal reforms, digitization, cross-sector collaboration (including integration with the health sector), and community engagement.

He said a robust legal framework supports universal, free, and mandatory birth registration; civil registration is now integrated across sectors such as health facilities, schools, and refugee camps.

"We inherited a colonial civil registration system governed by colonial laws. But progress has been made. Starting from the 1990s, we adopted new legal texts that allowed our civil registry system to evolve. Between 1990 and 2010, and later in 2020, new legislation was introduced, including a key ordinance that modernized the system and laid the groundwork for digitalization," Seid explained.

Since the integration of the civil registration system with health facilities in 2023, more than 120,000 children have been registered through hospital-based registry offices, with community health workers acting as intermediaries to record births in rural areas — ensuring coverage of about 70 percent of Chad's rural population.

Registration campaigns in schools have registered more than 180,000 pupils and students without birth certificates since 2023. "Over 80 percent of children in primary and secondary schools lack birth certificates. As part of a national education acceleration plan, these students are allowed to enroll without documents, but we've run regularization campaigns since 2023 to register them," Seid disclosed.

"We've also extended services to nomadic populations, deploying mobile phones with our application to enroll nomadic children and conduct biometric registration missions for older individuals."

"Our system connects civil registry offices, hospitals, vaccination centers, municipalities, mobile units, refugee camps, and schools. All data is transferred via SMS to our national agency, which validates the information and sends it back for issuance."

The Chadian government plans to increase the number of digitized hospital-based civil registration points from the current 97 to 600 in the next few years, as well as continue registration in schools, aiming for zero unregistered births by 2030. A World Bank–sponsored project sought to identify the factors hampering civil registration in Chad.

## Mali

Mali's birth-registration rate stands at 89.5 percent. Dr Abdoulaye Alkadi, director of Civil Status at the National Directorate of Civil Status, attributed progress to civil-registration infrastructure (almost every local council has a registration centre), major legal and institutional reforms including creating the National Agency for the Security of Civil Status Documents (ANSAEC) in 2025, free birth and death declarations and certificate issuance, and strong community engagement. Mali has 747 functional primary civil-registration centres (of 825 councils), 292 secondary centres and 8,000 declaration points (4,000 in health facilities, 4,000 in villages).

Future plans include expanding digitalisation of all civil-registration processes, digitising all civil-status records, strengthening mobile campaigns, and maintaining free access toward SDG 16.9. Death registration remains low at around 20 percent. In February 2025 Mali launched a platform for citizens abroad to access administrative and identity documents.

## Senegal

Senegal is awaiting legislation on digitizing civil registration (as of 2025-06-03).
