Digital economy’s systems adoption attracts rapid growth in key sectors

By Francis Kajubi , The Guardian Published at 01:03 PM Apr 21 2026 Photo: File President Dr Samia Suluhu Hassan inaugurated a transformative digital infrastructure expansion comprising 758 communication towers and new fiber optic connections across 85 districts of Tanzania in Dodoma capital recently, marking a significant milesto

The government-backed digital financial systems, applications and portals are impacting key economic sectors performance and growth at a steady pace.

Tanzania’s digital economy drive is bearing fruits across key economic sectors like financial services, aviation, transportation, marine, trade and businesses and agriculture where individuals and companies cut operational costs and time along the production line.

Within district councils, the ten percent special groups’ loans have since the past two year processed and disbursed via the digital state-run WEZESHA system.

Deputy Minister of State in the Prime Minister's Office - Regional Administration and Local Government, Dr Festo Dugange said a total of 118.03bn/- has been disbursed via the system to 8,165 youth groups comprising 44,754 members nationwide from July 2024 to February 2026 to start and expand small businesses.

He said the system has during the financial year 2024/25 attracted a remarkable milestone in digital financing as from July 2024 to December 2025 a total of 174 district councils were authorized to issue 253bn/- in loans to special groups.

Dugange asserted that of the approved amount, 230.66bn/- was successfully dished out via the system, an equivalent of 91 percent of the total authorized amount.

"The district councils managed to collect 100.27bn/- in loan repayments via WEZESHA system, equivalent to 82 percent of the disbursed loans during the period under review," he said.

According to him, this arrangement had prior started with a pilot disbursement in 10 district councils with allocation of 34.12bn/- of which 3.69bn/- was extended to special groups through banks integrated with the system with repayments amounting to 813mn/-, equipment to 22 percent of the total dished money.

Dugange further explained that the Ministry has also built the capacity of 736 district council loan managers on the management of the WEZESHA system, and continues to make improvements to it to increase the efficiency and productivity of loan disbursements to citizens.

The government ensures that the 10 percent loans reach special groups in accordance with the Local Government Finances Act, Cap 290, as well as the 2024 Regulations and Guidelines for the Issuance and Management of Loans to Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities.

Regulations 31, 32 and 36 of the Management of Loans to Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities 2024, direct and establish a procedure for the assessment of the disbursement and management of loans dished every year at district councils, and region levels.

In last Thursday's edition this journalist had exposed how the digital economy drive is impacting people's income growth alongside cutting costs in government expenditures and amplifying revenue collections, from views by individuals and top public officials.

Towards unlocking the digital economy opportunities the government has already allocated 200bn/- in funding startups, innovations and MSMES businesses, the money which will be largely accessed via the system and other financial channels.

Sauda Msemo, Bank of Tanzania's (BoT) Deputy Governor for Financial Stability and Deepening said that in line with the government’s digital economy drive, the central bank is collaborating with the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) to develop a special digital transactions system for Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs) engaged in cross-border trade within the East African Community (EAC) region.

Msemo said that digital payments for cross-border trade are essential for enhancing payment certainty and security at a time when Tanzania envisions the realization of a fully flagged digital economy stipulated in the National Development Vision 2050 (DIRA 2050).\

"The targeted borders in this development are Namanga, Tunduma, Mutukula, Sirali, and Rusumo. Once integrated, the system could enable MSMEs from EAC member states to conduct instant transactions," said Msemo.

Mwajuma Hamza, Chief Executive Officer Tanzania Women Chamber of Commerce (TWCC) was of the view that MSMEs operating at border posts face challenges that sometimes scare them away from utilizing digital payment systems.

According to her, most MSMEs at border posts don't engage in digital payments due to fears over transaction authenticity, with concerns that confirmation messages can be falsified.

"There are MSMEs who fear that payment notifications might be manipulated as there are cases of fraudulent transactions as a result, many prefer cash because it provides immediate assurance," said Hamza.

But realities on the ground seem to be the opposite of Hamza's fraudulent fears.

The Tanzania Communications Regulatory Authority (TCRA) reports a 24 percent decrease in mobile money fraudulent attempts during the fourth quarter ending December 2025.

TCRA notes that this decline comes at a critical moment for the nation's digital economy which continues to expand rapidly.

"In total, the mobile money ecosystem processed 43.4trn/- during the fourth quarter of 2025, the amount that highlights the sector's role as the primary engine of the national payment system," reads the TCRA quarterly report in part.

On the other hand, the latest East Africa Banking Industry Outlook 2026 Report by Deloitte states that Tanzania's financial institutions are leading in the region with the development of innovations that are fast and credible among digital financial solutions.

According to the report, Tanzanian commercial banks are increasingly leveraging transactional and behavioral data from mobile money to refine credit evaluations, particularly for MSMEs.

Fatma Kange, GS1 Tanzania Chief Executive Officer said that in a move to support growth, competitiveness and trust for MSMEs products in the domestic and cross-border trade, the firm looks forward to rollout 2D barcode technology early 2027 targeting at facilitating this group to fully capitalize on the digital economy opportunities.

According to her, the 2D barcodes will allow entrepreneurs to include more product information, enhance traceability and meet cross-border digital trade standards.

"Technology is not only about digital upgrades but a gateway for MSMEs to integrate into formal supply chains and expand beyond the country's borders," said Kange.

She asserted that adoption of technologies such as 2D can play a significant role in enabling local entrepreneurs to scale up, compete effectively and tap into emerging opportunities within the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) and the global digital economy.

Isidor Nkindi, Director of Companies and Business Licensing at the Business Registration and Licensing Agency (BRELA) said the Agency has initiated the BRELA Online Services (BOS) aimed at achieving fully digital economy operations that simply licensing procedures save time and cut costs.

According to him, BOS came into effect on April 08, 2026 enabling online services like registration of new business names and industrial licenses.

"The systems complement the existing Online Registration System (ORS) among other functions. It speeds up companies’ license termination process which previously took up to 100 days to complete," said Nkindi.

The National Economic Empowerment Council (NEEC) leverages Tanzania's digital economy to foster inclusive growth by aligning with the 2024/34 Digital Economy Strategy Framework.

NEEC through the National Policy for Economic Empowerment of Citizens integrates technology to support growth of Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs), enhance digital literacy, and improve financial inclusion, aiming for $1.1billion in GDP growth by 2034 through digitizing agriculture, trade, financial and other key economic activities.

The Tanzania Business Report 2024/25 states that the country's digital economy is experiencing rapid growth driven by the Information Communication Technology (ICT) sector projected to grow by 13.5 percent in 2026.

It notes that the current ICT contribution to GDP is approximately 1.5–2 percent directly and 7 percent broadly.

Through digital infrastructure development like the Tanzania Technology Stack, the expansion of connectivity to underserved areas, and the development of digital identity and interoperable platforms, Tanzania has laid a solid foundation for a digital society aligned with its long-term DIRA 2050 ambitions.

The report notes that as this digital ecosystem continues to evolve, the role of electronic data as a strategic national asset has become increasingly central.

In March this year, an agreement to validate and streamline the ten-year Tanzania Electronic Data Governance Strategy (TZeDGS) was signed between the government and the United Nations Economic Commission for Africa (ECA), making a decisive shift toward digital operational readiness.

With this strategy, the private sector is a key player towards the realization of the digital economy drive favoured by the establishment of startups, mobile money and other online businesses.

The Tanzania Startup Association's (TSA) Startup Ecosystem Status Report 2024 findings reveal significant progress: the number of active startups rose by 24 percent, reaching 1,041 ventures.

The report asserts that these startups have become major contributors to employment, creating over 138,453 jobs — a 23 percent increase compared to the preceding year, a remarkable milestone in the digital economy drive.

It states that investment into the ecosystem significantly surged with Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) doubling, reaching $53 million while Domestic Direct Investment (DDI) grew by 85.5 percent totaling $43.4 million.

"FinTech emerged as the leading sector, attracting $41.4 million — accounting for 78.3 percent of the total FDI — with AgriTech also maintaining a strong presence," reads the report in part.

The TSA report also points to positive developments in gender inclusivity, with the proportion of female-led startups increasing to 16 percent.

"Tanzanian startups continue to align their efforts with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly SDG 8: Decent Work and Economic Growth," states the report.

The digital economy systems impacts are further evident in the aviation, marine and transportation sectors.

At the Tanzania Ports Authority (TPA) the digital economy transformation is witnessed through the connection of all port stations using fiber optic cables via a Multiprotocol Label Switching Virtual Private Network (MPLS VPN), enabling instant information transfer, real-time cargo tracking, online payment systems, and enhanced security.

The Tanzania Civil Aviation Authority (TCAA) saves costs through digital transformation by shifting from manual processes to automated high-tech systems.

TCAA's key digital economy initiatives include implementing a 9bn/- Aeronautical Information Management (AIM) system, digitizing flight planning, updating radio systems with 31.5bn/-, and creating a digital information-sharing partnership with the Immigration Department.

On the other hand, the Tanzania Airports Authority (TAA) has implemented next-generation networks and Airport Management Information Systems (AMIS) to manage operations, allowing for automated and remote support for regional airports.

To this end, E-ticketing has transformed Tanzania's bus transport activities by replacing manual ticketing with digital solutions like Lands Transport Regulatory Authority’s (LATRA) Safari Tiketi and platforms such as Otapp.

E-ticketing has increased government tax revenue collections, standardized fares, reduced fraud, and improved revenue visibility for owners, and offered passengers convenient, cashless booking via mobile money.

In the agriculture sector where 65 percent of the country’s population is employed, the digital systems are amplifying the economy through the Tanzania Mercantile Exchange (TMX) and the Warehouse Receipt System which are designed purposely to protect both farmers and investors by ensuring transparency in competitive pricing and trade management.

Irene Mlola, Director Cereals and Other Produce Regulatory Authority (COPRA) said that during the current fiscal year as of early April 2026 the government has collected over 1trn/- through sales conducted via digital systems.

“This is a milestone that demonstrates the success of digital weighing scales and digital auctions of cash crops in the national agricultural trade,” she said.

The shared experience and testaments from individuals, experts from the private sector and public officials on the digitalization impacts on Tanzania's economy, offers a bright way forward that government-backed digital systems such as WEZESHA, TANCIS, TIPS, TMX, and NeST to mention a few, are ready leading to the realization of the $1trn/- GDP economy come 2050 as stipulated in the long-term National Development Vision Plan (DIRA 2050).

On this ground, President Dr Samia Suluhu Hassan recently said: “Digital economy is a critical engine for Tanzania's modernization, aiming for inclusive growth where no Tanzanian should be left behind;

Towards making the digital economy a reality, supporting youth-led tech startups and enhancing cyber security are essential to fostering trust in digital financial services.”
