SpaceX’s satellite Internet service Starlink has launched in the West African country of the Ivory Coast (Côte d’Ivoire), its 27th market on the continent.

Starlink is offering two uncapped Internet packages for home users, both of which can be accessed with its Mini or Standard dish.

The Mini dish is priced at 148,148 West African francs, working out to about R4,260 at the time of publication. The Standard dish costs 247,466 francs, or R7,110.

For speeds up to 100Mbps, users can get the francs 28,746 (R830) Residential 100Mbps plan. Those who want faster, best-effort speeds can take up the Residential for 40,244 francs (R1,160).

The launch in the Ivory Coast means that Starlink Internet is officially available in half the African countries recognised as sovereign states by the United Nations.

According to Starlink’s coverage availability map, it plans to roll out in another 18 UN-recognised African countries, as well as Western Sahara, before the end of 2026.

Starlink has had a challenging time dealing with many African states’ local business ownership policies and concerns over data sovereignty.

Launching as a direct-to-consumer product in South Africa will require the company to obtain telecoms licences only available to firms with 30% historically-disadvantaged group (HDG) ownership.

However, the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) plans to change the requirement to 30% black ownership for new licensees.

Another problem is that ICASA has not made new electronic communications network services licences available in 17 years.

Starlink would therefore have to buy a licence from an existing holder and apply for transfer with ICASA, which would also require meeting the 30% HDG criterion.

Unlike Amazon Leo, which plans to launch its service through Herotel in 2027, Starlink wants to deal directly with customers to keep subscription costs lower and maintain control over user experience.

## South Africa a lone stepchild in Sub-Saharan Africa

Starlink dish being installed in Eswatini.

Aside from South Africa, there are seven African countries where Starlink has no planned launch date, all of which are in Northern Africa.

Five of these countries are part of the Arab Maghreb Union, established to maintain close diplomatic relations on issues like trade and culture.

Several of these countries have state-owned telecoms companies with legislated monopolies on communications infrastructure.

South Africa stands out among Sub-Saharan African countries, as it is the only nation in this region with an “Unknown” estimated launch year.

All but one of South Africa’s six neighbours have allowed the service to launch, giving people in remote areas access to fast and uncapped Internet on packages starting under R500 per month.

One country in the region where Starlink is also facing significant licensing challenges is Namibia, where the Communications Regulatory Authority of Namibia (CRAN) has rejected Starlink’s licence application.

It also dismissed an appeal by the company of the initial dismissal, despite 624 reconsideration requests from members of the public.

CRAN requires telecoms licensees to be 51% owned by Namibian citizens. However, unlike in South Africa, these citizens can be of any race, gender, age, or historical background.
