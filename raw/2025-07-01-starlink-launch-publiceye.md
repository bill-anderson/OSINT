# Starlink blasts off in Lesotho

*Public Eye, 1 July 2025. Staff Reporter. Launches amid fanfare meeting lingering licence doubts.*

Elon Musk's satellite internet service, Starlink, officially launched in Maseru on Monday, marking its entry after months of opposition from local service providers and citizen skepticism. Prime Minister Ntsokoane Matekane and his deputy were abroad; Education Minister (acting PM) Professor Ntoi Rapapa attended.

Starlink's arrival followed six months of widespread public distrust — many Basotho feared the service could enable foreign surveillance and "snoop on the nation." T-Connect Lesotho CEO Phelane Phomane, whose company holds exclusive reseller rights, addressed these fears and pledged 60,000 jobs (10,000 direct). He unveiled plans for AI-powered data centres using Starlink across Lesotho, South Africa and Botswana, claiming the Lesotho facility "will be the most unique data centre in the world."

Operating under a 10-year licence, Starlink faced opposition partly over allegations the government fast-tracked the licence to appease the US after President Trump imposed steep 50% tariffs on US-Lesotho trade (still in place). Phomane detailed a disruptive pricing model — access sold by time at M10 per session rather than by data volume — with uncapped data during the access period and hotspots in all 10 districts. T-Connect is a consortium of local companies chaired by Nolo Letele, former MultiChoice Chairman and Group CEO.

Critics lambasted Starlink for being a wholly foreign entity without a physical address in Lesotho — a requirement they say Vodacom Lesotho and Econet Telecom Lesotho must meet. Activist Kananelo Boloetse challenged why Starlink was seemingly exempt: "Regulatory fairness and predictability are the bedrock of trust in public institutions."
