Burkina Faso's government has inaugurated two new mini data centers to strengthen the country's digital infrastructure and enhance data sovereignty.

In a push to strengthen the country's digital infrastructure as well as enhance its data sovereignty, Burkina Faso's government has inaugurated two new mini data centers.

In a Facebook post, Burkina Faso's Ministry of Digital Transition, Posts and Electronic Communications said the data centers offer a combined storage capacity of around 3,000 terabytes, a tenfold increase from existing systems.

The data centers will be able to support more than 7,000 connected virtual machines, with each ministry able to host between 100 and 300 virtual servers for deploying its digital platforms.

At an estimated cost of 16 billion CFA francs (US$28.9 million), the project forms part of Burkina Faso's digital sovereignty strategy, which prioritizes hosting national data within the country.

"This project is an intermediate step towards the construction of a national Big Data Center, intended to repatriate all digital data from the public administration and the private sector currently hosted outside the country," the Ministry of Digital Transition, Posts and Telecommunications said in a statement.

The Burkinabé government said the data centers offer a combined storage capacity of around 3,000 terabytes, a tenfold increase from existing systems.

The new data centers are among Burkina Faso's 12 flagship digital transformation projects.

They support the "zero external data" initiative, which seeks to keep all sensitive national information hosted and managed within the country.

The move underscores the government's drive to secure data, improve resilience and reduce dependence on foreign hosting.

According to Burkinabé authorities, the data centers are expected to generate savings of approximately 30 billion CFA francs ($54 million) over five years, largely by reducing expenses linked to hosting government platforms abroad.

## Burkina Faso's digital transformation

The inauguration of the two mini data centers is the latest example of the West African country working to advance its digital economy.

In December 2025, telecommunications operator Telecel Faso begun deploying 300 telecom sites – some of them towers, some just antennas – across Burkina Faso as part of the government's project to provide coverage to 750 underserved areas.

In September 2025, the Burkinabé government signed a memorandum of understanding with Presight International Services, a subsidiary of the United Arab Emirates-based G42 Group, aimed at strengthening Burkina Faso's digital economy.

In August 2025, Burkina Faso's communications regulator, ARCEP, and Ghana's National Communications Authority (NCA) held bilateral meetings to address concerns about cross-border signal interference.

In July 2024, pan-African operator Orange's Burkinabé unit chose Vox Solutions as the international application-to-person (A2P) short message service (SMS) and voice gateway for traffic into its network.

In November 2023, the country's military government began working on a law related to cybersecurity.
