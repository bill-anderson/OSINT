# Liberia: LTA Unveils Sweeping Telecom Reforms, Revokes Star Cell License After Six Years of Inactivity

By Gerald C. Koinyeneh — FrontPage Africa, July 17, 2026

Monrovia — The Liberia Telecommunications Authority (LTA) is embarking on what could be its most significant regulatory overhaul in nearly two decades, introducing a series of reforms aimed at expanding telecommunications access, strengthening consumer protection, and tightening oversight of service providers. In an exclusive interview with FrontPage Africa, LTA Board Chairman Clarence Massaquoi outlined an agenda that includes reforms to the licensing regime, the development of satellite and infrastructure regulations, an IMEI-based phone tracking system, and the revocation of Star Cell's operating license.

"The strength and relevance of a regulator depend on two things: the regulations you put in place and your ability to enforce those regulations," Massaquoi said.

New licensing regime targets rural connectivity. Since the passage of the Telecommunications Act in 2007, the LTA has operated under a universal licensing model allowing operators to provide both infrastructure and services under a single license. The Authority now wants to create a clear distinction between infrastructure providers and service providers to encourage investment. The reforms would provide special incentives for investors willing to deploy services in rural and hard-to-reach, commercially non-viable areas.

Infrastructure regulations to promote competition. The LTA is developing a dedicated infrastructure regulation governing tower ownership, network sharing, and co-location arrangements. Massaquoi said the absence of clear rules has resulted in duplication, with competing companies constructing separate towers in the same communities. The new policy would encourage infrastructure sharing while safeguarding services.

Satellite regulations in preparation. With the entry of Starlink and the anticipated arrival of other satellite internet providers, the LTA is drafting Liberia's first comprehensive satellite communications regulations. "Starlink is here today, but more satellite operators will come tomorrow," Massaquoi said.

Consumer protection. Massaquoi pointed to a recent LTA decision to fine Orange Liberia four million Liberian dollars over a breach of customer privacy involving the wrongful reassignment of a subscriber's SIM card. The LTA is also consulting stakeholders on an IMEI regulation designed to combat phone theft, counterfeit devices, and customs evasion, enabling stolen phones to be blocked across networks. Another initiative involves relocating Liberia's country internet domain (ccTLD) infrastructure from private management abroad to local control; the LTA is in the final stages of transferring management of the top-level domain to Liberia.

Star Cell license revoked. The LTA has revoked the operating license of Star Cell, which received a telecommunications license in March 2020 but failed to commence operations. "After six years, Starcell has not become operational," Massaquoi said. He warned that operators cannot continue holding valuable spectrum without providing services, and acknowledged that a lack of modern spectrum monitoring equipment remains a challenge — the LTA plans to invest nearly US$3 million next year to replace aging systems.

Starlink school connectivity. The LTA is overseeing the installation of high-speed Starlink internet in public schools; the project covers 156 schools nationwide and has reached approximately 90. Starlink supplied equipment valued at about US$156,000, while the LTA is financing distribution and installation at roughly US$135,000.
