1. Zambia will be the fifth operation of the Inclusive Digitalization in Eastern and Southern Africa (IDEA) Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) Project approved by the World Bank's Executive Directors on June 27, 2024, following Republic of Angola, Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), Republic of Malawi, and the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa (COMESA) as the coordinating Regional Economic Community (REC).

B. Introduction and Context

Country Context

2. The Republic of Zambia is a low-income country of around 20 million people which faces multiple challenges to its human, social, and economic development. Zambia's growth has been volatile, achieving middle-income status in 2011 and then slumping by 2019 to the lowest growth in two decades, compounded further by the COVID-19 pandemic. Zambia was reclassified as low income in July 2022 for one year. Most Zambians are employed in subsistence agriculture and the informal services sector, where wages are low. The lack of diversification within and outside of mining and agriculture, both highly vulnerable to shocks, caused economic growth to slow to 3.1 percent per year between 2015 and 2019, mainly attributed to falling copper prices and declines in agricultural output due to increased rainfall variability. The latest period of drought, which began in January 2024, is considered the worst for two decades and, because the country is so dependent on hydropower, it has caused regular loadshedding (20+ hours daily) which negatively impacts digital services.

3. Zambia's young and growing population presents significant opportunities and challenges for job creation and more inclusive growth. Zambia's population is young and growing rapidly, with 46 percent under 15 years and about 20 percent between 15 and 24 years. The young population means the countries is well-placed for a digital leap, but translating this into a demographic dividend will necessitate substantial investments in human capital to create a healthy and skilled labor force, particularly for women, to enable inclusive growth. Although Zambia is one of the most politically stable countries in Africa, weak institutions and limited accountability have hindered service delivery and more inclusive growth. Weak policy coordination and implementation of reforms, as well as limited transparency and accountability, have created space for vested interests to exercise a disproportionate influence in policy-making and allocation of public resources, thereby hindering pro-poor growth. On the positive side, the format of institutions in Zambia (laws, policies, systems, and structures) has shifted to mirror global best practices. The new Government has taken bold steps to stabilize the economy, refocus on private sector-led growth, restore public confidence, and strengthen institutions. Key measures have targeted the debt problem through improved fiscal management, scaling up social safety nets programs, and taking service delivery and resources closer to the people by increasing financing to constituencies while strengthening local governance and accountability.

Sectoral and Institutional Context

4. Digitalization can be a powerful tool for development, transforming entire sectors and delivery of services as well as creating employment. Higher levels of internet use are associated with significant developmental gains—increases in the number of jobs and a reduction in poverty. Digital can contribute to improved productivity and entrepreneurship. But the current level of use in the Eastern and Southern Africa region is inadequate and uneven. By putting in place a foundational basis for inclusive high-speed broadband networks, secure data hosting, digitally verifiable identification (ID), digital financial services (DFS), and data sharing, countries are establishing the highways of tomorrow that are needed to build inclusive and dynamic digital economies, societies, and governments. The benefits of getting digital public infrastructure (DPI) right can be enormous: empowered people, improved access to services and economic opportunities for all, more efficient delivery of social assistance, improved data for decision-making and innovation unlocked. Regulatory harmonization to extend trust across borders can widen the benefits of high-speed broadband, DPI, DFS and citizen-centric e-Government services to facilitate cross-border trade.

5. Digitalization can also play a critical role in tackling challenges, such as climate change, gender inequality and health emergencies. Zambia's population is highly exposed to many climate hazards, the most common being floods and droughts (see annex 1). Digital technologies can help build resilience to the effects of climate change, for instance, by supporting early warning systems for extreme weather events and by developing secure systems for DFS to facilitate electronic cash transfers and remittances. They also provide a platform for women to access knowledge, resources, and essential services, especially when supported by appropriate training and strategies. The use of accessible DFS, such as mobile money, has the potential to revolutionize financial services. Digital technologies can also expand access and improve efficiencies and effectiveness of emergency response and aid delivery in fragile contexts as well as to vulnerable communities in remote, rural areas.

6. Although Zambia has made significant strides on its path to digital transformation, there remain key challenges to inclusivity that the project will address. These include gaps in internet coverage particularly in rural areas where cell towers have not yet been upgraded to support mobile broadband and fixed line networks are largely absent. Cross-border infrastructure between countries is missing, which is hindering the development of seamless digital trade. There are notable disparities in internet access by women, persons with disabilities (PwD), and older people. Gaps in energy access are a further constraint and efforts to expand internet coverage will be aligned with expansion of energy access. On the services side, the lack of digitization of government records and public services means that citizens often have to travel considerable distances and queue to access basic services. Digital skills are also lacking, meaning that the country is not well placed to take advantage of new technologies, such as artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning. The project will tackle three critical challenges of connectivity, digital services and digitals skills, which correspond to the three main components of the project.

7. Although Zambia has a relatively competitive telecommunications market, connectivity is lagging the regional average (Component 1). With four licensed mobile operators (Airtel, MTN, Zamtel, and Zedmobile), at least six internet service providers (ISPs), and an experienced regulatory authority, Zambia Information and Communications Technology Authority (ZICTA), the country benefits from a favorable environment for further network expansion. The information and communication technologies (ICT) sector has experienced double digital growth and has shown resilience despite the shocks affecting the wider economy. However, the further expansion of usage is constrained by low incomes and relatively high prices, compounded by high excise duties on imported ICT equipment. Internet penetration, at around 21 users for every 100 inhabitants, is below the Eastern and Southern Africa regional average of 28 percent. Although prices for mobile services have become more affordable in recent years, owing to a competitive market, the price of fixed broadband services (5 GB per month) costs more than 12 percent of gross national income (GNI) per capita. However, the market entry of Starlink, a low-earth orbit (LEO) satellite provider in October 2023, is bringing greater price competition and providing new connectivity options in rural areas. The Government's broader goal is to increase internet penetration to 80 percent of the adult population, by 2026 as per the 8th National Development Plan (8NDP), though in 2021 the figure stood at only 21.2 percent, one of the lowest in the region. This will require a significant private sector investment to extend the current 10,000 km of fiber optic cable, and the project aims to add at least a further 2,000 km.

8. Digital transformation in Zambia will strengthen the economy and create more equitable society by extending the range of digital services available (Component 2). Digital technologies have a role to play in achieving many of the country's development outcomes. Zambia will increasingly need to rely on digital technologies to ensure that public services, businesses, and individuals are accessible to all. Additionally, these technologies will help withstand current and future hazards and develop a set of resilience measures, to ensure business continuity of government and avoid interruptions in service delivery. A combination of widespread access to broadband, digitally enabled services, and DFS can offer a powerful platform to remove barriers of distance and narrow gender gaps in digital skills as well as internet access and usage. It can also lower costs, improve efficiencies in the delivery of services, create new job opportunities, and enhance human development while also contributing to economic growth and reduced poverty.

9. A key element supported by the project is Digital ID. Zambia is transitioning from its legacy paper-based National Registration Card (NRC) system, introduced before independence in 1964, to a more modern and integrated digital identity ecosystem. The NRC system covered some 94 percent of the adult population by 2021, and subsequent advances through the Integrated National Registration Information System (INRIS) have registered 15 percent of adults using biometrics. However, the Government is planning to implement a homegrown foundational ID system, based on open standards, and building on existing investments. This new system will prioritize privacy, security, and interoperability, enabling the Government to manage its infrastructure independently and reduce reliance on external vendors. The Ministry of Home Affairs and Internal Security (MoHAIS) is collecting minimal demographic data (such as Name, Date of Birth, Address, Gender and biometric attributes --10 fingerprints and a photograph); only what is needed to support a unique ID. No sensitive or ethnic data is captured. ID enrollment is free of charge with the first issuance of the ID credential expected to be free. All citizens, marginalized communities, Refugees and Legal residents are eligible for enrollment. Through the ongoing ID4D technical assistance, the government is being supported in developing an operational plan, which includes expanding registration sites via post offices and implementing a public engagement strategy to accelerate registration campaigns.

10. Digital ID is part of Zambia's broader vision to establish a robust DPI, with digital ID serving as a foundational building block for inclusive service delivery. Complementary platforms -- such as ZamPay, the Government's eServices portal, ZamSign for e-Signatures, ZamConnect for data integration, ZamPay for interoperable digital payments and ZamConnect for data exchange -- are critical enablers of this vision, facilitating seamless access to public services, enhancing financial inclusion, and driving private sector innovation. ZamPortal, currently offers just 300 out of an estimated 1,500 services. The World Bank's interventions on Digital ID are guided by the ID4D Principles, implemented in close coordination with UNDP, UNHCR and other development partners in Zambia. The government plans to provide diverse authentication options, including biometrics, smart cards, QR codes, One-Time Passcodes (OTPs) and Paper-Based Credentials with Digital Features ensuring inclusivity and accessibility for all users. Through integration with various public and private services, this will enable seamless access to healthcare, financial services, and social welfare programs fostering trust and empowering individuals to manage their identity data securely. With technical assistance (TA) from the World Bank's Identification for Development (ID4D) Trust Fund, the Government is exploring cost-effective ID credential options, including digitally signed quick response (QR) codes, durable plastic cards, and polycarbonate cards. While significant progress has been made, the Government is still evaluating and will select the most suitable official ID credential to align with its comprehensive DPI strategy.

11. Enhanced connectivity and increased access to digital services requires a focus on building digital literacy and advanced digital skills (Component 3). The National Electronic Government Plan 2023-2026 emphasizes the need to increase the consumption of e-services by providing both basic and advanced digital skills training through Digital Transformation Centers (DTCs). The government plans to complement basic digital skills with a mobile e-learning application hosting the complete basic literacy curriculum, ensuring continuity and constant learning for citizens. Another priority is a targeted intervention to build a skilled and employment-ready workforce capable of leveraging emerging technologies responsibly. Ongoing discussions are exploring ways to link and complement these digital skills initiatives with other sectors, such as social protection and education, while identifying potential private sector partnerships to effectively scale training efforts.

Component 1. Affordable broadband (US$50 million as IDA grant, plus an estimated US$18 million in UCF)

14. This component focuses on expanding essential, high-quality, resilient, and affordable broadband infrastructure and services. Activities under this pillar aim to strengthen institutions and help drive private investment in telecom networks, which are a key foundation for using digital technologies and data productively. It will do this through investments, carried out alongside the private sector, to increase access and improve network speeds and connectivity. This will be done through a One World Bank approach, in close collaboration with IFC and MIGA.
