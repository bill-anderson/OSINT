By Matshepo Sehloho, Associate Editor, Connecting Africa. June 17, 2026.

SpaceX's satellite Internet service, Starlink, is set to launch its services in Côte d'Ivoire in July 2026, after receiving a 12-month provisional license to provide fixed high-speed satellite Internet across the country.

The country is also set to deploy 5G services in cities with more than 25,000 inhabitants, with coverage to be expanded progressively as telecom operators prepare and upgrade their networks.

Djibril Ouattara, the country's minister of digital transition and technological innovation, made these announcements during the government's Gouv'Talk online dialogue series.

Ouattara unveiled a roadmap built around seven strategic pillars: connectivity, digital government services, innovation, AI, cybersecurity, digital skills, and the modernization of La Poste Côte d'Ivoire.

He said the government aimed to expand high-speed Internet access, improve smartphone adoption in rural areas and create a single digital platform for public services.

He added that his department is also preparing a regulatory framework for AI and developing local AI applications in healthcare, education, and public administration.

## Starlink to face competition in Côte d'Ivoire

Starlink's entry comes at a time when satellite connectivity is becoming increasingly important in Côte d'Ivoire's broadband sector.

Unlike many African markets, where satellite Internet services are still in their early stages, Côte d'Ivoire already has operators providing comparable solutions.

In January 2026, Orange Côte d'Ivoire partnered with Eutelsat to launch a satellite broadband Internet service called Orange Sat, which is aimed at connecting the country's underserved areas.

In April 2026, Eutelsat and MTN Côte d'Ivoire announced a multi-year agreement to deliver satellite connectivity services across Côte d'Ivoire using Eutelsat Konnect's high-throughput capacity.

The entry of Elon Musk's company into the West African country introduces a new competitor to a market that is expanding, driven by rising demand for reliable Internet access nationwide.

Starlink getting a provisional license comes a month after the Uganda Communications Commission (UCC) also granted it a license.

However, even though the company has received a license in Côte d'Ivoire and Uganda recently, in March 2026, it was denied an operating license in Namibia.

Starlink is still trying to get an operating license in South Africa but is also battling ownership rules that apply to Namibia's neighbor.

Across Africa its services are available in Lesotho, Zimbabwe, Zambia, Eswatini, Malawi, Mozambique, Madagascar, Botswana, Ghana, Nigeria, Niger, Liberia, Benin, Guinea-Bissau, Sierra Leone, Chad, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Senegal, Somalia, Central African Republic, Kenya, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Burundi, as well as the island nations of Cape Verde and São Tomé and Príncipe.
