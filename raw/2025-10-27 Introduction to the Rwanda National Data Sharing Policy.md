To enable improved data-driven decision-making through access to shared government data, the Government of Rwanda (GoR) has approved a National Data Sharing Policy ("the policy"). Proposed by the Rwandan Ministry of ICT and Innovation (MINICT), and drafted through the Rwanda Economy Digitalisation programme, the policy could be a gamechanger in evidence-informed policymaking in the country.

Key policy objectives include: ensuring that data is shared in a protected and responsible manner among agencies of the Government of Rwanda to enhance decision-making, facilitate collaboration and provide the basis for fulfilling national policy objectives; providing a standardised approach to facilitate data sharing and free flow of information, while ensuring data privacy and security are maintained; and ensuring compliance with Rwanda's Data Protection and Privacy Law (the "DPPL").

The policy annexes include: (a) draft Terms of Reference for a Data Governance Unit and (b) draft ToR for a Data Sharing Technical Subcommittee; a Data Sharing Application Form; a Data Sharing Agreement; a High-Level Data Sharing Process Overview; the Five Safes Data Sharing Framework; Responsibilities of Government Entities Participating in Data Sharing; and a Data Sharing Technology Platform.

**Implementation.** Implementation will be overseen by MINICT, which will provide strategic direction and overall coordination. Execution responsibilities are shared between the National Institute of Statistics Rwanda (NISR) and the Rwanda Information Society Authority (RISA). NISR will lead the data-governance components through a soon-to-be-established Data Governance Unit, which will spearhead the adoption of national data-governance frameworks and the integration of unified national identifiers. RISA is entrusted with technological components, including the development and maintenance of the Data Sharing Platform to facilitate secure and efficient data exchange across institutions.

**Obligations on institutions.** The policy places obligations on data Requestors and Providers. It requires that organisations are held accountable for sharing data that is governed and of good quality, with adherence to a governance framework required. Each entity must nominate personnel who can approve data-sharing requests or sign off data-sharing agreements. Requests for access to shared data will require advance due diligence and governance, embedded into the request process. Personal-data sharing will require strict compliance with the DPPL, including legal justifications and signed agreements.

**Data Governance Unit responsibilities.** The policy places an onus on NISR's Data Governance Unit to put in place national data-governance frameworks through: adoption of national data standards (e.g. national identifiers conforming to a standard pattern; dates and codes such as country codes conforming to national or international standards such as ISO); adoption of data-classification guidelines to categorise data by sensitivity, importance or intended use; adoption of metadata management to maintain accurate descriptions of data sources, formats, ownership, collection methods, update frequencies and usage rights; and adoption of data-retention and disposal policies determining how long data should be stored and when it should be securely deleted, in compliance with legal, regulatory and institutional requirements.

**Technical Subcommittee (TSC).** The TSC, comprising members appointed by the Data Governance Unit (DGU), will serve as an advisory and technical body to the DGU, ensuring compliance with technical requirements and providing guidance on the development of key components to accelerate implementation of data-sharing activities.

The policy is seen as a major step forward in enhancing the capabilities of government institutions for data-driven decision-making, and will also facilitate access to open data for researchers, innovators and the public.
