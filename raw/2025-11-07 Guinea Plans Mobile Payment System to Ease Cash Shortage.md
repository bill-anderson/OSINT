To prevent recurrences of the recent cash shortages, the Central Bank of the Republic of Guinea (BCRG) has developed a strategy to reduce the economy's dependence on cash. The plan — presented on 4 November 2025 to the Planning, Financial Affairs and Budget Oversight Commission of the National Transitional Council (CNT) — includes a "merchant payment system inspired by models such as M-Pesa" (Kenya), developed with World Bank support, to "anchor electronic money in the habits of Guineans."

Guinea now has 11 licensed electronic-money institutions (EMIs), up from six in 2024; about 26% of adults hold an electronic-money account. The BCRG plans to limit cash payments and operationalize the national payment switch for interoperability among banks, EMIs, microfinance institutions, payment operators and fintechs. In August 2025 the government announced plans to ban cash in public administration; in June 2025 the PM suspended cash payments at the Prime Minister's Office (to extend to other bodies).

The BCRG said the liquidity crisis eased via air-freighted banknotes and large cash injections (two 20-ft containers of new bills worth over 1.4 trillion GNF / $161m in August 2025), but these are temporary. It attributed the crisis to strong cash preference — nearly 94% of issued banknotes never return to the banking system — plus production/delivery delays (up to seven months) and large clients' reluctance to fund accounts amid AML checks.
