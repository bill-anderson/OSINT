*IMF-published named ANALYSIS, not primary evidence — attribute. Supplies the 'why' behind the TIN-to-NIN shift: Uganda targets tax-to-GDP 13% to 18% by 2030 against a decade of stagnation near 13%. Its OECD/SSA comparators are reference figures — cite, do not absorb.*

State capacity — the ability of modern states to collect taxes efficiently, enforce contracts and provide public goods — is the foundation of effective governance. Uganda envisages enhancing its domestic resource mobilization by increasing its tax-to-GDP ratio from 13 to 18 percent by 2030.

Across the continent tax revenue remains far below what is needed. Per a 2024 OECD revenue statistics report, the average tax-to-GDP ratio in Sub-Saharan Africa stands at 16 percent, versus an OECD average of 34 percent. Uganda's ratio has stagnated at around 13 percent over the past decade, below the regional average, intensifying reliance on borrowing.

Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) presents an opportunity. In recent years the Uganda Revenue Authority (URA) has introduced several DPIs: the integrated Tax Administration System (ITAS) including Taxpayer Identification Numbers (TINs), the Electronic Fiscal Receipting and Invoicing System (EFRIS), Payment Identification Numbers (PINs) and Digital Tax Stamps (DTS).

Specific results cited:
- TINs have expanded the tax base by integrating national ID systems, registering over 350,000 informal taxpayers by 2022.
- EFRIS has enabled real-time transaction tracking, leading to a 12 percent rise in VAT collections and curbing tax fraud.
- PINs have streamlined payments by integrating mobile money and banking platforms, facilitating 25 percent of tax payments digitally.
- DTS has strengthened market regulation, reducing illicit trade and boosting excise duty and customs collections.

The author argues Uganda could further boost revenues by integrating URA systems with third-party information (employers, banks, pension/investment funds reporting taxable income directly) and by investing in business intelligence systems for data analytics.

Challenges noted: limited technical skills and capacity within revenue authorities; a digital gap where revenue administrations advance faster than other agencies, hampering data sharing; and many smaller taxpayers lacking access to digital platforms.
