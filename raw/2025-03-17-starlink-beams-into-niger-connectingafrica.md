SpaceX's satellite Internet service, Starlink, has officially launched in the West African country of Niger.

Matshepo Sehloho,Associate Editor,Connecting Africa

Starlink, the satellite Internet provider backed by SpaceX, has officially launched in Niger.

In a post on X, Starlink announced that its high-speed, low-latency Internet was now fully operational in the West African country.

The introduction of Starlink in Niger is timely, as the country grapples with significant connectivity challenges, with many areas lacking reliable Internet access.

Niger's Ministry of Communications, Post, and Digital Economy also announced the commercial availability of Starlink services in the country. The ministry said that residential and corporate kits can now be purchased online and through authorized sellers.

Communications Minister Sidi Mohamed Raliou said access to these services remains regulated.

"These Internet services are strictly for the personal or professional use of the sole customer with the Starlink kit. Users are also solely responsible for the use of their connection, its possible sharing, and the consequences thereof in accordance with current regulations," he said in a statement read on national television.

The service launch follows SpaceX's agreement with Niger's ruling military Junta in November 2024 to provide low-Earth orbit (LEO) satellite broadband in the country.

The launch in Niger comes at a time when Musk's company has been facing difficulties with its expansion plans on the African continent.

Just last week, the company's telecom license application faced opposition from a Lesotho rights group called Section 2 due to what it calls "a total absence of local ownership."

Section 2 argued that a lack of local involvement breaks with Lesotho's long-standing practice of ensuring citizen participation in key sectors, including telecommunications.

Niger's service launch stems from SpaceX's agreement with Niger's ruling military Junta in November 2024 to deliver LEO satellite broadband across the country. (Source: Timothy Sithole)

Lesotho is not the only country where Starlink has been facing opposition, in February 2025, the company withdrew its planned participation in regulatory hearings led by the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA).

SpaceX, Starlink's parent company, advised ICASA to reconsider a licensing policy mandating a 30% shareholding by 'historically disadvantaged' groups, which is holding back Starlink's entry into South Africa.

In November 2024, the Communications Regulatory Authority of Namibia (CRAN) ordered Starlink to cease operations in the country, citing the company's lack of a required telecommunications license.

Even though Starlink has been facing opposition in some African countries it has continued to expand across the region. Starlink is currently active in Nigeria, Burundi, Botswana, Ghana, Liberia, Zimbabwe, Benin, Rwanda, Malawi, Mozambique as well as Zambia, Kenya, Madagascar, Eswatini, Sierra Leone, South Sudan, and now Niger.

Soon, the company will be active in the Central African country of Chad. Late last week, Chad's Ministry of Telecommunications, Digital Economy and Digitalization announced that Starlink had signed an operating license that will enable it to offer services in the country. The license, which had been in the works since 2021, received approval from the ministry in November 2024.
