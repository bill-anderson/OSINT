Madagascar's central bank (Banky Foibem-pirenena Madagasikara) launched a 10-month pilot for its digital currency, the e-Ariary, on 23 May 2025, to modernise the financial system and boost inclusion. The initiative seeks to reduce reliance on physical cash, lower transaction costs, improve traceability and expand access, particularly in rural areas.

Governor Aivo Andrianarivelo noted that managing banknotes is very expensive: the 100 Ariary note (~$0.022) does not cover its printing cost and lasts about six months. The e-Ariary is designed for smartphones, basic mobile phones and offline methods (QR codes, smart cards, POS terminals), for everyday transactions — merchant payments, transport fares, salaries, social transfers. It complements, rather than replaces, mobile money. In 2023, Madagascar recorded over 10 million mobile-money accounts (vs 3 million bank accounts), with nearly 342 million transactions totalling 38,161 billion Ariary (~$8.5 billion). Trial participants include commercial banks, microfinance institutions, state utility Jirama, market vendors, the Treasury and the tax administration.
