# Congo boosts Internet access in rural areas

Matshepo Sehloho, Associate Editor, Connecting Africa. October 20, 2025.

The Republic of Congo has launched 20 high-speed Internet connectivity sites in rural areas, aiming to democratize Internet access across the country and bridge the digital divide.

Announced during a visit by a World Bank delegation for the Africa Region to Brazzaville, the deployment forms part of Congo's Digital Transformation Acceleration Project (PATN) and aligns with the broader goals of its 2030 digital strategy.

Backed by US$100 million from the World Bank and European Union (EU), PATN marks a landmark investment in Congo's digital future.

"As of today, seventy-six connectivity sites have been built and equipped across the country. Of these, twenty are already connected to high-speed internet, allowing the hinterland population to connect in real time," said Minister of Posts, Telecommunications and the Digital Economy Léon Juste Ibombo.

He added that the Congolese government has also allocated an additional $3 million to modernize infrastructure, prioritizing 4G technology to ensure stable and efficient connectivity.

The government has set a goal of connecting 76 sites to 4G by the end of the year, while opening up new economic and social opportunities for rural communities, particularly in e-commerce, e-learning and e-government.

## Republic of Congo connecting underserved communities

The launching of 20 high-speed Internet connectivity sites is timely as the country grapples with significant connectivity challenges, with many areas lacking reliable Internet access.

Statistics from the data platform DataReportal show there were 2.46 million Internet users in the Republic of Congo in January 2025, with an Internet penetration rate of just 38.4%.

The Republic of Congo has a population of 6.4 million, and Kepios analysis indicates that the number of Internet users increased by 58,000, or 2.4%, between January 2024 and January 2025.

However, the country has been working toward connecting underserved communities.

In August 2025, the Republic of Congo's Postal and Electronic Communications Regulatory Agency (ARPCE) and the Angolan Institute of Communications (INACOM) agreed to take a step forward in bilateral cooperation in managing the radio frequency spectrum along the shared border between the two countries.

That agreement followed many countries either signing roaming deals, partnerships, or upgrading infrastructure aimed at connecting underserved communities.
