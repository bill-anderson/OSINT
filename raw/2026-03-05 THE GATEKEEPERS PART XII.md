On February 17, 2026, the African Union Commission (AUC) signed a Memorandum of Understanding with Google to advance what it called “Africa’s sovereign AI and digital capacity.” The word sovereign appeared in the press release four times but it did not appear in any binding clause.

What the MOU actually delivers is African institutional data and the digital futures of 1.5 billion people, placed on infrastructure owned by a U.S. corporation, subject to U.S. law; the Clarifying Lawful Overseas Use of Data Act the CLOUD Act (18 U.S.C. § 2713, enacted 2018). This law requires American technology companies to produce data held anywhere in the world when a U.S. court or law enforcement agency demands it, without the knowledge or consent of the country where that data originated. Jurisdiction follows ownership, not location. A data residency clause, however well worded, does not change which country’s courts govern the infrastructure. As the Electronic Frontier Foundation documented when the Act was passed, “it represents a sweeping expansion of extraterritorial surveillance authority that no bilateral MOU can override”.

So this is not a partnership for African sovereignty, it is the formalisation of African exposure.

Before examining what the AUC has signed, we need to understand what the AUC is structurally, financially, and in terms of whose interests it is constituted to serve.

Walter Rodney’s foundational analysis “How Europe Underdeveloped Africa” established that African underdevelopment is not a natural condition but the direct product of deliberate extraction and structural dependency, the systematic redirection of African resources and decision making capacity toward external interests. Half a century on, that analysis finds its institutional expression in the AU’s own financing page, which states plainly that the organisation “is heavily dependent on donor funding to run its programmes and operations”.

The 2025 AU budget of $608 million paints the picture. Of the $388 million programme budget the money that funds the actual work of the union African member states, African institutions and internal sources combined covered 22.5%. External donors funded 77.5%. For peace support operations, the figure is starker, from 2021 to 2025, member state funding was 0% according to Africa Check and Amani Africa.

At the 2025 “Governance Weekend” held in Marrakech, Mo Ibrahim called this situation ‘a disgrace that 70% of the $650 million annual budget of the African Union is funded by Europeans. ‘When the foreigners fund us, they are ‘development partners, then tomorrow they are colonisers and imperialists’. We have to be serious. It is either we have an African Union and fund it, or we all go home.

The donors are named in the AU’s own financial documents and confirmed in independent analysis; the European Union, Germany, United Kingdom, United States through USAID, Canada, Netherlands, Norway, Sweden, Denmark, and Australia. These are not disinterested benefactors, they are governments with explicit foreign policy interests in Africa’s digital, economic, and political orientation. An intergovernmental organisation whose operations depend on continued foreign goodwill cannot credibly claim to negotiate against their interests. The architecture of dependency is of capture. As so many researchers examining Africa’s digital governance have documented, this produces a condition “compounded by donor superiority, where external countries often dictate Africa’s digital priorities. The AUC is not an exception to this dynamic, it is its institutional expression at continental scale.

**AI Strategy That Was Not Written for Africa**

The Google MOU is framed as an implementation of the AU Continental AI Strategy, adopted in July 2024. This framing implies the strategy itself is an autonomous African document that merely invites external partnership, it is not. and as Ngũgĩ wa Thiong’o identified, the deepest violence of colonialism is not as the seizure of land or labor but the colonisation of the mind, the replacement of indigenous conceptual frameworks with the coloniser’s vocabulary, so that the colonised comes to see their own reality through borrowed eyes and reach for borrowed tools.

The strategy was developed with UNESCO providing what its own liaison officer described as an “entry point” into the AU AI Working Group. technical support began before the conceptual framework was even drafted. Researchers affiliated with the Global Center on AI Governance led the drafting and expert consultation process. An independent consulting expert to the AU on the strategy simultaneously held a fellowship at the Brookings Institution’s Center for Technology Innovation, which is a Washington think tank deeply embedded in the policy networks that govern the technology sector, the strategy it is designed to regulate.

The strategy’s intellectual orientation confirms its provenance. Rather than grounding African AI governance in African legal and philosophical traditions, in indigenous knowledge systems, in the communal ethics of ubuntu, in customary frameworks for collective resource stewardship, or in the lived realities of how technology actually functions across informal economies, multilingual communities and varied state capacities, the document looks outward for its models. the EU AI Act, the ASEAN Guide to AI Governance and Ethics, and the 2024 Santiago Declaration.

Africa’s sovereign AI roadmap is architecturally modeled on European regulatory frameworks, and this was not inevitable. If external frameworks were to be consulted at all, the more urgent consultation was with African legal scholars, community technologists, and civil society practitioners working on the continent but also with farmers navigating digital payment systems they did not design, with health workers using AI diagnostics tools calibrated on data that does not reflect their patients, with journalists documenting platform censorship of African languages and political speech, with indigenous knowledge holders whose oral traditions and collective resource frameworks contain centuries of sophisticated thinking about communal governance that no EU regulation has ever attempted to encode, and with the millions of informal economy workers whose livelihoods are already being restructured by algorithmic systems built entirely without their input.

The governance traditions that predate the EU AI Act by centuries were not consulted. The choice to reach for Brussels before reaching for Nairobi, Johannesburg, Lagos, or Dakar was not a technical necessity. It was an orientation and orientations are chosen.

The consequence, as documented by strathmore university, has been fragmentation risks “countries failing to utilise the collective mission and adopting established approaches, such as those from the European Union, without considering local contexts” This risk was not mitigated during drafting instead it was embedded in it.

Abeba Birhane’s mapping of algorithmic colonialism shows what is in motion here. Birhane traces how Western developed AI and digital governance frameworks are imported to Africa “without adaptation, leading to outcomes that benefit the West more than Africa,” simultaneously impoverishing the development of local alternatives while entrenching continental dependency on Western infrastructure and conceptual categories. The Continental AI Strategy is not a counter to this dynamic, under examination, it is a local institutional vehicle for it, a document that invites the very partnerships it claims to govern, written in the intellectual idiom of the institutions it was supposed to hold accountable.

**The Apolitical Vector: Training the Governors**

The Google MOU does not deliver AI systems directly to African governments, it does something more durable, it trains the people who will govern AI, procure AI, and write the regulations for AI using Google’s frameworks, Google’s tools, and a delivery partner funded by Google.org.

The AI readiness training programme for African public officials announced alongside the MOU is delivered in partnership with Apolitical, described as “a global learning platform for government innovation.” Apolitical sits within Google.org’s partner ecosystem, the 2026 Google.org Impact Challenge for AI in Government designates Apolitical as one of only two external partner review organisations evaluating which nonprofits and institutions receive between $1 million and $3 million in Google.org grants plus Google Cloud Credits.

African public officials will receive AI fundamentals, ethical frameworks, and leadership training through a partner embedded in the architecture of Google’s philanthropic programme for government AI adoption. Google’s own communications are explicit about the scale, 50,000 public officials in Ethiopia alone with the stated goal of “expanding this AI literacy program across the continent together with the African Union Commission and the UN Economic Commission for Africa.”

Alongside the training programme, Google is providing free access to Gemini Pro and NotebookLM, which it notes now support local languages including Amharic. Google Translate added Amharic in February 2016 the same year the African Union, headquartered in Addis Ababa, officially recognised it as a working language, fifty-three years after the organisation’s founding. A corporation based in California indexed the language of Ethiopia into its global infrastructure in the same year the continental body headquartered in Ethiopia finally recognised it.

This is not capacity building, this is ecosystem conditioning. Officials trained to understand AI through a Google.org funded curriculum will, when they write procurement specifications, evaluate competing bids, and draft regulations, and will do so using the categories they were trained in. The dependency is not merely infrastructural, it is epistemic. By the time the regulatory frameworks are written, the cognitive architecture for what counts as AI, what counts as risk, and what counts as acceptable governance will have been shaped by the funder.

Timnit Gebru and Émile Torres’s analysis of the TESCREAL ideological bundle the cluster of Effective Altruism, longtermism, and techno utopianism that organises Silicon Valley’s philanthropic logic is directly relevant here. Gebru has noted that this ideology “aligns very well with large corporations and the billionaires who fund this movement, because you are not telling them they should not be billionaires or give away their resources right now for people who exist right now. Google.org’s AI for governement programme shares the structural logic that Gebru and Torres identify as characteristic of TESCREAL philanthropy; technology access framed as doing the most good for future populations, while present dependencies go unremarked and present data flows remain ungoverned. Whether or not Google’s leadership self identifies with EA or longtermist ideology, the architecture of the programme free tools today, trained officials tomorrow, 2030 targets throughout operates precisely as that framework predicts. The present extraction is acceptable because of the promised future.

Shakir Mohamed, Marie-Therese Png, and William Isaac’s decolonial AI framework identifies this mechanism by naming epistemic erasure as a core colonial technology the systematic substitution of local knowledge frameworks with external ones, conducted through institutions that present themselves as neutral capacity builders. The Apolitical programme is that mechanism executed at continental scale, with institutional AU cover, under a brand that sounds like civic neutrality.

**The House That China Built: A Precedent the AUC Has Already Forgotten**

The Google MOU is not the first time the AUC has formalised a dependency with a foreign technology power while calling it partnership. The precedent matters, because it demonstrates the AUC has encountered this dynamic before, watched it fail, and repeated it, several times over.

In 2012, the AU opened its new headquarters in Addis Ababa, a $200 million gift from the Chinese government, designed and built by Chinese firms, outfitted by Huawei, and framed as the architectural expression of Sino-African solidarity. The building’s very existence was a prize in foreign competition, Gaddafi had lobbied to build the AU headquarters as a Libyan vanity project in his hometown of Sirte, and China outmaneuvered him for that symbolic claim on the continent’s institutional center.

In January 2018, Le Monde Afrique reported what the AU’s own IT department had discovered the year before that the building’s systems had allegedly been connecting nightly to servers in Shanghai, uploading AU files and room recordings, for five consecutive years. The AU Commission Chairperson called the allegations “all lies.” Huawei told the BBC that any data leaks “did not originate in technology supplied by Huawei to the AU.”

What the organisation did next is instructive, in June 2019, a year and a half after the exposure, the AU Commission and Huawei signed a new MOU expanding their partnership across broadband, cloud computing, 5G, artificial intelligence, and the training of African IT personnel.

When asked about the espionage allegations, AU then Chair, Rwanda’s Paul Kagame said he was “not worried” but regretted that Africa had not built its own headquarters.

The disease is the refusal to fund African institutions until a foreign power fills the vacuum and then to receive that vacuum filling as a gift. The Commission administered more of the same medicine.

Academic research conducted in Kenya and Nigeria, published in the Journal of Chinese Economic and Business Studies, documented the limitations of knowledge transfer in Huawei’s African training centres, the gap between the volume of training activity and actual technological capability transfer on the ground. The Carnegie Endowment for International Peace’s analysis of Huawei’s localisation in North Africa reached the same conclusion, closer scrutiny indicates the company “improved its brand image without engaging in meaningful capacity building,” managing to “localise seemingly developmental activities without contributing much to technological upgrading.” Training African IT personnel in Huawei’s systems, running tours of Huawei’s training centers in Shenzhen, embedding Huawei’s cloud architecture in AU institutional infrastructure none of this transferred capability. It only transferred dependency.

The Google MOU replicates this structure in every essential feature, a major foreign technology power, a training commitment framed as capacity building, free access to proprietary tools, cloud infrastructure that remains under the vendor’s jurisdiction regardless of where the servers sit. The Commission has changed partners but not changed logic.

**Sovereignty Is Not a Word in a Press Release**

The word “sovereignty” appears throughout AU’s digital governance documents. The 2022 AU Data Policy Framework invokes it, the Digital Transformation Strategy 2020–2030 is organised around it, the Continental AI Strategy claims to embody it. The Google MOU press release celebrates it in the headline. But sovereignty invoked in a press release, a strategy document, or a framework declaration is aspirational language, it does not create enforceable obligation, no compliance mechanism, and no remedy for breach. The MOU itself is not a treaty, it binds no member state, establishes no data localisation requirement, no audit right, no exit clause, and no recourse if Google’s obligations under the US CLOUD Act conflict with the data protections the AU’s own frameworks claim to guarantee. The word does what it has always done in contexts like this, it provides political cover for an arrangement that the sovereignty language was presumably designed to prevent.

Sovereignty in the context of data and digital infrastructure means: whose courts govern the infrastructure and whose laws apply to the data; who owns and controls the physical and cloud architecture through which the data moves; and who determines what is extracted from that data, how it is modelled, and for whose benefit.

On jurisdiction and applicable law, the first and most basic test the Google MOU delivers the opposite of what it advertises. Whether or not data is hosted within African borders, if the infrastructure is managed by a US based provider, US authorities can legally access it without involving African courts, African regulators, or African governments. The CLOUD Act is not a hypothetical risk, it is the operational legal architecture of everything the MOU has just formalised. The remaining questions, who controls the infrastructure, and who determines what is extracted from it do not produce different answers.

The African continent has few proof points that genuine alternatives are possible and even those are partial. Nigeria’s NITDA has finalised frameworks requiring international cloud providers to host sensitive data locally, with domestic partners. The localisation mandate is real, domestically legislated, and enforced. But NITDA’s broader regulatory environment has been shaped in collaboration with the World Bank and WTO, and the surrounding digital ecosystem remains entangled with external interests.

This is not unique to Africa. India drafted toward hard localisation requirements, then retreated under pressure from domestic industry and trade partners.

Vietnam legislated data residency, but entangled it with domestic surveillance imperatives. Genuine data sovereignty is contested, partial, and hard won everywhere but the contest is real, and it is being waged. The AUC did not engage that contest, it bypassed it entirely signing away continental data governance before the fight had even been joined, and calling the surrender a landmark.

**Question That We Must Now Ask**

The evidence assembled here is not a collection of isolated failures. It is a pattern consistent over years, across various foreign technology powers, across multiple Commissions and Chairpersons. It is a pattern that the organisation’s own scholars, own financial documents, and its own governance frameworks have named and warned against and which the organisation has nonetheless reproduced, each time with better branding.

A continental body whose programme budget is 77.5% externally funded cannot make independent strategic decisions. An organisation whose flagship AI strategy was co drafted by external experts operating within external institutional frameworks, modeled on the EU’s regulatory architecture, cannot claim that strategy as sovereign. An intergovernmental organisation that deepened its Huawei partnership after reported espionage allegations, and has now signed a CLOUD Act-bound AI partnership while calling it sovereignty, has either failed to understand what sovereignty requires or has decided not to pursue it.

Both possibilities demand the same response, does the African Union Commission, as currently constituted and funded, structurally capable of serving African interests in the domain of digital governance?

The 55 member states of the African Union have collectively failed to fund their own continental body. Into that vacuum, external powers have inserted their priorities, their frameworks, tools, and their personnel with the organisation’s full cooperation and its official seal. The result is an African intergovernmental organisation that speaks the language of Pan-African sovereignty while executing the interests of its funders.

Birhane’s mapping of algorithmic colonialism locates the mechanism in its starkest terms here, control in the age of AI “occurs not through brute physical force, but through invisible and nuanced mechanisms, the control of digital ecosystems and infrastructure”. The AUC has just handed the governance of Africa’s AI ecosystem to a corporation bound by U.S. law, from a building whose alleged compromise by Chinese surveillance was reported across two international newspapers, under the auspices of a strategy drafted with UNESCO’s “entry point” and a Brookings Fellow’s pen.

In 2025, the African Union declared it the “Year of Reparations” a theme of reclamation, of debt acknowledged, of resources returned. The year produced formal declarations, committee mandates, and a decade long extension of the agenda. What it did not produce was a single dollar of reparations demanded, negotiated or received. The answer to what the year actually delivered is visible in the first weeks of 2026, a memorandum of understanding with Google, handing continental data governance to a CLOUD Act-bound corporation, signed by the Commission, funded 77.5% by the governments from whom reparations are owed. The “Year of Reparations” produced no reparations, instead it produced a pivot straight into the next extraction.

Nkrumah defined it neo-colonialism is not the absence of sovereignty but its simulation, the form of independence without the substance. The African Union Commission’s February 2026 MOU with Google is that simulation made contractual. The flag remains, the data does not.
