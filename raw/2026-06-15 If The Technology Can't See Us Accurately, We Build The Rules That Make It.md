Joy Buolamwini was building a project at MIT.

The facial recognition software she was using couldn't detect her face.

And I don't mean, misidentifying it.

It couldn't see her face at all.

She put on a white mask.

The system detected it immediately.

Her own words:

"A white mask was a closer fit to what the system had learned was a face than my actual human face."

Most people would have been frustrated and moved on.

But not Joy. She built a movement.

*She founded the Algorithmic Justice League.*

*Testified before the US Congress.*

*Changed how Microsoft and IBM build facial recognition systems.*

*Wrote a book that changed the global conversation.*

That moment with that one woman invisible to a machine, became the foundation of the most important bias research in AI history.

This is the same energy Africa needs to bring to this conversation right now.

And here's why…

**Because that same technology is running across our cities.**

- Uganda invested $126 million in a surveillance system with 1,800 cameras across Kampala.
- Nigeria has spent over $470 million on facial recognition systems.
- Kenya $219 million.

Eleven African countries have these systems operating in public spaces right now.

And a March 2026 report by the Institute of Development Studies found the same thing in every single one of them.

There is no adequate legal framework protecting citizens from what these systems can or omit to do.

Not one.

The technology came but the governance didn't travel with it.

And that is our problem to fix.

LET’S DO A QUICK DEEP DIVE

Here is what makes this urgent right now.

In February 2025 the EU AI Act banned real time facial recognition in public spaces for law enforcement.

Europe looked at this technology and said the risk to our citizens is too high without proper safeguards.

And while Europe was banning it,

Africa was spending hundreds of millions deploying it.

- With no equivalent protection for citizens.
- No requirement to test accuracy on African faces before deploying on African faces.
- No mechanism for a citizen to appeal a wrong identification.
- No named person accountable when the system gets it wrong.

And Joy's research showed clearly that these systems make significantly more errors on darker-skinned women than on lighter-skinned men.

Which means the people most likely to be misidentified are the majority of the people these systems are being deployed on.

In Uganda the systems have reportedly been used to identify and track opposition politicians.

In Zimbabwe specific groups fear being targeted.

In Mozambique cameras were deployed where political opposition is concentrated.

This is no longer a future problem, it's happening right now and it has a solution.

THE AFRICAN LENS

Security matters.

Keeping cities safe matters.

Technology can genuinely help with both.

But here is the question Africa needs to start asking before most of these systems go live.

Not after. Before.

- Does this system work accurately on African faces?
- Who is accountable when it gets it wrong?
- What can a citizen do if they are wrongly identified?

These are not complicated questions.

They are the minimum standard any government deploying this technology owes its people.

And right now the IDS report found that even where data protection laws exist across these eleven countries, they often contain explicit national security exemptions.

Meaning the very laws designed to protect citizens have gaps wide enough to make them ineffective.

These are some of what Africa changes next.

WE HAVE A GREAT OPPORTUNITY

Joy Buolamwini didn't just name the problem.

She built the solution.

Standards. Research. Policy change. Global accountability.

Africa does the same.

Before any facial recognition system is deployed in African public spaces, it must demonstrate accuracy across African faces.

Not accuracy on European datasets.

African faces.

Every citizen who is misidentified must have a clear mechanism to challenge that identification.

National security exemptions in data protection laws must be tightened.

Because national security cannot mean no accountability.

- Right now, Rwanda has human oversight requirements in its AI policy.
- Nigeria's CBN requires AI tools to explain themselves before operating in fintech.

The same standard applies here.

Before deployment.

Not after someone gets hurt.

Joy put on a white mask.

And then spent the next decade making sure nobody else had to.

She didn't wait to be invited into the conversation about AI bias.

She created the conversation.

From one moment of being invisible to a machine.

That is the frequency Africa brings to AI governance.

Not waiting for Brussels to tell us the technology is risky.

Not waiting for a misidentification to make the news.

Deciding now, before the harm that our people deserve accuracy, accountability, and the right to challenge a system that gets it wrong.

The room where these standards get written is open.

As Africa, we walk in.

With evidence.

With solutions.

With the authority of people who understand what is at stake because we are living it.

One thing this week.

If your organisation uses any facial recognition or biometric technology; ask your vendor this.

"What are the accuracy rates of this system across different skin tones and demographic groups? And has it been tested specifically on African faces?"

Their answer tells you everything.
