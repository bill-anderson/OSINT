On 30 May 2026, I was invited to attend the [Oxford Frontier AI Forum 2026, hosted by the Oxford AI Societ](https://forum.oxai.org/) y. One of the sessions that stayed with me was the Sovereign AI Seminar: an exploration of UK sovereign AI and institutional leadership. The room was full of investors, engineers, founders, and policy thinkers, and the discussion kept circling back to a deceptively simple question: what does it actually mean for a country to have sovereign AI?

The conversation was focused on the UK, but the questions it raised apply just as directly to Africa. This article recaps the key themes from that discussion and asks whether the frameworks being debated at forums like this one translate to a continent navigating the same questions with a very different set of starting conditions.

**One answer kept surfacing, dressed in different words: sovereignty is not self-sufficiency, it is leverage.**

That framing should matter enormously to Africa. Not as a borrowed European concern, but as a lens through which the continent’s own digital future looks clearer and more urgent.

### Rethinking Sovereignty

Most conversations about AI sovereignty start in the wrong place, with infrastructure tallies, GPU counts, and headline national strategies. A more useful starting point is a simpler question: what does leverage actually look like, and where does Africa already have it?

A metaphor from one of the speakers framed the choice well. At one end sits the Tower of Babel: centralised, hyperscaler-led AI, powerful and resource-intensive, concentrated in few hands. At the other sits Jerusalem: distributed, human-scale, built brick by brick. The instinct for many African nations has been to reach for Babel, to chase data centres and headline strategies, when the numbers suggest a Jerusalem path may be more durable, and more honest about where real leverage lies.

The panel was direct: no country achieves full AI independence. The EU depends on American chips. America depends on Taiwanese manufacturing. The UK built a world-class AI sector without owning a single frontier model from scratch. What matters is not control of every layer. What matters is having enough leverage at the right layers to protect your interests and deliver value to your people.

### Where Africa Actually Stands

The numbers are sobering, but they are also clarifying. [Africa currently holds less than 1% of global data centre capacity](https://insights.techcabal.com/africas-ai-sovereignty-problem-is-really-an-energy-problem/). [An estimated 80% of the continent’s internet traffic is processed on foreign servers, most of them in Europe](https://insights.techcabal.com/africas-ai-sovereignty-problem-is-really-an-energy-problem/). Nigeria, Kenya, and South Africa together account for 41% of Africa’s 223 data centres, leaving the rest of the continent, 35 countries and hundreds of millions of users, sharing fewer than 130 facilities.

In April 2025, [54 African nations signed the Africa Declaration on Artificial Intelligence in Kigali, expressing a desire to establish a $60 billion AI Fund targeting infrastructure, talent, and startups.](https://www.theafricareport.com/383301/african-ai-fund-five-questions-on-how-africa-plans-to-join-the-race/) A bold statement of intent. Over a year later, [governance structures, funding sources, and implementation details remain largely undefined](https://medium.com/@impactnews-wire/africa-promised-60-billion-to-build-its-own-ai-can-it-deliver-in-2026-7134faf28da4), while [three-quarters of all global AI venture capital continues to flow to the US alone.](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-28/africa-startups-turn-inward-as-us-ai-boom-drains-venture-capital) The gap between ambition and architecture remains wide.

There are, however, signs that homegrown actors are starting to fill it. [In July 2025, MTN Nigeria launched the Dabengwa Sifiso Data Centre in Lagos](https://techpoint.africa/news/mtn-largest-data-centre/), currently the largest Tier III facility in West Africa, with cloud infrastructure the company claims rivals offerings from Amazon, Microsoft, and Google. T [he $235 million project, led by MTN’s infrastructure subsidiary Bayobab](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/south-african-telco-mtn-launches-first-phase-of-data-center-in-nigeria/), is explicitly designed for AI processing readiness. MTN has since confirmed South Africa and Nigeria as priority markets for greenfield data centre development under its Ambition 2030 strategy. This matters not just as a business story, but as a sovereignty signal: an African-headquartered operator building infrastructure on the continent, under African jurisdiction, rather than waiting for a hyperscaler to arrive with conditions attached.

The contrast with the Microsoft-G42 Kenya project is instructive. That facility, positioned as a flagship of East Africa’s AI ambitions, stalled when Kenya’s government pulled back from the computing commitments the companies demanded. President Ruto’s explanation was blunt: powering it would require shutting off electricity for half the country. This is not a story about African failure. It is a story about what happens when a sovereignty strategy is built around the Babel model, chasing hyperscale infrastructure rather than building from genuine leverage.

Energy is the constraint that sits underneath this tension. Data centres are not abstract digital assets; they are physical systems dependent on stable and scalable electricity supply. In many African contexts, compute ambition is now advancing faster than the energy infrastructure required to sustain it. Until that gap is addressed, sovereignty strategies will continue to collide with physical limits, regardless of capital, intent, or partnership structure.

### The Leverage Africa Already Has

If sovereignty is about leverage rather than self-sufficiency, the question changes entirely. It stops being *can Africa build its own large language models?* and starts being *where in the AI stack does Africa have real, durable power?*

The answer is more interesting than most conversations admit.

**Data diversity:** African languages, healthcare demographics, agricultural contexts, and financial behaviours represent training data that global AI systems are acutely underrepresented in. R [wanda’s ICT Minister Paula Ingabire made the point plainly in April 2026: AI tools trained on foreign datasets misdiagnose diseases, misread economic trends, and misclassify agricultural outputs.](https://www.aipolicy.africa/) That is not a gap to apologise for. It is a leverage point. Whoever builds AI systems that work reliably across Swahili, Yoruba, Hausa, Amharic, and Igbo will not just serve Africa. They will build something the world’s largest labs have consistently failed to.

**Regulatory positioning:** N [igeria’s National Data Protection Commission issued its largest fine to date in July 2025,](https://techcabal.com/2026/03/19/why-data-protection-has-become-africas-default-ai-policy-tool/) penalising Multichoice for unlawful data transfers. Kenya’s Data Protection Commissioner has similarly begun enforcement. By early 2026, 44 African countries had implemented data protection laws. The regulatory infrastructure exists. The question is whether it is deployed strategically, to create leverage, or bureaucratically, to create friction.

**The open-source opportunity:** Several African AI initiatives are being built on open-source models fine-tuned on local data, fitting models to the demographic profile of local healthcare systems, training on local languages, and reflecting local context. This is not a consolation prize for lacking compute. It is, arguably, the smarter architecture. The UK’s own competitive edge in AI is not in the size of its models. It is in the quality of its applications and its talent for doing more with less. Africa can own that same position.

Of these three leverage points, regulatory positioning is the most immediately actionable: 44 countries already have the legal infrastructure and the question is political will. Data diversity is the most strategically durable, compounding over time in ways outside actors cannot replicate. The open-source path is the most practically accessible right now, requiring neither large capital nor government coordination to begin. Energy remains the binding constraint underneath all three. Sequence matters: build on open-source foundations while constructing the data and regulatory assets that will eventually make African AI genuinely irreplaceable rather than merely locally relevant.

### The Middle Power Question

At the Forum, one of the more interesting moments was a brief reference to Canada, listed alongside Singapore as a potential partner for the UK to work with. The UK, the panel noted, is a middle power. It does not have the scale of the US or China, but it has real assets: research talent, a strong regulatory voice, and an existing network of trusted allies. The question is not whether middle powers can play in AI. It is how they play, and on whose terms.

Most African nations are not yet middle powers in the AI stack. They are earlier than that, still building the foundations of digital infrastructure, regulatory capacity, and technical talent that middle power status requires. But that is precisely what makes the framing useful. The middle power position is not where Africa starts. It is where the continent could realistically aim, if the right choices are made now. The instinct for many African nations is to resolve this by picking a side: align with US hyperscalers, or look east toward Chinese infrastructure investment. But the Oxford discussion gestures at a third path, one built on collective positioning, shared leverage, and clarity about where your genuine strengths lie.

The African Union’s Continental AI Strategy, [the AfCFTA Digital Trade Protocol](https://techcabal.com/2026/03/19/why-data-protection-has-become-africas-default-ai-policy-tool/), and [Smart Africa's AI Council](https://restofworld.org/2026/africa-ai-sovereignty-big-tech/) are the architecture of exactly that kind of positioning. The risk is not that Africa lacks the frameworks. It is that the frameworks remain disconnected from each other, and from the commercial and technical actors who could give them teeth. What a nascent power building toward middle power status actually needs is not more strategies. It is intentional choices, made clearly, and defended across political cycles.

### What the Middle Ground Actually Looks Like

Morocco may offer the clearest proof of concept so far. [In June 2025, a consortium announced a 500-megawatt, renewables-powered AI infrastructure project on the Atlantic coast, under Moroccan jurisdiction and not subject to the US CLOUD Act.](https://interface.media/blog/2025/11/20/data-sovereignty-africas-strategic-imperative-in-the-age-of-algorithmic-power/) It is designed to export compute across Europe, the Middle East, and Africa. That is not self-sufficiency. It is strategic positioning at a chokepoint.

Nigeria and Kenya are attempting something similar at the policy layer. Both released national AI strategies in early 2025 that explicitly name dependence on US tech companies as a strategic risk. [Kenya hosted a joint OECD-UK AI governance workshop in Nairobi in March 2026, s](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-artificial-intelligence-case-studies_c517fcf5-en/ai-governance-in-africa_1ff55135-en.html) ignalling its intent to be a rule-maker, not just a rule-taker, in the emerging global AI order.

And then there is MTN’s move, significant precisely because it did not come from government. It came from a commercial actor making a strategic bet that African infrastructure, owned and operated on African terms, is the better long-term position. When private capital starts to reach the same conclusion as policy, the middle ground stops being a theory and starts being a market.

None of this is enough yet. But it points toward the right architecture: not building walls, but building choices.

### The Deeper Question

The Sovereign AI Seminar ended with a question from the floor about energy, which the panellists called the elephant in the room. Talent, regulation, compute: all of these can be addressed at the margin. Energy is the hard constraint, particularly for a continent where data centre ambitions consistently run ahead of grid capacity. The Kenya-Microsoft stall was, at root, an energy story as much as a sovereignty one.

That observation cuts both ways for Africa. It is a real limitation. But it is also an opportunity. The continent holds some of the world’s largest renewable energy potential, and the countries that invest in energy infrastructure on their own terms are not just solving a constraint. They are building the foundation that makes everything else possible.

The middle ground in the end is not a compromise position. It is a deliberate one. Know where you have leverage and build there first. Defend it across political cycles, not just within them. And resist the pull of the Babel model, not because scale is wrong, but because borrowed scale, on someone else’s terms, is the opposite of sovereignty. The countries that solve the energy-compute equation on their own infrastructure will not just achieve AI sovereignty, they will have something the world will want to buy.

### A Final Thought

The conversation at Oxford was largely about the UK as a single entity navigating a single question. But Africa is not one country. It is 54, each with a different resource base, regulatory maturity, energy profile, and relationship with technology. The most interesting version of the African AI sovereignty conversation is not a continental one. It is a country-by-country one, where each nation asks a specific question: where in the AI stack do we actually have leverage?

South Africa, with the continent’s largest installed energy capacity and its most mature enterprise technology ecosystem, is already attracting global cloud infrastructure investment, with Microsoft, Amazon, and Alibaba operating cloud services there, and MTN selecting it as one of two priority markets for its AI data centre build-out. Rwanda’s governance institutions and regulatory track record position it to lead on data standards and AI policy frameworks. Nigeria’s population size, language diversity, and consumer market give it significant leverage at the data and applications layer. Other countries will find their own positions, whether in renewable energy, talent development, research, digital trade, or specialised datasets.

The mistake would be to assume sovereignty requires every country to do everything. No nation, not even the United States, controls every layer of the AI stack. Sovereignty is not built through self-sufficiency. It is built through leverage.

For Africa, that means identifying where each country has durable advantages, investing deliberately at those layers, and connecting those strengths into something that functions as a continental ecosystem. The question is not whether Africa can replicate Silicon Valley or Shenzhen. It is whether African countries can build the pieces of the future that the rest of the world cannot easily replace.

That is the conversation worth having. Not how Africa catches up, but where Africa leads.
