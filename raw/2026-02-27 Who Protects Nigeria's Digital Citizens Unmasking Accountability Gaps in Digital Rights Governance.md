In recent years, Nigeria’s rapid digitalisation has [expanded](https://allafrica.com/stories/202510290078.html?utm_source=) access to online platforms while also raising concerns about restrictions on civic expression. Although the growth of the digital space has supported a more vibrant online public sphere in Nigeria, reports suggest that [criminal](https://guardian.ng/news/falana-urges-fg-others-to-end-digital-repression-uphold-press-freedom/?utm_source=) laws and surveillance measures have, at times, been used by security agencies in ways critics say may limit dissent, particularly as citizens, including young people and activists, use social media to express views on political leadership.

Available [reports](https://platformtimes.com.ng/press-freedom-under-siege-lagos-abuja-kano-named-worst-offenders/?utm_source=) further indicate that while some incidents affecting civic space have received widespread media attention, others have attracted limited coverage, highlighting concerns about inconsistencies in how such cases are reported.

Internet connectivity in Nigeria has expanded rapidly, with more than half of the population now having [access](https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2024-nigeria?utm_source=) to online platforms. However, political tensions have increasingly extended into the digital space.

For example, on June 5, 2021, the Nigerian government, led by Muhammadu Buhari, announced the indefinite [suspension](https://www.reuters.com/technology/nigeria-indefinitely-suspends-twitter-operations-information-minister-2021-06-04/) of Twitter (now X) after the platform deleted posts by the former president. Government officials at the time [justified](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-57368535) the decision, stating that the platform was being used in ways that could “undermine Nigeria’s corporate existence.”

According to [NetBlocks](https://netblocks.org/cost/), an international internet monitoring organisation, the suspension was estimated to have cost Nigeria about N6 billion within the first three days. Further estimates by the Lagos Chamber of Commerce and Industry (LCCI) suggested that the economic impact [rose](https://african.business/2022/01/technology-information/nigeria-lost-26bn-to-twitter-ban-businesses-say) to over N10.72 trillion during the seven-month restriction period.

Also, Freedom House, in its Freedom on the Net 2024 [report](https://freedomhouse.org/country/nigeria), assigned Nigeria an internet freedom score of 59 out of 100, classifying Nigeria as “partly free.” The report highlights ongoing concerns in the digital space and notes that, despite legislative reforms such as a new data protection law and amendments to existing cybercrime legislation, cases of state actions affecting online journalists, intermediaries, and activists have continued to be reported.

## Social Media Regulation and Online Censorship

Even where internet access remains uninterrupted, evolving regulations have raised concerns about online content moderation and free expression. In October 2022, the National Information Technology Development [Agency](https://freedomhouse.org/country/nigeria/freedom-net/2023#footnoteref2_idR9JSBMneeIrp-kqlkYnjBJdz6q6-yK844a7OLpFTM_dMamBN2iFd12#:~:text=,intermediaries%20to%20remove%20unlawful%20content) (NITDA) approved the Code of Practice for Interactive Computer Service Platforms, which requires internet intermediaries to remove unlawful content within 48 hours. Analysts note that the directive could have implications for freedom of expression by placing greater responsibility on platforms to regulate user content. The framework, implemented in collaboration with the telecom regulator, applies to companies such as Facebook, Google, and TikTok, requiring them to comply with Nigerian content regulations.

By mid-2025, the government [reported](https://thenationonlineng.net/breaking-fg-shuts-down-over-13-million-social-media-accounts-for-offensive-content/) that platforms had deactivated over 13.5 million Nigerian accounts for “offensive content” and removed nearly 59 million posts. NITDA spokesperson Hajiya Hadiza Umar described the development as a step toward promoting a safer, more responsible digital environment, stating that it reflected efforts to ensure a secure, trustworthy online space.

Efforts to regulate social media have also received high-level political support. In 2019, Nigeria’s former Minister of Information, Lai Mohammed, [stated](https://www.premiumtimesng.com/news/headlines/358690-again-nigeria-govt-vows-to-deal-with-purveyors-of-fake-news-hate-speech.html) that the government intended to “inject rationality into the social media space” by addressing misinformation and hate speech. However, when questioned about proposed legislation, including a draft “Protection from Internet Falsehoods and Manipulations” bill and a related hate-speech bill, he denied the existence of such proposals, despite earlier public discussions around regulatory measures that could impose penalties for certain online content.

The debate over digital regulation has [generated](https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/press-release/2019/12/nigeria-bills-on-hate-speech-and-social-media-are-dangerous-attacks-on-freedom-of-expression/) sustained public scrutiny. Civil society and human rights organisations, including Amnesty International, raised concerns about proposals perceived to place significant restrictions on freedom of expression. Some earlier drafts reportedly included severe penalties for certain forms of speech, drawing widespread criticism before the provisions were subsequently revised.

## Legal Harassment Under Cybercrime Laws

Beyond platform-level regulations, criminal laws have increasingly been applied to online expression. Nigeria’s Cybercrimes (Prohibition, Prevention, etc.) Act 2015, originally enacted to address fraud and hacking, has been used in recent years in cases involving journalism and political commentary. Of particular concern is Section 24 of the Act, which prescribes up to three years’ imprisonment or a fine for anyone who “knowingly or intentionally” sends a message considered false with the intent to cause annoyance or similar effects. Media advocacy groups argue that the provision’s broad and ambiguous wording makes it susceptible to misuse against journalists and critics.

In 2024, Reporters Without [Borders](https://rsf.org/en/nigeria-least-eight-journalists-prosecuted-under-cybercrime-law-despite-new-amendment#:~:text=At%20least%20eight%20journalists%20have,that%20reporters%20can%20work%20safely) reported that at least eight journalists had been arrested, prosecuted, or detained under the Cybercrimes Act, despite amendments intended to limit its scope. The organisation noted that the law’s vague provisions remain problematic and warned that continued prosecutions could undermine investigative reporting on corruption and governance.

Several reported incidents reflect these concerns. In May 2020, operatives of the Department of State Services arrested Ambrose [Nwaogwugwu](https://mfwa.org/how-critical-online-journalism-social-media-activism-became-cybercrime-in-nigeria/#:~:text=On%20same%20June%205%2C%20this,them%20under%20the%20cybercrime%20law), a social media manager affiliated with the Peoples Democratic Party in Imo State, following a satirical online post about the state governor. He was charged with cyber-related offences, including defamation and publication of false information, and detained for several weeks before being granted bail.

Similarly, in December 2024, Lagos-based nurse Olamide Thomas was [arrested](https://www.zikoko.com/citizen/arrests-of-citizens-by-nigerian-government/#:~:text=Months%20later%2C%20on%20Friday%2C%20December,to%20lead%20them%20to%20her) after livestreaming remarks critical of President Bola Tinubu and senior officials. She was charged with cyberbullying and detained until bail was granted days later. In another case in March 2024, Lagos authorities briefly detained activist Abdulganiu Olushipo on [allegations](https://www.zikoko.com/citizen/arrests-of-citizens-by-nigerian-government/#:~:text=Abdulganiu%20Olushipo%3A%20arrested%20for%20demanding,accountability%20on%20budget%20allocation) of cyberstalking after he publicly questioned a local government budget.

In March 2024, the editor of the online news platform FirstNews, Segun Olatunji, was [reportedly](https://www.zikoko.com/citizen/arrests-of-citizens-by-nigerian-government/#:~:text=On%20March%2015%2C%202024%2C%20Segun,Gbajabiamila%20of%20looting%20%2430%20billion) detained by military personnel after publishing a report alleging financial misconduct involving a senior government official. He was held in Abuja for 14 days before being released following public outcry. He later resigned from his position, citing security concerns.

These cases illustrate a pattern in which ordinary Nigerians risk severe consequences for online speech. The charges may vary (from “false publication” to “cyberstalking” or even “terrorism”), but the effect is similar: fear and self-censorship.

## Surveillance and Privacy Violations

Beyond content regulation and censorship, concerns have also been raised about the expansion of surveillance capabilities in Nigeria. In December 2020, researchers at Citizen Lab, based at the University of Toronto, [reported](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2020/12/8/nigerias-defence-agency-acquires-spy-equipment-says-report#:~:text=Nigeria%E2%80%99s%20Defence%20Intelligence%20Agency%20has,surveillance%2C%20security%2C%20privacy%20and%20accountability) that Nigeria’s Defence Intelligence Agency had reportedly acquired telecommunications interception technology from Circles, a company known for developing surveillance tools similar to those associated with NSO Group. According to the report, such systems could enable authorities to monitor mobile phone communications in real time without detection on targeted devices. The findings also suggested that similar technologies may have been used at subnational levels, including by political actors in some states, though these claims have been subject to public debate and official scrutiny.

Concerns have also been expressed about government policies on digital identity systems. Since 2020, authorities have required citizens to link SIM cards, bank accounts, and other services to a National Identification Number (NIN) as part of efforts to strengthen national identity management and security. Civil society organisations, including the Human Rights Writers [Association](https://www.icirnigeria.org/nin-registration-violates-nigerians-privacy-rights-huriwa/#:~:text=In%20a%20statement%20by%20its,internet%20users%20in%20the%20country) of Nigeria (HURIWA), have criticised the policy, arguing that it may pose risks to privacy and could enable monitoring of individuals’ online activities. The group has cited constitutional protections for the privacy of communications and called for stronger safeguards and oversight mechanisms.

Civil rights groups immediately warned that this violates constitutional privacy rights.

The [Human Rights Writers Association (HURIWA)](https://www.icirnigeria.org/nin-registration-violates-nigerians-privacy-rights-huriwa/#:~:text=In%20a%20statement%20by%20its,internet%20users%20in%20the%20country) filed complaints in 2021, pointing out that the NIN mandate effectively allows the state “to know the identity of internet users,” meaning the government could, in practice, “spy on citizens” simply by tracing their online accounts. (Section 37 of the Nigerian Constitution explicitly guarantees the privacy of communications.) In their view, such sweeping surveillance powers are a recipe for abuse.

Nigeria’s recent 2023 Data Protection Act (Nigerian Data Protection Act) did create a formal [Data Protection Commission](https://fij.ng/article/how-nigerias-data-protection-law-created-regulator-then-weakened-it/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CIf%20a%20regulator%20needs%20ministerial,Television%20in%20a%20September%20interview), but it alarmed critics by placing the commission under ministerial control. As data-privacy lawyer Habeeb Eyinade noted, a regulator that “needs ministerial clearance before acting” is hardly [independent](https://fij.ng/article/how-nigerias-data-protection-law-created-regulator-then-weakened-it/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CIf%20a%20regulator%20needs%20ministerial,Television%20in%20a%20September%20interview).

This matrix explains how many agencies claim parts of digital governance, but none have the full enforcement, oversight, coordination or citizen-redress capacity. Without reform, these gaps create a vacuum, one in which digital rights violations proliferate unchecked.

The fragility of identity protection became evident in 2024 when Paradigm Initiative alleged that up to 43 million Nigerians’ National Identification Number (NIN) records were exposed online, including names, addresses, phone numbers and passport photographs. The case, reported by [AllAfrica and other media platforms](https://allafrica.com/stories/202412050244.html), led to litigation but not to any publicly released independent audit or transparent breach-notification process for affected citizens. The episode illustrated the accountability vacuum around the National Identity Management Commission (NIMC): while it holds one of Africa’s largest biometric databases, citizens have little clarity about how breaches are investigated or how victims can seek redress.

In the telecommunications sector, the Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC) came under scrutiny during the 2021 suspension of Twitter, when telecom providers were directed to block access nationwide. The shutdown, which lasted over seven months, disrupted businesses, limited political discourse, and drew international condemnation. Yet there was no publicly accessible transparency report detailing the legal justification, review mechanism, or proportionality assessment of the action. [Freedom House’s *Freedom*](https://freedomhouse.org/country/nigeria/freedom-net/2024) *on the Net 2024* documents the suspension as a significant infringement on online freedom in Nigeria.

At the data protection level, the gap between legal frameworks and enforcement has also been visible. While NITDA oversaw the Nigeria Data Protection Regulation (NDPR) before the 2023 Data Protection Act, repeated reports of breaches across sectors rarely translated into widely publicised sanctions or transparency on compliance. [Freedom House](https://freedomhouse.org/country/nigeria/freedom-net/2024) notes that although Nigeria has adopted reforms, enforcement and protections remain inconsistent, reinforcing concerns that regulatory authority has not always translated into visible deterrence.

Security oversight has raised further concerns. The Office of the National Security Adviser (ONSA), working within the broader cybersecurity and intelligence architecture, operates alongside enforcement of the Cybercrimes Act, a law that is repeatedly used to arrest journalists and online commentators. [Freedom House 2024](https://freedomhouse.org/country/nigeria/freedom-net/2024) documents cases, including the arrest of journalist Daniel Ojukwu and the detention of media executive Saint Mienpamo Onitsha. Meanwhile, [SERAP](https://serap-nigeria.org/2025/01/12/serap-takes-tinubu-govt-governors-to-ecowas-court-over-misuse-of-cybercrimes-act/) has challenged aspects of the Cybercrimes Act at the ECOWAS Court, arguing that certain provisions remain vague and open to abuse. These cases illustrate the tension between cybersecurity enforcement and civil liberties, particularly where oversight reporting and judicial transparency are limited.

At the policy level, Nigeria’s National Digital Economy Policy and Strategy articulated ambitious goals for digital transformation. Yet [Paradigm Initiative’s LONDA report](https://paradigmhq.org/report/londa-digital-rights-and-inclusion-annual-report-2021-nigeria/) highlights the disconnect between policy ambition and lived digital realities, where unlawful arrests, weak data safeguards and limited redress mechanisms persist. This suggests that while the Ministry of Communications provides strategic direction, implementation across agencies remains uneven and coordination gaps persist.

Finally, the judiciary and civil society reveal the limits of institutional inclusion. Digital rights cases often involve prolonged detention or delayed resolution, as documented in [Freedom House reports](https://freedomhouse.org/country/nigeria/freedom-net/2024). At the same time, civil society organisations such as [Paradigm Initiative and SERAP](https://www.vanguardngr.com/2025/09/cso-raises-concern-over-safety-of-nigerians-online-data/) have frequently had to resort to litigation and public advocacy to demand accountability, rather than participating through formalised oversight structures. Vanguard and other outlets have reported repeated CSO concerns over online data safety and transparency deficits.

Taken together, these events demonstrate that Nigeria’s digital rights governance gaps are systemic rather than episodic. Identity breaches without transparent notification, shutdowns without accountability reports, arrests under expansive cybercrime provisions, uneven enforcement of data protection rules, policy–implementation disconnects, delayed judicial relief, and civil society operating from the margins all point to a fragmented ecosystem where mandates exist, but coordinated accountability remains weak.
