# House Reviews Privacy, Data Protection Bill

By Leroy M. Sonpon — Liberian Observer, June 11, 2025

The House of Representatives has directed the Committee on Post and Telecommunication to review a proposed legislation aimed at protecting individual privacy and promoting responsible data management. The decision was made during the 9th day sitting of the 2nd Quarter of the 2nd Session of the 55th Legislature on Tuesday, June 10.

The Bill titled "An Act for the Collection, Processing, Transmission, Storage, Protection, and Use of Personal Information in Liberia" was submitted to the Legislature by President Joseph Nyumah Boakai. "The Bill seeks to govern the processing of all forms of personal information by both individuals and entities handling the personal data of any resident in Liberia, the ECOWAS Region, or beyond, including data controllers and processors," Boakai wrote in his communication.

The proposed law, developed by the Ministry of Posts and Telecommunications (MoPT) with assistance from Internews as part of the European Union-funded Liberia Media Empowerment Project (LMEP), is a standalone comprehensive instrument. Currently, Liberia lacks a dedicated law specifically addressing personal data protection. Upon enactment, this legislation will take precedence over all other existing laws, decrees, executive orders, proclamations, and administrative regulations pertaining to personal data. It will also impose penalties for unauthorized access, processing, and use of personal data, including the concealment of breaches.

The instrument met the approval of dozens of stakeholders who validated it in December 2024 in Monrovia. Minister of Posts and Telecommunications Sekou M. Kromah said: "As we move towards a more digitized society, we must establish a robust legal framework to protect the privacy of our citizens." EU Ambassador to Liberia Nona Deprez said: "The EU has the strongest data protection rules in the world because we recognize that protection of personal data is a fundamental right," outlining risks posed by the absence of a data protection framework including erosion of trust, discrimination, disinformation, and legal uncertainty. Plenary unanimously agreed to refer the Bill to the Committee for review and recommendations within two weeks. The bill aligns with the ECOWAS Supplementary Act on Personal Data Protection agreed by member states in February 2010.
