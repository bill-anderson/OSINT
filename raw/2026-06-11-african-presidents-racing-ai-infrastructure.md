# Why African presidents are now racing on AI infrastructure

By Adonijah Ndege — TechCabal, 11 June 2026

When African leaders gathered in Nairobi on 12 May 2026 for the Africa Forward Summit, artificial intelligence took centre stage alongside energy, agriculture and international finance for the first time. Only a few years ago, AI policy on the continent revolved around ethics, digital literacy and startup incubation; now governments are discussing cloud infrastructure, sovereign data, regional computing capacity and local language models.

In the past two years, Kenya unveiled a National AI Strategy, Nigeria launched its National AI Strategy, Rwanda established a Centre for the Fourth Industrial Revolution, South Africa stepped up work on a national AI policy, and the African Union adopted its Continental AI Strategy calling for African-owned data, compute infrastructure and language models.

The shift reflects growing recognition that AI is becoming a geopolitical asset. That dependence informed Section 6 of the **Africa Forward Declaration**, adopted by African and French leaders in Nairobi and signed by 30 heads of state, documenting calls for investment in data centres, cloud computing, trusted data systems, broadband, and African-led ownership of data and AI systems. Despite ~20% of the world's population and fast-growing internet markets, the continent hosts less than 1% of global data-centre capacity. Mobile data consumption is growing ~40% annually (nearly twice the global average), but processing/storage infrastructure remains severely constrained. Most AI-infrastructure commitments come from the private sector and DFIs — in April the IFC committed $100 million to regional operator Raxio Group, its largest African digital-infrastructure investment, backing facilities from Ethiopia to Angola.
