Uganda is preparing to open a major new project called the Aeonian Project, described as Africa's first AI data center fully owned and run by Africans. The project costs $1.2 billion and is being built inside the Karuma Hydropower Plant on the Nile River.

The plant makes electricity from flowing water, so the data center will use clean, renewable energy — running on extra power from the plant (up to 100 megawatts) and staying cool using natural river water, avoiding pollution and wasted heat. The center will include a super-fast computer called USIO, powered by the latest NVIDIA technology. The first part should start working in 2026, with full setup complete by 2028. A long fiber-optic cable will connect it to the internet through Kenya and Tanzania.

The article frames the significance for everyday people: currently nearly all African data gets sent to computers in other countries for AI work, which can mean AI tools may not understand Ugandan languages, local farming methods or culture properly. With a local center, Ugandans could build AI fitted to local needs — apps for farmers to check soil and weather, faster health diagnoses, or improved learning tools. Professor Lawrence Muganga of Victoria University argued on TV that Africa must control its own AI to avoid biases.

The piece notes Uganda already has growing data centers — Raxio Group runs one in Namanve, and Centenary Group is opening a green one soon — and that the government is pushing for more eco-friendly centers to support cloud services, e-government and digital business. Experts say the Aeonian Project will bring thousands of jobs, attract investment and help Uganda lead tech in East Africa.
