Through an online advocacy campaign, SpaceX's satellite Internet service Starlink is intensifying lobbying to get an operating license in South Africa.

(Source: Image by natanaelginting on Freepik)

Low-Earth orbit (LEO) satellite provider Starlink is intensifying its push toward getting an operating license in South Africa, through an advocacy campaign aimed at drumming up local support.

Titled [Starlink's Efforts in South Africa](https://starlink.com/lc/support/article/6bf8d6d4-4bc1-7af7-f907-c2f036a5740c), the page aims to clarify myths and facts in response to critics' concerns about Black economic empowerment, national security, monopoly risks, and the company's commitment to the local market.

The page deepens the company's public lobbying efforts in South Africa.

It follows an email sent out in January 2026 to its South African database urging potential customers to send pre-written messages to the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) to [show support for proposed regulatory changes](https://www.connectingafrica.com/regulation/starlink-asks-sa-citizens-to-pressure-regulator-over-license) that will allow it to obtain a license in the country.

The advocacy page not only aims to clarify myths and facts, but it also creates an online platform for Starlink to plea its case.

"Starlink is committed to providing reliable, high-speed Internet access across South Africa – especially in rural and underserved areas where traditional connectivity falls short," Starlink's page explains.

It said that, however, it is unable to apply for the licenses required to operate due to current ownership regulations, but that may soon change.

As it stands, South Africa's telecom licensing regulations mandate that licensed companies must have at least 30% ownership by historically disadvantaged South Africans, in accordance with the country's Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (B-BBEE) laws.

## Starlink's central arguments

Starlink said in May 2025, South Africa's Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) Minister Solly Malatsi [issued a draft policy directive](https://www.connectingafrica.com/regulation/sa-minister-opens-door-for-starlink-with-new-policy-directive) proposing that companies like Starlink be allowed to meet local ownership requirements through Equity Equivalent Investment Programs (EEIPs).

"This model is already used in other industries and allows multinational companies to invest meaningfully in South Africa's transformation goals without requiring local equity ownership," Starlink said.

South Africa's Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies Solly Malatsi. (Source: South African Government)

Central to the latest wave of advocacy is Starlink's R500 million (US$31 million) commitment, which [it announced in June 2025](https://www.connectingafrica.com/investment/starlink-commits-27-6m-to-connect-rural-sa-schools), to provide free, high‑speed internet to 5,000 rural schools, a move the company said could impact over 2.4 million learners annually.

Starlink added that for the investment to go ahead, ICASA's licensing regulations must be aligned with existing law and the B-BBEE ICT Sector Code, which already recognizes EEIPs.

In October 2025, Starlink's parent company SpaceX – founded by South-African born billionaire Elon Musk – confirmed that it planned to comply with B-BBEE regulations and promised [to invest a total of R2.5 billion](https://www.connectingafrica.com/regulation/starlink-to-comply-with-b-bbee-invest-146m-in-south-africa) ($145.6 million at the time) in the country.

## South African push back

On December 12, 2025, Minister Malatsi issued the policy direction to ICASA [via a Government Gazette](https://www.gov.za/sites/default/files/gcis_document/202512/53855gen3692.pdf) calling for the regulator to urgently consider alignment of its regulations with the national ICT Sector Code.

ICASA noted the publication of the directive a day later and said it would review and consider the policy direction through its established regulatory processes.

However, the directive was not met with support from parliament.

The chairperson of the Portfolio Committee on Communications and Digital Technologies, Khusela Sangoni Diko, issued a statement [calling for the immediate withdrawal of the policy direction](https://www.parliament.gov.za/press-releases/media-statement-communications-committee-chairperson-calls-mr-malatsi-withdraw-his-starlink-directives).

"These policy directives are an affront to the centuries old fight for equity and redress by the Black majority in this country," Diko said.

"They further unintelligibly obfuscate existing law in a spirited attempt to circumvent the mandatory 30% equity ownership by historically disadvantaged groups as a requirement for electronic communications services licensing as under section 9(2)(b) of the Electronic Communications Act (ECA)," the chairperson added.

## Starlink's African business

Starlink has increased its African footprint considerably and just last week [launched its services in Senegal](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/spacex-s-starlink-goes-live-in-senegal).

It has been making inroads on the continent since launching in Africa's most populous country of [Nigeria in January 2023](https://www.connectingafrica.com/broadband/elon-musk-s-starlink-goes-live-in-nigeria).

In August 2025, the company launched services in [Somalia](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/starlink-lights-up-in-somalia), three months after it was [granted it an operating license](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/starlink-gets-greenlight-in-somalia).

The Internet service [launched in Chad](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/starlink-goes-live-in-chad) in July 2025 and a month before that in [the mountain kingdom of Lesotho](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/starlink-lights-up-lesotho).

Starlink's entry into Lesotho followed launches in [the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC)](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/starlink-live-in-drc) and [Guinea-Bissau](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/starlink-enters-guinea-bissau).

Starlink is fully up and running in Lesotho, [Botswana, Ghana](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/botswana-ghana-get-starlink), DRC, [Zimbabwe](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/starlink-beams-into-zimbabwean-market), [Zambia](https://www.connectingafrica.com/broadband/spacex-s-starlink-launches-in-zambia), [Malawi](https://www.connectingafrica.com/broadband/spacex-s-starlink-launches-in-malawi), [Mozambique,](https://www.connectingafrica.com/broadband/spacex-s-starlink-goes-live-in-mozambique) Eswatini, [Madagascar](https://www.connectingafrica.com/broadband/starlink-beams-into-madagascar), [Nigeria](https://www.connectingafrica.com/broadband/elon-musk-s-starlink-goes-live-in-nigeria), [Niger](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/starlink-beams-into-niger), Liberia, [Benin](https://www.connectingafrica.com/broadband/elon-musk-s-starlink-enters-benin), Guinea-Bissau, [Sierra Leone](https://www.connectingafrica.com/broadband/spacex-s-starlink-goes-live-in-sierra-leone), Somalia, Senegal, Chad, Cape Verde, [Kenya](https://www.connectingafrica.com/broadband/elon-musk-s-starlink-enters-kenya), [South Sudan](https://www.connectingafrica.com/regulation/south-sudan-unveils-approved-starlink-tariffs), Rwanda, [Burundi](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/elon-musk-hosts-african-leaders-while-starlink-faces-challenges-in-kenya) and São Tomé and Príncipe.
