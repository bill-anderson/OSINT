> **This document is broader than the queue line describing it.** It is not a Guinea-Bissau-only
> PAD but the **regional SOP1 PAD** (Operation ID P176932) covering The Gambia, Guinea,
> Guinea-Bissau and Mauritania plus the AU, Smart Africa Alliance and ECOWAS, total

>
> **The two Guinea-Bissau figures are not an inconsistency — they are different quantities.**
> Guinea-Bissau appears as **US$60.00m** in the IDA Resources table (an IDA *grant*, SDR 45.7m
> equivalent) and as **US$60.50m** in the component-cost table (Table 2). The US$0.50m difference
> is the **national-government counterpart contribution**, which Table 2's country columns include
> and the IDA table excludes. The PROJECT FINANCING DATA block confirms it: Counterpart Funding
> **1.50** = National Government **0.50** + National Government **1.00** — matching Guinea-Bissau
> (60.00 → 60.50) and Mauritania (60.00 → 61.00) exactly. Write it as **US$60.00m IDA grant plus
> US$0.50m government counterpart = US$60.50m total (2023-11-07)**, not as a bare "US$60M grant"
> and not as a discrepancy. *(Table 2 does carry a minor arithmetic looseness of its own: its
> country cells sum to 268.00 because they include the 1.50 counterpart, while the row total is
> printed as the IDA-only 266.50.)*
>
> The captured portion **does** contain the ECOWAS **Amílcar Cabral Submarine Cable** project
> rationale and the Guinea-Bissau legal covenants (Schedule 2 obligations).
>
> **On what is absent — state this carefully.** Neither the **2,877 km backbone** figure nor the
> **govcloud / data-protection-regulator** specifics attributed to WARDIP-GB appear anywhere in the
> **captured portion**. But this capture is an `excerpt`: extraction was capped at 200,000
> characters and stops **mid-way through the results-framework tables**, which is precisely where
> per-country indicator targets would sit. So the honest finding is that **this PAD, as held, does
> not evidence those claims** — *not* that the PAD contradicts or omits them. Establishing either
> would need the untruncated results framework or the Guinea-Bissau-specific project documents
> (PIM / RNEP-GB final report).

I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

A. Country Context 
1. Member countries of the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) 1 are amongst the poorest in the 
world and are experiencing a growing development gap with other regions. The population of the Western Africa, 
comprising of 15 ECOWAS countries and Mauritania, is 420 million people. The majority reside in rural areas and are 
among the poorest in the world. The average gross domestic product (GDP) in 2021 in the region was less than 
US$2,000 per capita and as low as US$595 (Niger) and US$516 (Sierra Leone). 2 Within the ECOWAS, eight countries 
are members of the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU) and share the same currency, the CFA 
franc, and monetary policies. 3 
2. Political instability and the threat of civil conflict in the Western Africa region impede economic growth, good governance, and high living standards. Five countries in the ECOWAS + Mauritania region are classified as fragile and conflict affected situations (FCS): Burkina Faso, Guinea-Bissau, Mali, Niger, and Nigeria. 4 Many countries depend on commodity exports as a driver of economic growth, however rising food insecurity due to pest outbreaks, climate change and weather shocks have significantly increased the risk of falling into a regional fragility trap. Other factors, such as rising global inflation, are continuing to impact food imports and domestic prices. 
3. Inequality is pronounced for marginalized people, including women and people with disabilities (PWDs). Women in the region are more likely than men to be informally employed due to structural barriers that limit their access to the formal workforce with significant differences in earnings and productivity between genders. In Mauritania, women's participation in the formal labor force is 29 percent, while men's is 63 percent. 5 In Gambia, women represent 46 percent of graduates in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) but only 35 percent of STEM related labor. 6 Despite a 10 percent population average of PWDs in Western Africa, their access to economic opportunities and jobs are limited due to low formal education rates as well as social, economic, and cultural obstacles. 7 For instance, in The Gambia, 79 percent of PWDs in 2018 were employed in the informal sector and the proportion of youth with disabilities (aged 15-24 years) not in education, employment or training was 33 percent. 8 

4. Western Africa faces severe impacts from climate change. Climate change is expected to affect the region, in particular the Sahel, with increasing incidences of drought, floods, desertification, and rising temperatures. B. Sectoral and Institutional Context 
5. West African economies can accelerate economic growth and reduce poverty through wider adoption of digital technologies fueled by affordable high-quality broadband, inclusive and safe digital public infrastructure, digitalization of high impact use cases, and integration of digital markets. The robustness of the regional digital economy heavily relies on economies of scale and network effects, for which regional integration is key given the many small countries in the region. Specifically, coordinated movement toward a single digital market (SDM) in Africa, based on the development and integration of national digital markets at the regional level and continental level, could help unlock new pathways for innovation, trade, and job creation. 9,10. Single digital markets remove cross border barriers, boost digital connectivity, and increase consumer access to online content across the continent. Experience from the European Union (EU) has shown that this endeavor requires several prerequisites, all of which remain challenges in Africa, including policy alignment at the regional level, similar levels of digital connectivity, homogenous data frameworks and governance structures, and a strong regional online market. Developing a Single Digital Market in Western Africa aligned with digital integration initiatives at Continental Level

Regional connectivity market in Western Africa: Improving affordability and resilience of broadband connectivity 
9. Western Africa faces significant disparities in terms of broadband connectivity, access, and usage - hindering inclusive access as well as growth of data and online markets. The average adoption rate of mobile broadband service by unique user in 2022 was below 40 percent in the region. In 2022, 3G and 4G mobile broadband coverage in Western Africa reached 94 percent and 73 percent, respectively 17, but 55 percent of people in the region live within range of broadband and do not use the Internet. The high retail prices for broadband services, utilizing fixed fiber and 3G+ mobile network technology, poses a barrier to usage. Entry level mobile broadband service costs approximately 7 percent of average monthly Gross National Income (GNI) per capita, compared to International Telecommunications Union’s (ITU) recommended 2 percent. Significant variations exist between countries, ranging from 9 percent (Sierra Leone) to 0.50 percent (Ghana). Main drivers for improving affordability of broadband services include competition at retail level, sector specific taxation, affordable and abundant international connectivity, and existence of cross-border wholesale fiber operators.

F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design

65. The project design draws on lessons learned from other World Bank-financed regional and/or digital projects and the wealth of analytical work on digital economy under the Digital Economy for Africa Initiative. 75 These include: (a) SOP approach allows flexible mechanism for countries and regional bodies to join in the future and to ensure coherence of country-level interventions. Although the program has a common continental and regional approach, the design enables each country, regional, and continental institution to implement its programs in parallel, which will help mitigate the risk of implementation delays. In addition, based on experience from previous regional projects in Africa, the project embeds strong TA to support regulators and line ministries in developing strong and successful private participation models, that can maximize use of private funding. Additional support and strong mitigation measures have been put in place to increase the technical capacity of the implementation units and minimize delays due to lack of understanding and experiences using World Bank procedures. This includes for example for AU using a central implementing unit with broader and more extensive experience in implementation. (b) Achieving an SDM requires cross-border integration of the multiple submarket structures, which form distinct yet interconnected layers of the overall SDM: connectivity, data, and online markets. Each layer builds on the other, creating a virtuous cycle to reinforce the development, expansion, and integration of the SDM. Simultaneous efforts building on ecosystem approach are required to build different layers of the SDM as well as to build domestic digital economies. This is a material evolution from previous regional engagements that focused on the connectivity foundation of the digital economy which is aligned with the continental framework for SDM. (c) PPP holds significant value for ensuring efficient and equitable management of deployed infrastructure, facilitated by public funds. The mechanism allows a qualified and performant technical partner to provide necessary technical expertise, while simultaneously incentivizing private partners to contribute to the commercial success of the project by sharing risks. This private participation in the management structure and risk-sharing arrangement not only ensures that private entities have a vested interest in the successful execution and operation of the project but also encourages innovation and cost efficiency. C. Sustainability

74. The DTfA/WARDIP SOP1 design emphasizes unlocking economic growth and cost savings through digitalization 
that will continue to grow beyond the life of the project. All infrastructure investment in telecommunications and 
data will be made in partnership with the private sector, and assets will be managed following the most suitable 
public-private partnership model within each country context. Additionally, the project focuses on upstream reforms 
necessary to unlock greater private sector investment, innovation, and market access in all three market layers of a 
SDM in Western Africa. It will also foster better employment opportunities by strengthening the digital innovation 
ecosystem, promoting digital financial skill adoption and e-commerce adoption in SMEs, and upgrading digital skills. 
It will introduce cost savings in public administration and service delivery by digitizing and automating core 
government functions that would accelerate digital transformation in a regionally harmonized effort and includes 
activities to build and retain technical expertise within government through capacity-building programs on aspects of 
the digital economy, including telecommunications, cybersecurity, and data. While many international 
firms/contractors and individual consultants will be hired to support the various technical activities envisaged, heavy 
emphasis will be placed on knowledge transfer to ensure that local staff benefit and can continue the work. 
75. A strong emphasis in capacity building and training programs supports the sustainable implementation of the DTS 
and establishment of the SDM. By supporting the SADA program, the project is ensuring that many decisions makers 
in the region and beyond have similar and sound digital foundations to ensure strong implementation of digital 

transformation. In addition, provided that SAA has a strong link to regional organizations, governments and private stakeholders in the region, it can leverage those networks to support the implementation of the SDM. 76. Many activities supported under the project are central to internal government operations and fall within core public service delivery mandates and therefore will require commitment from governments to sustain them financially after project closure. Measures to support sustainability for the SOP-1 have been developed according to component.

102. Grievance Redress. Communities and individuals who believe that they are adversely affected by a project supported by the World Bank may submit complaints to existing project-level grievance mechanisms or the World Bank’s Grievance Redress Service (GRS). The GRS ensures that complaints received are promptly reviewed in order to address project-related concerns. Project affected communities and individuals may submit their complaint to the World Bank’s independent Accountability Mechanism (AM). The AM houses the Inspection Panel, which determines whether harm occurred, or could occur, as a result of World Bank non-compliance with its policies and procedures, and the Dispute Resolution Service, which provides communities and borrowers with the opportunity to address complaints through dispute resolution. Complaints may be submitted to the AM at any time after concerns have been brought directly to the attention of Bank Management and after Management has been given an opportunity to respond. For information on how to submit complaints to the World Bank’s GRS, visit http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For information on how to submit complaints to the World Bank’s AM, visit https://accountability.worldbank.org. VI. KEY RISKS

103. The overall risk for the project is considered Substantial after accounting for mitigation measures. 
104. Political and governance risk is rated High. Western Africa has a challenging governance and fragile political environment. For example, in Guinea 2020 changes in its constitution resulted in episodes of violence reflecting popular frustration and culminated in seizure of power in a military coup on September 5, 2021 and led to a de facto government that will transition to a civilian government at the end of the 24-months period in December 2024 as agreed with ECOWAS; and legislative elections took place in Guinea-Bissau on June 4, 2023. Such events may negatively impact the ability to address the backlog of pending digitalization legislation to be adopted at the national 

level and approval of the new regional digital economy regulation. Mitigation measures include (i) provision of TA at 
the country level to augment the capacity of the relevant key policymakers, and awareness raising about the benefits 
of legal and regulatory modernization; and (ii) TA at the continental and regional level to monitor and disclose progress 
in implementation at the national level. 
105. Sector strategies and policies risk is rated Substantial. The establishment of an SDM is highly complex, due to the number of institutions involved in the implementation of its different layers and limited institutional and enforcement capacity points. Mitigation measures include (i) TA to support the establishment and strengthening of key digital public institutions; (ii) comprehensive capacity building and training program on digital economy policies and regulations for government officials; and (iii) mechanisms to monitor implementation of regulation at the national, regional, and continental level. Despite mitigation measures, the residual risk remains Substantial. 
106. Macroeconomic risk is rated Substantial. This rating reflects the risks the project may face at the country level. The region’s economies are struggling to recover from the 2020 recession, induced by the COVID-19 pandemic, and now face new economic growth challenges, compounded by the Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, which is severely impacting supply chains as well as contributing to inflation. The region’s economies are highly vulnerable to these external shocks, including devaluation of currencies. Macroeconomic trends will impact the ability of telecom operators to import the necessary equipment for infrastructure deployment, which is imported and usually purchased in US dollars, as well as their investment appetite. These factors will also impact citizens’ ability to afford new broadband services. As a mitigating measure, the project will provide gap financing to encourage private sector players to co-invest and deploy broadband infrastructure, as well as provide technical assistance to ensure a competitive environment that is conducive to the provision of affordable and innovative digital services. 
107. Technical design of project risk is rated Substantial. The project is complex due to its continental-regional and regional-national nature. However, efforts have been made in the design to minimize the interdependencies between continental, regional, and country-level activities. The project consists of various technical layers that are distinct but mutually reinforcing. Each country's activities are tailored to their specific needs. To learn from past experiences and mitigate risks, appropriate coordination mechanisms have been included at the continental, regional, and national levels to monitor progress towards reforms. The project also takes advantage of existing ECOWAS initiatives to leverage positive results, reducing the need for introducing new reforms that may require consensus and lengthy discussions. Despite these mitigation measures, there is still a substantial level of residual risk. 
108. Institutional capacity for implementation and sustainability risks is rated Substantial. Weak institutional implementation and low absorption capacity are seen as key risks, as the project will entail implementation of significant reforms in all layers of the SDM, and procurement of goods and services with which the governments may not have adequate prior experience. The institutional capacity risk is heightened by the fact that continental and regional instruments will need to be implemented by national institutions. Further, low implementation capacity within ECOWAS and AU is recognized as a risk. Where there are recent or ongoing national and regional projects, the expertise of existing PIUs will be leveraged, and additional capacity-building support will be provided as needed. The project has also made provisions to cover comprehensive advisory services to help manage/oversee major commercial transactions and provide quality assurance in connection with implementation of key investments. The risk of time bound advisory services, reliance on international expertise creates risks of sustainability. This will be mitigated through supporting knowledge transfer and local capacity building throughout implementation. Despite mitigation measures, the residual risk remains Substantial. 
109. Fiduciary risk is rated substantial. Risk assessment and mitigations measures are described in Section IV. B. 
110. Environmental and social risk is rated substantial. Risk assessment and mitigations measures are described in Section IV. D.
