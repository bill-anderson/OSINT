The South African government’s plan to launch a digital identity (ID) system is already behind schedule and is likely to take several more years, despite recent statements punting a rollout in 2026.

During a recent media briefing, Minister for Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation, Maropene Ramokgopa, outlined key progress in the government’s digital transformation ambitions, including:

- The launch of the MyMzansi citizen platform prototype
- The implementation of the MzansiXchange information-sharing pilot; and
- Progress in digital ID and digital payment reforms

Ramokgopa said that a medium-term development plan (MTDP) implementation assessment showed digital ID reforms were progressing well and the system was expected to roll out by the end of 2026.

However, Ramokgopa’s optimism is at odds with the actual progress being made, as South Africa’s digital ID policy must still be finalised and approved by Cabinet.

Regardless, a spokesperson from the department told MyBroadband that the minister’s statement aligns with the MTDP’s goals for the digital transformation of public services, centred on the digital ID system.

The department said the relevant clusters and departments were expected to provide further updates and information.

The digital ID system is a joint initiative spearheaded by the Department of Home Affairs, in collaboration with the Presidency and the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies.

Home Affairs minister Leon Schreiber [first announced](https://www.sanews.gov.za/south-africa/home-affairs-submit-digital-id-policy-cabinet-approval) that the digital ID policy would “shortly” be submitted to Cabinet for approval in April 2025.

However, following Ramokgopa’s briefing, a spokesperson for the Home Affairs ministry confirmed to MyBroadband that the digital ID policy was still under development.

Once that is finalised, it will need to be approved by Cabinet first. If approved, it must be published for public comment before the necessary technology can be put in place and the system rolled out.

According to the government’s [official roadmap](https://www.mymzansi.gov.za/roadmap/initiative-1) for digital ID on the MyMzansi website, a functional system was planned to be launched by the fourth quarter of the 2025 financial year.

That target won’t be met, as it will take much longer for the digital ID policy to undergo public consultation before it can be signed into law.

## Already behind schedule

Maropene Ramokgopa, Minister of Planning, Monitoring, and Evaluation

The system will give South African citizens and permanent residents complete digital identities, which will be linked to their biometrics.

Instead of presenting a physical document — such as a smart ID card — users will be able to authenticate their identities with their fingerprints or a facial scan.

The Digital ID will also store key records such as driving licences, marriage certificates, and grant status to provide simple access to basic public services.

In addition, it will support integration with private companies to enable faster and simpler access to banking, insurance, and other services.

While the government has promoted the digital ID’s capability to ease citizens’ daily lives, privacy advocates have cautioned that an authoritarian government could abuse it.

Research ICT Africa experts have warned that the consolidation of data in a single place could amplify inequalities and introduce new vulnerabilities.

“Like all technologies, digital IDs aren’t neutral and can come with both promise and peril,” they said. “The potential outcomes of these technologies are shaped by factors such as how they are designed and implemented.”

The Department of Home Affairs has already landed in trouble with the courts for unlawfully blocking roughly 2.5 million IDs it suspected of being fraudulent without conducting proper investigations.

While malicious misuse of identity data is one concern, another is negligence, especially in ensuring the system is adequately protected against cyberattacks.
