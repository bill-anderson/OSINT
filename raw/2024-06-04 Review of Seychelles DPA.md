Introduction

On December 19, 2023, the Data Protection Act ("DPA" or "the Act") was enacted following the conclusion of public consultation on the Data Protection Bill. In August 2023, the Department of Information and Communications Technology (DICT) issued a call to the public to submit comments on the draft bill, which was finally passed in December. The Act repeals the 2003 Data Protection Act ("repealed Act"), which was not implemented, despite being one of the earliest attempts to enact a law in Africa. The enactment of the Act signifies a renewed commitment to data protection, the principles for processing personal data in the digital age and the country's digital economy agenda. The Act significantly improves the repealed Act as it now addresses the gaps in the former, for example, it creates a data protection authority to enforce the law, provides more data subject rights, and addresses the mechanisms for cross-border data transfer. The Act provides an 18-month transition period for data controllers and processors to comply.

Key provisions

- Scope of application - Section 3. The Act applies to the processing of personal data through automated or semi-automated means, and where the data form part of a filing system within the territory of Seychelles. Unlike other laws with clear extraterritorial application, the Act diverges — the territorial scope suggests it may not apply to data controllers and processors outside Seychelles. The provision is similar to the one in the repealed Act.

- Establishment of the data protection authority - Section 5. The Act designates the Information Commission ("the Commission") as the regulatory body responsible for enforcing the Act. Although the Information Commission was created under the Access to Information Act, the Act designates it as the data protection authority. This is similar to South Africa, where an existing authority is designated. The Commission has authority to establish additional rules and procedures, conduct investigations and audits, raise public awareness, impose fines, cooperate with foreign supervisory authorities, request a preliminary injunction from the court, and issue enforcement notices.

- Data retention - Section 16. Data controllers must store data only for the period it is needed; once no longer needed it must be deleted, archived, or anonymised (via data masking, pseudonymisation, encryption, or removal of PII).

- Obligation to ensure accountability - Section 21. Data controllers must demonstrate accountability through data protection by design, accurate records of processing activities, and formal contracts with processors and third parties.

- Processing of sensitive personal data - Section 22. Prohibited except with consent or where necessary for employment/social protection law, vital interests, non-profit processing, legal claims, public interest, archival purposes, or to cover misconduct/inadequate behaviour (the latter is not clearly defined).

- Processing of children's data - Section 23. A child is anyone under 18. Consent of parent/legal guardian is required. The Act provides no mechanism for verifying a child's age or validity of third-party consent.

- Notification for data breach - Sections 43-44. Controllers have 72 hours after discovering a breach to notify the supervisory authority, with possible extension (requiring explanation for delay). Controllers must promptly notify data subjects where a breach is expected to negatively affect a significant number of them. The Act does not specify the extension time frame or the subject-notification deadline.

- Designation of a person responsible for safe custody of data - Section 33. Controllers must designate one or two persons responsible for compliance, non-delegable, with contact information for at least one made public.

- Data logging and blocking - Sections 38-39. Controllers must keep logs of processing activities (consultations and disclosures), open to the Commission on request, and must block access to personal data subject to rectification or erasure.

- Designation of a data protection officer - Sections 45-46. Controllers whose core activities require regular and systematic large-scale monitoring, or large-scale processing of special categories, must appoint a DPO.
