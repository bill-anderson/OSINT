# Mobile Money Taxes and Affordability in Sub-Saharan Africa

> ## Excerpt
> GSMA study on how taxation shapes mobile-money affordability. Headline finding: **levies above ~0.2% of
> transaction value cause significant detrimental effects** on affordability and consumer behaviour.

> **Provenance note.** GSMA is an industry body (mobile operators) arguing against taxes on its members'
> services — the 0.2% threshold is a GSMA finding-cum-recommendation; attribute framing plainly. Country
> snapshots as of 2024; levy table as of Q3 2025.

**Artefact:** ![[2026-03-01 Mobile Money Taxes and Affordability in Sub-Saharan Africa.pdf]]

## Key facts

- Mobile money drove SSA account ownership from ~20% (2011) to ~60% (2024), but a wave of "e-levies"
  since 2019 erodes the affordability advantage; levies are often regressive and raise ~1% of tax
  revenue. Five deep dives: **Zambia** (Levy Act 2023, doubled 2025), **Mali** (1% withdrawal levy, Feb
  2025), **Kenya** (excise 10%→15%), **Nigeria** (₦50 EMT levy), **Cameroon** (0.2% + XAF 4 flat).
- IMF evidence (Barczay et al. 2025): after tax introduction, taxed transaction value fell **40% in
  Cameroon, 47% in CAR**. Nine recommendations centred on lower rates and inflation-indexed exemption
  thresholds. Frames the tension as domestic revenue mobilisation vs financial inclusion.
