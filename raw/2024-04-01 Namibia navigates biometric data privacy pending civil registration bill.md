Analysis by legal expert Dianne Hubbard (a technical adviser to the Ministry of Home Affairs on the bill) for the Association for Progressive Communications' *Southern Africa Digital Rights* series (Issue 3). It argues that biometrics are collected across many contexts in Namibia — ID cards (photo plus ten fingerprints), the 2018 biometric e-passport, an Integrated Border Management System, voter registration, driving licences, police certificates of conduct, social grants and SIM-card registration — mostly without a governing data-protection law.

The forthcoming Civil Registration and Identification Bill is presented as the leading vehicle for data-protection safeguards: it links e-notices of birth and death to identity management, assigns a unique identifier at birth, and imposes strict rules on database access, confidentiality, data-sharing (only via published memorandums of agreement) and law-enforcement / intelligence access. A separate draft Data Protection Bill remains under development and needs strengthening.

Hubbard highlights MTC's "Verifi" biometric KYC tool — made a requirement for MTC services in January 2023 — as private-sector biometric collection running ahead of legal safeguards, and notes Namibia's narrow constitutional privacy provision (Article 13) and its ratification of the not-yet-in-force Malabo Convention.
