# Rethinking Ownership, Authorship, and Value Creation in IP Policy for African AI

> ## Excerpt
> A CIPIT policy brief arguing Africa needs a context-aware IP regime for AI — not imported models built
> on individual authorship — that keeps value (datasets, annotation, infrastructure, Indigenous
> Knowledge) local rather than legitimising extraction.

> **Provenance note.** Produced under the AI4D programme (IDRC / UK / Hewlett funding). `published` is a
> proxy from PDF metadata (2026-04-19; no printed date). The AfCFTA **IP Protocol** (2023) is distinct
> from the Digital Trade Protocol.

## Key facts

- Three linked challenges: the legal status of AI (advocates **AI-as-tool**, ownership/liability to human
  actors — citing *Thaler* rulings, UK 2023 / US 2025, and a 2025 Kenyan tribunal decision); constrained
  **data access** (generic open-data licences disadvantage African data owners); and **Indigenous
  Knowledge** protection (collective/intergenerational, a poor fit for individual-rights IP).
- Recommends AI–IP policy guidance; **tiered, benefit-sharing data-licensing** (e.g. the Nwulite Obodo
  Open Data Licence, NOODL); IK governance via prior informed consent and compensation; and investment
  in African-language datasets, national AI research centres and affordable compute. Aligns with the AU
  Continental AI Strategy (2024) and AfCFTA IP Protocol (2023). Frames Africa as a "standard taker."
