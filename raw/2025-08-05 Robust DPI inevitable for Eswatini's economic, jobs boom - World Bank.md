A World Bank economic update for Eswatini, "Harnessing the potential of digital technologies for Eswatini's economic growth and job creation," suggests building an effective digital public infrastructure (DPI) ecosystem as one of the sure ways of ensuring economic buoyancy. The report highlights digital transformation as a cross-cutting solution to enable inclusive growth, efficient public services, and resilience against economic shocks. The country's economy is projected to grow by 5 percent this year (2025).

The 58-page report notes that DPI should include a robust and inclusive biometric national digital ID system to facilitate access to digital government services such as digital Know Your Customer (eKYC) and Government-to-People (G2P) payments. Such a system should also help establish interoperability standards to facilitate integration with other national systems including health, education, and social protection.

The country's national population register is identified as the foundation upon which to build robust DPI, but the report advises the register should be fully equipped with digital potential and a resilient digital security architecture. It also calls for putting in place a biometric authentication system.

The World Bank also advises the Kingdom to strengthen digital governance by redefining roles to prevent overlap, and to fully operationalize the Cybersecurity Agency and Data Protection Office. It calls for reform of the country's Posts and Telecommunications Corporation to enhance and expand connectivity services, digital upskilling programs tied to labour market needs, and fostering digital entrepreneurship with "stronger intellectual property protections, startup access to government contracts (e-GP), and a blended finance fund for high-growth tech firms."

Financial inclusion will gain more traction if the digital payment system (National Payment Switch) is fully utilized; at the moment, more than 90 percent of People-to-Business (P2B) payments are still cash-based. The report also urges Eswatini to fully digitize tax administration, set up an online business registry, roll out a digital procurement system, and enable open access to backbone connectivity infrastructure.

Eswatini launched a digital government app, the GIYH App, in May, but the move faced criticism over data privacy issues.
