Ghana and Burkina Faso have joined forces to address cross-border signal interference to help mitigate challenges along their common borders.

The National Communications Authority (NCA) of Ghana and the Autorité de Régulation des Communications Électroniques et des Postes (ARCEP) of Burkina Faso held bilateral meetings earlier this week to address concerns about cross-border signal interference.

According to the NCA, the meeting brought together representatives from both countries, regulators and mobile network operators. They deliberated on strategies and finalized a frequency coordination agreement to help mitigate interference challenges along their common borders.

NCA Acting Director General Edmund Fianko said Ghana was mindful of the negative effects of signal interference, had already signed a frequency agreement with Togo and looked forward to concluding a similar arrangement with Burkina Faso.

Fianko explained that the initiative was a response to complaints received from consumers along the Ghana-Burkina Faso border regarding interference issues.

The meeting deliberated on strategies and finalized a frequency coordination agreement to help mitigate interference challenges along Ghana and Burkina Faso common borders.

"I urge all stakeholders to respect the agreed thresholds, as provisions will be made for minimum signal strength across the border. Operators on both sides must comply with the new thresholds once they are agreed," he said.

Fianko indicated the Authority's intention to advance discussions on ECOWAS roaming, highlighting Ghana's successful implementations with Togo and Benin.

ECOWAS is a regional political and economic union of 15 countries located in West Africa and its aim is to achieve "collective self-sufficiency" for its member states by creating a single large trade bloc through the creation of a full economic and trading union.

Sibiri Ouattara, ARCEP's technical advisor to the executive secretary, said the meeting was a step in the right direction, as both regulators and operators worked together to build a robust framework for a better coordinated and more effectively managed spectrum.

"I am convinced that the conclusions and recommendations resulting from our discussions will represent a major step, guaranteeing optimal and harmonious use of frequencies without harmful interference, for the benefit of our citizens," Ouattara continued.

## African bilateral agreements

The meeting between Ghana and Burkina Faso comes at a time when many African countries are signing agreements aimed at providing a quality network to citizens.

Last week, Congo-Brazzaville's Electronic Communications Regulatory Agency (ARPCE) and the Angolan Institute of Communications (INACOM) signed a frequency coordination agreement on the common border, which is 231km long.

At the beginning of the month, Liberia and Sierra Leone announced the rollout of the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) free roaming initiative, designed to ease cross-border mobile roaming costs.

Ghana expressing it intention to also advance ECOWAS discussion with Burkina Faso comes after several ECOWAS member states took proactive steps to expand telecom infrastructure and connect underserved communities through strategic agreements.

In November 2024, Ghana's government, the NCA and mobile network operators held talks with representatives from The Gambia to implement the ECOWAS free roaming initiative.

A free roaming initiative between Benin and Ghana took effect on July 1, 2024, while a similar agreement involving Ghana, Togo and Benin was launched in October 2024.

Ghana signed a free roaming agreement with Côte d'Ivoire in February 2024, allowing citizens of both nations to use mobile services in each other's countries without incurring international roaming charges.

Ghana's National Communications Authority and Togo's Regulatory Authority for Electronic Communications also agreed in November 2023 to establish bilateral roaming services between their countries.
