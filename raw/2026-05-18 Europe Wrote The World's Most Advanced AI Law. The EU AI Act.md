On August 1, 2024, a law came into force in Brussels.

And I know you were not notified, but nothing to worry about, that’s why you have me.

Even if you had no notice, today parts of this law apply to businesses in Lagos, Nairobi, Kampala, Accra, and Johannesburg.

Whether they know it or not.

Most don’t.

I want to change that today.

Because as Africa, we need to make some serious power moves, and this right here, is a power move.

And Africa needs to make it.

---

The EU AI Act is the world’s first comprehensive law on artificial intelligence.

10 years in the making with hundreds of drafts.

Thousands of hours of lobbying, compromise, and political tension.

And it is genuinely impressive.

But here is what I need you to understand before anything else.

This law was not written with Africa in mind and that’s ok but truth is, we missed out on some opportunity.

Not once did a provision say;

***“what about the woman in Accra whose loan was denied***

***because the AI didn’t understand her chama?”***

Not once did it ask;

***“what about the farmer in Uganda getting planting advice***

***from a tool trained on data from Iowa?”***

Not once did it consider;

***“what about the two thousand languages through***

***which most Africans actually live their lives?”***

It was written for European realities.

Formal economies. Formal credit. Formal legal infrastructure.

And then it applied to us anyway.

---

**First: How the law actually works**

The EU AI Act puts every AI system into one of four boxes based on how much harm it can cause.

Think of it like a traffic light with four colours.

🔴 **Unacceptable risk - banned completely.**

***These AI tools are not allowed to exist in EU-connected markets. Full stop.***

🟠 **High risk - allowed, but with strict rules.**

***These tools can operate. But with human oversight, documentation, transparency, and accountability built in.***

🟡 **Limited risk - transparency only.**

***You can use them. But if it’s an AI your customer is talking to, they must know it’s an AI.***

🟢 **Minimal risk - mostly unregulated.**

***Your spam filter. Your music recommendation. No specific requirements.***

That’s the whole structure.

Now let me tell you which box your business tools are in.

---

### 🔴 Some tools have already been banned - since February 2, 2025

FYI, these are already illegal.

#### Emotion recognition in workplaces.

If your HR technology is reading how your employees feel; that feature cannot legally operate for EU-connected operations.

So the question you should be asking is:

**Are these tools being deployed in African workplaces right now**

**with zero disclosure? Zero governance? Nobody watching?**

#### Facial recognition scraping.

The tools that built their databases by scraping CCTV footage and internet images, well that’s banned now.

Which raises a harder question.

**What databases are the facial recognition systems deployed at**

**African borders and in African cities built on?**

**Were those databases built legally? Did anyone ask?**

#### Social scoring by governments.

Any AI that rates citizens based on their behaviour for government use. Well, that’s banned too

If any African government is using European-built AI to assess or rank citizens for public services; that cannot happen for EU-connected operations.

---

### 🟠What’s high risk? Full enforcement happens on August 2, 2026

*Only three months away.*

This is the section that should make every African CEO sit up straight.

#### Credit scoring and creditworthiness assessment.

Every AI tool assessing loan applications. If European clients are involved or the tool was built in Europe; high-risk obligations apply.

- Human oversight is required.
- The AI must be able to explain its decisions.
- Risk management documentation is required.

If you’re a lending platform across Africa using AI credit scoring tools built on European infrastructure; this is your deadline. The tools you are using might abruptly stop working unless you prove the above.

#### AI hiring tools.

Every AI tool screening CVs. Shortlisting candidates. Scoring applicants.

If you are a recruitment firm in Nairobi placing candidates for European employers, the AI you use to screen applications is high risk under EU law.

You probably don’t know this.

#### Healthcare AI.

Diagnostics. Clinical decision support. Medical devices with AI embedded.

Matibabu. Ubenwa. The extraordinary African health tech innovations, if they ever seek European partnerships or clients, these obligations apply.

Understanding this now is a competitive advantage to you.

#### Education systems.

AI that determines access to education or shapes someone’s professional path.

The AI-powered scholarship selection tools. The EdTech platforms African universities are using. If any European connection exists; it’s high-risk.

---

**What high risk means in practice**

For every tool in the high risk category, the law requires the same things.

1. ***A risk management system.***
2. ***Human oversight before decisions affect real people.***
3. ***Technical documentation.***
4. ***Transparency to users.***
5. ***Accuracy standards.***

Not eventually.

**By August 2, 2026.**

**In 3 months!**

---

**You might be wondering, does this apply to me?**

The law applies based on one question.

***Where does your AI’s output land?***

Not where you are based. Not where your servers are. Where the impact lands.

A South African company developing an AI hiring tool marketed to EU employers,must comply. Even if everything is built in South Africa.

A Kenyan manufacturer selling smart home devices with embedded AI to EU consumers must comply. The product is placed on the EU market.

But here is the nuance others miss but not you…

If you use a European AI tool only for African customers, you are likely not directly subject to the law.

But you are still inside it.

Because the tool you are using was built to comply with EU rules. When those rules change; the tool changes. Features disappear. Requirements shift.

***You are operating inside a European legal framework whether you chose it or not.***

That is what power looks like when you don’t write the rules.

Someone else’s decisions shape your tools.

And you don’t even know it’s happening.

---

**Here is what the EU AI Act gets genuinely right.**

- The idea that AI risk should be measured by consequence and not just by sector is correct.
- The idea that humans must be accountable for AI decisions affecting real people is correct.
- The idea that transparency is not optional is correct.
	Take all of that. Keep it.

Here is what it misses and where we come in as Africans building in this space.

- *It does not know what a chama is.*
- *It does not understand that for millions of Africans, mobile money is not an alternative financial system. It is the only one.*
- *It does not have a provision for the 2000 languages through which most African people live, think, and express their reality to systems making decisions about their lives.*
- *And it does not address what happens when high-risk AI is deployed in communities with no specialist to catch the error, no lawyer to appeal to, and no regulator watching.*

These are not gaps at the margins by majority of the African experience.

**And they are exactly where Africa builds something the world doesn’t have yet.**

---

The beauty with this is that Africa doesn’t start from zero.

We start from the EU AI Act, the world’s most tested governance framework and we extend it into the realities it never imagined.

We take the risk-based approach. And we redefine high risk for African contexts.

Not just by sector. By consequence. If an AI tool is making decisions about someone’s access to capital in a market where there is no legal recourse, that is high risk. Regardless of what sector it sits in.

***We take the human oversight requirement.*** And we extend it to account for the overworked community health worker who is the only human available to review an AI diagnosis.

***We take the transparency obligation***. And we extend it. If AI is making decisions about you in a language it doesn’t speak accurately, you have the right to know that.

And we build three things the EU Act doesn’t have.

1. ***Informal economy protections.***
2. ***Language accuracy requirements.***
3. ***Communal redress mechanisms.***

Not as aspirations.

And this shouldn’t be for aspirational purposes only.

It should be specific, enforceable provisions built from African realities by African practitioners.

**Rwanda showed us what governance infrastructure looks like.**

**Nigeria showed us what an operational AI gate looks like.**

**Kenya showed us what data sovereignty sounds like in writing.**

The pieces are here.

Now someone needs to put them together and that someone is YOU.
