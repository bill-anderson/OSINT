“Political language is designed to make lies sound truthful and murder respectable, and to give an appearance of solidity to pure wind.” George Orwell

Early December 2025, Kenya signed a Health Cooperation Framework with the United States of America. The same day, the Trump administration published its National Security Strategy. Read together, these documents reveal the precise playbook for capture being deployed across Africa’s digital future.

Kenyan analyst John-Allan Namu observes of the 1884 scramble for Africa “they called it a scramble a word as soft as eggs, but vague enough to conceal the violence underway.”

In 2025, the language is “cooperation,” “partnership,” “capacity building.” Same structure, language softened to mask hard, violent extraction.

**Asymmetry by Design**

The Kenya/USA Health Cooperation Framework grants the United States 25 years of access to Kenya’s health data, pathogen specimens, and genetic sequences. In return, Kenya receives funding commitment for 5 years.

Article 3(f) promises “benefit sharing arrangements” requiring “subsidiary agreements to be determined.” Article 5(g) clarifies “this framework does not constitute an international agreement giving rise to rights and obligations under international law.”

Meaning, data extraction happens now. Benefit sharing happens later, maybe, through agreements not yet written, under terms not yet negotiated, with no enforcement mechanism if they never materialise.

George Orwell understood this “The great enemy of clear language is insincerity. When there is a gap between one’s real and one’s declared aims, one turns, as it were, instinctively to long words and exhausted idioms, like a cuttlefish squirting out ink.”

Observe tech/internet governance spaces, someone once called it “acronym hell” it is a landscape where ICANN, GAC, NCSG, RIPE NCC, AFRINIC, IETF, IAB, RIRs, NSO, IGF, AI, and countless other initialisms obscure power relationships beneath technical sounding neutrality.

**The Soft Language of Power**

Most of Africa’s current borders are straight lines drawn in Berlin in 1884. The shortest distance between the resources Europe wanted and the world they intended to build.

Namu notes “Borders are a helluva thing arbitrary, yet manifest in every way that we live our lives.” We still live inside those boundaries. And in 2025, new maps are being drawn, not of land, of data.

The pattern continues, immediate access to resources in exchange for future promises. Maps get drawn while benefits are still being negotiated.

Ta-Nehisi Coates observed “It may seem strange that people who have already attained a position of power through violence invest so much time in justifying their plunder with words. But even plunderers are human beings whose violent ambitions must contend with the guilt that gnaws at them when they meet the eyes of their victims. And so a story must be told.”

Kenya’s agreement tells such a story. “Cooperation.” “Partnership.” “Mutual benefit.” The soft language obscures what the articles specify; 25 years of data access of over 50 million people for 5 years of funding; benefit sharing “to be determined,” and no international legal recourse.

**The mechanics are Familiar**

**Frame the Problem to Fit the Solution**:

Kenya’s healthcare challenges are real. But notice what does not get asked, could Kenya build these systems while retaining control over its own health data? could regional partnerships create technical capacity without surrendering sovereignty? Instead, the problem gets framed to make external intervention appear inevitable.

**Legitimisation Through “Consultation”**:

The agreement went through some process. Meetings took place and “stakeholders” were invited. But who designed the framework determining that 25 years of health data access of over 50 million people was appropriate exchange for 5 years of funding? A legal challenge currently before Kenya’s courts argues the agreement violated constitutional requirements for public participation. The challenge names what happened; Kenyan participation legitimised a framework not designed by Kenyans.

**Extraction Is Immediate, Compensation Conditional**:

Article 3 grants data access with specified timelines and mechanisms defined. Article 3(f) promises benefit sharing but later through “subsidiary agreements to be determined.” What is not defined are the benefits, whose determination, and what enforcements?

Ruth Wilson Gilmore documented how “capitalism requires inequality and racism to enshrine it.” Kenya’s agreement embeds this principle, the valuable resource flows immediately but the promised return requires future negotiation with parties who have already received what they needed.

**Enforcement Removed, Extraction Preserved**:

Article 5(g) states the framework “does not constitute an international agreement giving rise to rights and obligations under international law.” Therefore, if promised benefits do not materialise, Kenyans have no international legal recourse. But the data access specified in Article 3 is ongoing.

**Converging Parallel Processes**:

Days after signing the US agreement, Kenya’s National Intelligence Research University launched a hackathon inviting citizens to develop “surveillance cameras with facial recognition capabilities” for the National Intelligence Service.

Shoshana Zuboff documented how surveillance capitalism “unilaterally claims human experience as free raw material for translation into behavioral data.” The Kenyan government trusts its citizens to build AI-powered surveillance infrastructure that will render their own lives as behavioral data, yet does not trust Kenyans/ Kenyan institutions to manage health data without 25 years of American oversight.

“ **How now?”**

In Kenyan English, this phrase exposes the contradiction. It means, **explain this absurdity.**

This ongoing normalisation of surveillance infrastructure treats African populations as subjects to be monitored rather than citizens to be served. Edward Snowden’s revelations exposed how the NSA “recorded, stored and analysed metadata relating to telephone calls and text messages transmitted in Mexico, Kenya and the Philippines.” Kenyans have already been subjected to mass surveillance by foreign powers. Now, through this agreement, Kenya formalises data extraction while simultaneously building domestic surveillance capacity.

According to the 2021 State of Internet Freedom in Africa report by the Collaboration on International ICT Policy for East and Southern Africa (CIPESA), surveillance has become “a principal threat to digital rights in Africa, a weakening force to civil society and independent voices, and ultimately a driver of authoritarianism.“ The study, which covered 34 African countries including Kenya, found that surveillance has undermined the ability of democracy actors to use digital communication channels, with some stopping their use altogether or severely restricting their communications. Organisations report increased operational costs from implementing security measures against state monitoring, while the psychological toll includes constant feelings of anxiety, stress, fear, and isolation.

**The AI Race No One Is Naming**

The US National Security Strategy provides context that Kenya’s government will not state explicitly. The document outlines America’s Africa approach “ **trade not aid,**” strategic focus on minerals and energy. Healthcare data does not appear in the Africa section, it appears in the AI race section, noting “data scarcity” limits AI development in critical sectors.

Namu explains “The US is in perhaps the most important race against other world nations, notably China, to develop the most powerful artificial intelligence. However, training data for various models is becoming more and more scarce, specifically healthcare data.”

African health data solves an American scarcity problem. The benefit sharing that “requires subsidiary agreements” “might solve a Kenyan development problem”. One is happening now. The other requires future negotiation.

Audre Lorde understood this structure “In a society where the good is defined in terms of profit rather than in terms of human need, there must always be some group of people who, through systematised oppression, can be made to feel surplus, to occupy the space of the dehumanised inferior.”

The same day Kenya signed this agreement, Trump’s National Security Strategy assessed NATO’s future: “It is more than plausible that within a few decades at the latest, certain NATO members will become majority non-European. As such, it is an open question whether they will view their place in the world, or their alliance with the United States, in the same way.”

This is published American national security doctrine, brown and black Europeans cannot be trusted with Western alliance commitments. As Namu notes: “Trump has simply said, through this document, what others have tried to deodorise in more innocuous language.” If non-white Europeans cannot be trusted with NATO, why would non-white Africans be trusted to manage their own health data?

The logic is not new it has simply moved from subtext to text.

Kenya is being asked to trust a government whose published strategy states that certain populations, defined by the colour of their skin fundamentally cannot be trusted.

**The Comprador Complex**

Colonialism was maintained not merely through external force but through what many scholars term the “comprador complex” local elites who internalised colonial logic and implemented it with missionary zeal. Today’s data agreements follow the same architecture, African officials signing frameworks designed elsewhere, legitimising extraction through local participation.

Frantz Fanon warned of this in The Wretched of the Earth, “the colonised intellectual…has thrown himself greedily into Western culture. He will not rest content until he has said yes to every aspect of that culture…But the time comes when he is informed that his culture is ‘underdeveloped,’ that his people are a collection of inferiors.”

The political economist Samir Amin argued that postcolonial Africa inherited not independence but reconfigured dependency, where “comprador bourgeoisie would sustain hegemonic rule” after formal decolonisation.

The mechanism is structural, when career advancement, institutional funding, and professional recognition all flow from external validation, the incentive is to align with external interests rather than challenge them.

Kwame Anthony Appiah identified a “comprador intelligentsia as a relatively small, Western style, Western trained group of writers and thinkers, who mediate the trade in cultural commodities of world capitalism at the periphery.”

Many African AI practitioners mediate AI deployment without controlling the infrastructure that makes it possible. African expertise exists mostly at the application layer, the foundational layer where real power resides remains external.

Many policy frameworks appear African in authorship but are written by foreign consultants, paid by foreign funders, then adopted by African governments seeking external validation. What appears as sovereign policymaking is what Ngũgĩ wa Thiong’o described as “a bourgeoisie that is not national but comprador, more wedded to global capital than to national development.”

The dependency is material, not just ideological. Many African experts from tech organisations to civil society organisations, think tanks and more, can hardly survive without foreign funding. This dependency determines which questions get asked and which remain unspoken. When budgets depends on external donors, one cannot afford to interrogate the systems these donors represent.

Walter Rodney documented this structure “If economic power is centered outside national African boundaries, then political and military power in any real sense is also centered outside, until and unless the masses of peasants and workers are mobilised to offer an alternative to the system of sham political independence.”

This is what funding forecloses; the independence to build systems serving African interests first.

Tricontinental’s 2025 analysis “Sovereignty or Surrender” names what scholars have documented for decades “Africa’s comprador bourgeoisie, the class of educated elites, serve as transmission belts for neoliberal policies that maintain Africa’s peripheral position in global capitalism. They talk like us and look like us, but they live in total disconnect from our national realities.”

Amílcar Cabral understood this, proposing “class suicide” where native elites must abandon their class interests to lead genuine liberation. What we witness instead is what Mahmood Mamdani calls “decentralised despotism” African elites occupying positions of nominal authority while ultimately serving the interests of global capital.

Audre Lorde understood this and the cost of silence “I have come to believe over and over again that what is most important to me must be spoken, made verbal and shared, even at the risk of having it bruised or misunderstood.”

And Zora Neale Hurston warned “If you are silent about your pain, they will kill you and say you enjoyed it.”

So the facilitators of extraction must recognise their role before they can contribute to liberation.

**The Double Standard**

Would America offer European nations 5 years of funding for 25 years of health data access?

Consider the Marshall Plan. When Europe needed reconstruction after World War II, America provided aid from 1948 to 1951 over 90% in grants, not loans. There was no requirement that Europeans surrender sovereignty over their resources, Europeans designed their own recovery plans; America provided financing. The aid was authorised by Congress as firm commitments, not vague promises of future benefit sharing.

Kenya gets the inverse; 25 years of data extraction for 5 years of conditional funding, through an agreement that explicitly states it “does not constitute an international agreement giving rise to rights and obligations under international law.”

**Alternatives that directly refute the logic underlying Kenya’s agreement.**

Estonia has built one of the world’s most advanced digital health infrastructure without surrendering data sovereignty. The country implemented radical transparency, every access to health records is logged, and citizens can see exactly who accessed their data, when, and why. If a doctor, insurance company, or government agency views medical records, the citizen knows.

This was not a compromise between sovereignty and capacity, transparency enabled capacity. Estonians trust the system precisely because they control visibility into it. The government maintains sovereignty over health data while achieving interoperability, efficiency, and security that rivals any system built through external partnerships.

Estonia proves the false choice underlying Kenya’s agreement, that African nations must trade sovereignty for technical capacity. The real question is not whether sovereignty enables or prevents development, but do those designing these frameworks want Africans to retain control.

The question now is; will it be partnerships that preserve sovereignty to enable development, or partnerships that trade sovereignty for promises that enable extraction?

Tragically, it is often Africans who become the facilitators of extraction against their own region and people. Young people’s movements like Gen Z activists have mobilised against economic exploitation and government overreach, yet they face criticism from the very class that benefits from maintaining the status quo.

Steve Biko understood this dynamic “The most potent weapon in the hands of the oppressor is the mind of the oppressed.” The comprador class has internalised the logic that African sovereignty must be traded for external validation, that African institutions cannot be trusted, that extraction is the price of development. They then turn this internalised oppression outward, criticising those who refuse to accept these terms as naive, unrealistic, crazy, compromised or dangerous to “progress.”

**The Court Challenge**

Kenya’s courts are currently reviewing whether the Health Cooperation Framework violated constitutional requirements for public participation. The legal challenge argues that while consultation processes occurred, they served primarily to legitimise a framework that Kenyans did not design and whose terms were predetermined.

Can African institutions recognise extraction while it is being formalised, or only after it is already underway?

This matters because the pattern will continue repeating across generations otherwise. Berlin 1884 was not challenged, maps were drawn, treaties signed, extraction began. Recognition came later, after generations experienced its effects.

Kenya has the opportunity to recognise data colonialism while maps are still being drawn. Yet many who could raise alarm remain silent, choosing personal and institutional stability

**Unfinished Business**

We still live inside boundaries drawn in Berlin in 1884. in 2025, new maps are being drawn around data. The scramble for Africa never ended with independence it simply evolved from minerals to data, land, labor to information infrastructure and more.

From 1884 to 2025, “cooperation” frameworks now formalise the extraction.

The court’s decision on the health framework is more than legal. it is a verdict on whether Kenyans will recognise the new maps being drawn over their future while there is still time to contest them. The decision will determine whether data sovereignty is achievable through institutions designed to protect it, or whether those institutions will legitimise extraction.

Will Africans recognise the new scramble while maps are being drawn, or look back decades from now at 2025 as the moment Africa’s digital future was divided without firing a shot?

The answer depends partly on courts, but more fundamentally on whether those positioned to recognise and name extraction will choose to do so even when silence is more comfortable, more funded, more professionally rewarding.

Angela Davis reminds us “It is in collectivities that we find reservoirs of hope and optimism.” The court case represents one such collectivity; Kenyan civil society organisations, constitutional lawyers, and citizens willing to challenge their own government’s surrender of sovereignty. Their legal challenge is itself an act of collective resistance, a refusal to accept that 2025 must repeat 1884.

The scramble continues to evolve. Will it succeed without needing soldiers? This depends on whether Africans particularly those with the expertise, platform, and institutional access to name extraction, will choose truth over comfort.
