(Source: Nyasha Simon)

SpaceX's satellite Internet provider Starlink has been denied an operating license in Namibia.

The Communications Regulatory Authority of Namibia (CRAN) this week confirmed that Starlink's application for both a Class Comprehensive Telecommunications Service Licence (ECS and ECNS) and a Spectrum Licence for fixed satellite services had been denied.

The regulatory authority said the low-Earth orbit (LEO) satellite provider had only met three of the six criteria required by law, which was grounds for refusal of the combined ECS and ECNS license.

In Namibia a spectrum license cannot be held without having one of the other licenses so that application was also rejected.

A [statement from CRAN](https://www.cran.na/download/cran-concludes-assessment-of-starlink-licence-application-24-march-2026-windhoek/) explained that the three criteria not met by Starlink were: ownership and control; national defense and public security; and compliance history.

Under ownership, the main issue is that Starlink does not comply with the ownership requirements prescribed under section 46 of Namibia's Communications Act, because the company is wholly foreign owned.

"Starlink also did not obtain an exemption from the statutory obligation mandating a minimum of 51% Namibian ownership," CRAN said.

Under national defense and public security, CRAN flagged the importance of data sovereignty and national control and found that Starlink's "100% foreign ownership raises material regulatory considerations concerning jurisdiction, enforceability of compliance obligations, and the ability of the Authority to exercise effective oversight as required under the Act."

Back in November 2024, CRAN issued a cease-and-desist order to Starlink [for operating in the country without a license](https://www.connectingafrica.com/regulation/starlink-ordered-to-cease-operations-in-namibia) and ordered the confiscation of all Starlink terminal equipment across the country.

This caused non-compliance in the final category as CRAN found that Starlink had contravened the act by operating without a license.

"Failure to respond to the Authority's summons shows a total disregard for the governance framework of the sector and raised doubt on Starlink's ability to honour license conditions in future," CRAN said in its statement.

The criteria that Starlink did meet were around competition; technical and financial capacity; and frequency availability.

## Starlink reacts to failed Namibian foray

Starlink confirmed via its website on a page titled " [Starlink for Namibia](https://starlink.com/en-kz/support/article/6a4afd37-67be-8b12-3cf8-40b65eae3347?srsltid=AfmBOoqZeWT7vu4ri_MzW1nnPaedrCRzfDNnj2GDpAmvNDZzKebh1wBq) " that its application to operate in the Southern African nation had been declined.

"Despite 98.6% public support during the consultation process, the application was not approved," Starlink said.

The satellite provider said CRAN's regulatory process allows for continued public input following this decision and urged Namibian to appeal directly to CRAN.

This was similar to Starlink's recent [efforts to gather public support in South Africa](https://www.connectingafrica.com/regulation/starlink-asks-sa-citizens-to-pressure-regulator-over-license), another country where it is facing regulatory push back.

Starlink also argued its case around the non-compliance cited by CRAN, saying it warranted additional context.

"The government's decision has been attributed to non-compliance. We believe this characterization is misleading," Starlink said.

"Over the past three years, Starlink made clear its commitment to establish a local entity, comply with national security requirements, and pay all applicable taxes and fees, consistent with how we operate in 164 markets worldwide," it added.

The Communications Regulatory Authority of Namibia said Starlink met only three of the six criteria required by law to gain a telecommunications license. (Source: Allexxandar on Freepik)

Starlink said the primary issue raised by the regulator related to local ownership requirements, but that due to global shareholding restrictions it cannot accept local ownership.

"While Namibia's framework encourages local shareholding, it allows for exemptions at the Minister's discretion. In this case, an exemption was not granted," Starlink explained.

"The decision cites public interest concerns, which appear to reflect a misunderstanding of how Starlink complies with local laws, protects data sovereignty, and supports national security across the markets where we operate," the LEO satellite provider stated.

Starlink argued that the digital divide was stifling growth across Namibia and put forward ways that Starlink services could provide connectivity to rural areas as well as schools and health facilities.

"More than 30% of Namibians still lack access to the Internet. For those who can access the Internet, especially the 70% of Namibians in rural areas, reliability and speed are major issues. Namibia has some of the slowest fixed broadband speeds on the planet," Starlink claimed.

## Door remains open for other LEO providers

Interestingly, CRAN said it recognizes the transformative potential of LEO satellites in expanding connectivity, particularly in rural and underserved areas.

"The Authority will thus continue to champion the deployment of LEO satellite technology and investment into other emerging technologies as critical pillars to achieve universal connectivity in Namibia," it said, potentially opening the door for other LEO providers which meet its licensing requirements.

Starlink is still trying to get an operating license [in South Africa](https://www.connectingafrica.com/regulation/starlink-pushes-for-sa-license-with-advocacy-campaign) but is also [battling ownership rules](https://www.connectingafrica.com/regulation/sa-minister-opens-door-for-starlink-with-new-policy-directive) which apply to Namibia's neighbor.
