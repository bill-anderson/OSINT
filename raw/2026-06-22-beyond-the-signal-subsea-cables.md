# Beyond the Signal: Why Africa's Next Telecom Era Will Be Built Beneath the Surface

Telecom Review Africa (feature), 22 June 2026

For most of the past two decades, investment in African telecommunications concentrated at the network edge. But the constraint binding the continent's next phase of digital growth sits deeper — in the subsea cable systems that determine how much capacity enters the continent, at what cost, and through whose infrastructure. Capital deployment into African subsea infrastructure is accelerating, and the ownership profile is shifting from traditional telecom consortia toward hyperscale technology companies.

Africa currently has **77 active or planned subsea cable systems as of 2025**, yet more than half of the continent's international bandwidth remains concentrated in just five countries: Nigeria, South Africa, Egypt, Algeria, and Kenya — with direct consequences for resilience and pricing.

Urban fibre has accelerated (South Africa leads with FTTH penetration surpassing 20% in urban areas; Abidjan the fastest mover, propelling Côte d'Ivoire from eighth to third in Africa's fibre rankings in a single year), but submarine capacity is entering the fast lane. Systems including **2Africa** (one of the longest ever built) and **Equiano** are significantly expanding international bandwidth. The March 2024 simultaneous disruptions to the WACS, SAT-3, and ACE cables off the West African coast — causing outages in Nigeria and Ghana — underscored that infrastructure diversity is not optional.

New redundancy is layering in: in mid-2025 Telecom Egypt and NaiTel completed the Coral Bridge cable (Egypt–Jordan); Algérie Télécom and Sparkle signed an MoU for an Italy–Algeria cable; and a four-operator consortium agreed to build the Asia-Africa-Europe-2 (AAE-2) system. In May 2026, a consortium including Orange, Canalink and regional operators announced the **Via Africa** submarine cable, a different subsea route contributing to Europe–Africa resilience. Historically consortium-governed intercontinental cables are giving way to hyperscaler-owned infrastructure, raising sovereignty and geopolitical stakes.
