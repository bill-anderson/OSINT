(Originally published 22 July 2024; updated 4 November 2024. First appeared in Issue 3 of Southern Africa Digital Rights.)

The Kingdom of Eswatini has been striving to catch up with its Southern African counterparts in ICT development. Until 2017, citizens had only one mobile operator, MTN Eswatini, which had a monopoly since 1998. The regulatory role shifted to the Eswatini Communications Commission (ESCCOM), established in 2013 under the Swaziland Communications Act of 2013. ESCCOM oversees all ICT-related laws, including the Electronic Communications Act of 2022, Electronic Communications Transactions Act of 2022, Computer Crime and Cybercrime Act of 2022, and the Data Protection Act of 2022. Rollout of most biometric/digital systems started before comprehensive legal frameworks were in place - in SIM card registration, voter registration/verification, civil registration, and financial services.

SIM card registration: In 2016, the Minister of ICT published Legal Notice No. 26 of 2016 providing a framework for registration of all mobile subscribers and protection of subscriber information. Information collected includes names, addresses, identity numbers, and facial images, for both local citizens and non-Swazi individuals. Service providers must retain this information for up to five years after termination of the customer's contract.

Biometric voter registration: In 2013, the Elections and Boundaries Commission (EBC) first used a Biometric Voter Registration System, capturing photo and fingerprint biometrics of each voter. The same system was used in the 2018 elections.

National identification card: In 2003, the Kingdom initiated the National Identification Card, which includes facial identity, full names, date of birth, chief code, and Personal Identity Number (PIN), centrally stored by the Ministry of Home Affairs. Despite recent developments allowing service providers like commercial banks to access the civil registry for identity verification, the National ID card does not yet meet the criteria for digital identification - the facial identity alone does not grant direct access to the PIN or other details.

More time needed for stakeholder input: The national government has not started rolling out digital identity in earnest. All four Acts of 2022 were tabled before Parliament with certificates of urgency, shortening the time for stakeholder and public input. There are indications that, through the Royal Science and Technology Park (RSTP), initiatives are being conceptualised. Developments in biometrics and digital identity find expression in national strategies such as the National Information and Communication Infrastructure (NICI) Implementation Plan.
