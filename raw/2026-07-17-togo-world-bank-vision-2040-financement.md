Togo Signs CFA257.4 Billion in Financing Agreements for Vision 2040

(Togo First) - Togo and the World Bank Group signed five financing agreements worth a combined $429 million, or about 257.4 billion CFA francs, in Lomé on Thursday, July 16, 2026. Signed during a visit by World Bank Managing Director of Operations Anna Bjerde, the agreements will support several strategic projects aligned with Togo Vision 2040 and the government’s new 2026-2031 roadmap.

“The partnership that brings us together today is strategic and transformative, aligned with Vision 2040 and anchored in the new 2026-2031 roadmap,” Finance and Budget Minister Georges Barcola said at the signing ceremony.

Togolese authorities expect the new financing to help the country meet its 2040 targets of doubling average living standards and reducing the poverty rate to below 15%.

Financing Targets Energy, Social Cohesion and Logistics

Barcola said the agreements would turn the government roadmap’s ambitions into concrete measures. The roadmap is structured around three priorities: “Protect, Unite and Transform.”

“These agreements show how those priorities will be put into practice. They turn them into investments and concrete measures designed to deliver tangible benefits for the Togolese people,” he said.

The financing covers several priority development sectors. Under the “Protect” pillar, two energy projects will receive a combined $140 million. The Promoting Gas Power Market Integration Project, known as PRIME-GAS, will receive $40 million to support regional electricity-market integration through gas-fired power generation. The West Africa Regional Electricity Market Program, or WA-REMP, will receive $100 million to expand access to electricity, particularly in rural areas.

Under the “Unite” pillar, the West Africa Unique Identification for Regional Integration and Inclusion Project, known as WURI, will receive an additional $20 million to expand access to secure identification. Another $69 million will go to the Regional Social Cohesion Project, or COSO, with a particular focus on northern Togo.

Most of the financing will support the “Transform” pillar. The Logistics and Transport Systems Improvement Program, or PASLT, will receive $200 million to upgrade transport and logistics corridors, improve links between production areas and markets, and strengthen Togo’s position as a regional logistics hub.

The new financing adds to the World Bank’s already substantial portfolio in Togo. Official data show that the institution’s active commitments now exceed $2 billion, up from $174 million in 2017. The portfolio currently stands at $2.1 billion and includes six national projects, several results-based programs, budget support operations and 10 regional projects.

The Togolese government said the expanding portfolio reflects the World Bank’s renewed confidence in the country’s reforms and its ability to deliver projects with substantial economic and social benefits.
