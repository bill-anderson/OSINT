# Optimism but uncertainty over Africa’s AI Declaration - African Law & Business

> ## Excerpt
> The Africa Declaration on Artificial Intelligence promises ethical AI governance, but participating states will need to confront the challenges of cross-jurisdictional law if they are to make it a reality.

---
**The Africa Declaration on Artificial Intelligence promises ethical AI governance, but participating states will need to confront the challenges of cross-jurisdictional law if they are to make it a reality.**

Aimed at making African nations full participants in the AI revolution, the _Africa Declaration on Artificial Intelligence_ was signed in Rwanda last month, promising AI investment and innovation to “benefit all African communities” and raising legal issues, including the need for proper governance, data protection and ethics.

The declaration was signed on Friday 4 April at the Global AI Summit on Africa, hosted in Kigali by Rwanda’s Centre for the Fourth Industrial Revolution and Ministry of ICT & Innovation, and the World Economic Forum.

Of Africa’s 54 states, 52 were signatories, along with the African Union and Smart Africa – a multi-state organisation which promotes technology, with only Tanzania and Western Sahara absent.

The declaration “was pretty much in line with international standards, but it is interesting because it is going to trigger declarations and AI policies on national bases”, explains **Aissatou Sylla**, counsel with **Dentons**’ technology, media and telecommunications practice group in Paris and an expert on African data laws, but “in order to implement all those strategies and policies and give life to those declarations, you need to have some sort of infrastructure”.

“AI is really about who holds the rights to AI-generated work, or how you protect privacy and data from scraping” Sylla continues, so advice on privacy, intellectual property and cybersecurity law will be essential, as well as on the building and ownership of the physical infrastructure, including data centres, telecommunications and fibre optic cables.

### **GOOD GOVERNANCE MAKES GOOD NEIGHBOURS**

The declaration’s guiding principles call for safeguards “to prevent harm and protect privacy, ethics, transparency, and explainability while prioritising human dignity, rights, freedoms, and environmental sustainability”.

It also suggests the establishment of “a continent-wide knowledge sharing platform to inform best practices in AI governance” and promotion of “intergovernmental and regional cooperation including robust frameworks for cross border data flows”.

Sylla warns that “there have been a lot of declarations in Africa about many, many things and the percentage of them following through is very minute”. The content of those frameworks and how they can be developed between many nations with differing legal systems, economies and digital infrastructure, will be easier said than done.

There is inherent risk in the declaration’s plan to facilitate the collection, digitalisation and interoperability of data across “critical sectors”, including healthcare, agriculture, education and climate resilience, due to the sensitivity of that data to individuals, governments and private companies. The authors of the document acknowledged as much, promising to “develop robust data governance mechanisms, ensuring security and privacy through standardized data formats, metadata, exchange protocols, and encryption standards”.

Sylla says the most feasible legal infrastructure is through bilateral agreements between individual states, rather than a top-down infrastructure imposed by a multi-lateral organisation such as the African Union, Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) or even Smart Africa. “These are not the European Union, the level of integration is not to the level that you can have something that looks like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).”

She suggests the creation of treaties between three or four countries, with, for example, an IP code, which other states could then sign and implement individually. That would likely be led by larger economies such as Nigeria or Kenya, or by groups of countries with common ground, such as francophone nations with similar legal systems.

Sylla is also quick to downplay concerns about drafting laws across borders. “We are always talking about having African laws harmonised, but the reality is that they are very similar to one another. There is a lot of copy and pasting going on, so \[though\] you might get some slight differences, most of the time they look very similar,” she says, adding: “In Africa, the laws don’t differ that much.”

### **GOOD DATA, GOOD ETHICS**

The data protection question highlighted some of the wider ethical questions around AI development. The declaration also promised: “To safeguard African data, we shall incorporate data practices and systems that promote diversity, inclusion, consumer protection, and intellectual property rights that lead to equitable benefits and just outcomes for all.”

There are a number of tricky issues that are universal to AI as a whole, including whose data is used to train AI and how it is used.

The declaration acknowledged these broader ethical challenges, insisting that “safeguards must be implemented to prevent harm and protect privacy, ethics, transparency, and explainability while prioritizing human dignity, rights, freedoms, and environmental sustainability”.

It further promised “to position Africa as a global leader in ethical, trustworthy, and inclusive AI adoption”, but how that will be achieved, when so many governments and corporations around the world have struggled to balance the rights of the public with technological development, is lacking in detail at the moment.

### **MAKING IT REAL**

The legal implications of the declaration are myriad, including the proposed “Africa-first approach to AI procurement” and creation of a USD 60 billion Africa AI Fund, which, as with the establishment of any multi-national fund, will again require sound legal foundations.

Despite the lack of existing infrastructure and little current detail, Sylla is optimistic that progress can be made, at least on a national level, suggesting that “in five years most countries will have something”.

However, Sylla offers a cautionary tale. Of the multiple African governments she has advised on AI and data protection matters, all have said the same thing about past proposals for data governance: “Yes, that is beautiful, but we don’t have the means to implement it.” There is clearly a lot of work to do before the AI Declaration becomes a true reality.
