South Africa emerged as one of the biggest beneficiaries of Africa's tech start-up funding rebound in 2025, as total capital raised across the continent surged 46.2% year-on-year to $1.64 billion.

This is according to the latest African Tech Start-ups Funding Report by Disrupt Africa, compiled using funding data gathered throughout the year. The team tracks all publicly disclosed investment rounds and supplements this by polling start-ups, investors, hubs and other ecosystem stakeholders.

While the number of funded start-ups declined to 178, capital became more concentrated in larger rounds and established ecosystems — with SA emerging as one of the biggest beneficiaries. Capital remained concentrated in the "big four" countries — SA, Nigeria, Egypt and Kenya — which together accounted for 129 funded start-ups (72.5% of Africa's total) and $1.45 billion in funding (88% of total capital raised).

"In South Africa, a total of 42 start-ups secured funding — up 68% from 25 in 2024 — marking a clear reversal after several years of year-on-year declines. Collectively, South African ventures raised $335.9 million, accounting for 20.5% of Africa's total start-up funding. This represents a 234% surge from the $100.4 million raised in 2024. Although still below the $512.3 million peak seen in 2023, the rebound signals renewed investor confidence in the country's tech ecosystem."

The average deal size in SA climbed to $7.99 million, nearly doubling from $4.02 million in 2024.

**Fintech retains dominance**

Fintech start-ups accounted for 31% of funded ventures and attracted $124.97 million — 37.2% of SA's total funding. The local energy sector followed with $94.6 million (28.2%), while AI and IoT start-ups secured $22.83 million (6.8%). Agritech raised $19.6 million. Only 63.2% of disclosed rounds were pre-Series A or earlier — lower than peer markets — suggesting investors are increasingly backing scale-stage businesses locally.

Gender diversity continues to decline: only 16.7% of funded start-ups had at least one female co-founder, and just one company (2.4%) was led by a female CEO. One-third of local funded start-ups participated in incubator or accelerator programmes.

**Continental context**

Nigeria retained its position as Africa's top-funded start-up ecosystem in 2025, raising $464.8 million (28.4% of the continent's total) despite only 25 tech start-ups securing funding, with the highest average deal size in Africa at $18.6 million. Egypt ranked second, raising $378.95 million across 43 start-ups. Kenya placed fourth, with 19 start-ups raising $273.2 million (energy-dominated). Morocco raised $29.7 million (nine start-ups); Ghana jumped to $41.2 million; Tunisia's nine start-ups secured $12.25 million.

According to Disrupt Africa, the report reflects a minimum funding benchmark for 2025, with conservative estimates applied to undisclosed rounds.
