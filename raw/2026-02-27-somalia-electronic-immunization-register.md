Officials overseeing the implementation of the Electronic Immunization Register (EIR) system

On a bright morning in Kahda, a district on the southern edge of the Somali capital Mogadishu, a young mother stands at the immunization desk without the small, creased card that once governed her child’s medical future.

For years, that card easily misplaced during eviction, flood, or displacements was the only proof that a child had been vaccinated. When it disappeared or lost. Health workers would start again, guessing which doses had been given, searching through paper registers, repeating injections, or marking children as “zero-dose” because there was no way to know.

advertisements Now, nurse Naima Muse turns from a crying toddler, enters a number into a desktop computer, and retrieves the child’s immunization history within seconds.

“Before, if a mother lost the card, we had nothing,” she says. “Now, we just search.”

The system she is using is the Electronic Immunization Register (EIR) which was piloted in 14 health facilities across Banadir region in 2025. It assigns each vaccinated child a unique identification number, stores dose histories digitally and sends SMS reminders to caregivers. Health officials at district, state and federal levels can see aggregated data in near real time. The Ministry of Health says it plans to expand the system to more than 80 facilities nationwide by 2026.

At first glance, it is a story about digitizing a health registry. But in Somalia, where paper systems have long stood in for institutional continuity, the shift represents something larger: an experiment in Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI), the idea that interoperable digital systems, like ID-linked registries, can function as public goods. The question is not simply whether the software works. It is whether such infrastructure can alter how power, accountability and trust flow through a fragile state.

Until recently, immunization at Kahda Health Center followed a routine familiar across much of the country. A mother arrived with a vaccination card. A nurse flipped through thick paper registers to find the child’s name. If the card was missing, the process slowed. Staff would ask the mother to recall previous doses. Sometimes they began the schedule again. In busy periods, health workers filled out forms for hours after patients left.

“Previously it took time for follow-up,” says Naima, a nurse at the center. “There were campaigns and more worries regarding duplicate or zero-dose child.”

The term “zero-dose” refers to children who have not received even a first routine vaccine, a group estimated to be unusually large in Somalia. Conflict, drought, displacement and weak infrastructure have left roughly 60 percent of children without a single routine immunization dose in recent years, according to international estimates. Outbreaks of measles and diphtheria recur with regularity. In the first eleven months of 2025, Somalia recorded more than 3,000 suspected diphtheria cases and over 130 deaths.

In such conditions, paper records are not merely inconvenient; they are structurally fragile. Registers can be damaged or lost. Data cannot be easily consolidated across districts. Children who move and many do effectively start over.

Kahda district has become a major, overstretched hub for internally displaced persons (IDPs) fleeing conflict, drought, and floods, particularly from the Lower and Middle Shabelle regions. Overcrowded informal camps such as Biyo iyo Caano, Horseed, and Danyar shelter thousands of families facing acute shortages of food, water, and sanitation.

“We saw that many mothers did not remember how many vaccines their child had received or which doses were still due,” Naima says. “It was up to our team to determine and correct the vaccination schedule.”

During a recent visit, Naima administered an oral vaccine to a toddler whose mother had come without documentation. The woman looked anxious when asked for her card. Naima turned the monitor toward her and retrieved the record electronically. The mother relaxed. “This is one benefit,” Naima says. “The mother should not worry.”

Still, the system is not fully seamless. “Sometimes we record on paper and upload later,” Hani Ali notes. “It is not always real-time.” Electricity cuts, weak connectivity and staffing gaps persist. The digital layer overlays older constraints rather than eliminating them.

The Electronic Immunization Register is built on DHIS2, a widely used open-source health information platform. It includes a mobile application for frontline workers, dashboards for managers and automated SMS reminders for caregivers. It is supported technically by the World Health Organization and HISP Tanzania but owned by the Somali government.

Officials describe it as part of a broader move toward Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI, a framework that treats certain digital systems, including identification and registries, as foundational public utilities. In theory, such systems are secure, interoperable and inclusive. They reduce duplication, create shared standards and enable real-time oversight.

In practice, DPI is not just about efficiency. It reorganizes who sees information and who controls it.

“When a child is vaccinated, the data that the user has entered can be seen at the district, regional, state and national levels,” says Abdirahman Mohamed Mohamud, the Expanded Program on Immunization data manager at the Federal Ministry of Health.

“For example, child X, his age, his dose and his vaccination level is known.”

Under the old model, upward reporting was slow and often incomplete. Now, federal officials can monitor facility-level trends as they occur. In theory, this allows quicker identification of “zero-dose” pockets and defaulters. It also centralizes visibility.

The system generates a unique ID when a child first receives a vaccine. If the mother travels to another participating facility, staff can retrieve the record using that ID. Duplicate entries, Abdirahman says, are reduced. “No mistakes will happen.”

Yet experience from neighboring Kenya and Tanzania suggests that digital immunization systems rarely eliminate paper overnight. There, high maintenance costs, unreliable internet, system downtime and parallel reporting structures have limited impact. In some cases, data fragmentation persisted despite digitization.

Somalia faces similar risks. The EIR currently operates in only 14 facilities in the capital. Mogadishu alone has hundreds, if not thousands, of health points when private and informal clinics are counted. Outside the capital, expansion will require stable connectivity, trained personnel and sustained funding.

Digital infrastructure succeeds not because it exists, but because it is embedded in daily workflows and governed with clarity.

If paper records were fragile, digital records raise different concerns.

“The data belongs to the government,” Abdirahman says. “It is under the national data.” Access is tiered: federal, state, regional and district managers can view information according to role. “Not everyone can enter,” he says. “There are different layers to protect the data.”

At the facility level, individual users are issued credentials. “A data recorder is given to the user; no one other than him can access it,” he says.

Yet Somalia does not yet have a comprehensive, widely enforced data protection framework comparable to those in some other countries experimenting with DPI. Questions linger. Who audits access logs? How long is child-level data retained? What happens if a device is stolen? In insecure or contested areas, could data be misused?

Consent, officials say, is obtained at the point of registration. Mothers are given brief explanations before their child’s information is recorded and SMS reminders are activated.

Halimo Adan, 25, a mother of four, recalls her initial reaction when she received her first text reminder. “Firstly, I thought I need to pay the center for message,” she says. “Later they told me it is a new system to help children. She signed a consent form at the clinic. “We need this message to remind us of the time because we usually move place to another and we lost the card.”

Maryan Ahmed, 34, who has three children, was also asked to consent. She was given an ID number and told me she could retrieve it if lost.

“They found my son’s record immediately,” she says. “Before, I had to explain everything again.” Still, she hesitates. “I don’t know who else can see his information.”

In countries with stable regulatory institutions, data governance frameworks often evolve alongside digital systems. In Somalia’s federal structure where political disagreements between federal and state authorities persist harmonizing standards may prove complex.

If immunization data expands nationwide, it will create one of the most comprehensive child-level datasets in the country. Whether that dataset strengthens trust will depend less on software design than on transparency about how it is governed.

Officials attribute early improvements in follow-up rates partly to digital alerts. SMS reminders reduce reliance on radio announcements and manual phone calls.

“It is a 100 percent follow-up,” Abdirahman says, describing the system’s design. At Kahda, nurses report fewer missed appointments among mothers who receive messages.

But even supporters concede that the pilot’s scale limits measurable conclusions. Fourteen facilities cannot shift national immunization rates. Data quality improves within participating centers, yet many children still receive vaccines in facilities without digital registration.

Operationally, some changes are already visible. Nurses spend less time calling parents. Duplicate entries decline where IDs are used consistently. Managers can compare facilities’ performance without waiting for monthly paper submissions.

Still, paper has not disappeared. During connectivity outages, staff record information manually and upload later. The coexistence of two systems risks prolonging double work, a problem seen in other countries.

Health workers are becoming data operators, not just vaccinators. Supervisors can question discrepancies in real time. Accountability becomes more granular, Naima says.

Expanding from 14 facilities to more than 80 will test more than server capacity. Somalia’s federal system divides authority between the central government and member states. Political tensions have at times slowed cooperation in health initiatives.

There are also financial questions. International partners supported the pilot’s design and configuration. Long-term sustainability will require domestic budget allocations for maintenance, upgrades and training. Kenya and Tanzania’s experiences suggest that without consistent funding, systems stagnate or revert to partial paper use.

But trust is cumulative. It depends on whether data remains secure, whether consent is meaningful and whether expansion is equitable.

On her desk in Kahda, Naima keeps a stack of paper forms. The toddler on his mother’s lap is crying; the vaccine stings briefly before he quiets. The computer hums beside her. The system is still being tested. Sometimes she enters data later.

But the real measure will not be whether a dashboard updates instantly. It will be whether the system remains funded, governed and trusted and whether children who once disappeared between ledgers become, finally, visible.
