Changes in Sub-Saharan African communications markets have made most of the former Government telcos an irrelevance. Except where they have protected monopolies, they have lost market share and no longer know what they are for. This post looks at some examples (from a long list) and suggests ways of finishing their torment.

**The Context**

Before mobile operators launched on the continent, there were – with a few historic anomalies – only fixed line incumbent operators. They possessed a monopoly in their markets and in several cases also played the role of simultaneously regulating their own activities.

Of the more than 435 million fixed telephone lines in existence globally in 1988, only 6.4 million were in Africa. At the time, Sub-Saharan Africa’s population was 493 million. Between 1995 and 2000, the number of mobile subscribers in Sub-Saharan Africa went from just half a million subscribers to ten million. The new deregulating markets meant competition from a number of different directions for the telco incumbents.

With the introduction of pre-paid calling, the mobile operators received cash in advance for their services. The incumbent’s customers – most notably Government ministries – always paid late, if at all. Competition between mobile operators pushed down the overall cost of voice calling. Most people were calling mobile numbers so the new interconnection fees the incumbents paid increased rapidly. Accessing mobile services was faster than getting a fixed line. No more having to ‘dash’ the incumbent’s engineer to install a line.

An assessment of Ghana Telecom in this period noted that: “…the problem was inefficiency and poor management…The company was heavily over-staffed, slow to respond to customer complaints and employees frequently demanded side-payment.” It did not update its payment records and did not track government repayment.

In 2003, Telkom Kenya had 19,500 employees, 3,000 of whom were drivers. According to one report in 2005: “The levels of indebtedness ratchets relentlessly upward as the interconnect charges accumulate and the swollen wages bill continues to be paid despite advice from Telkom Kenya’s senior management that the nettle should be grasped.” In 2006, it had 100,000 people on a waiting list for a fixed line. These organisations seemed to benefit those who worked for them but failed to deliver more widely for all citizens.

One of the incumbents’ cash cows was international calling. Rates were set very high (in 1993 it cost US$7.50 a minute to call the UK or USA) and carriers outside Africa paid these “settlement rates’. Since the majority of calls were going into Africa at this point, international carriers were paying large sums to uncompetitive incumbents. A combination of action by the US regulator the FCC and pirated Voice over IP (VoIP) calls – either in cyber-cafes or corruptly in the carriers themselves - began to destroy this income stream. When a new international fibre cable, SAT3, was landed, they had set themselves up unsustainable fibre monopolies that cracked under wider scrutiny.

Some Government incumbents were able to secure themselves mobile licences but outside of the Ethio Telecom’s historic monopoly, not a single one of them was able to create a business where they achieved the number one or number two spot where there were three carriers or more.

Opportunities like ADSL, fixed wireless and rolling out key fibre routes, seemed to float past most of them. They lacked the vision to spot opportunities, the capital to invest, the market drive to make things happen and the skills to do new things. The mobile operators became the new incumbents and swept the old incumbents to one side.

**The core problems**

There are four core problems that make these former Government telco incumbents ‘stranded assets’ for which it is extremely hard to find external investment.

**1\. Lack of vision**

In the light of the issues outlined below, it is hardly surprising that the former telco incumbents have a lack of vision. But everything starts with some idea of what role they might play and for whom? Do they deliver public benefits to the Government other than seeming to give jobs to large numbers of people? Even if they deliver public benefits, how do they fund these? By generating market profits? From Government subsidy?

Almost from the point that they lost their monopolies, the former incumbents have begged Governments for mobile licences as a way of creating a modern ‘cash cow’ to help support their operations. This has been a delusion as they have neither the skills nor capital required to make a success of this kind of business. For example, SBIN’s Celtis in Benin is still in third position after three years in existence. After 15 years of operations, Zamtel’s Zambia MNO remains in third position. Even well-funded experienced operators (like Orange in East Africa) have found it more or less impossible to climb out of third or fourth position in an existing market.

The former incumbents have failed to play almost any part in the more recent dynamic parts of the market: data centres, Artificial Intelligence, Fibre-To-The-Home, digital content and mobile money.

**2\. Lack of capital**

Whatever part of the communications business you look at in Sub-Saharan Africa, it is extremely capital-intensive. MNOs have been investing hundreds of millions of dollars in keeping their networks up-to-date and efficient. Building new fibre routes – whether to homes, around countries or linking to neighbours – requires similar amounts. The investment in data centres runs into the billions,

Funding of this kind will come from three main sources: capital markets, internally generated profits, Government subsidy or international donors. Only one of the five cases of former incumbents given below generates sufficient profits to re-invest and therefore be able to make a case for investment. The World Bank provides funding to try and effect transitions from loss-making state ownership to private owners but the road is very narrow. The example of Gamel below is one such case illustrating the perils and limitations of this route.

Governments reluctantly provide on-going subsidies to former incumbents because: they don’t want to make lots of people redundant; they don’t have a better idea of what to do with them and feel somehow that ‘national sovereignty’ will be threatened in some unspecified way if they no longer have them.

**3\. Lack of skills**

The former incumbent telcos were founded in a time when their main business was voice and fixed lines. Employees are often now elderly and most have had little or no experience outside of the former incumbent. Most of the new skills are internet and fibre-related and only in a very few instances have the former incumbents successfully made any part of that transition.

At its best, the requirement to make profit drives certain kinds of management skills and attitudes among employees. The better the business is doing, the more likely you are to keep your job and be paid well. MNOs are public companies where the scrutiny of their performance affects their share price and the valuation of the business.

**4\. Patronage, workforce culture and corruption**

Almost all former incumbents were over-staffed even when they were just providing voice on fixed lines. As organisations, they have been a significant source of patronage for Government politicians, their relatives and friends.

In the absence of market discipline, the former incumbents simply expanded. The 2006 report on Benin Telecoms (the predecessor to SBIN) discovered that multiple layers of management had been put in place and that their salaries were unrelated to performance. Being a manager in a former incumbent came with certain privileges: how else could you account for Telkom Kenya at one point having 3,000 drivers?

There were a number of well-documented corruption cases where managers in the wholesale voice departments sold local access and international minutes for their own benefit based on being bribed. There are also both documented (eg EthioTelecom) and undocumented cases of managers taking bribes to favour particular equipment vendors. The resulting equipment choices were not always beneficial to the organisation.

**Cases in point**

Nearly every former incumbent telco is alike but each is an unhappy story in its own way. I have chosen five cases to look at: Telkom Kenya, Sotel Tchad, Gambia’s Gamtel and Cameroon’s Camtel. The table below summarises their profit and loss position as far as can be ascertained. State-owned telcos financial reporting leaves much to be desired. Often former telco incumbents will include outstanding Government bills as creditors. However, they are either unlikely to be paid or only very slowly. Only Camtel shows a significant profit but this is based on having both national and international wholesale monopolies, something that means that retail data is both more expensive for Cameroonians and wholesale data is more costly for its Chadian neighbours (see Sotel Tchad below).

**Case 1: Telkom Kenya**

After a World Bank transition process, the Kenyan Government sold 60% of Telkom Kenya to Orange for $390 million. Orange bought it as part of an East African expansion and believed its expertise would make it a mobile market leader. In 2013, it increased its stake to 70%. Having failed to challenge Safaricom’s dominance, it sold 60% to Helios in 2016 and returned 10% to the Government. The acquisition price paid by Helios is not in the public domain. Six years later after a dispute about the sale of its stake, Helios sold its 60% back to the Kenyan government for US$46.85 million. It emerged that Telkom Kenya has liabilities of US$291.12 million.

In other words, the overall value of the company declined dramatically and its liabilities have continued to grow to unsustainable levels. Leaving aside the skills of the management and the workforce, the business model of fixed lines + network infrastructure and cables + mobile has proven to be unprofitable. Its mobile operation is down to less than 1 million subscribers and large part of its debt is owed to American Towers for its 4,000 cell sites. Its mobile money operation has only tens of thousands of users. There are two special circumstances: Safaricom’s position in the market as a dominant operator and the Government’s favourable treatment of Safaricom because of the dividends it derives from its shareholding that will soon be reduced but not eliminated.

As one industry source told me: “Private equity was not the right choice for a telco partner. They came with a fixed investment amount and were chasing growth in the short term. They needed to spend US$200-300 million to build a network. But Government are also not the best partners either. They don’t have deep pockets and are not patient. You have to be ready to make mistakes.” So, what’s the Government’s next move?

In public purpose terms, Telkom Kenya is unlikely to be an effective tool. It ran the contract for the Government-funded national network (NOFBI) at a loss and by all accounts at below par service levels.

In for-profit terms, the remaining assets of any value are its national fibre networks and landing stations. If the company is to survive on this basis, it needs to radically downsize and it may simply be cleaner to “sell it for parts.” The mobile operation is unlikely to ever be profitable.

**Case 2: Sotel Tchad**

Former telco incumbent Sotel Tchad was sold in 2010 to the Libyan state investment vehicle Lap Green. Because of the civil war in Libya following Gadaffi’s downfall, the Government nationalised the company. At one point, it had 10,000 employees but this is now down to under 500. Its fibre network is largely in the capital Ndjamena and parts of the south of the country. It once had protective monopolies on national and international networks but these were removed as part of the Government’s response to widespread public discontent with both the price and quality of internet services.

The Government first announced its intention to reprivatize the company in 2014 and has re-announced its intentions several times. In February last year its CEO announced the process again, offering a 60% stake, saying: “The selected advisory bank will be responsible for the operator’s valuation process, proposing a sale strategy and facilitating contacts with potential buyers.” There is no sign of the advisory bank having been appointed. In 2024, the Minister responsible for telecoms visited Huawei in China and asked for funding for a 5G platform that Sotel Tchad might use to relaunch its former mobile service, Salam Mobile. This idea has been sold rhetorically as a way of providing more competition and therefore improving prices and service.

The issue that neither Sotel Tchad nor the Government have solved, which is fundamental to the future success of the industry in the country, is the creation of a reliable international fibre link. As a landlocked country, it faces civil unrest in five out of the six countries that are its neighbours. The historic fibre route has been through Cameroon which although not plagued by civil strife, has both been unreliable and costly in wholesale terms because of the Camtel monopoly. Unlike in more competitive markets, the wholesale fibre price in Chad is a large percentage of the final retail internet price.

Frustrated by Sotel Tchad’s inability to maintain this link or to build new ones, it gave a 20-year concession to a new entity called SudaChad Telecom on a PPP basis to build, operate and maintain a link from the Sudanese border to Cameroon in the west of the country. Eventually both Camtel and Sudatel joined this entity but no progress has been made. With this plan, it was able to get a commitment of 20.5 bn CFA of funding from the EU and the African Development Bank.

In April 2025, the Government signed an agreement with Sotel Tchad and Airtel to open a second N’Djamena–Mberé fibre corridor and the month following Camtel committed to building the connecting section to its side of the border. Then the Government had second thoughts and started trying to revise the terms of the contract for reasons of ‘national sovereignty’ and the state’s strategic interests. There is no clarity was about what the Government is seeking but it is likely to be about its capital contribution being too small to guarantee overall control and Airtel’s desire to manage the link.

The Government has announced the Tchad Connexion initiative where it says it will spend US$1.5 billion by 2030 on digital initiatives. The strategy includes: telecoms sector reform, infrastructure upgrades and e-government.

In terms of public purposes, Sotel Tchad is clearly a failure as a vehicle to drive a solution to the country’s need for reliable, cost-effective, redundant fibre links. Nothing shows this more clearly than the need for the creation of a PPP, build-operate-and-transfer special purpose vehicle. The Government’s funding of the Tchad Connexion initiative clearly illustrates that it has the kind of funding that could solve this key issue. I’m left feeling that maybe the Government finds performative rhetoric easier than actually getting stuff done.

In for-profit terms, it’s hard to make a case for Sotel Tchad’s continued existence except perhaps as a wholesale and retail fibre operator. But then there’s the ‘chicken-and-the-egg’ challenge: it’s not really possible to make that business case successful without those international fibre links.

**Case 3: Gamtel**

Gamtel was launched as the national telecommunications in 1984 and it launched its mobile operation as a separate company called Gamcel in 2000. In a market of four operators, Gamcel has always been in the third or fourth position. Gamtel has had a monopoly over domestic fibre infrastructure, control of an international voice gateway. Its national network has been under-invested, badly managed and poorly maintained. To overcome some of the barriers created by the monopolies, Gambia Submarine Cable Company was set up to own and manage the ACE international landing station. Shareholders included: the Government (19%), Gamtel (20%) with the balance being made up from contributions from local MNOs and lSPs. It started operations in 2012.

Following a trend that began around 2005, Gamtel set up a monopoly international voice gateway, ostensibly to try and ensure that more revenue went to the incumbent and there was no tax ‘fraud’ about undeclared international call revenues. In 2006, this revenue control gateway was run by Global Voice Group and thereafter by Spectrum, System 1, TELL and MG1. All these contracts were deal with directly by the Office of the President (Jammeh).

Similarly in 2007, the President’s Office took a decision to sell 50% of Gamtel to one of these contractors, a Lebanese group called Spectrum. Initially, they were given a management contract but that was terminated after a year and the sale was called off.

With a democratically elected Government in 2016, the Government engaged with the World Bank to look at what options there might be for Gamtel’s future. In 2019, the World Bank report identified three possible options: sell to another operator, a management buy-out or spin-off cost-centres as separate enterprises. For reasons that will become clear below, the legacy of the existing management precluded the last two.

In June last year, a Gambia Court ordered Gamtel to pay US$2.9 million to Bankai International for unpaid telecoms services. Again, this related to the contract for the voice gateway. The owner of this company and others, Bankim Brahmbhatt, went bankrupt in November 2025 and is facing allegations of fraud elsewhere.

A tender process for the sale of 80% of Gamtel has been carried out and the buyer is Y Cell, owned by a local company called Yoona with interests in micro finance, insurance and commodities trading. Gamcel will be sold separately. Yoona has had no previous telecoms experience. Meanwhile, the company has entered into a US$50 million Public Private Partnership agreement with Senegalese company Sysroad to upgrade and modernise the country’s national broadband and digital infrastructure (from 50Gbps to 800Gbps). Sysroad is a systems integrator with an impressive portfolio of corporate projects, which has carried out telecoms projects for Orange Business Services and Expresso. The contract will be financed by the Gambian Government out of the purchase price.

There must be doubts about whether the local shareholders have sufficiently deep pockets to make further investment and where the management and local skills will be found to turn around this deeply troubled company.

**Case 4: Camtel**

Government telco incumbent Camtel is included as the exception that proves the rule. Despite efforts to liberalise the market, Camtel has retained its near monopoly on wholesale networks, international terrestrial fibre links (See Sotel Tchad above) and its ownership of all the international fibre landing stations, one of which the Government forced MTN to sell. It has launched a mobile operation but remains in third position, a very long way behind Orange and MTN. It recently signed an agreement with EthioTelecom to provide it with a mobile money product but will find it hard to break into the existing market.

However, its near monopoly has allowed it to set prices on its network that have given it something approaching a healthy financial balance sheet. Appointed in 2018, the D-G of Camtel, Judith Yah Sunday, is energetic and has driven the business on the basis of business model combining networks + mobile. She was married to the late former Prime Minister, Simon Achidi Achidi Achu. A ‘success’ at Camtel would guarantee her the opportunity of a political role later in life.

In January this year, Camtel signed a financing agreement with Commercial Bank Cameroon for US$81.2 million. The bank is 100% Government-owned. This forms part of an overall investment package of US$94.47 million, the balance being financed by Afriland First Bank, Union Bank of Cameroon, and National Financial Credit (also Government-owned). The funds are targeted to the first phase of mobile network expansion. In 2025, it raised US$65.65 million from the Bank of Central African States (BEAC), which is owned and run by its Government members.

The company is betting over US$150 million on creating a successful mobile company, with the majority of funds coming from Government controlled sources. No Government telco incumbent – outside of Ethiopia – has achieved it. Being third, will not guarantee sufficient profits to keep re-investing.

Much rests on the protected revenues of its near monopoly and how long they can be maintained. With the country run by a recently re-elected 92-year-old President, things will change some time in the next three years.

**Case 5: SCPT**

SCPT, which handles both post and telecoms in DRC, was founded in the colonial period and has survived civil wars and political upheavals. In 2011 the WACS international fibre cable was due to come ashore with SCPT as its landing party but no landing station had been built. Not only had the contract been given to a local company without the required knowledge and experience but the US$3 million worth of Government funds allocated for the project had vanished. The D-G was subsequently charged with high treason and jailed.

The link from Kinshasa to the landing station at Muanda was poorly built, with the fibre insufficiently buried and subsequently, poorly maintained, leading to frequent outages on this vital international link. According to one industry insider, it would cost US$7-9 million to redo the link properly.

Financial reporting on the performance of SCPT is almost completely absent. A World Bank report in 2020 noted its monopolistic attitudes and that several key parts of its network were in poor shape. There has been no sign of major investment in the network since 2020. Excluding the postal workers, it has around a thousand employees, a significant proportion of whom are over 55.

In 2021, an Egyptian company Benya (key investor, UAE’s G42) signed a shareholders’ agreement with the Government to buy SCPT. The telecoms minister said the aim was to connect 70% of the citizens of DRC, building a 16,000 kms network. Benya left after 6 months, presumably feeling that this was not likely to be profitable and that the expectations were unrealistic.

The current CEO, Sandra Tshibonge Mbiye is related to the President but had had many years telecoms experience in DRC with Orange, Tigo and Vodacom, particularly on the B2B side, before being appointed. One industry insider conceded that she was “doing a good job under difficult circumstances.”

In terms of public purposes, SCPT has not proved an effective tool for managing the existing network, particularly the strategic international link and landing station. Without a full transition process, it’s hard to imagine how this situation could be transformed. Even were that to occur, it’s very hard to see any kind of investor in the network assets the company currently has.

**My view**

In relation to former telco incumbents, African Governments have tended to view them as a problem for tomorrow and this indecision has meant that the ground on which they might survive has narrowed considerably. African Governments and effective business performance are like oil and water: they don’t mix. Most former telco incumbents have histories that contain at best dubious governance and at worst, full-blown corruption scandals. As anyone who has taken one over will tell you, the lack of reliable, commercial accounting leaves too many ‘skeletons in the cupboard’ that emerge later at some cost.

• If there was a role for Government-owned telco incumbents, it was to achieve objectives that were not solely achievable by the private sector in the market. But the limits of the market have proved to be far wider than originally envisaged in the early 2000s.

• So what are national strategic objectives that might not be met by the private sector? In those countries where Government insists on say building a fibre network, it’s often because there is no real competition beyond a couple of dominant mobile operators.

• Leaving that aside, there are probably two national, strategic objectives that are worth consideration: a national fibre network that actually reaches most of the population and providing digital services in un-serviced areas. The latter is probably better tackled by community networks and the companies that extend mobile coverage. The continuing failure to deploy Universal Service Funds to address some of these key objectives illustrates a lack of imagination. (When they were used in Kenya at the beginning of the TEAMS project for this major project, there was a terrific furore.)

• There is a track-record in Africa of PPPs that have tackled strategic objectives, like the building and operating of landing stations and the creation of national fibre networks. But these have worked best with narrow objectives where Governments have understood that getting something done is not the same as needing to control it.

• At the risk of sounding like a cracked record, creating a mobile business is not a successful way to financially protect failing telco incumbents. The same is probably true of FTTH. The only part of the business that might work is the network infrastructure but this would be a much smaller entity with a focus on service and maintenance.

• The former telco incumbents have become an irrelevance. One measure of this is how they can only attract investors whose names are not easily recognizable. Also that they are frequently sold, before being bought back by Government, before being sold on again. Perhaps now is the time for Governments to admit that they no longer have a purpose and to ‘break them up for spare parts’?
