*A brief note before we begin: I write this from an implementation perspective. My work sits closer to data, AI, and digital transformation than to policy analysis, so the argument that follows is less about governance theory and more about what technological change looks like when institutions have to absorb it.*

Everyone is racing to get “AI-ready.” But AI was never the destination, it was the warning. The real question is whether your foundations can absorb whatever comes next. No country has fully cracked this yet, but the lessons of the last few years, and the choices Africa’s anchor economies make now, will determine who is ready when the next wave arrives.

## The Race Everyone Is Running

Walk into any African ministry of digital affairs or any government innovation office today, and you will hear a version of the same sentence: *“We need an AI strategy.”*

The numbers explain the urgency. The African Union [reported that over 83% of AI startup funding](https://au.int/en/pressreleases/20250517/africa-declares-ai-strategic-priority-investment-inclusion-and-innovation) on the continent in the first quarter of 2025 flowed to just four countries: Kenya, Nigeria, South Africa, and Egypt. The AU adopted a [Continental AI Strategy in 2024](https://carnegieendowment.org/posts/2025/09/understanding-africas-ai-governance-landscape-insights-from-policy-practice-and-dialogue?lang=en). National strategies followed in a cascade: [Kenya in March 2025](https://www.globalcenter.ai/research/african-countries-are-racing-to-create-ai-strategies-but-are-they-putting-the-cart-before-the-horse), Egypt already on its second iteration, Nigeria publishing drafts, South Africa finalising policy in 2026. Globally, the pattern is identical. By [one count](https://www.index.dev/blog/ai-readiness-index-statistics), the number of new national AI strategies launched in 2024 tripled compared to the year before.

Governments are, in other words, building AI stacks: procuring models, standing up chatbots, drafting governance frameworks, signing memoranda with hyperscalers.

This is understandable, and in many cases it reflects genuine urgency and political will. But it may also be a strategic miscalculation. Not because AI doesn’t matter, but because *the AI stack is the wrong unit of planning*. Governments that build for AI are building for a technology. Governments that build a **dynamic tech stack** are building for change itself.

The difference will define which states absorb the next thirty years of disruption and which states are forced to rebuild from scratch each time the next wave arrives.

## What AI Actually Taught Us

[ChatGPT reached 100 million users in two months](https://www.businessofapps.com/data/chatgpt-statistics/). TikTok took nine months to reach the same milestone. Instagram took two and a half years. [In twenty years tracking internet adoption, UBS analysts wrote at the time, they could not recall a faster ramp for any consumer application](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/chatgpt-sets-record-fastest-growing-190911828.html). Generative AI went from research curiosity to policy mandate in roughly eighteen months.

In doing so it ran an unplanned stress test on every government on earth and the results were instructive.

**Governments with foundational infrastructure absorbed it quickly.** Estonia, Singapore, and the UAE, which had invested in digital foundations for years, plugged AI into existing infrastructure within months. Singapore had [treated digital capability as national infrastructure for a decade](https://www.allaboutai.com/resources/ai-statistics/global-ai-adoption/), with the overwhelming majority of government services already online and a national AI commitment running to 2027. The UAE had [appointed a Minister of State for AI in 2017](https://blogs.microsoft.com/on-the-issues/2026/01/08/global-ai-adoption-in-2025/), five years before generative AI became a mainstream conversation; when the wave arrived, governance frameworks were already in place. Estonia added another layer to a thirty-year-old architecture built on one principle: digital identity and interoperable data first, applications later. Estonia is now [contracting its post-quantum cryptography transition](https://cyber.ee/resources/news/estonia-pqc-transition/), preparing for the next wave while others are still working through this one.

**Governments without those foundations found AI had nothing to stand on.** Without clean data, without interoperable systems, without a functioning identity layer, they purchased tools that couldn’t connect to the information they needed to be useful. The AI itself was not the problem, the infrastructure underneath it was.

The lesson is important, and it points beyond AI: **governments that absorbed this wave well had built foundations that didn’t care which technology arrived.** They had not built for AI specifically, they had built for change.

That is the dynamic tech stack, and it matters now because AI is not the last wave. Agentic systems that act rather than answer are already deploying. Quantum computing will eventually require a wholesale upgrade of the cryptographic foundations underneath government systems, which is why the EU has set a [2030 deadline](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202601/cybernetica-to-develop-estonias-roadmap-for-pqc-begins-e-state-upgrade-for-crisis-resilience) for post-quantum cryptography in critical infrastructure. The interval between waves is shrinking and transformation is no longer a project with an end date, it is the permanent operating environment.

## The Framework: AI Stack vs. Dynamic Tech Stack

It is worth being precise about the distinction, because the two approaches look superficially similar (both involve cloud, data, and models) but they answer fundamentally different questions.

An **AI stack** answers: *“How do we deploy artificial intelligence?”*

A **dynamic tech stack** answers: *“How do we absorb any technology we cannot yet name?”*

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LWMR!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc46d2c84-d669-437d-8df8-e58e6d3e6e09_921x808.png)

The five foundations of a dynamic stack, in order of permanence:

1. **A trust and identity layer:** Verified digital identity for every resident and business, under transparent governance. This is the social contract rendered as infrastructure, and every subsequent technology, from AI agents to autonomous public services, authenticates against it.
2. **A sovereign data layer:** Data that the state owns, governs, and can move: in open formats, with clear lineage, privacy architecture, and quality standards. Models, vendors, and paradigms will change; the data layer is the one asset that compounds in value across all of them.
3. **An orchestration and abstraction layer:** Every capability exposed as an API. Every dependency swappable. This is what allowed well-prepared governments to avoid lock-in with any single AI vendor, and what will allow them to plug in quantum compute or agentic systems as they mature, without rebuilding from scratch.
4. **An adaptive governance layer:** Procurement frameworks that allow modular, specialist vendors rather than decade-long monolithic contracts. Regulation written for capabilities rather than specific products. Institutions that can issue, revise, and retire rules at the pace technology actually moves.
5. **A human absorption layer:** Workforce capability, leadership literacy, and public trust. Three of the five layers of a dynamic stack are not technical. This is why technology procurement, on its own, has never produced transformation.

A government with these five layers in place does not need to predict what comes next. It needs only to be ready to plug it in.

## Africa’s Big Four and the Stack Question

Nowhere is this distinction more consequential right now than in Africa, and nowhere is the decision more visible than in its four anchor economies.

The concentration of digital capacity on the continent is significant. South Africa alone holds [roughly 70% of Africa’s total digital infrastructure capacity](https://www.inonafrica.com/2026/04/22/governing-the-digital-economy-africas-shift-from-connectivity-to-control/). Egypt, Kenya, Morocco, and Nigeria account for a further 15%, leaving fifty other AU member states to share the remainder. The four biggest tech economies attract the overwhelming majority of AI investment. Whatever foundational architecture these four choose will shape, in practice, the trajectory of digital governance across the continent.

Before assessing each, it is worth stating the shared context honestly. All four are navigating infrastructure constraints that most wealthy-nation comparisons ignore: unreliable power grids, limited connectivity outside urban centres, thin technical workforces, legacy systems inherited from pre-digital eras, and the ongoing reality that nearly [half a billion Africans still lack foundational legal identity](https://advox.globalvoices.org/2025/04/21/digital-id-systems-in-africa-a-dream-of-inclusion-or-a-threat-to-privacy/). What these governments are attempting, under these conditions, is genuinely difficult. The question is not whether they are working hard enough. It is whether the strategic direction, given the constraints, is one that builds durable foundations or risks creating new dependencies.

The tension is visible in the policy record itself. Nigeria, Egypt, and Kenya have each published [draft AI policies since early 2025 that explicitly name dependence on foreign technology companies as a threat to national security](https://restofworld.org/2026/africa-ai-sovereignty-big-tech/), while each continues to rely on those same companies for compute, capital, and expertise. This is not hypocrisy, it is the difficult reality of building sovereign capability in a world where the infrastructure you need is largely owned by others. The dynamic stack framework is one way to navigate that tension: own the permanent layers, rent the transient ones, and reduce dependency over time.

The geopolitical context sharpens the stakes. The Atlantic Council and others now describe a global [“battle of the AI stacks”](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/dispatches/eight-ways-ai-will-shape-geopolitics-in-2026/), with the United States and China actively competing to export their full technology verticals (chips, cloud, models, applications) to third countries. A government that adopts an exported AI stack wholesale is not building national capability. It is, in effect, leasing it. The terms of that lease, including the upgrade cycle, the data conditions, and the exit costs, are set elsewhere.

The dynamic stack is the structural alternative: own the identity layer, the data layer, and the governance architecture, and use market competition among vendors for the layers that change faster. With that structure in place, no single provider becomes indispensable.

How do the big four measure against that standard?

**Nigeria is building the identity layer at genuine scale.** With [more than 130 million National Identification Numbers enrolled](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202606/nigeria-looks-beyond-enrollment-as-digital-id-passes-130-million), Nigeria operates Africa’s largest digital ID system, backed by roughly $430 million from the World Bank and European partners. It is [migrating to MOSIP](https://www.vanguardngr.com/2026/04/why-digital-identity-is-central-to-nigerias-digital-transformation-agenda-olasiyan/), an open-source identity platform, a deliberate move toward reducing vendor dependency. The NIN already underpins SIM registration, banking verification, academic registration, and social protection schemes. This is meaningful progress on a genuinely hard problem at enormous scale. The next challenge, which Nigeria is actively grappling with, is building the ecosystem layer on top of that foundation: actual usage of digital credentials remains low, services remain fragmented across agencies, and the country currently [loses an estimated $850 million a year](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202606/nigeria-links-digital-identity-ambitions-to-digital-sovereignty-agenda) to foreign hosting and digital service dependency. The foundation is real, the work of converting it into a functioning stack is ongoing.

**Egypt is making the case for sequenced, state-led delivery.** Egypt [stands out in continental assessments](https://columncontent.com/the-state-of-ai-policy-in-africa-in-2025/) as one of the few countries with an AI strategy that has moved from policy document to active implementation, now in its second phase, with applied work in education, energy efficiency, and water management. The strength of Egypt’s approach is its clarity of sequencing and the institutional capacity to execute. The open question, from a dynamic-stack perspective, is how the architecture evolves as it matures: centrally coordinated systems can deploy with speed and coherence, but the most durable stacks also create conditions for distributed innovation, the startups and unexpected applications that emerge when infrastructure is open and accessible to builders beyond government.

**South Africa has the infrastructure and is now building the architecture to match.** Holding the large majority of the continent’s data-centre and connectivity capacity, South Africa is Africa’s most significant compute base. That is a considerable asset, and one the continent depends on. The policy process has taken longer than the infrastructure investment, with the national AI policy finalised in 2026 after an earlier draft was withdrawn for revision. That revision process, working through the implications carefully rather than publishing prematurely, reflects the seriousness with which South Africa is approaching the governance question. The challenge now is ensuring that formidable physical infrastructure is matched by the integration layers, the APIs, the identity architecture, the data standards, that turn raw capacity into a stack others can build on.

**Kenya presents the most layered picture, because it arrived at some foundational elements earlier than almost any comparable economy, through a path nobody designed.**

## Kenya: An Early Foundation, and the Work Still Ahead

In the early 2000s, Kenya had limited formal financial infrastructure outside its major cities and one of the most unbanked populations in the world. What it had was widespread mobile phone adoption, and a population that began [using prepaid airtime as an informal currency](https://www.pmi.org/learning/library/top-50-projects-m-pesa-11761), trading minutes for cash and goods in the absence of accessible banking. Safaricom and Vodafone formalised that behaviour in 2007 as M-Pesa, and what followed became one of the most studied cases in development economics.

Kenya bypassed the conventional pathway of branch-banking infrastructure entirely. Mobile money accounts [came to outnumber traditional bank accounts](https://theawarenessnews.com/2026/03/27/m-pesa-how-mobile-money-transformed-financial-inclusion-and-redefined-development-finance/). [Research from MIT and Georgetown](https://fintechassociation.africa/blog/at-18-m-pesa-faces-its-adult-future-kenyas-mobile-money-giant-at-a-crossroads) estimated that M-Pesa alone lifted nearly 200,000 Kenyan households out of extreme poverty. A network of over a hundred thousand agents extended financial services into communities that physical infrastructure never reached.

Viewed through the dynamic stack framework, something deeper is visible. Kenya had inadvertently developed early versions of three foundational layers that most countries, including wealthy ones, were still building:

- **A trust layer:** Long before digital government services became standard elsewhere, ordinary Kenyans developed comfort and confidence in digital financial transactions. That cultural familiarity with digital services became, over time, one of Kenya’s most valuable assets for subsequent innovation.
- **A transaction and data layer:** A real-time record of economic activity that later made it possible to build AI-driven credit scoring for populations with no formal financial history.
- **A distribution layer:** The agent network became, in effect, a last-mile delivery infrastructure for any future digital service.

Subsequent developments built on that base. The government’s [eCitizen platform](https://www.jepaafrica.com/insights/fw7dll38wtil0rywpyvkbdtrs7mzkl), now integrating thousands of services and processing millions of applications, works in part because digital payment infrastructure and a baseline of digital trust already existed. Kenya’s strength in fintech, its position as a leading destination for AI venture investment on the continent, its March 2025 National AI Strategy positioning it as East Africa’s hub for AI development: all of it reflects the cumulative effect of a foundation that was laid early, even if unintentionally.

The more complex part of Kenya’s story is what the two decades since M-Pesa reveal about the difference between an accidental foundation and a deliberate, open stack.

Kenya still does not have a fully functioning national digital identity system. [Huduma Namba](https://dpi.africa.com/africas-digital-public-infrastructure-is-powering-a-new-era-of-inclusion/), the initiative to consolidate identity records into a unified platform, faced significant legal challenges around privacy and data governance, and its successor is still in rollout. Government data remains fragmented across agencies. And the payments infrastructure that could have evolved into open national infrastructure remains largely a private platform: Kenyan developers have noted that India’s UPI, an open, publicly governed payments rail, has [enabled thousands of fintechs to build at near-zero cost](https://fintechassociation.africa/blog/at-18-m-pesa-faces-its-adult-future-kenyas-mobile-money-giant-at-a-crossroads), while Kenya’s ecosystem depends on a dominant private actor as gatekeeper.

None of this diminishes what Kenya built. The point is a structural one; the layers that arrived by accident were built to serve a product, not to serve as public infrastructure. Converting them into a sovereign, open foundation requires intentional decisions that market forces alone will not produce. That is the work Kenya is now undertaking, and the question of whether it does so through open architecture will shape how much of its early advantage compounds into the next wave.

The Kenyan experience offers a clear and generalisable principle: **an early foundation is a significant advantage, but only open architecture sustains the compounding.**

## Rwanda: What Deliberate Architecture Looks Like

Rwanda is not among Africa’s big four by economic scale, but it belongs in this analysis because it represents the clearest available example of what intentional, layered digital public infrastructure looks like in an African context.

Beginning with the [IremboGov platform in 2015](https://govinsider.asia/intl-en/article/how-rwanda-built-a-credible-execution-ready-digital-public-infrastructure-dpi), Rwanda set out to build digital government as a governance strategy, not as a technology procurement exercise. IremboGov now hosts over six hundred government services, with roughly 80% of applications completed fully online. Rwanda’s newer payments infrastructure settles transactions across all banks and mobile wallets in under fifteen seconds and was deliberately built as open-source, openly governed infrastructure, designed to integrate savings cooperatives and microfinance institutions that commercial platforms had historically excluded. Interoperable electronic health records are being connected into the same identity and service architecture.

The significance of Rwanda’s approach is less about the specific platforms and more about the sequencing logic: identity first, then payments, then services, then data interoperability. And at each layer, a deliberate choice toward openness and public governance rather than proprietary vendor architecture. Rwanda is not leading with AI procurement, it is building the layers on which AI, and whatever follows it, will run.

Rwanda’s model is not without its own questions. It is a small, highly centralised state, and the degree of central coordination that made its digital strategy coherent may not translate directly to larger, more federated economies. And it too remains years from a complete stack. But as an illustration of architectural intent, of what it looks like to build layers deliberately and openly, it is the most instructive example currently available on the continent.

The contrast with Kenya is instructive precisely because both countries started from comparable positions. Kenya had an earlier and larger foundation, arrived at accidentally. Rwanda began its deliberate build later, with fewer resources. Neither has a complete stack today; but Rwanda’s trajectory is more predictable, because it is being driven by architecture rather than by accident.

The more pressing question for the big four is not whether they have the resources Rwanda lacked, but whether they are directing the resources they have toward the permanent layers or the transient ones.

## The Choice Ahead

The AI wave was an accelerated version of a pattern that has repeated with every major technology cycle: the organisations and governments that absorbed it best were not the ones that had predicted AI specifically. They were the ones that had built foundations capable of absorbing change in general. Estonia, Singapore, and the UAE benefited from decisions made years and in some cases decades before generative AI existed. Kenya benefited, partially, from an accident that happened to produce foundational layers.

What is different now is the pace. The interval between technology waves is compressing, and the consequences of being caught unprepared compound with each cycle. A government still rebuilding foundational infrastructure when the next wave arrives will face a more compressed timeline than the one AI provided.

Africa’s big four face a version of this decision that will have consequences well beyond their borders, given the concentration of the continent’s digital capacity in their hands. The path of least resistance is to continue acquiring AI capabilities from the major technology providers, achieving real and visible results in the near term, while the underlying architecture remains dependent on external infrastructure and external terms. Their own published policies name this risk clearly. The harder path is to prioritise the permanent layers: sovereign identity architecture, open data standards, interoperable payments rails, governance frameworks built for capabilities rather than products, and the procurement reforms that allow modular, competitive vendor relationships rather than long-term lock-in. That path is slower to show results, it is also the one that compounds.

The [$720 million Cassava-Nvidia partnership](https://carnegieendowment.org/posts/2025/09/understanding-africas-ai-governance-landscape-insights-from-policy-practice-and-dialogue?lang=en) to build AI infrastructure across the big four economies represents exactly the kind of investment the continent needs. The question is whether the states receiving that infrastructure own the layers that allow them to use it on their own terms, or whether they are, in effect, plugging external infrastructure into external architecture with limited national control over either.

One continental analysis asked whether Africa’s rush of AI strategies was [putting the cart before the horse](https://www.globalcenter.ai/research/african-countries-are-racing-to-create-ai-strategies-but-are-they-putting-the-cart-before-the-horse). The more precise framing may be this: AI is one horse among many that will need to be harnessed over the coming decades, the question is who builds the road.

For government and policy leaders, the practical agenda follows from the framework:

**Audit by layer, not by technology:** Rather than asking “what is our AI readiness?”, ask: who governs our identity layer? Is our data held in formats we control? Can we change any technology vendor within a reasonable timeframe? Is our cryptographic infrastructure designed to be upgraded as standards evolve?

**Fund the permanent, create market conditions for the transient:** Public investment belongs in identity, data governance, open payment rails, and human capability. Models, applications, and services are best sourced competitively. Nigeria’s move toward open-source identity infrastructure and Rwanda’s open payments architecture are examples of this logic applied. The test for any new investment: does this increase or decrease our structural dependency?

**Design governance for capabilities, not for products:** Frameworks written specifically for current AI tools will need to be rewritten when those tools change. Governance anchored in principles (accountability for automated decisions, rights over personal data, transparency in public-sector use) will apply to AI, to agentic systems, and to whatever follows.

**Treat public trust as a foundational layer:** The legal and governance challenges that slowed Kenya’s identity programme and required South Africa to revise its AI policy are not obstacles to digital transformation. They are signals that the governance layer needs to be built with the same rigour as the technical one. Public trust, once lost, is the hardest layer to rebuild.

The next technology wave is already forming. Governments will not choose whether it arrives, they will only determine what it lands on.

Build for the technology you can see, and you will rebuild with every cycle. Build for the capacity to absorb change, and the investment compounds across every wave that follows.
