Welcome back to “Digital Products Everyday,” where we discuss everything related to digital product development. This year our main focus has been on DPI, especially payment systems.

Today, it’s still the same. We are going to discuss Rwanda’s instant and interoperable payment system, **eKash**, powered by RSwitch. I wish you could see my face crafting this piece and the smile on it. I am so happy not only to write about eKash, but to know that the day this article is released will be the day eKash is officially launched.

We have discussed instant and interoperable payment systems before on this blog, and their contribution to the ecosystem and society in general, but today, allow me to introduce Rwanda’s Instant Payment System: **eKash**.

I like to say we don’t build such systems just for the sake of building them or having them. There is always a reason behind it. When it comes to payments, systems that are built come as solutions to different problems and as opportunities to different audiences — but we like to summarize all those problems solved and opportunities created as “Financial Inclusion.”

Let’s now dive deep into understanding why eKash was needed in the first place and its journey as of today as we officially launch it. To explain the value proposition of eKash, I will approach it through the lens of Financial Inclusion.

## Financial Inclusion Part

I will explain this in layman’s language, because it helps to understand eKash’s value proposition easily. For you to understand this well, let me explain what I understand by Financial Inclusion.

Why do people have to be financially included, and how do we know they are now included?  
This is a question I have asked myself multiple times.

The answer to the first question was quite simple and quick: almost everyone in this world, in one way or another, needs money to function. Money is now the main exchangeable representation of value. Everything you want, and you don’t have, you need money to obtain it. That is the same in Rwanda.

The knowledge that people need money and have to use that money to achieve their goals is what brings us to Financial Inclusion. So, Financial Inclusion for me has two main parts: **ACCESS** and **EMPOWERMENT**.

## ACCESS

For people to profit from financial services like loans, remittances, paying utilities, and many more services, the foundation is first to **access** those financial services. We are in the digital era, and when it comes to payment systems and other systems, the foundation of “Access” is **Connectivity**. This means it’s through connection that you get to access different services.

**96% of Rwandan adults use at least one formal or informal financial product or service.**  
In Rwanda, access has been achieved to an impressive level thanks to mobile money, commercial banks, microfinance institutions, SACCOs, etc.

But as I have mentioned, Financial Inclusion doesn’t stop at access. It is more about what people can *do* with that access.

### What was possible with the access (Before eKash):

For institutions to enable their customers to transact beyond their own environments, they had to rely on costly bilateral integrations. This was very difficult, especially for MFIs and fintechs who don’t necessarily have enough funds to afford that.

We have more than 20 financial institutions in Rwanda. Now imagine someone telling you that to empower your customers to do business with others using different institutions, you must do more than 20 bilateral integrations. Can you imagine that cost?

You will say, “No, but bank-to-bank, RIPPS was there to help.” I will tell you: Rwanda is home to more than 75 fintechs, which means RIPPS was assisting with just a small percentage.

Do people have access? Yes.  
How about empowerment?  
That’s where the problem was and that is where **eKash** came in.

## EMPOWERMENT

In a fragmented market with many financial services, some working in silos and others relying on costly bilateral integrations, end users were unable to achieve or complete some business transactions because of those silos. That’s where eKash stepped in and changed the game.

eKash achieved this in two steps: **Connect & Empower**.

They started by connecting institutions to one switch.  
So far, we have more than 20 financial institutions connected.

### What problem does this connection solve?

Institutions are no longer working in silos.  
No more closed loops.  
No more spending huge amounts of money on bilateral integrations.

It’s just **one integration**, and you’re connected to the whole ecosystem.  
Isn’t that a commendable thing RSwitch has managed to achieve?

Connection was the first and most important part of empowerment.

After connecting financial institutions, the next step was to empower them and their users to achieve something *on top of* that connection. eKash did that through its use cases.

### P2P (Person-to-Person)

P2P was the first use case, technically launched in May 2022. It started with Airtel and MTN, and later banks joined. Now friends and families can exchange funds on their mobile wallets without necessarily being on the same operator.

That is what we call **empowerment**.

Since May 2022:

- More than **30 million** transactions have happened
- More than **160 billion Rwandan francs** have been exchanged on these rails

If you’re reading this on launch day (5th December 2025) or after, keep in mind that these numbers were achieved **before the official launch** of the product itself. You get how big it is?

The second step to empowerment that eKash made was the payment to Merchants (P2M)

### P2M (Person-to-Merchant)

This year, another great step was taken by technically launching Payments to Merchants (P2M). Before, you couldn’t pay a merchant who didn’t use the same institution as you. Now, as we speak, eKash made it happen.

eKash has said *no* to silos.  
eKash has said *no* to access without empowerment.  
eKash means business when it comes to Financial Inclusion.

This doesn’t only give merchants the freedom to choose who they want to work with without fearing losing customers, but it also fosters fair competition, benefiting both institutions and end customers, especially in quality of service.

Since its technical launch in February this year:

- Close to **35,000** merchant payments have happened ( these are the payments of one person paying a merchant who uses a different financial service/institution)
- More than **2 billion RWF** has been exchanged

Again, you may not have known this was already happening, but these are the numbers **before the official launch**.

eKash still has more use cases coming, including cross-border and many others that will empower society not only to access financial services but to *achieve something meaningful* with them.
