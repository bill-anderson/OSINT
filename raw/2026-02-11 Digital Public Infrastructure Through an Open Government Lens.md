**Author:** Hlengiwe Dube, senior digital-rights and policy expert.

**Abstract.** Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) is rapidly being deployed worldwide, yet its governance is a significant blind spot in open-government reform. While governments focus on digital service delivery, the underlying systems that determine access, inclusion and fairness often operate without transparency, accountability or public participation. The article argues DPI must be governed through open-government principles to prevent systemic harm — exclusion from essential services, algorithmic bias, eroded public trust — and calls for explicit inclusion of DPI governance in frameworks like the Open Government Partnership.

## Introduction

DPI has become a central reference point in global debates on digital transformation. Brazil, India, Singapore, Australia and Thailand are frequently cited as examples of how shared digital systems — identity, payments, data exchange — underpin inclusive digital economies. The questions are especially salient in the African context: as governments digitise core public functions (digital identity, data exchange, automated social protection, electoral technology), DPI increasingly shapes citizens' everyday interactions with the state.

## Why open government matters for DPI

DPI sits at the heart of public administration and is fundamentally a governance project. Choices about system design, data flows, interoperability and access are not neutral — they affect rights, equality and democratic accountability. Well-governed DPI can:

- **Enhance transparency** — India's Aadhaar publishes extensive documentation on its architecture and authentication; open APIs in Kenya's Huduma Namba could enable external auditing, though Huduma Namba has been criticised for lack of transparency on data handling, procurement and legal frameworks.
- **Establish accountability** — Brazil's Cadastro Único social registry incorporates grievance mechanisms for those wrongly excluded; Nigeria's BVN/SIM registration combines financial and telecoms identity but raises governance questions.
- **Enable meaningful participation** — Thailand's National e-ID consultations engaged privacy advocates and civic groups; Uganda's social-protection platforms have engaged civil society, though exclusion via digital-access and verification hurdles persists.
- **Strengthen safeguards** — South Africa's Home Affairs National Identification System has been critiqued for gaps in data protection and error remediation ("ghosts" and incorrect statuses that deny services).
- **Build public trust** — trust is essential for adoption and requires inclusivity, safety, equity and accountability.
