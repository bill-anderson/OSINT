## Why this matters for citizens

Press enter or click to view image in full size

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/1*2McraMHKd-ynsTcIyAHIMw.jpeg)

As of the end of 2025, 44 countries across the continent have enacted data protection laws, covering 80% of African Union member states. (Source: CfA using Canva)

Africa reached a tipping point on data protection. As of the end of 2025, [44 countries across the continent have enacted data protection laws](https://digitalpolicyalert.org/blog/data-protection-in-africa-roundup), covering 80% of African Union member states. Thirty eight of those countries now have fully operational data protection authorities, up from 34 just a year earlier.

This shift reflects a combination of maturing regulatory institutions, growing digital economies, and increasing pressure to govern global technology platforms operating across African markets.

New laws came into force in Djibouti, The Gambia, and Botswana, while Algeria, Ghana, Kenya, Nigeria, and Senegal moved to close gaps in their existing legislation. Liberia, Mozambique, Namibia, and Sierra Leone are processing draft bills, and South Sudan has signalled plans to introduce one. At the current pace, Africa is expected to surpass 50 data protection laws by the end of 2026.

A decade ago, fewer than 20 African countries had any data protection legislation. The growth since then has been steady, but for much of that period, having a law meant little in practice.

Despite this progress, gaps remain. Eleven African Union member states still have no data protection law at all, and fifteen more have enacted laws without establishing functioning regulatory bodies to enforce them.

The real shift in 2025 was about enforcement. Data protection began to move beyond formal compliance, with regulators and courts holding local actors and global technology companies accountable for how personal data is handled.

In July, Uganda’s Personal Data Protection Office secured the country’s first criminal conviction under its Data Protection and Privacy Act. A [lending app director](https://www.monitor.co.ug/uganda/news/national/digital-money-lender-remanded-over-shs60-000-loan-5017638) was convicted for processing personal data without registering with the regulator and for misusing a borrower’s personal information in a threatening WhatsApp video. Weeks later, Uganda’s regulator found Google in breach of the same law for operating in the country without registration, [ordering the tech giant to comply within 30 days](https://ursb.go.ug/2025/08/12/ugandas-data-protection-office-finds-google-in-breach-of-privacy/).

In South Africa, a [court sentenced](https://www.protectionweb.co.za/cyber-security/cybercrime-conviction-former-it-employee-sentenced-to-eight-years-for-fraud-and-extortion-plot/) a former employee to eight years’ imprisonment for installing software that enabled a ransomware attack. In Kenya, [the High Court ordered Worldcoin](https://www.businessdailyafrica.com/bd/corporate/technology/worldcoin-deletes-kenyans-biometric-data-after-court-order-5333600) to permanently delete all biometric data it had collected from Kenyan citizens, ruling that the company’s iris-scanning operations violated the constitution and the Data Protection Act. In Nigeria, the Competition and Consumer Protection Tribunal [upheld a $220 million fine against Meta](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/boards-policy-regulation/nigerian-tribunal-upholds-220-million-fine-against-meta-violating-consumer-data-2025-04-25/) for discriminatory data practices on Facebook and WhatsApp.

For a continent where data protection laws were, until recently, absent or weakly enforced, 2025 marked the point at which those laws began to bite. But, as in many other areas, their reach depends entirely on who is enforcing them, and what capacity they have.

For many regulators on the continent, the gap between what the law says and what they can realistically enforce remains significant. Even countries with the most active enforcement records have publicly [flagged concerns](https://www.techhiveadvisory.africa/insights/roundup-on-data-protection-in-africa-2024-projections-for-2025) about insufficient and unpredictable funding. A regulator dependent on donor grants cannot sustain enforcement campaigns, and one whose budget is controlled by the ministry it is meant to scrutinise cannot easily pursue politically sensitive cases.

This dynamic shapes which cases are pursued, and which are not. High-profile fines against global tech companies is often far easier to politically defend than enforcement against state owned or politically connected institutions.

Capacity constraints extend beyond funding. Limited expertise within regulatory bodies, and among organisations they oversee, continues to slow compliance. The burden also falls unevenly. Requirements such as registration, audits, and appointing data protection officers are manageable for large organisations but can strain for small businesses with limited resources.

A comparison with other regions highlights the scale of the challenge. Europe’s General Data Protection Regulation(GDPR), in force since 2018, had by 2025 [generated over 2,200 fines totalling approximately €5.65 billion](https://www.enforcementtracker.com/?insights=), with €2.3 billion issued in 2025 alone. Africa has no equivalent framework, as the continent lacks a binding cross-border framework, consistent extraterritorial reach, or a single coordinating authority.

Most laws do not impose strict breach notification deadlines or mandate records of processing activities. Rights such as data portability and protections against automated decision-making remain limited or undefined in most jurisdictions. This does not mean that Africa’s approach is flawed, but it underscores the unevenness of implementation and importance of sustained institutional support.

Across the 44 countries with data protection legislation, citizens generally hold a common set of rights, though awareness and enforcement vary. These include the right to access personal data, correct or delete it, withdraw consent, object to marketing, and be notified of breaches.

In 2025, courts and regulators across the continent began actively enforcing them. South Africa now requires organisations to respond to [erasure and rectification requests within 30 days](https://www.techhiveadvisory.africa/insights/bimonthly-update-on-privacy-in-africa-march---april-2025). Kenya’s Worldcoin ruling ordered a [blanket deletion of all biometric data](https://digitalpolicyalert.org/blog/data-protection-in-africa-roundup) the company had collected from Kenyan citizens. [Opt-out-by-default mechanisms](https://cms.law/en/int/expert-guides/cms-expert-guide-to-data-protection-and-cyber-security-laws/south-africa) no longer count as valid consent in several jurisdictions. And in 2025, a Nigerian High Court awarded [approximately $13,000](https://www.techhiveadvisory.africa/insights/bimonthly-update-on-privacy-in-africa-september-october-2025) to a person whose privacy was violated by a media house, establishing that individuals, not just regulators, can hold organisations to account.

Compliance is no longer limited to multinational corporations. Domestic fintechs, lenders, hospitals, and media houses all faced enforcement action. Any organisation processing personal data, local or foreign, must now register with the relevant authority, appoint a data protection officer if processing at scale, and treat consent as something that must be actively obtained rather than implied.

For companies operating across multiple African markets, the regulatory landscape remains fragmented. Nigeria, Kenya, South Africa, and Egypt share similar principles but differ on registration thresholds, audit requirements, and enforcement timelines. There is no single African requirement of the GDPR.

Data protection is also becoming the foundation for broader digital governance. Regulators are now turning their attention to artificial intelligence.

Nigeria is poised to become one of the first African countries to pass formal [AI legislation](https://ncair.nitda.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/National-AI-Strategy_01082024-copy.pdf). The proposed National Digital Economy and E-Governance Bill would extend regulatory oversight to algorithms, data systems, and digital platforms, with higher-risk applications subject to stricter scrutiny.

Morocco has embedded AI governance into its broader [Digital X.0 framework](https://cadeproject.org/updates/morocco-introduces-digital-x-0-law-to-drive-ai-governance-and-digital-sovereignty/), integrating data governance, digital identity, and AI systems across public and private sectors. Rwanda has been building institutional capacity since adopting its [National AI Policy](https://www.unesco.org/ethics-ai/en/rwanda) in 2023.

At the continental level, the African Union’s [Continental AI Strategy](https://au.int/sites/default/files/documents/44004-doc-EN-_Continental_AI_Strategy_July_2024.pdf), adopted in 2024, provides a roadmap for harmonised regulation, ethical standards, and capacity building. While non-binding, it is already shaping national policy approaches.

## Why this matters for citizens

These developments shape how the personal data of more than 1.4 billion Africans is collected, stored, and used. The shift from paper laws to real enforcement is also redistributing power.

When regulators hold global technology companies accountable to local laws, it signals that African markets are no longer lightly regulated environments. When courts order the deletion of unlawfully collected data, they reinforce the principle that innovation must operate within the law and respect fundamental rights.

Yet awareness of digital rights remains low. As enforcement expands, so does the need for digital literacy and public scrutiny. Journalists, civic technologists, and civil society groups are central to monitoring how personal data is collected and used.

Training initiatives focused on digital investigations, data literacy, and open-source intelligence, like Code for Africa’s [academy.AFRICA](https://academy.africa/) are helping build that capacity. Open data ecosystems also play a critical role, enabling researchers and regulators to trace how information flows through digital systems and identify patterns of misuse. Without accessible, structured information, neither regulators nor citizens can hold powerful institutions to account.

As digital systems become more embedded in everyday life, the ability of institutions and citizens alike to interrogate those systems will become increasingly important to holding governments and companies accountable.
