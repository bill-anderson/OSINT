# Zambia begins enforcing controversial Cyber Crimes Act amid digital economy growth - Africa Business Insight

Zambia has begun enforcing its **Cyber Crimes Act No. 4 of 2025**, warning that anyone who records, publishes or distributes private communications without authorization could face criminal prosecution under the country's strengthened cybercrime laws.

In a statement issued on Saturday, the Ministry of Information and Media said the law was now fully operational and urged citizens, public officials and institutions to comply with its provisions.

The ministry said law enforcement agencies would investigate suspected violations where sufficient evidence exists, following reports that private conversations and meetings had been secretly recorded and shared online without the consent of those involved.

The Cyber Crimes Act forms part of a broader legal framework that also includes the **Cyber Security Act** and the **Data Protection Act**, which together regulate online communications, cybersecurity and the handling of personal data.

The legislation places additional compliance obligations on businesses operating in Zambia's expanding digital economy, including financial institutions, telecommunications companies, digital platforms, mobile money providers and e-commerce businesses.

The government says the laws are designed to strengthen cybersecurity, protect personal information and combat growing digital threats such as cyber fraud, identity theft, online scams and financial crime.

The enforcement comes as Zambia continues to expand digital financial services and internet connectivity.

According to the GSMA, mobile internet usage and digital financial services are growing rapidly across the country, while the World Bank has identified digital infrastructure and digital public services as key drivers of private sector development and productivity.

Officials say a stronger legal framework is needed to support confidence in the digital economy as more businesses and consumers adopt online services.

However, the Cyber Crimes Act has drawn criticism since it was enacted in 2025.

Opposition political parties, media organisations and digital rights advocates argue that some provisions could expand state surveillance powers and limit freedom of expression, particularly regarding the monitoring and sharing of digital communications.

The government has rejected those concerns, insisting the legislation is intended solely to address the increasing sophistication of cybercriminals and to safeguard citizens, businesses and public institutions from online threats.

Cybersecurity has become an increasingly important policy issue across Africa as governments seek to protect digital infrastructure and financial systems from rising cybercrime.

Interpol's **2025 Africa Cyberthreat Assessment** identified business email compromise, ransomware, online fraud and financial cybercrime among the fastest-growing threats facing governments and businesses across the continent.

As Zambia deepens its digital transformation, businesses operating in the country are expected to strengthen compliance with the new legal framework while balancing privacy protections, cybersecurity requirements and the rights of users in an increasingly connected economy.
