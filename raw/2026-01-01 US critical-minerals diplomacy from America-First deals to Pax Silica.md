President Donald Trump has turned the United States’ critical-minerals policy into a three-pronged, state-capitalist strategy: faster permitting at home, equity-style deals and price floors for strategic producers, and a new wave of bilateral pacts backed by public finance. Washington is now trying to weld these tools into a ‘coalition of capabilities’ – but the model’s success will hinge on implementation.

Since January 2025, US President Donald Trump has adopted a forceful, [state-capitalist approach](https://www.iiss.org/online-analysis/online-analysis/2025/09/americas-quiet-turn-towards-state-capitalism/) – greater government interventions in corporate decision-making: accelerating permit approvals, brokering private capital and deploying public financial institutions as strategic instruments. China’s export controls and licensing restrictions, issued in April 2025, furthered the shift towards state capitalism, as American public investment in critical-mineral supply chains surged.

The evolving US strategy runs on three tracks. Firstly, it introduced White House-driven ‘America First’ deals, focused on deeper state intervention in domestic projects. Secondly, it expanded into bilateral agreements overseas, supported by government-backed finance and public-private partnerships. Thirdly, the Pax Silica initiative emerged, aiming to combine capital, reserves, processing knowledge, and downstream demand across allied jurisdictions into a coalition of capabilities. Now increasingly intertwined, all three tracks are geared towards maintaining secure and reliable supply chains and access to critical minerals, recognised as a priority in the 2025 [National Security Strategy](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/2025-National-Security-Strategy.pdf). The challenge will be execution – and whether Washington can sustain coalition-building worldwide while simultaneously wielding tariff threats and other measures that strain transatlantic and wider allied trust.

### America-First deals

In the first months of Trump’s second term, the administration focused on increasing US domestic production, establishing the regulatory foundation and making the necessary structural changes. In March 2025 the White House issued Executive Order 14241, intended to [‘facilitate domestic mineral production to the maximum possible extent](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/03/immediate-measures-to-increase-american-mineral-production/) ’ by fast-tracking permitting processes, widening the federal government’s financing reach, and relaxing environmental constraints. Using the 1950 Defense Production Act, the order authorised the defence secretary, in consultation with departments and agencies including the departments of Energy and the Interior – along with the National Energy Dominance Council (NEDC) – to deploy loans and other instruments to support domestic mineral production. Concurrently, decision-making [shifted](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/former-newmont-executive-tapped-oversee-mining-us-energy-council-2025-03-20/) towards the White House, with key advisers coordinating directly with the president and the NEDC.

In July 2025, the [One Big Beautiful Bill Act](https://www.whitehouse.gov/obbb/) (OBBBA) put significant money behind the agenda. It appropriated US$7.5 billion to the Pentagon for critical minerals, including US$2bn for stockpiles, US$5bn for supply-chain investments, and US$500 million for loans, guarantees and technical assistance as directed by the Office of Strategic Capital (OSC). Crucially, it also confirmed that the Pentagon may take equity positions in companies through these funds – an explicit departure from the traditional US preference for arm’s-length industrial support.

This shift becomes most visible in deal-making. While former president Joe Biden relied heavily on grants and loans to catalyse investment, the current administration [has moved](https://wpintelligence.washingtonpost.com/topics/2026/01/09/inside-state-departments-pax-silica-initiative/?pwapi_token=eyJ0eXAiOiJKV1QiLCJhbGciOiJIUzI1NiJ9.eyJzdWIiOiJzYXZhZ2VsQHdhc2hwb3N0LmNvbSIsInJlYXNvbiI6ImdpZnQiLCJuYmYiOjE3NjgwMDE1MDUsImlzcyI6InRlc3Qtc2VydmljZSIsImV4cCI6MTc3MjI1NDc5OSwiaWF0IjoxNzY4MDAxNTA1LCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL3dwaW50ZWxsaWdlbmNlLndhc2hpbmd0b25wb3N0LmNvbS90b3BpY3MvMjAyNi8wMS8wOS9pbnNpZGUtc3RhdGUtZGVwYXJ0bWVudHMtcGF4LXNpbGljYS1pbml0aWF0aXZlLyJ9.8OpAJGJ_KjRhARfIB-8KQBEu5keglauPswAZ4u6Z4So) towards a harder set of state-capital tools: buying stakes in private companies, underwriting long-term demand through offtake agreements, and introducing price floors to stabilise investment incentives. The administration has taken equity stakes in, or provided direct loans to, firms across the value chain: exploration and development (Trilogy Metals), mining (MP Materials, Lithium Americas), refining (ReElement Technologies), and downstream components such as magnets (Vulcan Elements). While this is not unprecedented, it marks an escalation in the US, given the scale of defence-linked financing and the willingness to take direct positions.

[The landmark example](https://mpmaterials.com/news/mp-materials-announces-transformational-public-private-partnership-with-the-department-of-defense-to-accelerate-u-s-rare-earth-magnet-independence/) of this shift is the July 2025 deal with MP Materials, a California-based mining company. Backed by the Pentagon, the deal includes a US$400m equity investment from the Pentagon, a further US$150m loan from the OSC, offtake arrangements with major corporates such as General Motors and Apple, guaranteed price floors, and a reported US$1bn private-financing commitment from JP Morgan and Goldman Sachs. The strategic intent is to solve a problem that private capital alone struggles to address: rare-earth projects are exposed to volatile prices and to the risk of market shocks – precisely the conditions that keep commercial lenders cautious. Direct state participation signals confidence in a volatile market and encourages private finance. It also expands governmental influence over corporate decisions on location, capacity and investment, tightening the link between industrial outcomes and national security. Moreover, as lobbying by mining and processing firms to secure government funding [has intensified](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/critical-mineral-firms-boost-washington-lobbying-us-expands-investments-2025-10-16/), transparency around these deals remains limited.

### Bilateral deals

Washington’s quest for critical-mineral dominance is not confined to America-First deals in the Western Hemisphere. The administration has also pursued bilateral arrangements to secure access to resources and strengthen supply chains with key partners, including Australia, the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), Japan, Malaysia, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, Thailand and Ukraine. These span upstream investment, processing cooperation, and long-term offtake structures – reflecting an effort to reduce US dependence on supply chains where China retains disproportionate leverage.

Within these bilateral arrangements, public-private partnerships proliferate. The deals are marked by state intervention – through sovereign wealth funds, government-backed equity and public-finance guarantees – while directing the allocation of private capital towards the selected strategic assets. A flagship example is the [Orion Critical Mineral Consortium](https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/us-abu-dhabi-governments-invest-18-billion-with-orion-into-critical-minerals-2025-10-23/), announced in October 2025: the US government and Abu Dhabi’s sovereign wealth fund committed US$1.8bn (reportedly US$600m each), along with Orion Resource Partners, to invest in mining and refining projects globally.

In November 2025, the Saudi Arabian Mining Company (Maaden), the Pentagon and MP Materials [announced](https://investors.mpmaterials.com/investor-news/news-details/2025/MP-Materials-and-the-U-S--Department-of-War-Partner-with-the-Saudi-Arabian-Mining-Company-Maaden-to-Develop-Rare-Earth-Refinery-Joint-Venture/default.aspx) a joint venture to establish a rare-earth processing facility in Saudi Arabia to supply both markets. The structure is [revealing](https://www.fdiintelligence.com/content/4839465b-c07a-4aca-9664-aea645834ceb): Maaden operates the facility and provides more than half the capital; MP Materials contributes technical know-how and experience from Mountain Pass, the US’s only operating rare-earth mine and refinery; and Washington derisks the venture by financing the US portion on a non-recourse basis.

Other bilateral arrangements are more transactional and asymmetrical, linking mineral diplomacy to security commitments, reconstruction funds, or preferential access rights. With Ukraine and the DRC, critical-minerals diplomacy has been linked to security guarantees, reconstruction agendas and future US investment.

Two public-finance institutions underpin the expansion of US overseas minerals activity: the newly reauthorised International Development Finance Corporation (DFC) and repurposed Export-Import Bank (EXIM). The administration [has steered](https://wpintelligence.washingtonpost.com/topics/global-security/2025/10/02/new-direction-development-inside-trumps-approach-international-finance-institutions/) these institutions away from a primarily development-oriented strategy towards investments in critical sectors. Historically, neither had a dedicated minerals mandate; both are now being retooled as instruments of economic security.

Created in 2019, DFC has become the key financial vehicle for several bilateral arrangements: as part of the US–Ukraine investment fund, DFC supported a US$150m joint reconstruction and minerals development mechanism while mobilising Ukrainian co-investment.

EXIM [finances](https://grow.exim.gov/blog/what-is-exim-supply-chain-resiliency-initiative) international projects where long-term offtake contracts with US companies ensure the output flows into US-linked supply chains. Through its Supply Chain Resiliency Initiative (launched in January 2025), EXIM can tie the size of its financing to the proportion of offtake committed to US buyers, in effect using public credit to ‘purchase’ supply security. In practice, this means EXIM supports US exports into foreign projects while increasingly using its import authority to secure raw materials produced abroad for US processing and manufacturing.

Both institutions are [designed](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GdanG51lU4Q&t=1760s) to provide guarantees where private markets struggle with political and operational risks and market volatility. They tend to favour projects that are relatively advanced, have infrastructure and can come online soon, reflecting the administration’s preference for near-term ‘wins’ and visible capacity growth.

### Pax Silica

In December 2025 the US launched [Pax Silica](https://www.state.gov/pax-silica), an initiative aimed at building secure and resilient supply chains for the technologies foundational to artificial-intelligence (AI) – especially silicon and critical minerals. Pax Silica reflects a recognition that domestic build-out alone cannot solve the security problem, while bilateral deals can be fragmented and slow to scale.

Initial membership [includes](https://www.state.gov/releases/office-of-the-spokesperson/2025/12/pax-silica-initiative) Australia, Israel, Japan, the Netherlands, Singapore, South Korea, and the United Kingdom, with Qatar and the United Arab Emirates reportedly joining subsequently. Canada, the European Union, the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, and Taiwan participate as guest contributors. India has been invited to join as a full member. Led by Under Secretary of State for Economic Affairs Jacob Helberg, Pax Silica aims to secure private-sector and allied collaboration on important infrastructure projects and joint ventures. Major companies like ASML, Rio Tinto and Samsung have been integrated into the ecosystem, reflecting how mineral diplomacy now hinges on combining public guarantees with private execution.

What makes Pax Silica different is its explicit ‘coalition of capabilities’ logic: countering China’s dominance requires not simply a club of like-minded countries, but a coordinated ecosystem spanning energy, processing, logistics and advanced manufacturing – all of which turn geology into secure supply chains. State Department officials have signalled a priority for partnerships that combine complementary strengths across jurisdictions – for example, pairing Australian or Canadian mineral assets with Japanese or South Korean processing expertise, Middle Eastern energy and capital, European equipment suppliers, Israeli venture capital, and US downstream-manufacturing demand.

This systems-level approach is a genuine advance on earlier initiatives. It effectively replaces the Biden-era Minerals Security Partnership, while overlapping with – and competing against – broader G7 efforts to develop standards and investment pipelines.

Yet Pax Silica also exposes the core tension in American strategy: partnership versus leverage. Coalition frameworks require predictability and trust, but many participants face renewed tariff threats and other unilateral US measures. Conflicting tools risk undermining the reindustrialisation agenda that Pax Silica presupposes – especially if partner countries face trade penalties while being asked to invest in long-term industrial projects. When economic relationships are increasingly treated as instruments of statecraft, how can allies and partners ensure that ‘America First’ imperatives do not eclipse the more collaborative mode that Pax Silica seeks? In that sense, Pax Silica may be the most sophisticated expression of US critical-minerals diplomacy, but the hardest to sustain.

For allied governments, Washington’s renewed emphasis on international cooperation is broadly welcome – but it comes with strings attached. Greater state involvement, opaque deal structures and tacit political commitments complicate partners’ assessments of costs, risks and long-term reliability. Rather than being a genuinely coequal framework, Pax Silica builds on a hierarchical model where Washington defines the rules and calibrates access. Some actors are brought in as ‘capability nodes’, while others – notably the EU and Taiwan – are treated more as markets rather than full strategic co-architects.

For firms, domestic and international, especially those accustomed to government-backed capital, the current moment may look like a windfall. Yet US government support will increasingly be conditional: access to US finance, diplomatic backing, offtake guarantees or equity participation will hinge on strategic expectations – onshoring capacity in the US and aligning investment decisions with American national-security priorities.
