## Introduction

At the beginning of my career, I remember being involved in an Initial Public Offering for a company in Kenya. Whilst the public sees the glitz and glamour of roadshows and investor presentations, there’s an uglier sweatshop like element of preparing all the retail investor orders. This involved compiling all the orders that were coming in from your regular folks and keying them into the system. It was an arduous process that involved many late nights and take-out food. A key aspect of this period for me was that I came across the acronym KYC for the first time. I remember colleagues asking me “do you have the KYC for this customer?”. It was odd. What on earth was KYC? I remember asking my colleagues and they were like “just the documents that show who a customer is”. In my head, I kept wondering “how do we actually know this customer” and more so, “what are we supposed to know about this customer?”. I didn’t know any of the customers whose forms I was processing and was that a bad thing? Should we know about his family, his relationships, whether he supports Liverpool or Manchester United, how he likes his steak? The idea of KYC therefore perplexed my then amateur mind.

One of our supervisors was kind enough to explain it to me in a way that made sense. She explained and I’m paraphrasing that

> Ultimately as a company we’re taking on a lot of responsibility on behalf of these clients. We’re enabling them to invest significant sums in a company and we have fiduciary responsibility to our regulators and other stakeholders. Our responsibility is to ensure that we’re not allowing criminals or bad actors to participate in this process and moreover, we are accountable for all the money we allow into this IPO. To that extent, we need to know who the other counterparty is.

Finally, someone explained the rationale behind KYC to me and what it actually means. It finally hit me that I didn’t need to know John Doe at a personal level, of course some relationship managers could, but as an institution what mattered was that we have sufficient information to validate that John Doe was an actual person, that he actually lived somewhere and that we knew enough about his income and capacity to invest. KYC was therefore an institutional imperative to collect as much information about a client as necessary, be able to validate the accuracy of this information and ultimately, to be accountable for any actions taken on the back of this information.

## Persona in Ancient Rome

This realization, that an institution needs to know a customer not personally but *formally*, is not a modern quirk of finance.It's an idea with roots stretching back two millennia to the very foundation of Western law: the Roman concept of persona. The word itself originally referred to the masks worn by actors in a Roman theatre. The mask wasn't the actor, but it was the character they presented to the public, the recognizable entity with which the audience and other actors engaged. Roman law brilliantly co-opted this idea. A citizen's persona was their legal 'mask'; the recognized identity that allowed them to act in the public square, the formal 'veil' that separated "Samora the citizen," who could enter into a contract or own property, from "Samora the man" with all his personal complexities. Possessing this persona was the gateway to all civic rights and economic privileges. It was what allowed you to participate in the 'IPO' of Roman society. Conversely, those without it, most notably slaves, were not legal persons. They were property.

They had no mask to wear in the forum and thus, no recognized identity with which the system could transact. Seen through this historical lens, the supervisor's explanation becomes profound. The stack of KYC documents I was processing wasn't just administrative paperwork, it was the modern, bureaucratic equivalent of validating each investor's persona. It was a ritual, thousands of years old, to confirm that a recognized legal entity stood behind the application, ready to bear the rights and responsibilities of their investment. The question "Do you have the KYC?" was simply a modern echo of a Roman magistrate asking, "Who is the persona before this court?" KYC, I realised, was an ancient institutional imperative.

Once I understood KYC and the implications of KYC, it became clear to me that there was a fundamental gap that limited the ability of regular citizens to participate in economic activity. Without proper address systems, comprehensive identity frameworks and similar infrastructure, a huge section of society couldn’t take part in IPOs and by extension, most financial services.

## Digital Public Infrastructure

Now in 2025, this fundamental gap is much more complex particularly as we are firmly in the digital era. The idea of enabling a Persona as per the Romans is much more complex. With digital financial services and digital lives, our personas no longer exist on manual records or forms, they should be digital and transferrable through APIs. This brings to the fore the idea of Digital Public Infrastructure and the need for governments to lay down the rails that enable us to fully benefit from our Personas in a digital world.

It’s one of the least talked about yet in my view, extremely consequential policy areas that Africa needs to contend with. Its implications for Fintech are significant. In this article, we define the idea Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI), particularly as a public good. We discuss why it’s so important particularly for Fintech and how Fintech markets will evolve in the continent, we give the State of Play for 6 key markets - Nigeria, Egypt, Ghana, South Africa, Kenya and Rwanda and we end by highlighting some core recommendations for the continent.

## DPI & Public Goods

The concept of digital citizenship has evolved beyond simple identity verification to a more holistic framework known as Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI). DPI comprises an interconnected trio of essential primitives for modern digital life. The first is **Digital Identity**, the state-sanctioned, verifiable answer to "Who are you?" online. Building on this is **Digital Payments**, the circulatory system for the digital economy that enables instant, secure, and low-cost transactions. The final, transformative primitive is the **Data Exchange** layer, the connective tissue allowing citizens to securely use their own information; such as transaction history or educational credentials to access opportunities like credit.

How governments should treat this trio is a key policy question. Some societies, like the US, have largely allowed the private sector to provide these primitives. In contrast, India has centralized them as a government function under the **India Stack**, which includes the **Aadhar** identity system, the **UPI** payments layer, and data exchange frameworks like **Account Aggregator**.

My core argument is that DPI functions best as publicly governed utility infrastructure that produces large positive externalities, which markets underprovide without policy. In economics, a public good is defined as being non-rivalrous and non-excludable. "Non-rivalrous" means one person's use does not diminish another's, like a streetlight's glow. "Non-excludable" means it is difficult to prevent people from benefiting from the good once it is provided.

These characteristics often lead to market failure. Private companies struggle to profit from public goods because they cannot capture the immense positive externalities, or societal spillover benefits, they create. This is known as the "free-rider problem." A firm cannot easily bill every business that benefits from the increased commerce a new streetlight provides, so it lacks the financial incentive to build one, leading to systemic underinvestment. This is why the state typically provides such goods.

DPI maps onto this framework well. A digital ID system, the rails of a national payments network, and the protocols of a data exchange are all inherently non-rivalrous. When designed as a public utility available to all citizens, they also become non-excludable. The resulting positive externalities are significant. For instance, India’s **TReDS**, a government-supported receivables data exchange platform, spurred an explosion in the nation's supply-chain finance industry by increasing the volume of available loans while simultaneously decreasing average borrowing rates; a clear example of a public good fostering broad economic growth.

## Challenges with DPI

African countries can either proceed with private digital public infrastructure, think Google authentication or India Stack like systems. The former is less ideal due to the basic concept of sovereignty and whether we allow a permanent external tax on our digital identities. I think it’s not a palatable policy by any African country. This leaves India Stack like systems as the only game in town. Nonetheless, this has its own complexities in terms of execution and design. There are some nuances that as a continent, we have to understand if we’re to get DPI right.

1. In the case of a road for instance, there are existing frameworks on how to build roads and moreover, the possible outcomes for a road project fall within a narrow spectrum, it’s either good or bad. Moreover, even if it’s bad, it still fulfils its function. There is very little public pushback particularly in Africa if a road design is not top notch because people are not expecting miracles from their roads;
2. In public defence, most countries are not at war and therefore the ordinary citizen has a great deal of apathy towards defence expenditure and the design of defence procurement systems. Most people don’t care;
3. Therefore with most public goods in Africa, the combination of low complexity e.g. roads and high apathy create the perfect conditions for procurement centred policy. Simply, the provision of public goods is the perfect way to “eat” in the continent and this is how fortunes are made.

With DPI, this is not the case. The following should be considered;

1. This [document](http://documents1.worldbank.org/) from the World Bank shows that a lot needs to go into the design frameworks for DPI. Scheme thinking is more important than the underlying technology. As the document states “ *Policy makers and other champions of inclusive financial services infrastructure tend to approach these issues as technology problems. However, infrastructure is about more than technology. The ability of financial services infrastructure to effectively serve poor people depends heavily on building sustainable and inclusive schemes. A scheme generally is defined as a large-scale plan or arrangement”* Procurement centred thinking around technology is likely to fail.
2. The above is validated by India’s neighbour Pakistan whose ID system NADRA has failed vis-a-vis Aadhar - You can read [here](https://medium.com/@CustosClarus/nadra-a-quagmire-of-digital-identity-misgovernance-c859ecb49e5c).

Unlike roads or defence, DPI is actually highly complex with a wide spectrum of outcomes. Therefore, you can’t get the same level of apathy as you do with traditional public goods. Kenya’s [experience](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2019/8/6/kenyas-huduma-data-commodification-and-government-tyranny) with both the Huduma Namba and Maisha Number show that procurement centred thinking cannot work in as complex a domain as DPI.

The point is simple, successful DPI is a complex and often one-shot project, setting the right institutional incentives and scheme design is critical from day one unlike defence spending where the outcome many not be apparent for years.

## Why it Matters;

To understand why it matters, we’ll look at the success of Brazil and India, introduce the concept of Ecosystem Debt and its corollary, Generative Public Platforms.

## India and Brazil

India and Brazil offer compelling models of state-led Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI). India constructed its comprehensive "India Stack" on the Aadhaar biometric identity system, which underpins services like the Unified Payments Interface (UPI). Brazil's central bank launched PIX, an instant payment system that serves as the cornerstone for its broader Open Finance initiative, forcing competition and data sharing.

The success is evident. In India, the **TReDS** (Trade Receivables Discounting System) platform, a data exchange layer, revolutionized supply chain finance by allowing small businesses to auction unpaid invoices, unlocking critical working capital. In Brazil, **PIX** became the dominant payment method, bringing millions into the formal digital economy. This expanded the overall market, leading to a surprising and significant growth in [credit and debit card volumes as newly](https://insights.flagshipadvisorypartners.com/pix-is-redefining-brazils-payment-mix) banked citizens adopted a wider range of financial products. DPI led to Fintech growth.

## Ecosystem Debt

The alternative to public platforms is private platforms such as M-Pesa. Whilst the success of M-Pesa and its impact on the broader financial ecosystem is undoubtable, some questions can be raised about the value of having a single platform provide both the foundational national payments platform as well as counterintuitively, the identity layer through the SIM card.

This concentration of power can create "Ecosystem Debt": the hidden, long-term economic cost of a closed, for-profit company. This debt manifests as high fees that are regressive i.e. lower income people pay more as a proportion of their income, act as a "walled garden" that blocks competition, or stifle innovation focused on extracting value from users rather than creating it for society. While M-Pesa was revolutionary for financial inclusion, its dominance has led to high fees hurting the informal economy. Innovation is also leashed to the platform's terms, risking that new ideas are co-opted, shifting returns from entrepreneurs to managers. Ultimately, the economy pays this debt through lost opportunities and a chilling effect on fintech investment.

## Generative Public Platforms

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YUmf!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb4edf18f-6c22-41ad-ad08-01a699c8b089_1456x1048.png)

The alternative to Ecosystem Debt is to foster **Generative Public Platforms**. This approach performs a "market inversion." Instead of a private firm owning the infrastructure and capturing value by charging rent, a public platform treats the core identity, payment, and data layers as a low-cost public utility. This commoditizes the basic functions of the digital economy, shifting competition and value creation from the closed infrastructure layer to the open, competitive application layer above it.

The platform becomes "generative," enabling a vast and unpredictable range of new products and services to be built on top of it, much like the internet's open protocols enabled the creation of countless businesses its designers never envisioned. Access must be open, non discriminatory, with published APIs and clear liability rules. That guards against a public monopoly becoming a new bottleneck.

The economic surplus created is enormous. In India, the open APIs of the Unified Payments Interface (UPI) allowed hundreds of fintechs to compete on user experience rather than on controlling payment rails, leading to an explosion of innovation. In Brazil, the central bank's PIX system not only generated massive direct cost savings by displacing more expensive payment methods but also increased overall financial inclusion, bringing millions of new users into the formal digital economy. This proves that by providing the basic rails as a public good, a generative platform creates permissionless innovation, competition, and inclusive growth for the entire society.

## State of Play

Luckily, a number of African countries are starting to see their role in the provision of DPI and the long-term value of DPI. I’m starting to see the wisdom behind Nigeria’s regulatory stance towards Mobile Money and how despite a choppier digital financial services experience, there’s arguably a healthier Fintech market and more decentralised innovation. Nonetheless, as always with Frontier Fintech, what matters is to create repeatable frameworks that cement our claims in facts and logic rather than in simple opinions. The aim is to evaluate where different countries in Africa are from a DPI perspective. We’ll look at what we have traditionally considered the core markets of Nigeria, Egypt, South Africa, Ghana and Kenya. We will also include Rwanda which performs really well when put to the test.

We’ll start by defining the framework. To evaluate the state of Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) in any African nation, you must look beyond simple metrics like internet penetration. A robust assessment requires a multi-dimensional framework that analyses the quality and maturity of the core systems and the environment that enables them. The evaluation is structured around four essential pillars: **Identity** (the foundational system for knowing who a person is), **Payments** (the rails for exchanging value), **Data Exchange** (the infrastructure for consented data sharing), and **Governance & Legal** (the laws and institutions that build trust). By systematically scoring a country against specific criteria within each pillar, a clear picture of its strengths and weaknesses emerges.

The crux of this framework is its scoring system, which moves from a simple "yes/no" to a nuanced 1-to-5 scale of maturity, from "Nascent" to "World-Class." A score is arrived at by matching a country's real-world conditions to descriptive anchors. For example, a country scoring a **1 (Nascent)** would have fragmented, paper-based ID systems, closed-loop payments with high fees, and no data protection laws. A country at a **3 (Developing)** shows progress: it might have a foundational digital ID with some coverage gaps, basic payment interoperability that is slow or not widely used, and a data protection law that exists on paper but isn't strongly enforced. A country achieving a **5 (World-Class)** demonstrates global leadership, with near-universal digital IDs that can be verified remotely, free and instant payment systems like UPI or PIX, a clear legal framework for data sharing complete with user-controlled consent dashboards, and an independent judiciary that actively protects digital rights.

Most importantly, it helps policymakers and investors pinpoint the most critical bottlenecks in the system; whether it's a weak legal framework holding back data sharing, or a lack of payment interoperability creating high costs for consumers. This targeted insight enables the prioritization of investments and policy reforms that will have the greatest impact on building a truly inclusive and innovative digital economy.

## Where Do African Countries lie

### Ghana

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DEk1!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1eb399ad-748a-4380-a58f-61d1a1b3154e_1300x648.png)

**Ghana** excels as a model of a state-led DPI strategy, scoring impressively in the **Identity** and **Payments** pillars. Its strength in Identity is anchored by the **Ghana Card**, a modern, biometric ID with high registration numbers that has been mandated as the sole identifier for all financial transactions, creating a powerful, single source of truth for the economy. This success is matched by an advanced regional achievement in Payments, where Ghana has implemented a **fully interoperable** system connecting different mobile money wallets and bank accounts. This deliberate, public-led approach to building foundational identity and interoperable payment rails demonstrates a clear and effective national strategy.

However, the country's progress is significantly weaker in the more advanced **Data Exchange** pillar and is hampered by cost issues in its otherwise excellent payments system. Ghana lacks a specific Open Banking or data-sharing framework and has no mandated API standards, which severely constrains innovation in data-driven services like modern lending. Furthermore, while its payment system is functionally superb, high transaction costs, exacerbated by policies like the **e-levy**, create a significant barrier for users. To build on its strong foundation, Ghana's priority should be to establish a formal Open Finance framework with clear rules and technical standards. It should also re-evaluate policies that create cost barriers to ensure its world-class infrastructure is affordable and accessible for all citizens.

### South Africa

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Cptn!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4400d338-b1a1-4e2c-9d25-c35946f8dbbd_1600x675.png)

**South Africa** presents a case of a country with an exceptionally strong foundational and "enabling" environment for DPI, scoring impressively in the **Governance & Legal** pillar. Its digital ambitions are underpinned by a robust, GDPR-equivalent data protection law (**[POPIA](https://popia.co.za/)**), an active Information Regulator, and a strong, independent judiciary. Furthermore, the government has published a clear " [Roadmap for Digital Transformation](https://www.stateofthenation.gov.za/assets/downloads/South_Africa_Roadmap_for_the_Digital_Transformation.pdf) " that explicitly embraces a DPI approach, demonstrating high-level strategic intent. This is complemented by a mature national ID number system that already provides very high population coverage, giving the country a solid base to build upon.

However, there is a significant gap between South Africa's excellent policies and the current functional reality of its digital infrastructure, which scores poorly on key criteria. The country's high-coverage ID system remains "largely physical," with the new Digital Identity framework still in development. While its formal payment system is sophisticated, it is plagued by **notoriously high transaction costs**, creating a major barrier to financial inclusion. Critically, the **Data Exchange** pillar is nascent, with no specific Open Banking legislation or mandated API standards to enable innovation. South Africa's clear priority must be to accelerate the implementation of its planned Digital ID and legislate a formal Open Finance regime. Addressing the critical issue of high payment costs is also essential to ensure its strong legal and policy foundations translate into an inclusive and dynamic digital economy for all. Nonetheless, the SARB recently came out in favour of a UPI-like payments framework under its control.

### Rwanda

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OygQ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1aa1f437-7c51-421f-99de-27265a8176b4_966x586.png)

🇷🇼 **Rwanda** is a standout example of what can be achieved through a state-driven, top-down digital transformation, earning a perfect score for its **Clarity of Digital Policy**. This long-term, consistent vision has translated into significant strengths in the **Identity** pillar, with a high-coverage national ID that is well-integrated into public life through platforms like **Irembo**, the country's official e-governance portal. The widespread availability of real-time mobile money also provides a solid foundation for its payments ecosystem. This approach has allowed Rwanda to build a highly functional and organized digital state infrastructure with impressive efficiency.

However, Rwanda's centralized model reveals weaknesses in the areas crucial for fostering a dynamic, open economy and ensuring long-term trust. Its **Data Exchange** pillar is nascent, lacking a formal Open Banking framework or mandated open API standards, which limits private-sector innovation beyond the state-partnered Irembo platform. Furthermore, its payment system still faces challenges with interoperability and cost. To evolve, Rwanda must complement its strong state-led vision by strengthening the rule of law to build trust, and by developing a formal Open Finance framework to foster a more competitive and innovative open ecosystem.

### Nigeria

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Vv0e!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0d562782-f74d-42da-9393-b4895d078a88_1600x1143.png)

🇳🇬 **Nigeria** presents a paradox of a highly dynamic digital economy thriving atop a fragmented institutional foundation. The country's primary strength lies in its **Payments** pillar, where the **NIBSS Instant Payments (NIP)** system provides a robust, interoperable, and relatively low-cost backbone for real-time transactions. This solid payment infrastructure has fueled the rise of one of Africa's most vibrant fintech ecosystems, which has innovatively used existing identity systems like the **Bank Verification Number (BVN)** to deliver financial services at scale. This combination of a world-class payment switch and entrepreneurial energy demonstrates Nigeria's immense market potential.

However, this dynamism is severely undermined by a critical failure in governance, particularly in the **Identity** pillar. Nigeria suffers from a "fragmented governance" model, with two parallel ID systems; the **NIN** and **BVN,** managed by different agencies with overlapping mandates, leading to policy clashes and user confusion. More alarmingly, this has created governance and security concerns, including allegations of [large-scale data fraud](https://technext24.com/2025/07/28/nimc-cautions-youths-selling-nins-fraud/#google_vignette). This foundational weakness, combined with a nascent Open Banking framework and persistent challenges in inter-agency coordination, holds back the country's full potential. Nigeria's most urgent priority must be to resolve the insecure and fragmented governance of its identity systems to build trust. Accelerating the full implementation of its Open Banking framework is also crucial to empowering its innovative fintech sector.

### Egypt

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BhXA!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F72fb827d-5a99-45a2-bdfe-c1cbfafb1abb_1600x1067.png)

Egypt demonstrates a powerful, state-led commitment to digital transformation, driven by high-level political will. This is reflected in its strong score for Clarity of Digital Policy, with a National Payments Council chaired by the President signaling a clear national agenda. This top-down approach is building upon a solid foundation, including a high-coverage national ID system that the government is actively working to digitize through strategic partnerships. Although, it’s questionable whether working with payments companies strengthens the independence of an ID system. Proactive initiatives from the Central Bank of Egypt and the launch of the " [Meeza](https://meeza-eg.com/) " national payment network further underscore the country's deliberate and centralized strategy to build a modern digital economy.

However, Egypt's strong political vision is yet to be matched by mature technical and legal infrastructure, revealing significant weaknesses. The country scores poorly on Rule of Law, with concerns about institutional independence posing a long-term risk to trust in its state-run digital systems. The Data Exchange pillar is extremely nascent, with "no specific Open Banking framework" and "no mandated open API standards," which severely limits private-sector innovation. Furthermore, its payment and identity systems are still developing, and the enforcement of its new data protection law is not yet fully operational. To progress, Egypt must focus on strengthening the rule of law to build citizen trust, and move to establish a formal Open Finance framework to unlock the broader innovation potential beyond its current state-directed projects.

### Kenya

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OCwe!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc7af53cd-785e-4795-8e19-0d5c993f5a7d_1600x900.png)

Kenya presents a stark paradox where a world-class private sector innovation in payments coexists with a near-total failure of public-sector attempts at foundational DPI. In short, Kenya Kenya’s Fintech ecosystem is centred on M-Pesa. The country's digital economy is functionally dominated by M-Pesa, which provides ubiquitous real-time payment capabilities. However, the government's efforts to create a digital ID, the controversial and legally-challenged Huduma/Maisha Namba project, have been stalled due to a lack of trust, transparency, and a sound legal framework. Ironically, Kenya's highest-scoring institutional asset is its strong Rule of Law, with an independent judiciary that has repeatedly acted as a crucial check on the government's flawed identity initiatives.

This dynamic has resulted in a classic and severe case of Ecosystem Debt. The dominance of the closed-loop M-Pesa ecosystem leads to poor scores on payment interoperability and high transaction costs that act as a "regressive tax on the poor." Furthermore, the country's Data Exchange pillar is virtually non-existent, with no mandated Open Banking framework or common API standards, stifling broader innovation. To move forward, Kenya must first rebuild public trust by designing a digital ID through a transparent and legally sound process. The critical next step is for the state to actively offer an alternative to the current private "walled garden" by establishing a public-led Open Finance framework that mandates full interoperability and open standards, fostering a more competitive and inclusive digital economy.

## Conclusion and Recommendations

To realize the potential of Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI), a coordinated, strategic approach is required from all key actors. For **public sector stakeholders**, the playbook involves establishing a clear, whole-of-government DPI vision, strategically sequencing implementation with identity as the foundational layer, and adopting a public utility model for core infrastructure. This must be supported by regulation that mandates interoperability and enforces strong data protection to build trust. For **private sector investors**, the strategy shifts to analyzing a country's DPI maturity to identify opportunities, focusing on investing in the innovative application layer built on top of public rails, and pricing in the political and regulatory risks associated with "Ecosystem Debt" from closed, proprietary systems. Finally, for **entrepreneurs and builders**, the path to creating value lies in leveraging existing public rails rather than rebuilding them, focusing intensely on user experience and trust, and identifying gaps in the ecosystem; such as bridging legacy systems with the new digital economy or building cross-border solutions.

**Conclusion**

The development of foundational infrastructure has always been a catalyst for societal transformation, and Digital Public Infrastructure is the 21st-century equivalent of the railways and electricity grids that powered past eras. African nations are at a historic tipping point, facing a strategic choice between two futures: one defined by **Ecosystem Debt**, where closed, proprietary platforms lead to high costs and stifled innovation, and a more promising future built on **Generative Public Platforms**. This second path, where identity, payments, and data exchange are treated as open, low-cost public utilities, has been proven by models like India's Stack and Brazil's PIX to unlock immense economic surplus, private-sector innovation, and financial inclusion.
