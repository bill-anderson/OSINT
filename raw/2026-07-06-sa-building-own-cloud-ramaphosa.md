# South Africa building its own cloud infrastructure, says Ramaphosa

By Myles Illidge, MyBroadband, 6 July 2026

President Cyril Ramaphosa has confirmed that South Africa is investing in its own cloud infrastructure to reduce its reliance on global giants in the space.

In his weekly newsletter, Ramaphosa welcomed the billions invested by players such as Google, Microsoft, and Amazon, but emphasised that South Africa must develop its own capabilities.

He explained that, in the digital age, sovereignty isn't measured by geographic borders but by a nation's ability to secure data, develop its own capabilities, and exercise meaningful control over its technology.

At the same time, he welcomed investments from international giants like Amazon Web Services, Microsoft, and Google.

The President said these companies injected well over R35.8 billion into the South African cloud and data centre space.

Ramaphosa also highlighted the risks of allowing sensitive public and private data to be held by private firms from outside of South Africa.

"That is why government is investing in its own cloud infrastructure through institutions like the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research," Ramaphosa wrote.

He added that South Africa's regulatory and policy environment must align with innovation and safety, and take lessons from other nations.

"We must learn from other countries where vast amounts of sensitive public and private data have been held by private firms and outside national jurisdictions," Ramaphosa said.

South Africa first announced plans to develop its own digital and cloud systems in 2022, when former communications minister Khumbudzo Ntshavheni announced the State Digital Infrastructure Company.

Her announcement followed the Cabinet's approval of a spectrum policy amendment on 10 March 2022, which removed the requirement to license a national wholesale open-access network (WOAN).

The amendment gave the impression that South Africa was considering shelving its plans for a WOAN, which was a welcome development, given that it had not been proven and had failed in Mexico.

However, a subsequent announcement from Ntshavheni revealed that the WOAN plan was being replaced by the State Digital Infrastructure Company (SDIC).

## One of the worst ideas in South African telecoms history is back

The South African government planned to merge the existing networks of Sentech and Broadband Infraco to create the SDIC.

In her announcement, she revealed the government's plans to give radio frequency spectrum to the SDIC.

"We accept the responsibility that we cannot leave our fate and the fate of the country in the hands of the telecommunications operators that are commercially driven," Ntshavheni stated.

"We hold as a department and government a State Digital Infrastructure Company that has a responsibility to make sure that all South Africans have access to technology."

The SDIC would be tasked with providing backhaul for state connectivity while expanding its network into rural, underserved, and remote areas.

It would also be required to use its digital infrastructure to support transformation objectives, reduce barriers to entry into the industry, and enable SMME participation in the sector.

However, the plans fizzled out for various reasons, including a lack of a convincing business case, Broadband Infraco's poor financial situation, governance issues, and shifting priorities.

In May 2026, communications minister Solly Malatsi renewed plans for a WOAN in South Africa, announcing that his department was committed to finalising a policy direction for the plan.

Presenting his Budget Vote speech, the minister said the policy direction aimed to create greater competition and provide more affordable data for all South Africans.

"My mission in this job is to ensure that every person in South Africa has access to affordable and meaningful connectivity that they can use to improve their lives," Malatsi said.

However, while this sounds ideal on paper, it is very unlikely that it will work. The government has time and again demonstrated that it cannot manage broadband infrastructure projects.

Even if it were to run a WOAN as a public-private partnership, it would likely fail. The investment required to compete with Vodacom and MTN's networks is astronomical.

Cell C no longer operates its own radio access network (RAN) for precisely this reason — it is simply impossible to match the larger players' level of investment and network quality.
