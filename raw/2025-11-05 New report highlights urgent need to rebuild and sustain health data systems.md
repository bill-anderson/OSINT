A report from Open Data Watch calls for long-term investment in sustainable, country-led health information systems—an approach central to the DHIS2 and HISP model.

A [new report](https://opendatawatch.com/publications/rebuilding-global-health-data-scale-risks-and-paths-to-recovery/) by Open Data Watch, *Rebuilding Global Health Data: Scale, Risks, and Paths to Recovery*, warns that local and global health data systems are at risk from sudden funding shocks and fragmented support. The report calls for renewed investment in sustainable, country-owned systems—an approach strongly aligned with the DHIS2 and HISP network model.

The report notes that health management information systems ([HMIS](https://dhis2.org/health/hmis/)), often built on DHIS2, form the backbone of routine data collection in most low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). More than [75 LMICs rely on DHIS2](https://dhis2.org/in-action/) as their national HMIS, supporting health service delivery for over 40% of the world’s population. Yet these systems face growing risks when donor support is reduced or short-term consultancies replace sustainable local capacity within Ministries.

To address this, the report’s authors recommend that governments resource permanent roles and career paths for survey design, field management, data processing, civil registration and vital statistics, HMIS and administration, data protection, analysis, and communication within their recurrent budgets. They also note that domestic partners should include dedicated budget lines for routine health information systems (e.g., DHIS2) operations and maintenance, among other key areas.

Development partners should back multi-year training and mentoring programs, including partnerships between national agencies and universities. When temporary technical gaps arise, regional organizations—in coordination with WHO, UNICEF, and the [HISP network](https://dhis2.org/hisp-network/) —can provide short, time-bound assistance to ensure continuity without displacing national staff.

These recommendations echo the message from the recent [DHIS2 investor meeting](https://dhis2.org/2025-investor-meeting/) and the HISP Centre’s call to [invest in routine data systems](https://dhis2.org/invest-in-routine-systems/): that sustainable health data infrastructure depends on long-term country ownership, not project cycles. As highlighted during the meeting, stable investment in the core DHIS2 platform and national capacity helps countries weather funding disruptions, integrate new data sources, and maintain data quality.

The Open Data Watch report identifies three main risks to local and global health data, including over reliance on limited donor funding, weak domestic financing and governance, and insufficient coordination at the country level. It also outlines practical steps toward recovery, emphasizing interoperable, open-source systems, stronger governance, and sustained technical collaboration—principles that are central to the HISP approach.

As governments and partners work to rebuild and safeguard global health data capacity, the HISP model—supporting locally managed, interoperable platforms through a global network of in-country experts—offers a proven foundation for resilience and sustainability.
