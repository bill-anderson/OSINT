The Data Protection Act 5 of 2022, enacted by the Kingdom of Eswatini, governs collecting, processing, storing, and disclosing personal information. Effective from 4 March 2022, the legislation aims to balance privacy, data security, and the need for information in a modern digital economy, and aligns Eswatini with global and regional data protection standards such as those set by the Southern African Development Community (SADC).

Who needs to comply: The Act applies to data controllers (entities/individuals determining the purpose and means of processing, including public and private organisations); data processors (e.g. cloud service providers, marketing agencies, IT vendors); entities operating in Eswatini; cross-border data processors transferring data in or out of Eswatini (including SADC and non-SADC countries); businesses outside Eswatini targeting Eswatini residents (e-commerce, digital services); and organisations appointing data protection officers (DPOs).

Exemptions: personal/household activities without public disclosure; fully de-identified information; processing by state authorities related to national security, defence, or public safety; and purely journalistic, artistic, or literary activities.

Penalties: fines up to E5,000,000 or 2% of an organisation's annual turnover. In severe cases, non-compliance may result in imprisonment for individuals.

Key provisions:
- Scope: applies to controllers/processors operating within Eswatini or using its infrastructure; covers automated and non-automated processing.
- Data subject rights: access and right to challenge accuracy; correction or deletion of outdated/irrelevant/unlawfully processed data; protection against decisions based solely on automated processing.
- Controller obligations: transparency; security measures; notify the Eswatini Communications Commission and affected parties of any data breaches.
- Sensitive data: prohibits processing sensitive personal data (health, race, religion, political beliefs) unless explicitly authorised.
- Trans-border flows: permits transfers within SADC Member States under certain conditions; requires adequate protection for transfers to non-SADC countries.
- Enforcement: the Eswatini Communications Commission monitors compliance, investigates violations, and imposes penalties (fines up to E5,000,000 or 2% of annual turnover); violations can lead to civil remedies or criminal penalties including imprisonment.
- DPOs: organisations must designate officers to ensure compliance and liaise with the Commission.
- General provisions: regulations for unsolicited electronic communications and whistleblowing; support for class actions and transitional provisions.
