A landmark subsea fiber-optic agreement between Equatorial Guinea and Nigeria is set to enhance broadband capacity, cybersecurity and digital communication in West and Central Africa.

(Source: Meta)

Equatorial Guinea and Nigeria have signed a landmark subsea fiber-optic agreement aimed at expanding connectivity, regional cybersecurity and uninterrupted high-speed Internet.

The agreement was signed this month in Malabo, Equatorial Guinea by Backbone Connectivity Network Nigeria, represented by CEO Ibrahim Dikko, and Equatorial Guinea's Minister of Transport, Telecommunications and Artificial Intelligence Systems, Honorato Evita Oma.

While technical specifications, investment figures, and project timelines have yet to be officially disclosed, the fiber-optic agreement is expected to aid in reducing external connections by creating a direct high-capacity link and construction of a commercial data center.

## Internet connectivity in West and Central Africa

According to the Speedtest Global Index, the [global average download speed in January 2026](https://www.speedtest.net/global-index#mobile) was 107.14 Mbit/s on mobile and 118.02 Mbit/s on fixed broadband.

However, for Côte d'Ivoire - which has the fastest download speed in West Africa - the average download speed on fixed broadband was just 59.45 Mbit/s, with less than [40% of the region's population](https://litempowermentsupport.org/internet-access-for-rural-students-in-west-africa/) having regular access to the Internet.

The lack of regular Internet access in West and Central Africa is mainly attributed to limited infrastructure, high cost of access, erratic electricity supply, limited coverage in rural areas and lack of digital literacy.

Equatorial Guinea has one of the slowest Internet speeds in the region with speeds still being measured in Kbit/s and an [online penetration of around 60.4%](https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2026-equatorial-guinea).

Although Nigeria has better Internet speeds and a larger online penetration, it is susceptible to [digital attacks from militant groups](https://jamestown.org/nigerian-militants-increasingly-employ-digital-warfare/) who embark on sporadic cyberattacks while West African subsea cables have been impacted by [alleged sabotage](https://african.business/2024/03/technology-information/severed-internet-cables-highlight-africas-vulnerability) by rebel groups.

Experts believe the subsea fiber-optic agreement is set to address some of those issues.

In an interview with Connecting Africa, Dr. Adebunmi Akinbo, the founder and CEO of DNS Africa Media and Communications, said the fiber-optic agreement is a significant milestone for regional digital diplomacy.

"This agreement is a masterstroke in regional redundancy. While Nigeria is already a heavy hitter with seven major subsea cables such as [Equiano](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/equinix-s-mainone-acquires-equiano-capacity) and [2Africa](https://www.connectingafrica.com/connectivity/meta-backed-2africa-subsea-cable-completed), Equatorial Guinea has historically been more isolated, relying heavily on the Africa Coast to Europe cable," Akinbo said.

"By linking these two nations, we aren't just adding more Internet, we are creating a resilient cross-border mesh," he added.

In addition, Dinfa Dashe, the co-founder of Web3.0 Alliance, said the fiber-optic agreement has set a precedence in West Africa's digital integration journey.

"Subsea fiber infrastructure is the backbone of modern digital economies, and stronger interconnection between Nigeria and Equatorial Guinea enhances redundancy, resilience, and regional data sovereignty," Dashe told Connecting Africa.

"Beyond connectivity, this move signals growing collaboration among African nations to take greater control of their digital futures rather than relying solely on external routing and infrastructure dependencies," added Dashe.

Internet connectivity [in West Africa averages 30%](https://www.telecomreviewafrica.com/articles/features/4670-sub-saharan-africa-s-state-of-connectivity-as-of-2024/), however, the region has been taking strides in reducing Internet coverage gaps, particularly in rural areas, as well as reducing the pricing of a monthly gigabyte of data.

## Prospects of new fiber-optic agreement

According to Yusuf Tuggar, Nigeria's Minister of Foreign Affairs, the fiber-optic agreement will [strengthen digital communications](https://x.com/YusufTuggar/status/2020081395851235447), support regional cybersecurity cooperation, strengthen regional integration, enable African-led digital solutions, and support investor confidence through projects that expand connectivity, resilience, and shared growth.

West Africa has a [high youth unemployment rate](https://www.ecofinagency.com/news-services/2209-48874-with-youth-unemployment-at-25-ecowas-plans-overhauls-to-vocational-education) which over the years has seen a surge in young people emigrating to destinations outside of the sub-region - resulting in a reduction of a competent skilled workforce, weakened public services and slow industrial growth.

Dignitaries from Equatorial Guinea and Nigeria at the signing of the subsea fiber-optic agreement in Malabo, Equatorial Guinea, in February 2026. (Source: Nigerian Minister of Foreign Affairs Yusuf Tuggar)

The fiber-optic agreement is expected to augment some of the region's digital policies which focus on new technologies, AI, digital transformation, communication and social media.

Last month during a three-day gathering in Lagos, Nigeria, Omar Touray, the President of the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) Commission, said [digital transformation must no longer be viewed as an optional add-on](https://www.thisdaylive.com/2026/02/22/ecowas-youth-and-the-future-of-work-in-west-africa/) but must become a catalyst for inclusive development, institutional efficiency and regional solidarity.

According to Dashe, reliable, high-capacity, and secure Internet infrastructure is foundational to the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) in the region through AI, the Internet of Things (IoT), cloud computing, blockchain, fintech, smart agriculture, and e-governance.

"Enhanced subsea connectivity improves latency, data throughput, and reliability, all of which are critical for digital innovation. It also strengthens cybersecurity posture by enabling better traffic management, redundancy, and regional data exchange. In short, infrastructure like this is not merely about faster Internet, it is about enabling digital transformation across industries," Dashe said.

He believes digital infrastructure has a direct correlation with GDP growth, job creation and foreign direct investments, noting that the fiber-optic agreement is not just a telecommunications upgrade but a strategic economic and geopolitical investment in West Africa's digital future.

"Improved connectivity attracts data centers, tech startups, outsourcing hubs, and global digital service providers. It also empowers SMEs to participate in cross-border e-commerce and digital services markets," Dashe said.

"In the long term, such infrastructure deepens regional trade integration and enhances Africa's competitiveness in the global digital economy," he added.

Moreso, Akinbo said the fiber-optic agreement reduces the detour data takes through European servers, which is crucial for high-speed AI processing and fintech transactions noting that a 10% increase in broadband penetration can boost GDP growth by over 1% in the region.

"The agreement includes a commercial data center component. Localizing data storage means African startups can host their 4IR solutions on home soil, significantly lowering the cost of innovation. Beyond the over 20,000 jobs already being projected through related digital initiatives, this infrastructure supports the gig economy," concluded Akinbo.
