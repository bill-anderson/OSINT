Nearly one billion people across Africa remain offline despite living in areas already covered by broadband networks, according to the African Telecommunications Union (ATU), which is calling for stronger regional collaboration to address the continent's persistent digital divide.

The warning highlights a growing recognition that infrastructure expansion alone will not guarantee meaningful internet access. Even as mobile connectivity and digital innovation continue to grow across the continent, and governments increasingly adopt digital technologies to improve public service delivery, millions of Africans are still unable to benefit from the digital economy.

According to the ATU, closing this gap requires addressing affordability, access to suitable devices, digital skills, trust, and relevant local content, not just extending network coverage.

Nadungu Gagare, Permanent Secretary of Nigeria's Federal Ministry of Communications, Innovation and Digital Economy, said:

Almost one billion people in Africa do not use mobile internet despite living within broadband coverage. Infrastructure expansion must therefore be matched by affordability, suitable devices, digital skills, trust and local services.

The ATU is also pushing for African countries to adopt a common position on global digital governance, arguing that regional priorities should be reflected in decisions made at international forums. Stronger cooperation, the union suggests, would help African nations share experience, attract investment, strengthen cybersecurity, expand digital infrastructure, and ensure no country is left behind in the continent's broader digital transformation.

The ATU's Secretary-General, John Omo, emphasized that any institutional reforms guiding the union's future work must be legally sound, procedurally credible, and grounded in an honest assessment of what has—and hasn't—been achieved so far.

South Africa's Department of Communications and Digital Technologies Director-General, Nonkqubela Jordan-Dyani, framed the challenge in even broader terms, noting that climate-related disasters, rapid technological change, and the lingering effects of the COVID-19 pandemic continue to weigh on trade, investment, and economic growth across the continent. She argued that a stronger ATU would help advance the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA), deepen regional integration, and support the African Union's Agenda 2063, while calling on governments, development partners, the private sector, academia, and civil society to collaborate in shaping Africa's digital future.

Together, these perspectives point to a shared conclusion: Africa's digital transformation depends as much on affordability, skills, and inclusive policy as it does on laying down cables and towers.
