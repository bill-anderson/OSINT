*Named practitioner/academic analysis. Carries the canonical PDPA dates (passed 2022-11-27, in force 2023-05-01) and the s.15 registration deadline that expired 2025-04-30 — one week before this piece. Complementary to the Clyde & Co breach-notification piece: non-overlapping sections of the same statute.*

On 27 November 2022, the National Assembly of Tanzania passed the Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA), which came into force on 1 May 2023. The PDPA regulates the processing of personal data in the United Republic of Tanzania and aligns with broader global trends, particularly the EU GDPR. It is supplemented by the Personal Data Protection Regulations.

The PDPA establishes the Personal Data Protection Commission (Commission) as the supervisory authority for enforcement and compliance. A key provision (Sec. 15) requires entities to register as data controllers or processors with the Commission; the final registration deadline expired on 30 April 2025. Given the broad definition of "data processing", arbitration proceedings may inadvertently be subject to compliance requirements.

**Territorial scope.** Under Sec. 3, the Act applies whenever personal data about a data subject is processed within the jurisdictional scope of the PDPA — to all controllers and processors operating within Tanzania and, in certain circumstances, to those outside the country processing data of individuals or entities in Tanzania. It applies to both Tanzania Mainland and Zanzibar; in Zanzibar it applies only to 'Union Matters' under Art. 4(3) of the Constitution (per Sec. 2).

**Cross-border transfers.** Sec. 31 et seq. impose restrictions on cross-border transfers, requiring adequate safeguards or express consent from data subjects. Unlike the GDPR, the PDPA does not adopt an adequacy decision for third countries; instead it requires controllers to assess whether a third country's framework ensures adequate protection. The Minister for Communication, in consultation with the PDPC, can issue regulations specifying where transfers are not permitted. These restrictions may conflict with arbitration rules prioritising procedural efficiency.

**Controllers/processors.** Arbitral institutions handle sensitive personal and commercial data and are to be considered data controllers/processors. Arbitrators, as neutral third persons who determine the means of processing personal data in proceedings, correspond to the definition of a data controller under Sec. 3. Tanzanian arbitral institutions such as the Tanzania Institute of Arbitrators (TIArb) and the Tanzania International Arbitration Centre (TIAC) currently lack explicit data-protection rules or practice guidance.

**Obligations.** Sec. 5 provides an extensive set of safeguarding obligations — lawful, fair and transparent processing with a defined purpose, data minimisation, and accuracy with rectification or erasure without delay. Sec. 27(3) requires the appointment of a data protection officer (DPO) to ensure control and security measures are in place, though its application to temporary arbitration settings remains uncertain. The authors assess these obligations against the ICCA-IBA Task Force Report on Data Protection in International Arbitration and Kluwer's White Paper on Confidentiality and Data Protection in International Arbitration.
