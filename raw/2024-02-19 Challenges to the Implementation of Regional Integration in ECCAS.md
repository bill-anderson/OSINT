Analysis by Tanyi George Orang and Dr. Lidwina Gabsa (Pan-African University, Institute of Governance, Humanities and Social Sciences), IJRISS, DOI 10.47772/IJRISS.2024.801166 (received 16 Jan 2024, published 19 Feb 2024).

The article argues that ECCAS's protocol on free movement of persons and goods (Annex VII, ECCAS Treaty, 1983) is not effectively implemented, due to poor infrastructural and communication links within the region; ethnic divide; political instability; the rise of nationalism; hostile relations; rivalry/enmity between Central African heads of state; and regular roadblocks within and across national borders.

ECCAS was established on 18 October 1983 by members of the Central African Customs and Economic Union (UDEAC) — Cameroon, Central African Republic, Chad, Congo, Equatorial Guinea, Gabon — together with members of the Economic Community of the Great Lakes States (CEPGL: Burundi, Rwanda, Zaire) as well as Angola and São Tomé and Príncipe. It began functioning in 1985 and experienced a period of hibernation from 1992 to 1998 owing to financial difficulties and regional conflict.

The protocol legalises, for all citizens of member states, the right to move freely within the community and to take up and pursue activities as self-employed persons and to set up and manage undertakings under the same conditions as nationals of the host state. Free movement is presented as a lynchpin of market integration, since people carry goods, technologies, services, businesses and knowledge across borders.
