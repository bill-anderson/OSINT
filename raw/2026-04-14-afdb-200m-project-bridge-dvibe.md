AfDB approves $200M towards Nigeria's fiber expansion

The African Development Bank (AfDB) has approved a $200 million loan towards Nigeria's Project Bridge, a fiber-optic program aimed at expanding broadband access in the country.

Matshepo Sehloho,Associate Editor,Connecting Africa

The African Development Bank (AfDB) has approved a US$200 million loan towards Nigeria's Digital Value Chain Infrastructure for Boosting Employment (D-VIBE) project, also known as Project Bridge, a fiber-optic program aimed at expanding broadband access as well as strengthening the country's digital economy.

The loan will help Africa's most populous country deploy 90,000km of open-access fiber nationwide, expand digital skills and drive job creation across the country.

According to the AfDB, Project Bridge is expected to close connectivity gaps and support productivity and job creation in the West African country.

"It will achieve this by extending Nigeria's national fiber backbone from around 30,000km to about 120,000km, connecting all 774 local government areas – including schools, health facilities, agro-industrial zones, rural communities and commercial hubs – to high-speed broadband and establishing cross-border links with neighbors Benin, Cameroon, Niger, and Chad," AfDB said in a statement.

The bank said the loan is part of an $800 million sovereign financing package for the project, alongside $500 million from the World Bank and $100 million from the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD).

It added that the total project financing is estimated at $2 billion, and includes an EU grant of €22 million, a $2.6 million Multilateral Cooperation Center for Development Finance (MCDF) project preparation grant, and at least $1.2 billion investment from the private sector.

"Nigeria has the talent, the market and the ambition; what it has lacked is the backbone infrastructure to connect that potential to opportunity. D-VIBE changes that. From the north to the south, from farms to factories to classrooms, this investment will make high-speed connectivity a reality for every Nigerian community and give young people the tools to build their futures digitally," explained AfDB Nigeria Office Director General Abdul Kamara.

The AfDB's loan to Nigeria comes as many African countries and companies are signing deals with international organizations to further their digital transformation ambitions.

In November 2025, Kenya and Angola signed a memorandum of understanding to collaborate on technology advancement, satellite networks, ICT training, the digital economy, rural connectivity, cybersecurity and digital literacy.

In October 2025, Kenya signed an agreement with Romania to establish a collaborative framework for information sharing, joint incident response, capacity building and the development of advanced cybersecurity solutions.

In the same month, Ethiopian state-owned Ethio Telecom partnered with Chinese Android smartphone manufacturer Tinno to boost smartphone adoption and accelerate access to devices across the country.

In February 2025, the African Union Development Agency-New Partnership for Africa's Development (AUDA-NEPAD), Meta and Deloitte launched Akili AI – an AI-driven platform that empowers micro, small and medium-sized enterprises (MSMEs) across the African continent.

Ethio Telecom initiated strategic discussions with the AfDB in January 2025 to help advance Ethiopia's digital transformation strategy and enhance Africa's broader digital economy.
