Starlink gets greenlight in Uganda, Airtel tests direct-to-cell

Starlink has been granted a provisional license by the Uganda Communications Commission (UCC) while Airtel Uganda has begun testing Starlink's direct-to-cell (D2C) LEO satellite service.

Matshepo Sehloho,Associate Editor,Connecting Africa

3 Min ReadStarlink has been granted a provisional license where it is required to establish a national gateway with a physical point of presence in Uganda.(Source: State House Uganda)

SpaceX's satellite Internet service, Starlink, has been granted a provisional license by the Uganda Communications Commission (UCC) while Airtel Uganda has begun testing Starlink's direct-to-cell (D2C) LEO satellite service.

In a post on X, Ugandan President Yoweri Museveni said Starlink and the UCC signed a memorandum of understanding as well as an operational license agreement.

"This marked an important step towards the commencement of their [Starlink] operations in Uganda," Museveni said.

He added that Uganda's interest was security, revenue assurance, and proper accountability within the telecommunications sector so that it knows who is operating and who the customers are.

"I am pleased that Starlink has agreed to comply with Uganda's laws and regulatory requirements as it prepares to begin service delivery in the country. I wish them good luck," the president added.

Under its license conditions, Starlink is required to establish a national gateway with a physical point of presence in Uganda, ensure that all devices activated in the country are properly registered, and maintain a staffed local office with essential technical, legal and support personnel.

In addition, the UCC has set out a formal operating framework to oversee and support Starlink's rollout, including clear requirements to ensure full compliance with national regulatory standards.

Uganda's Ministry of ICT and National Guidance Permanent Secretary Dr. Aminah Zawedde said the license agreement followed engagements between Uganda and Starlink officials to ensure compliance with the country's regulatory framework.

Ryan Goodnight, Starlink's senior director for market access, said the company intends to support education and health sectors by donating Internet connectivity devices to selected facilities across the East African country.

"We believe this partnership will lower Internet costs and enable more people to join the digital economy. We are ready to comply with Uganda's laws and work closely with the government and UCC to ensure successful implementation," Goodnight explained.

UCC Executive Director Nyombi Thembo and Starlink's Senior Director for Market Access Ryan Goodnight, after the regulator granted Starlink a license. (Source: State House Uganda)

At the back of Starlink getting an operational license in the East African country, Airtel Uganda announced it has begun testing Starlink's D2C LEO satellite service.

In a LinkedIn post, Airtel Uganda's Managing Director and CEO Soumendra Sahu said the telco had entered the testing phase for D2C satellite services through its partnership with Starlink.

"Today, we are pleased to begin our journey of exploring our long-awaited partnership with Starlink. This marks the start of testing a highly advanced technology that enhances smartphone network connectivity through Direct-to-Cell satellite services," Sahu said.

The announcement comes a year after Airtel Africa partnered with SpaceX to deliver high-speed Internet through Starlink across its African markets.

The test also forms part of Airtel Africa's December 2025 agreement with SpaceX to roll out Starlink's direct-to-cell service across all 14 of its markets by the end of 2026.
