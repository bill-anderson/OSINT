## 1.0 Introduction

Every year, hundreds of thousands of African businesses; distributors, manufacturers, trading companies, move billions of dollars to pay their supply chain partners in Asia. They pay for the electronics, the machinery, the solar panels, the construction equipment that their businesses run on. They pay slowly, expensively, and through infrastructure designed for different corridors and different counterparties. The money gets there, eventually. But the friction is the story.

In April 2021, [David Goldman writing in Asia Times](https://asiatimes.com/2021/04/chinas-digital-yuan-displaces-the-dollar/) sketched a future in which that friction disappears entirely; AI systems tracking shipments in real time, triggering payment at each node, making the current infrastructure redundant. From the Venetian merchants who invented the letter of credit to the correspondent banking system that followed, payments have always followed trade. The question is whether we can build payment systems that reflect the current reality of Africa’s deepening trade relationship with Asia.

The energy in global cross-border payments today feels like a market collectively trying to build toward that future. Stablecoin settlement volumes hit $27.6 trillion in 2024, exceeding Visa and Mastercard combined. On-ramp and off-ramp costs have compressed from 3-4% to below 0.5%. Regulators are catching up rather than trailing. The direction of travel is not seriously in dispute.

But the practitioners are more careful than the headlines. Some argue that stablecoins of today are the unstable coins of the future; instruments pegged to a dollar whose stated policy is annual devaluation. Others note that enterprise clients are not opening crypto wallets; the infrastructure to act on the theoretical case does not yet exist in a form compatible with how a CFO runs a business. The emerging consensus is to use stablecoins as backend rails while keeping the customer experience fiat-facing. The technology should be invisible. The payment should just work.

Both observations point to the same conclusion: the future Goldman described requires bridges built for the world as it is, not as it will eventually be. The question is where to build them first.

For Africa, the answer starts with Asia. Liquidity is a pool into which all trade streams merge, and Africa’s most significant and fastest-growing stream runs east. Whoever builds the steady, reliable pipe to Asia will become Africa’s trade financial infrastructure for the next generation. This piece examines what that requires, why it is harder than it looks, and how it might be done.

## 2.0 Stablecoins - Costs, Liquidity and Velocity

## 2.1 The Cost Argument

Stablecoins are, in their simplest form, digital tokens pegged to a fiat currency; typically the US dollar and settled on a blockchain. They combine the price stability of traditional money with the settlement properties of cryptocurrency: instant, borderless, and programmable. For most of their existence, they were associated primarily with crypto trading, a way for investors to park value between positions without converting back to fiat. That association has changed. In 2024, stablecoin transfer volume reached $27.6 trillion, B2B transfers became the largest single use case, and the infrastructure supporting them; exchanges, custodians, on-ramp and off-ramp providers, has matured considerably.

The scepticism that greeted stablecoins in banking circles was not irrational. April Long, CEO and cofounder at Waka has been a corridor banking and then fintech practitioner for all her life. 10+ years in Africa-Asia corridor financial services. She understands and deeply empathises the pain from both ends, and the price people have to pay to trade with each other. For her, the problem was cost. Two years ago, on-ramping in Nairobi and off-ramping in Singapore carried combined costs of 3-4%, enough to make stablecoins more expensive than the correspondent banking workarounds they were supposed to replace. That is a reasonable place for a transactional banker to stop. The correspondent banking system, for all its friction, has known costs and known counterparties. A settlement rail that costs more and introduces new operational risks is not a compelling alternative, regardless of its theoretical elegance. Anyone who spent their career managing FX desks and payment operations reached the same conclusion: stablecoins were a solution looking for a problem that the existing system, imperfect as it was, already solved more cheaply.

What has changed is the cost curve. On-ramp and off-ramp costs in active corridors have compressed from 3-4% to below 1% over two years, driven by the same dynamic that drove down the cost of internet bandwidth or cloud computing; speculative capital building infrastructure that productive capital subsequently inherits. The cost curve is still declining. As it does, the set of transactions for which stablecoins are the cheapest available settlement rail expands. This is the core dynamic April identified: you have to watch it as a curve, not a snapshot. The question is never whether stablecoins are cheaper than SWIFT today. The question is where the crossover point is, and how quickly it arrives.

## 2.2 The Trade Nuance

Having said that, the cost argument, on its own, understates what stablecoins are actually doing in the context of African trade. There is a second problem at work, distinct from settlement cost, and it is the problem that makes this corridor genuinely difficult. In 2024, Africa ran a trade surplus of roughly $26 billion with the European Union and a further $7 billion with the United States; earnings from flowers, tea, coffee, copper, and crude that flow reliably into African accounts every quarter. The problem is what happens to those dollars next. Africa ran a trade deficit of $62 billion with China in the same year, consuming nearly two-thirds of the continent’s total trade gap as manufacturers, distributors, and importers bought the machinery, electronics, and consumer goods that China produces more cheaply than any other supplier. The surpluses earned in Europe and the United States are the same dollars that must eventually reach Chinese suppliers. The job to be done is recycling; moving dollar liquidity earned in one corridor to settle obligations in another.

The correspondent banking system is poorly designed for this recycling function, but the deeper problem runs beneath the plumbing. Africa has no financial hub of its own. Every dollar that moves in or out of the continent passes through financial centres; London, New York, Paris, that were built for other markets, other counterparties, and other risk appetites. When a Kenyan importer needs to pay a Chinese supplier, the payment does not travel a direct route. It travels through institutions that were never designed to understand it, staffed by compliance teams applying heuristics built for European and American trade flows, making judgments about African transactions from a distance and with incomplete context. The dollars exist. They are simply stuck, held in accounts governed by relationships that were never designed with Africa’s trade patterns in mind, interpreted by intermediaries whose primary instinct when they see an unfamiliar corridor is caution.

Asia is further along this road. China has Hong Kong, a sophisticated financial hub that understands and intermediates Chinese commercial flows with institutional fluency. Singapore performs the same function for Southeast Asia. Both cities have developed the regulatory frameworks, the institutional expertise, and the market infrastructure to serve as genuine clearing centres for regional trade. Africa has no equivalent. Its liquidity pools sit offshore in financial centres that are geographically distant, institutionally indifferent, and compliance-configured for a different world. The result is a structural crunch, a shortage of infrastructure that can interpret African flows on their own terms and move them to where the trade requires.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!roO5!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd88c3184-7a1c-40ee-846b-fb73bcda5565_1878x1032.png)

Source: Waka Seed Deck

This is where stablecoins play a role that goes beyond settlement cost. A stablecoin operating on a public blockchain eliminates the geographic constraint on dollar liquidity. Dollar balances held in fragmented offshore accounts across London, New York, and Dubai can settle against each other instantly and without the intermediary hops that the correspondent banking system requires. The physical separation between liquidity pools, the reason a Paris bank asks uncomfortable questions about an African payment bound for Hong Kong, ceases to be a structural barrier. Liquidity that was previously trapped by the architecture of the correspondent banking network becomes accessible across the network simultaneously. The effect is aggregation: the same dollar liquidity that exists in the system today becomes more productive because it can be deployed faster and across a wider set of settlement needs.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bc8W!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F43d175c9-978b-416e-98e1-a116a32e9f4e_1526x808.png)

Source: Waka Seed Deck

There’s also value in this increased velocity. If Dollars can be cleared instantaneously global wise, then what’s holding back the speed of your circulation is the settlement time for your own currency.

Africa does not have a dollar problem. It has a dollar velocity problem. Stablecoins, at their most useful, are not a new currency. They are a mechanism for making the dollars that already exist move at the speed that trade actually requires. It may not be the end state of things, but it’s a very robust intermediary step.

## 3.0 Africa-China Trade

China-Africa trade surged to a record [$348 billion in 2025, a 17.7% annual increase](https://www.ntu.edu.sg/cas/news-events/news/detail/china-africa-trade-hits-record-us-348bn-as-deficit-balloons) that makes the relationship’s trajectory impossible to ignore. When bilateral trade crossed $100 billion for the first time in 2008, it was treated as a milestone. It has since more than tripled, and the pace is accelerating rather than plateauing.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wM5E!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5038cd92-1bb8-48bf-914f-18268d898c32_1634x1330.png)

The composition of that trade matters as much as the volume. Of Africa’s exports to China, the overwhelming majority are raw commodities; crude oil, copper, cobalt, iron ore. China’s exports to the continent consist mainly of higher-value manufactured goods, including machinery, electronics, and green technologies.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KAOb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05d1090a-0677-469e-b6a2-d76c02113fb0_1628x1274.png)

The depth of China’s commercial presence reinforces the trade data. Roughly [10,000 Chinese](https://africacenter.org/spotlight/chinese-state-owned-enterprises-market-capture-africa/) firms operate across Africa, accounting for approximately [12% of its industrial output](https://africacenter.org/spotlight/chinese-state-owned-enterprises-market-capture-africa/). Four of Africa’s top five smartphone brands are now Chinese, with Huawei and Xiaomi recording the largest market share gains.Hikvision supplies a significant share of commercial surveillance infrastructure across major cities. African nations awarded [$30.5 billion worth](https://www.ntu.edu.sg/cas/news-events/news/detail/china-africa-trade-hits-record-us-348bn-as-deficit-balloons) of contracts to Chinese construction firms in the first half of 2025 alone, a five-fold rise from a year earlier.

The US tariff regime has reinforced rather than reversed this trajectory. When Washington imposed comprehensive tariffs averaging 47.5% on Chinese goods, China’s commercial response was redirection. Chinese exports to African nations climbed 25.8% to $225 billion in 2025 as the Global South absorbed the surplus capacity that the US relationship could no longer take.

[David Goldman’s](https://asiatimes.com/2024/07/chinas-long-march-through-the-global-south/) framing is useful here: China is not primarily executing a geopolitical strategy in Africa. It is assimilating commercial relationships at scale, building supply chain dependency that creates durable economic ties regardless of what happens at the diplomatic level. The Belt and Road was the groundwork. The consumer goods, the smartphones, the construction machinery, the solar panels, those are the products.

Against this backdrop, the payments infrastructure serving the Africa-China corridor is a critical bottleneck. The trade is large, growing, structurally entrenched, and accelerating. Any serious payment solution built for African trade has to be built with China and Asia at its centre as the defining commercial relationship the infrastructure must serve. Payments follow trade. And Africa’s trade, increasingly and irreversibly, goes east.

## 4.0 The Unique Nuances of Settling into China

So if stablecoins help aggregate liquidity and increase the velocity of trade, and if the core job to be done is to support trade with China, then how do you actually do it? Before we answer, we need to understand the nuances behind paying into China.

The first challenge is trust. China and Africa operate in fundamentally different institutional environments; different legal systems, different documentation standards, different business cultures. A Chinese bank receiving a payment instruction from Lagos or Nairobi is dealing with a counterparty it has no established relationship with, in a market its compliance teams have limited visibility into. This is not unique to China. But China’s compliance architecture makes the trust gap unusually consequential.

The compliance philosophy on the mainland is outcome-based. In Western banking, institutions demonstrate compliance by following a process. In China, the bank is accountable for the result. If a payment turns out to be fraudulent or unverifiable, the institution that processed it bears liability. China’s major banks are majority state-owned, which means they approach regulatory requirements as institutional participants in the same system, not as external parties with room to negotiate. The practical consequence is hyperconservatism on anything unfamiliar. An African counterparty with no established track record falls squarely into that category.

China’s approach to its currency reflects a fundamental tension: internationalise the renminbi while retaining control over how it flows. The result is a bifurcated system. The offshore renminbi: CNH, trades freely in Hong Kong, Singapore, and London. The onshore renminbi; CNY, is legally distinct, accessible only through approved channels, with a daily trading band set each morning by the People’s Bank of China. Most Africa-China payments today settle offshore in CNH or USD precisely because the mainland compliance requirements are significantly heavier. The payment moves with less friction. But the cost of staying offshore is real: FX conversion spreads, slower working capital cycles, and a more cumbersome documentation process for the Chinese supplier.

Sitting beneath all this is a verification requirement that has no real equivalent in Western payment systems. SAFE; the State Administration of Foreign Exchange and the PBOC together require that every cross-border payment into China be verifiable against the underlying trade. Invoice values must match customs declarations. Payment amounts must correspond to the declared shipment. Counterparty details must align with the original contract. The data that travels with the payment that makes the payment legitimate in the eyes of the system. A payment that arrives without it will clear eventually, but only after delays that compound every other friction in the chain.

The corridor’s problem is therefore not just a rail problem. It is a trust problem, a compliance problem, and a documentation problem simultaneously. Any solution that treats it as only one of the three will find, eventually, that the other two remain.

## 5.0 How to Solve This From First Principles

Understanding the compliance architecture behind China-bound payments clarifies why most existing solutions fall short. Players like XTransfer have done genuinely important work; onboarding hundreds of thousands of Chinese exporters and building compliance infrastructure for high volumes of smaller transactions. That is a real and valuable market. But it is a different market from the one that sits beneath Africa’s $102 billion annual payment obligation to China.

The large enterprise importers; distributors moving container loads of electronics, manufacturers sourcing capital equipment, trading companies running multi-million dollar quarterly invoices, are not being served by the retail-oriented rails built for SME trade. These are precisely the clients for whom the cost of the current system is most visible. They have treasury teams. They understand FX. They are already asking their banks why a $500,000 payment to Shenzhen takes longer and costs more than it should.

Building a solution that genuinely serves this segment requires three capabilities assembled simultaneously.

The first is stablecoin infrastructure used in two ways: as a treasury function that aggregates fragmented FX liquidity across multiple corridors into a single deployable position, and as a settlement rail that increases the velocity of trade. The stablecoin layer is what makes the entire flow work.

The second is a two-sided relationship framework. On the African side, relationship managers onboard enterprise importers and build the documentation package every China-bound payment requires. This means invoice values, customs references, transaction purpose codes, all assembled before the payment moves. On the China side, relationship managers support manufacturers with the compliance package their onshore bank requires to process an incoming settlement. This is the layer no pure routing play can replicate.

The third is a SaaS platform that makes the operation repeatable at volume; payment visibility and document management for Chinese sellers, invoice tracking and FX management for African importers.

What this architecture produces is a payment that does the full job. Local currency in. Stablecoin rail across. Compliance evidence attached. Onshore CNY out. Settlement in hours rather than days.

## 6.0 Waka

This is the thesis behind Waka, the company April Long founded after a decade of managing the China-Africa corridor from inside Standard Chartered Kenya and Gulf African Bank. The insight that animates the business is the one this article has been building toward: that Africa’s payments problem and Africa’s trade problem are the same problem, and that any solution which addresses the payment rail without addressing the compliance architecture, the liquidity fragmentation, and the documentation requirements of the Chinese receiving system will eventually hit the same wall that every offshore workaround hits.

The traction to date reflects that clarity of focus. Waka has processed over US$ 100m in annual flow across eight African markets, generating a respectable ARR on modest working capital. The portfolio is structured deliberately across three tiers: high-volume stability anchors, a core profit layer, and high-spread alpha positions — each calibrated to balance throughput against margin. Over 100 liquidity providers are connected across 20 currencies, with partners backed by Visa Ventures, Coinbase Ventures, and Tether. On the China side, the company has begun serving large Chinese enterprises with continent-wide B2B distribution networks; manufacturers and technology exporters whose African sales operations generate the recurring, high-value payment flows the infrastructure is designed to handle.

The commercial roadmap builds from that foundation in two directions. The first is deepening the enterprise relationship on both sides of the corridor; working with large corporations in Africa and China who need a payments partner that understands the compliance environment on each end, not just the rail in the middle. The second is expanding into the payment company channel: African fintechs and cross-border platforms that are already processing trade-related flows and need a compliant, liquid path into China that they cannot currently build themselves.

Compliance sits at the centre of the model throughout. What Waka is a fully integrated service that runs from local relationship managers and client onboarding, through FX liquidity management and market making, across a stablecoin settlement rail, and all the way to compliant CNY settlement into the Chinese mainland, with the documentation package that makes each payment legible to the SAFE and PBOC frameworks delivered as part of the service, not as an afterthought. That end-to-end stack is what separates an enterprise solution from a routing play.

## 7.0 Conclusion

The structural opportunity is clear. Africa’s trade with Asia is growing faster than the infrastructure built to support it, and the infrastructure that exists was never designed for this corridor in the first place. The correspondent banking system, the offshore dollar clearing relationships, the compliance frameworks inherited from a different era of global trade, all of it was built to serve corridors that ran west, not east. As trade increasingly runs east, the gap between what the infrastructure can do and what the trade requires grows wider every year.

Closing that gap is not a technology problem. It is a market problem that requires dedicated players, institutions with the relationships on both sides of the corridor, the compliance sophistication to navigate two entirely different regulatory environments simultaneously, and the operational depth to do the work that no routing layer or API can do on its own. The window for building that position is open now, while the corridor is growing and the incumbent solutions are still oriented the wrong way. It will not stay open indefinitely.

Whoever builds the dedicated infrastructure for Africa-Asia trade will not just be solving a payments problem. They will be building the financial rails for the fastest-growing commercial relationship on the continent and doing it at the moment when the stakes are highest and the competition is thinnest.
