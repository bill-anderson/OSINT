The number of smartphones connected to mobile networks in Kenya has crossed the 50 million mark for the first time, underlining the country’s accelerating shift toward internet-enabled devices and digital services.

Fresh data from the Communications Authority of Kenya (CA) shows smartphone connections rose to 50.2 million as of March this year, up from 48.7 million recorded last December.

The 1.4 million increase during the quarter, representing a growth rate of three per cent, pushes smartphones further ahead as the dominant mobile device category in the country.

This came at a time when Kenyans continued abandoning basic feature phones, with the number of such devices connected to mobile networks dropping by 1.1 million during the period.

Feature phone connections stood at 28.5 million as of March, down from 29.6 million recorded at the end of December last year, representing a 3.7 percent dip.

“Mobile phone devices connected to mobile networks totaled 78.7 million, translating to a penetration rate of 147.6 per cent at the end of the reference period. Smartphones accounted for the largest share of 63.7 per cent of the total mobile phones,” the CA said.

The figures highlight the changing nature of Kenya’s telecommunications market as consumers increasingly migrate towards devices supporting new-age capabilities such as internet access, digital payments, streaming services and online commerce.

According to the regulator, the shift from feature phones is expected to continue as smartphone ownership becomes more accessible.

“It is noteworthy that the number of smartphones connected to the network increased during the quarter, while the number of feature phones, which primarily support basic voice calls, text messaging, and low-speed data services, declined,” CA said.

“This trend is expected to continue considering the falling device costs, expanded high-speed mobile network infrastructure, and the growing necessity of mobile-based economic and social services in the country.”

Rapid adoption has effectively turned mobile devices into the country’s most important digital infrastructure, particularly in areas where access to computers remains limited.

Smartphones uptake has also been supported by sustained investment in mobile broadband infrastructure.

Safaricom, Airtel Kenya and other operators have spent heavily expanding 4G coverage while laying the groundwork for broader adoption of faster network technologies such as 5G.
