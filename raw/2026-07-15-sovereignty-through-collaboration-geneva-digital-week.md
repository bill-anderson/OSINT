_**Emrys Schoemaker makes the case for more research on impossibility and potential of digital sovereignty.**_

On Monday 6 July, António Guterres opened the first [Global Dialogue on AI Governance](https://www.un.org/global-dialogue-ai-governance/en) at the Palexpo conference centre in Geneva with a line that will be quoted for some time: [for the first time, every country has a seat at the table](https://press.un.org/en/2026/sgsm23211.doc.htm). Over the following five days, in the same building, the [World Summit on the Information Society Forum](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/global-dialogue-ai-governance-geneva-6-7-july) and the ITU's [AI for Good Global Summit](https://aiforgood.itu.int/summit26/) convened alongside it. Three processes, one venue, one week. [More than four thousand registered participants from close to one hundred and seventy member states](https://dig.watch/updates/global-dialogue-ai-governance-2026-closing).

The physical compression was striking. It was also, I think, misleading. Proximity is not integration, and the most interesting thing about Geneva's Digital Week was not what the three processes had in common but the different theories of governance they each embodied — and the gaps that opened up between them.

Two questions ran through the corridors all week, and neither was answered. The first was structural: what, exactly, happens next? The Dialogue reconvenes in New York in May 2027. Between now and then there are no working groups, no mandates, no deliverables — only a co-chairs' summary. The Ada Lovelace Institute had argued before the meeting that [its success would hinge on establishing the structures required in the intervening year](https://www.adalovelaceinstitute.org/blog/the-case-for-a-global-ai-governance-floor/), and had called for dedicated working groups per thematic cluster with mandates, timelines and deliverables, and for Global Majority representation among their chairs. That call went unanswered. The Dialogue succeeded as an event. It remains unresolved as an institution.

The second question was harder, and it is the one I want to take seriously here: in a fragmenting multilateral order, what is the United Nations actually for?

### The sovereignty that cannot be exercised alone

The dominant reading of the week — in the plenaries, in the press, in the pre-summit commentary — was that the Dialogue's achievement is universality. Every state has standing. [Developing countries participate as shapers rather than observers](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/un-global-dialogue-opens-urgent-call-safe-and-inclusive-ai-benefits-all). Set against Bletchley, Seoul and New Delhi, each convened by a host government with a curated invitation list, this is a genuine and non-trivial correction.

But universality is a legitimacy asset, and legitimacy is an input to governance rather than a substitute for it. Co-chair Egriselda López was careful about this in her closing remarks: the Dialogue, [she said, is intended not to replace existing initiatives but to connect them](https://dig.watch/updates/global-dialogue-ai-governance-2026-closing). A convening body, then, and not a governing one. Which returns us to the question of what it connects, and on what terms.

The answer, I think, is emerging in a direction that most of the commentary has missed. Listening to the interventions from smaller and capacity-constrained states, what came through was not a demand for global rules so much as an insistence on sovereignty — the prerogative of states to shape the Scientific Panel, to set their own terms, to be active partners rather than norm-takers. CSIS, reporting on the vote that established the Dialogue and on the speeches within it, [noted precisely this emphasis](https://www.csis.org/analysis/what-un-global-dialogue-ai-governance-reveals-about-global-power-shifts).

Here is the paradox, and it is one I have written about before in the context of digital public infrastructure. For a state with limited compute, limited data, limited technical institutions and limited regulatory capacity, sovereignty over AI is not achievable alone. The pursuit of absolute sovereignty is a chimera — it was a chimera for the internet, and it is more obviously so for a technology whose frontier is, as co-chair Rein Tammsaar [put it plainly](https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/07/1167862), concentrated in two countries. What capacity-constrained states are reaching for is something more interesting: **sovereignty through collaboration**. Regional and plurilateral arrangements are not a dilution of autonomy for these states. They are the practical form autonomy takes.

This inverts the standard framing. We are accustomed to reading regional AI governance as evidence of fragmentation — as the failure mode that occurs when the global level cannot deliver. Read from the position of a state that will never build a frontier model, regionalism looks different: it is the level at which agency and capacity can plausibly be assembled. Pooling regulatory expertise, negotiating collectively with model providers, building shared linguistic and data infrastructure, developing common evaluation capabilities — none of this is available to a single small state, and none of it requires waiting for the General Assembly.

Language is where this becomes concrete. One of the sharpest sessions of the week was a panel on multilingualism and open models, [moderated by Claire Melamed of the UN Foundation](https://dsup.substack.com/p/from-grassroots-to-the-global-stage) and featuring the Kenyan Cabinet Secretary for the Digital Economy, the Spanish Minister for Digital Transformation, the Permanent Representative of the Organisation internationale de la Francophonie, and Daniel Pimienta of the Observatory of Linguistic and Cultural Diversity in the Internet. Note the composition. This was not a technical session about low-resource languages. It was a bloc argument — Francophone, Hispanophone, African — about who owns linguistic infrastructure and on what terms it gets built. Current models are [trained and optimised for a very small number of the world's roughly seven thousand languages](https://www.techpolicy.press/the-un-scientific-panel-on-ais-preliminary-report-does-not-establish-its-independence/). The response to that is not going to come from any one ministry. It is coming from linguistic and regional associations acting together, which is exactly what sovereignty through collaboration looks like in practice.

The tension this produces at the multilateral level is real and it was visible all week. There are member states that want a strong UN role in AI governance, and there are member states that emphatically do not — and the second group is increasingly content to let regional and plurilateral arrangements do the work. For them, fragmentation is not a failure mode. It is a strategy. The Council on Foreign Relations has warned that [absent a credible central coordinating mechanism, fragmentation gives way to normative competition](https://www.cfr.org/articles/the-world-is-trying-to-govern-ai-the-un-wants-in), with states diffusing governance models that lock in strategic advantage. That warning is correct. But it will not be answered by asserting the UN's centrality. It can only be answered by the UN offering something that regional arrangements cannot.

### Where the participants are

Which brings me to the composition of these venues, and to why I think multistakeholder governance has been under-theorised in this debate — treated as a values commitment, a matter of inclusion and good practice, when it is better understood as a structural answer to a structural problem.

Consider the composition.

Set against the venues that came before it, the Dialogue is strikingly inclusive in intention. It carries forward the ethos of the Internet Governance Forum — the principle that governing a shared technology should involve more than governments — and it remains one of the few global fora that sets out to do so at genuinely universal scale. That intention matters, and it should be credited before it is qualified.

But intention and practice diverged, and the divergence was widely noted. In practice the Dialogue was mostly states. Two constituencies that any serious account of AI governance has to include were largely outside the room.

The first is industry. The [Independent International Scientific Panel](https://dig.watch/updates/un-scientific-panel-ai-assessment-global-dialogue) presented its first report to the plenary on Monday morning; Yoshua Bengio [told delegates](https://media.un.org/unifeed/en/asset/d360/d3601605) that frontier models have shown themselves capable of deceiving humans and of recognising when they are being tested, and that there is no sign the pace of development will slow. Balaraman Ravindran, presenting the findings on security and alignment, [reported](https://dig.watch/updates/un-scientific-panel-ai-assessment-global-dialogue) that AI development is outpacing risk mitigation and that alignment problems — bias, sycophancy, loss of control, AI-initiated deception — remain unresolved. These are findings about what a specific set of firms is building. Those firms had no formal standing in the Dialogue, which is, by design, a forum of member states.

Agentic AI illustrates what follows. Systems that plan and execute multi-step tasks with diminishing human oversight are the live governance problem of 2026, and the Dialogue said so — Guterres told the opening plenary that [our institutions were built to govern machines that follow commands, and are not ready for machines that decide](https://press.un.org/en/2026/sgsm23211.doc.htm). Naming the problem is what a deliberative forum of member states can do. The technical response emerged elsewhere: on 9 July the ITU announced a [Focus Group on Trust and Identity for Humans and Agentic AI](https://www.itu.int/en/mediacentre/Pages/PR-2026-07-09-focus-group-agentic-AI.aspx), tasked with developing frameworks for agent identity and authentication and with preserving meaningful human control over tasks such as executing financial transactions and operating critical infrastructure. It reports to ITU-T Study Group 17, meets in Paris in November and Geneva in January, and is open to all interested experts. Standards are governance — those adopted at the ITU carry weight across [194 member states](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/319766/20260706/itu-ai-summit-day-zero-what-new-44-member-un-commission-can-cannot-do.htm) — and much of the substance is being worked out in technical venues that operate on a different timetable, and with a different cast, from the Dialogue itself.

The second constituency is citizens and civil society. Public voice entered the Dialogue through written submissions — [more than fifteen hundred of them](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/un-global-dialogue-opens-urgent-call-safe-and-inclusive-ai-benefits-all) — and through side events. The submissions themselves are worth dwelling on: governments were the only stakeholder group to rank capacity-building first, while most other groups ranked safety first. That divergence has been recorded, and it has not been reconciled. Civil society organisations anticipated the difficulty: a [joint letter to the co-chairs](https://www.apc.org/en/pubs/joint-letter-un-global-dialogue-artificial-intelligence-co-chairs-multistakeholder) ahead of the meeting asked for synthesis reports explaining how contributions had been addressed, and for outcomes to be carried into existing processes such as the IGF. Written submissions and side events generate inputs. They do not generate mandates, and they do not persist between sessions.

These are two versions of the same problem, and it needs addressing directly rather than deferred to the next session's goodwill.

Governance moves in cycles of years; AI capabilities move in cycles of months. Between Geneva 2026 and New York 2027 there are ten months in which the technology will change substantially and the multilateral process will not convene. Much of the substantive work will happen in that interval, in the technical and standards venues that keep sitting. If the Dialogue's inclusive intention is to mean anything, the constituencies it left outside the room need durable channels into it — and those channels have to be built, not asserted. This is less a criticism of the Dialogue than the next task it sets.

Understood this way, multistakeholder participation is not a gesture toward inclusion. It is a **continuity mechanism**: the means by which a slow institution stays connected to a fast world between its own sessions. The case for bringing industry and citizens into the architecture is, in the first instance, a claim about function rather than fairness — though it is also, of course, a claim about legitimacy.

What is needed now is a serious exploration of the mechanisms that could carry each constituency in. On the citizens' side, there are workable models already taking shape. The Citizens' Track for AI governance that I have been involved in is one: a structured channel through which public deliberation in communities can feed into, and be answered by, the multilateral process, rather than arriving as unweighted written submissions. Ada Lovelace made the point sharply before Geneva — the Dialogue [has a concrete opportunity to institutionalise public deliberation from the outset, and it is easier to do this now than to fix later](https://www.adalovelaceinstitute.org/blog/the-case-for-a-global-ai-governance-floor/).

On the industry side the question is more open, but it is not unaddressed. Bodies convening AI developers alongside states and UN agencies — the AI for Good Global Commission launched during the same week is one example — suggest at least the form such a mechanism might take, whatever one concludes about any particular instance. The design questions are the substantive ones: whether a body of that kind reports into the mandated process, whether it engages the Scientific Panel's findings, whether the commitments made within it become legible to the states whose populations they affect. A channel that connects industry to the Dialogue would supply something the current architecture lacks. One that runs alongside it would leave the gap where it is. Working out the difference is exactly the kind of question the field now has to take seriously.

### What this asks of research

There is a version of this reflection that ends by calling for political will, and I want to resist it, because the constraint here is not primarily will. It is knowledge. We do not have good comparative evidence about which governance arrangements actually hold under geopolitical stress; about how regional blocs consolidate technical and regulatory capacity, and at what point pooling begins to deliver real autonomy; about what a continuity mechanism between multilateral sessions would need to look like to function; about whether deliberative processes can be made to feed decisions at the pace governance now requires. These are empirical questions, and they are answerable.

They are also, notably, questions that cannot be answered from a distance. The leading centres working on digital governance engage with the multilateral system largely from outside it. What Geneva's Digital Week made clear is that the interesting action is in the seams — between the universal forum and the technical standards bodies, between the global process and the regional bloc, between the state and the community. Those seams are only visible from close up.

It is the reason the Global Digital Governance Lab is being established at the Graduate Institute: a social-science research institution, rooted in international relations and global governance, sitting inside the multilateral hub where these rules are actually negotiated. Not to produce another set of principles — the world has plenty — but to bring the analytical tools of the social sciences to bear on what these processes make possible, what they foreclose, and how they might be made to work better.

Somewhere in Geneva last week, a diplomat declared that artificial intelligence must benefit all of humanity. Michael Bąk [predicted as much before the meeting opened](https://www.techpolicy.press/the-world-doesnt-need-another-un-ai-declaration-it-needs-architecture/), and observed that the statement is easy to make and nearly impossible to disagree with. He is right that the test is not whether we can agree on the sentiment. The test is whether the architecture gets built, by whom, and for whom — and whether, when the Dialogue reconvenes in New York in May, anyone can point to something that was constructed in the ten months in between.

That is the standard to hold it to. It is also, I think, a research agenda.

_Emrys Schoemaker, Executive Director of the Global Digital Governance Lab at the Global Governance Centre, Graduate Institute, and Senior Associate for Digital Policy at Caribou._
