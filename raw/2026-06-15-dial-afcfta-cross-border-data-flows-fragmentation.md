# Africa's digital future depends on seamless data flows. But fragmented governance threatens the promise of a single digital market.

*Expert comment by Jackline Akello, Digital Impact Alliance (DIAL), 15 June 2026. Third-party analyst comment (2026 DIAL Fellowship) — cited as analysis, not evidence.*

Africa's digital future is being actively imagined and actualised in real time. Across high-level forums and policy spaces, the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) is increasingly positioned as the engine that could unlock a truly integrated digital economy on the continent. Projections suggest that the AfCFTA could boost intra-African trade by over 50%, with digital trade expected to play a central role in that transformation.

At the heart of this promise lies a critical enabler: data.

The AfCFTA's Protocol on Digital Trade, particularly Article 20 on Cross-Border Data Flows, signals a bold ambition to facilitate the seamless movement of data across borders for the purpose of digital trade. It's even more striking when taken together with Article 16 of its Annex which calls on States not to *"apply measures that restrict the cross-border transfer of data, including personal data between their territories and the territories of other state parties."* In principle, this is a game-changer. It recognises that data is not just a byproduct of the digital economy, but its backbone. But here is the tension: while the AfCFTA calls for openness, many African data protection frameworks impose restrictions on cross-border data transfers, often in the name of safeguarding privacy and sovereignty.

So the question becomes unavoidable: Can Africa truly unlock the promise of digital trade if its legal regimes are pulling in different directions? Bridging this gap is no longer a technical exercise, it is a strategic imperative for the continent's digital future.

## The AfCFTA promises seamless data flows. But are African laws ready?

Beneath the promise of the AfCFTA lies a more complex and fragmented reality. Across the continent, cross-border data flows are governed by a patchwork of national data protection regimes, many of which were not designed with a continental digital market in mind. For businesses, innovators, and even public institutions, this fragmentation translates into real friction. Moving data across borders can require navigating multiple layers of compliance: securing explicit consent from individuals, obtaining authorisation from Data Protection Authorities, or demonstrating that the receiving country offers "adequate" levels of protection.

In practice, these requirements are not just technical, they are exclusionary. A telling example lies in how "adequacy" is defined and recognised. Morocco's 2015 adequacy list did not recognise a single African country. Tunisia's 2018 list identified only five, while Botswana's list only had 2 African countries. This limited mutual recognition effectively constrains intra-African data flows, undermining the very integration the AfCFTA seeks to achieve.

At the same time, a growing emphasis on "data sovereignty" across African states has introduced additional layers of restriction. Policies mandating data localisation or imposing broad consent requirements are often grounded in legitimate concerns over national security, economic value retention, and the protection of personal data. However, in their current form, these measures can pull in the opposite direction of the AfCFTA's vision of a more open and interconnected digital market.

## Who pays the price for Africa's fragmented data rules?

For everyday Africans, the implications are far from abstract. Fragmented data regimes can limit access to digital services, increase the cost of doing business, and slow the growth of homegrown innovation. A fintech startup seeking to scale across borders, a researcher collaborating across countries, or even a consumer using a regional e-commerce platform may all encounter invisible but significant barriers rooted in regulatory misalignment. For example, a fintech company operating across multiple African markets may be required in one country to obtain prior authorisation from a data protection authority before transferring personal data abroad, while another country may prohibit transfers unless the destination country is deemed to provide "adequate" data protection and contractual safeguards. Navigating these divergent legal requirements increases compliance costs, creates legal uncertainty, and can delay or discourage cross-border digital expansion.

If Africa's digital economy is to move from promise to reality, these tensions must be resolved. Harmonising cross-border data transfer frameworks is not simply a legal exercise, it is foundational to enabling trust, unlocking innovation, and ensuring that the benefits of digital trade are broadly shared across the continent.

## Unresolved questions remain at the heart of Africa's digital future.

These tensions raise a set of urgent and unresolved questions that go to the heart of Africa's digital integration project. If the AfCFTA is to deliver on its promise, what does a *coherent and workable* model for cross-border data governance actually look like in practice? More specifically, how can African states reconcile the AfCFTA's obligation to enable cross-border data flows with domestic legal frameworks that impose restrictions grounded in privacy, security, and sovereignty?

A central question, then, is whether alignment is possible without dilution. Can African data protection regimes be harmonised in a way that preserves high standards of privacy and fundamental rights, while still enabling the free flow of data necessary for digital trade? Or does the current landscape risk entrenching a false trade-off between economic integration and rights protection?

Equally critical is the issue of trust and mutual recognition. What mechanisms can support the development of interoperable or mutually recognised data protection frameworks across African jurisdictions? In the absence of widespread "adequacy" recognition between African states, should the continent move toward alternative models such as regional certification systems, standard contractual clauses, or binding intra-African data transfer agreements that are better suited to its legal and institutional realities?

There is also a deeper political economy question that cannot be ignored. To what extent are data localisation measures and restrictive transfer requirements genuinely advancing national interests, and where might they be inadvertently constraining innovation, competition, and the growth of African digital enterprises? Put differently, are current regulatory approaches positioning African countries to capture value from the digital economy or isolating them from it?

Finally, and perhaps most importantly, what would a truly *people-centred* approach to cross-border data governance look like in the African context? How can regulatory frameworks be designed not only to facilitate trade, but also to ensure that individuals' rights are protected, that benefits are equitably distributed, and that smaller actors, startups, researchers, and civil society are not locked out by compliance burdens they cannot realistically meet?

I am setting out to engage directly with these questions in my research as part of the 2026 DIAL Fellowship. The goal of my research is to move beyond the binary of "open flows" versus "data protection" debate and instead explore how Africa can build a model of data governance that is both enabling and rights-respecting, anchored in its own developmental priorities and legal traditions.
