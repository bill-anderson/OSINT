Guinea has officially put into use a biometric verification system to combat 'ghost' workers in the public service. The Unique Administrative and Payroll Management Platform (FUGAS) was launched during a ceremony attended by Prime Minister Amadou Oury Bah, the Ministry of Labour announced.

A pilot launched the previous year enabled the government to save an estimated $26.9 million after a biometric audit; of ~270,000 registered civil servants, fewer than 150,000 were confirmed to have effective work presence (per Ecofin Agency). PM Bah called FUGAS a major step toward modernizing the civil service and a critical tool in anti-graft efforts. Labour and Public Service Minister Faya François Bourouno said it is a milestone in the country's public-service digitization drive that began in 2021, enhancing transparency, traceability and payroll performance.

Bourouno noted FUGAS aligns with the Simandou 2040 strategy, whose digital-technology pillar was launched at the 2025 Transform Africa Summit; the strategy outlines Guinea's multisectoral development plan to 2040.
