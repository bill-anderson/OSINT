# State-backed South African satellite Internet and phone company launched

By Luis Monzon, MyBroadband, 1 July 2026

Deputy President Paul Mashatile has announced the launch of BrainSAT Satellite Services, a South African satellite Internet and communication provider, on Tuesday in Johannesburg.

The company is described as a "strategic partner" of the United Arab Emirates (UAE) satellite communications firm Space42, which provides a range of services, including broadband Internet.

BrainSAT is an independent South African company that has a strategic partnership with Space42 and uses the UAE company's satellite infrastructure.

Mashatile also announced the local availability of Thuraya satellite phones, a service offered by Space42, which has been available locally since at least February.

"The launch of BrainSAT Satellite Services and Thuraya satellite phones mark an important milestone in our nation's pursuit of secure, resilient and reliable satellite connectivity," the deputy president said.

"This collaboration is a clear indication of South Africa's readiness to embrace the digital future as well as positioning our country as a leader in Africa's digital marketplace."

Mashatile explained that a memorandum of understanding was signed with Space42 and BrainSAT Technologies during a state visit to Dubai, UAE, in April 2024.

The Department of Communications and Digital Technologies signed the memorandum on behalf of the South African government at the time.

He said that the memorandum was more than a formal agreement and was a commitment to bring "world-class satellite and digital communications solutions to South Africa."

The launch is part of a broader plan from government to ensure network availability across South Africa's most connectivity-dependent sectors.

This included sectors such as energy, mining, maritime and humanitarian operations. Mashatile said that satellite connectivity is essential for maintaining operational continuity.

"The launch of Thuraya Satellite Phone and BrainSAT Satellite Services will support the roadmap for digital transformation," he said.

"It will do this by providing a resilient infrastructure that supports modern and accessible public services."

According to BrainSAT's website, the company offers high-throughput YahClick satellite broadband through its partnership with Space42.

"Yahclick is the most widely deployed Ka-band high-throughput satellite broadband service across Africa, born from the Yahsat (now Space42) joint venture with Hughes Network Systems," it said.

"BrainSat brings always-on internet to homes, businesses, public services and remote enterprise sites — including locations where fibre and 4G will never reach."

There are four broadband Internet bundles on offer, but pricing is not indicated:

- Yahclick Lite — 10Gb
- Yahclick Empower — 20Gb
- Yahclick Premium — 50Gb
- Yahclick Extra Premium — 100Gb

It should be noted that YahClick has been available in South Africa through Internet service providers like Vox Telecom for years.

BrainSAT's website also highlights offerings in rapidly deployed field broadband, maritime satellite voice, maritime VoIP, uptime, geospatial intelligence, and data centre infrastructure.

## South Africa's national satellite communication strategy

Mashatile said that the launch of BrainSAT's services was part of the National Satellite Communication (SATCOM) strategy, which was developed by the Department of Communications.

The strategy is focused on the establishment of a South African-owned satellite system to achieve the goal of enhancing digital connectivity in remote areas through a satellite-based network.

"Additionally, the SATCOM strategy addresses several government priorities, and the deployment of the satellite system," said Mashatile.

Benefits of a locally-owned satellite system included "improved efficiencies in service delivery in rural areas, catalysed economic growth, and the development of new industries and markets."

He said that it would also drive the creation of new skills and increase access to broadband Internet across South Africa.

"It is commendable that Space42 brings us world-class infrastructure: the Thuraya-4 NGS satellite, the Foresight SAR constellation, and YahClick broadband," the deputy president said.

"BrainSAT guarantees the translation of these global innovations into local empowerment."

In February, the Department of Science, Technology, and Innovation (DSTI) said that a South African communications satellite system would save taxpayers billions of rands every year.

"South Africa currently spends approximately R100 billion a year on foreign communication service providers," the DSTI said previously.

"In contrast, a locally owned satellite system with a lifespan of up to 20 years would require a one-time capital investment of approximately R6 billion."
