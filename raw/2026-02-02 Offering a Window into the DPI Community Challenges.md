**Reflections from the 2025 State of DPI Report Launch**

As part of the recent release of our first State of DPI Report, we hosted a launch event on January 16, 2026, to bring together members of the community and gather their reflections and questions about where DPI stands today and where it needs to go tomorrow.

The report pulls from the [DPI Map](https://dpimap.org/) data, establishing a global baseline of 64 digital IDs, 97 digital payments, and 103 data exchange systems, and further analysing the presence of certain attributes — such as interoperability and privacy — among them.

With 288 registered participants joining the launch from across the globe, the event reflected an eagerness to understand and contribute to this conversation, with questions arising that show that we’re moving beyond definitional debates into the territory of implementation and measurement. The concerns raised by attendees weren’t about whether DPI matters, but about how to make it work and ensure it serves everyone.

In this blog, we reflect on these key questions that emerged from the event’s polls and Q&A session in order to offer a window into what the DPI community is grappling with right now.

## Who’s engaged in DPI Measurement?

Our first poll asked participants to identify their role in the DPI ecosystem. The results paint a picture of a diverse community.

## Q1: What best describes your role in the DPI ecosystem? (n=67)

Researchers and academics led the way with 20 respondents, followed by private sector partners (12), civil society advocates (9), multilateral organizations (8), and government policymakers (7). Smaller but important contingents included funders, evaluators, and others working at the intersection of these roles.

**Q2: Where are you joining from today? (n=67)**

Geographically, participants located in Europe were the most represented group of attendees with 37 participants, followed by North America (15), Asia (7), Africa (6), and Latin America & the Caribbean (2).

While DPI dialogue is increasingly global, we need to ensure that those building and living with these systems around the world have equal representation in shaping how we understand and measure them.

## Questions from the Community

When we asked participants about their biggest questions regarding DPI, a clear set of concerns emerged.

## Q3: What’s your biggest question about DPI right now? (n=33)

**Out of 33 total respondents, eleven** — more than any other concern — asked: *How do we ensure DPI doesn’t exclude vulnerable populations?*

DPI holds the promise of expanding access to essential services, but that promise is hollow if the infrastructure itself creates new barriers or reinforces existing ones. Whether it’s rural populations without reliable internet, elderly citizens unfamiliar with digital interfaces, or marginalized communities who lack the documentation required to enroll, exclusion remains the critical risk that could undermine DPI’s legitimacy and impact.

**Nine respondents** wanted to know: *How do we measure if DPI is actually working?*

Assessing whether DPI systems are delivering real-world outcomes — either by reducing time to service delivery, expanding financial inclusion, or enabling national sovereignty — is where measurement becomes especially challenging, and it’s a gap participants seem eager to close.

**Seven respondents** raised concerns about balancing innovation with privacy and security. This tension is fundamental to DPI design: how do you experiment and build new systems to deliver tangible value at speed while also safeguarding personal data and protecting against surveillance and misuse?

These concerns shape whether DPI earns public trust or becomes a source of resistance and harm.

## Key research questions in the Community

The Q&A session surfaced 21 questions that clustered into five clear themes. Together, they reveal the complexity of what it takes to build DPI that truly serves the public interest.

- **Digital ID Architecture and Rights**

Multiple participants questioned the architecture of digital ID systems and their implications for rights. What human rights safeguards are most effective in practice, and are digital IDs generally mandatory for accessing essential rights like education and healthcare?

One particularly insightful question challenged the assumption that all digital IDs are the same: *Authentication systems like Aadhaar are not the same as verifiable credential issuance and verification systems. Are you tracking the protocols different systems are using?*

The DPI Map currently treats digital ID systems as a category, but the underlying architectures — centralized vs. federated, credential-based vs. biometric, government-controlled vs. user-controlled — have profound implications for privacy, security, and user autonomy.

- **Adoption and User Experience**

Several questions focused on how DPI is being adopted in specific sectors — particularly healthcare — and whether meeting user needs drives adoption as much as technical interoperability does. One participant raised the intriguing question of whether there are groups working on citizen-to-citizen infrastructure that supports democratic dialogue, such as tools for deliberation or media analysis.

This line of questioning highlights DPI’s potential to enable not just government-to-citizen services, but also horizontal, peer-to-peer forms of civic engagement. While our current measurement framework focuses primarily on three fundamental DPI components, there’s clearly an appetite for thinking about DPI’s role in strengthening democratic participation and collective action.

- **The Money Question**

The most repeated question in the Q&A was about costs. Participants wanted to know: Can we build a database of DPI project costs? Are you exploring sustainable funding models, particularly for lower-income countries? What role do private intermediaries (e.g. data centers) play in the total cost of ownership?

As one participant noted, investment analysis requires both estimates of benefits *and* costs, and right now there’s very little public data or benchmarking on the costs of DPI systems. Without cost transparency, it’s difficult for governments to make informed decisions or for funders to allocate resources efficiently.

Similarly, if DPI depends heavily on proprietary infrastructure like cloud services, then understanding these dependencies and their associated costs are critical for ensuring DPI remains sustainable over time.

- **Research Methodology and Scope**

Participants pushed us to think about what we’re measuring and what we’re missing.

Questions included:

- What’s the oldest DPI system, and what can we learn from it over time?
- Why weren’t Digital Public Goods explicitly mentioned in the report?
- Could this analysis be done at the subnational or municipal level?
- Is anyone researching the *process* of DPI implementation — the digital transformation journey itself, not just the endpoint?

These provocations remind us that measurement is shaped by the questions we ask and the boundaries we draw. Expanding the DPI Map to capture subnational variation or the relationship between DPI and the broader Digital Public Goods (DPG) ecosystem could enrich our understanding significantly.

- **Emerging Frontiers and Risks**

Finally, a few questions pushed into less explored territory. One asked whether DPI is being used for climate action. Another questioned whether DPI must be government-backed or could be privately led. And another: *What do we do with data on DPI misuse, especially on surveillance?*

Documenting where DPI is being misused or enabling harm, as well as where governance safeguards are lacking is a critical part of the measurement puzzle. Building accountability mechanisms into measurement itself will be crucial for ensuring DPI serves the public interest rather than undermining it.

## What the Community Wants Next

Our final two polls asked participants to help us prioritize future research and reporting efforts.

## Q5: Which DPI measurement challenge should we prioritise researching in 2026?

When asked which measurement challenge we should prioritize, the answers were as follows: t racking real-world adoption and inclusion outcomes (17 votes). This was followed by understanding interoperability performance beyond technical specifications (8 votes), creating sector-specific measurement approaches (5 votes), and better measuring governance enforcement (2 votes).

People really want to understand whether DPI is reaching the people it’s meant to serve and whether it’s making their lives better. This reinforces the urgency of developing impact evaluation frameworks that can capture not just the presence of DPI, but its performance and equity in practice.

## Q6: What would be most valuable to you in next year’s State of DPI Report? (n=33)

For next year’s State of DPI Report, participants wanted **deeper analysis of what drives successful DPI adoption** (18 votes) and **sectoral deep-dives** into areas like health, education, and social protection (11 votes). Regional comparisons and more country case studies also received interest.

These preferences point toward a shift from describing what exists to explaining why it does or doesn’t work. Moving beyond prevalence toward understanding what conditions and arrangements enable DPI to deliver public value will be the next stage for this research.

## Get Involved

The DPI Map launched in October 2024 as the first systematic attempt to document DPI prevalence globally. In just over a year, it has become a reference point for researchers, implementers, and policymakers trying to make sense of a rapidly evolving field.

Measurement is a tool for accountability and learning. The questions participants raised about inclusion, governance, costs, and impact reflect a community that’s hungry for evidence to guide better decisions and a shared commitment to ensuring DPI lives up to its promise.

This shift toward assessing maturity is critical if DPI is going to become the kind of infrastructure that advances the public interest.

If you want to be part of this journey, there are several ways to engage:

- **Join the DPI Map Community of Practice** to participate in open sessions on measurement challenges and contribute your expertise.
- **Explore the dataset** and use it in your research, advocacy, or policy work.
- **Contribute data** from your country or region to help us build a more complete global picture.
- **Collaborate on research** to deepen our understanding of what makes DPI effective, inclusive, and sustainable.
