Across Africa, governments are investing heavily in Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI). National digital identity systems, interoperable health and education databases, biometric voter registers, and integrated social protection platforms are increasingly presented as the backbone of efficient service delivery, state modernisation, and digital inclusion. From digital IDs to data exchange platforms, DPI is rapidly becoming the infrastructure through which states collect data, allocate resources, and exercise authority.

Yet DPI is not a neutral technical project. It is a deeply political undertaking that reshapes how power is exercised in the digital age. Decisions about system design, data collection, interoperability, and access have implications for privacy, equality, and democratic accountability. In the absence of strong governance frameworks, DPI risks entrenching surveillance, exclusion, and unchecked concentrations of power.

### DPI as a Site of Power

DPI systems sit at the foundation of digital governance. They determine who can access public services, how identities are verified, how data is shared across government agencies, and how individuals are monitored or profiled. In Africa, these systems are often justified in terms of efficiency, inclusion, and economic development. While these goals are important, they can obscure the risks that arise when large-scale data systems are deployed without adequate safeguards.

When digital identity systems become mandatory gateways to healthcare, social protection, or voting, errors, exclusion, or misuse of data can have severe consequences. Centralised databases and interoperable platforms can enable function creep, where data collected for one purpose is repurposed for surveillance, law enforcement, or political control. These risks are amplified in contexts where oversight institutions are weak, transparency is limited, and individuals lack effective remedies.

### Norms Exist, Practice Lags

Africa is not operating in a normative vacuum. Continental and sub-regional frameworks recognise that data protection and privacy are foundational to legitimate digital governance. The African Union Convention on Cyber Security and Personal Data Protection, the African Union Data Policy Framework, and the ACHPR’s Declaration of Principles on Freedom of Expression and Access to Information in Africa all affirm principles such as purpose limitation, data minimisation, consent, transparency, and independent oversight.

These standards make clear that DPI must be governed in ways that respect human rights and democratic accountability. However, in practice, DPI initiatives often move faster than the institutions meant to regulate them. Many data protection authorities remain under-resourced, human rights impact assessments are rare, and procurement decisions are shaped by vendor interests rather than public accountability. As a result, DPI is frequently rolled out as a technical solution, rather than debated as a political choice.

### Privacy, Access, and Democratic Accountability

DPI also reshapes the political economy of data. As states digitise public administration, vast quantities of public-interest data are generated. Yet access to this data is often tightly controlled, shielded by national security claims, weak access-to-information regimes, or proprietary contracts with private vendors. This opacity undermines public oversight, limits civic participation, and erodes trust in digital government.

African human rights standards increasingly recognise that access to data is itself a democratic safeguard. Without transparency about how DPI systems function, how data is used, and who has access to it, citizens are unable to scrutinise decisions that directly affect their lives. DPI governance therefore raises fundamental questions: who controls public data, whose interests shape system design, and how can individuals challenge exclusion, errors, or abuse?

### Reclaiming DPI as a Democratic Project

If DPI is to support inclusion rather than control, it must be grounded in privacy-respecting and rights-based governance. This means embedding privacy-by-design principles into system architecture; limiting data collection to what is necessary; ensuring clear purpose limitation; and subjecting algorithms, data flows, and procurement processes to independent oversight. It also requires strengthening data protection authorities, enabling legislative and judicial scrutiny, and creating meaningful avenues for public participation.

Crucially, DPI governance must centre the experiences of those most affected: people excluded by faulty identity systems, communities subject to heightened surveillance, and groups with limited power to challenge digital decisions. Treating DPI as a purely technical infrastructure risks masking its real function as an instrument of governance and control.

Africa’s investment in DPI presents a critical choice. With strong safeguards, DPI can support accountable service delivery and democratic participation. Without them, it risks becoming the backbone of digital authoritarianism or costly systems that primarily serve private and external interests. Reclaiming DPI as a democratic project is therefore not optional, it is essential to ensuring that Africa’s digital transformation serves the public interest.
