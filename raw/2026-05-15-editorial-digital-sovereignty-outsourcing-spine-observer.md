# Liberia Cannot Build Digital Sovereignty by Outsourcing Its Digital Spine

Editorial — Liberian Observer, May 15, 2026

Barely weeks after President Joseph Boakai issued Executive Order No. 163 declaring digital modernization a national priority, Liberia now finds itself confronting an uncomfortable contradiction. At the very moment government is speaking the language of digital sovereignty, interoperability, cybersecurity, and national innovation, the country is once again turning over one of its most sensitive national systems — the National Identification Registry (NIR) — to a foreign technology firm.

According to recent reports, the Government of Liberia has turned to an Austrian company to rescue the country's troubled national identification system amid growing concerns from civil society over data security, transparency, and oversight. The deeper question, the editorial argues, is how Liberia can claim to be building digital sovereignty while continuing to outsource the infrastructure of sovereignty itself. A national ID system, it says, is "the digital spine of the modern state," touching voter registration, banking, passports, immigration, taxation, telecommunications, social protection, and national security.

The editorial stresses this is not an argument against foreign expertise — Liberia has genuine technical gaps — but warns that "partnership must not become dependency. Assistance must not become surrender." It notes Liberia has steadily handed over critical national digital systems to external entities (passport, visa, work-permit, traffic-management, procurement, and biometric systems) while local technology firms complain they are sidelined through procurement frameworks that exclude Liberian participation. "One cannot repeatedly speak of 'Build Liberia' while systematically bypassing Liberians in the construction of Liberia's digital future."

It observes that EO 163 itself acknowledges interoperability, cybersecurity, centralized standards and coordinated digital governance — admissions that data has become a strategic national asset — yet Liberia is "racing deeper into digitization without first establishing the full legal and institutional protections such a transition requires," still lacking a comprehensive modern data protection framework. A breach of a national identity system, it warns, "is not simply an IT problem; it is a governance crisis." It closes by posing sovereignty questions: who stores the data, where, who has access, who audits the system, what happens if contracts collapse, and what local capacity is being developed alongside these partnerships.
