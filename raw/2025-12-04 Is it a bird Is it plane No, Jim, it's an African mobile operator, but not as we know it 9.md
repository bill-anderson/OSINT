Africa’s mobile operators are undergoing another major change with data beginning to exceed voice revenues in the leading countries. This post describes how this and other changes are reshaping the business models of different operators.

**The Context**

*(NB. This post will refer to mobile operators as MNOs and their subsidiaries as opcos throughout. It will also refer to capital expenses as CAPEX.)*

Sub-Saharan African MNOs have been the beating heart of economic growth on the continent for well over a quarter of a century. They have been the second largest source of investment after oil and mining but have arguably created more opportunities than getting stuff out of the ground. They have bred a cadre of African managers with wider regional and international experience and created thousands of new jobs with increased skills. Beyond these ‘hard’ indicators, they have created energy and aspiration that is very different from the decade before they arrived. Nevertheless, the final verdict is nowhere near being written as the story continues to change and unfold.

In its first period (1986-2006), MNOs set out across the continent to ‘conquer’ new territories. The ambition was to acquire as many territories as possible. The MNOs challenged the sleepy and often inefficient telco incumbents within the opening framework of liberalisation. This was their ‘entrepreneurial’ phase where new rules and fresh ideas were being tested. MNOs actually did things like running networks in the middle of civil wars.

I wrote a book called Less Walk, More Talk about the team at Celtel that captures the incredible excitement and achievement of this period. (See: [https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/Less+Walk+More+Talk%3A+How+Celtel+and+the+Mobile+Phone+Changed+Africa-p-9780470743201](https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/Less+Walk+More+Talk%3A+How+Celtel+and+the+Mobile+Phone+Changed+Africa-p-9780470743201) ) Similar books could easily have been written about the teams from MTN, Vodacom and others. Some of the opcos the larger MNOs found themselves with were acquired during this period from other companies who got there first. For example, MTN purchased Investcom in 2006 and Celtel itself was acquired by Zain, who in turn sold it to Airtel.

These were vertically-integrated companies that did more or less everything themselves because there were few service providers to help. When it started, MTN Nigeria had 144 tanker lorries delivering fuel to base stations across the country. MNOs delivered voice and SMS and knew how to do it profitably. As things developed, they began to put parts of their operations (like towers) out to service companies but they remained convinced that they could handle any opportunity that came their way and should do. Success on the scale that they experienced was sometimes a poor teacher. It did not breed a sense that new opportunities might need new partners.

In an article I wrote in 2008 I described them as ‘the masters of the universe’ before pointing out the challenges ahead. They went from being the challengers to the established order to becoming in large part, the new incumbents. They were much less keen to be understanding of new market challengers and as they grew, inevitably, they became a great deal more bureaucratic. I shall never forget one manager of a leading MNO, whose blushes I will spare, confessing in an AfricaCom session that it took six months to go from an idea to a new product.

Many things changed but perhaps the decisive factor was the arrival of first cheaper wholesale data (itself the product of breaking telco consortium monopolies) followed some years later by affordable retail mobile data. The unholy triangle of mobile data, smartphones and international social media companies (most notably Google and what is now Meta) upended the operating assumptions of this opening period. After a lot of testosterone-filled, shouty stuff about not being ‘a dumb pipe’, the MNOs learned to stop worrying and love the hyperscalers.

**African MNOs – Where are we now?**

As one veteran of the early days told me: “The African gold-rush is over. There’s not any form of flag-planting any more. We don’t see ourselves entering new countries.” African MNOs continue to pursue growth but watch their margins closely. Currency risk (as happened with the Naira and other currencies) can take away growth very quickly.

The table below shows the different players in the Sub-Saharan African mobile space. The power players are the four companies with the greatest reach and financial heft. If you think of playing cards, these are companies that largely have the aces and the picture cards. At the next layer down is the Second Division, which consists of companies that have the numbered cards, all the way down to the two’s and three’s. How else can one describe São Tomé and Príncipe with its 168,000 subscribers split between two operators?

As public companies, the power players have a greater capacity to attract investors but as private companies, those in the Second Division can sometimes afford to take a longer view. Among these companies is Viettel, which with one of those surreal touches, is actually owned by Vietnam’s Ministry of National Defense. The second division contains a couple of ‘coulda been a contender’ companies: Strive Masiyiwa’s Econet and Mike Adenuga’s Globacom. Telecel bought out Vodafone Ghana, MTN’s opco in Guinea-Bissau but failed to finalize its purchase of MTN’s Guinea opco. Out of the second division, the most promising contenders are Africell and Axian, both of which have successfully raised funding for expansion. The latter has recently announced a US$600 million raise. Telecel has announced its intention to go public.

The long list of Government-owned MNOs is only really successful if the game is rigged. For example, Ethio Telecom is giving Safaricom Ethiopia a nightmare taste of what it’s like to deal with an actual monopoly rather than being a de-facto monopoly. (The Zen slap?) Another state-run country is Namibia where more or less everything is Government-owned.

In the long run, most of these companies will not succeed. Why? They have neither the capital, nor the skills to compete with the private sector. They won’t cease to exist but a rut is just a long grave. Furthermore, for reasons described below, the capital cost of running them is likely to increase. That doesn’t really stop many governments (for example, Benin and Cameroon) from believing that if only they launched an MNO, their struggling incumbent would become profitable.

Then there are the ‘also rans’, the ‘stranded assets’ and the perpetual optimists who have come late to the party. The stranded assets are single operators (like Cell-C, 9Mobile and Ntel) or multi-territory operators too small to make much sense (QCell). Late to the party? G Mobile in Malawi.

Under liberalisation, the idea was that if there were more than three MNOs, they could not ‘price-track” and monopolies would be avoided. In the event, the EBITDA margins for the number 3 or 4 in a given country market are often half of those in the number 1 or 2 positions. On top of which, the number one player often operates either as a de-facto monopoly or duopoly. The four most striking examples are Sonatel/Orange in Senegal, Safaricom/Vodacom in Kenya and MTN in Ghana and Nigeria.

None of these examples of significant market power are likely to be challenged as in three instances, the Government owns shares in the company. Interestingly, Ghana is one of those few places where the Government insisted that the then incumbent unbundle its wholesale activities, something the South African Government managed to get its former incumbent Telkom to do. Perhaps mindful of these precedents, MTN has separated out its wholesale division into a company called Bayobab.

The table below looks at which countries the power players have first or second market positions in Sub-Saharan Africa. Bear in mind that Orange operates in Egypt, Morocco and Tunisia in North Africa and Vodacom’s parent company Vodafone in Egypt. The biggest by number of opcos is Airtel but amongst this number are smaller countries like Chad, Gabon and Niger. It retreated from Ghana, selling its opco (already the product of a merger with Millicom) to the Government. MTN slimmed itself down selling off both a range of international opcos, as well Guinea-Bissau. It closed Guinea. Orange, after a disastrous attempt to enter the English-speaking East African market, sold both its Uganda opco (to Africell) and Telkom Kenya (bought by Helios). It has also sold its opco in Equatorial Guinea and its management contract in Ethiopia did not lead to a wider opportunity. Millicom quit the continent completely. Vodacom has not adjusted its portfolio and probably has, along with MTN, one of the strongest set of opcos.

How do you tell the difference between these mobile operators? Outside of the power players, there are probably marginal differences in network coverage and quality and customer service. In most cases, for voice and data, all operators can, if they have the will, the skills and the capital, arrive at the same place. Increasingly, the power players and some others have begun to look at what are tagged ‘the verticals’ with the aim of differentiating themselves. But as will become clear below, everyone with access to capital is pretty much doing the same as everyone else.

**The exploding universe – the different businesses MNOs might want to be**

The coloured table below represents the range of possible businesses African MNOs might choose to run. Fibre-To-The-Home (FFTH) broadband? Data centres? Cloud services? AI data centres and services? A shadow banking infrastructure? Video streaming? Gaming? Social media? The range of potential businesses can form quite a long list and only the larger MNOs are actually seriously considering the full list.

The issue is alarming because there are enormous pressures here on management attention and investment resources. Each new G iteration involves another round of investment and it’s not yet clear what 5G is good for. More efficient carriage of data. Errrr…FTTH involves shovelling dollars into the ground ahead of income. Data centres are capital intensive and running them as a completely commercial enterprise involves skills an MNO might not have. A shadow banking infrastructure cannot be built without the close involvement of competitors. A social media platform with an ad sales capacity is hard to build from scratch. There’s a problem here of reach and grasp.

The larger MNOs have for some time been spinning off parts of the business: Airtel NXtra (data centres), Axian (data centres), MTN Momo (minority share to Mastercard), etc, etc. In this configuration, an MNO becomes something more like a holding company. Then the question is which parts of the business merit the love and attention and which parts fall further down the investment committee’s agenda? At first glance, the built-in gravity in this model is that voice and data are core and it’s reassuring and profitable to have wholesale to underpin it. Mobile money works better for some than others and no-one is really earning much from the digital life. But let’s take this first glance apart based on revenues and recent developments.

**So many businesses, so little time…**

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gcqF!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff9659ac9-ac88-494f-bcd8-ebbb43b06032_904x292.png)

An investment case document on Vodacom’s investor relations site describes pre-paid voice as in ‘managed decline.” Across four of Africa’s biggest and best developed markets – Ghana, Kenya, Nigeria and South Africa – there are operators whose data revenues exceed those taken for voice. In its Historical Data Sheet H1 2026, Airtel is predicting that data revenues will exceed voice in its francophone opcos. In the same document, data revenues exceed voice by the end of FY 2025.

Users have figured they can use WhatsApp (other services are available) for calling and messaging. MTN is proudly using WhatsApp to allow its South African customers to renew their data bundle and co-operating with Meta to improve the service in Nigeria.

Do a mind exercise for me. Imagine that the larger operators take over all the customers willing to pay for data and make calls using data and that smaller operators will specialise in the 2G customers using legacy voice and SMS. Of course, it’s not going to be as neat as that but it gives you some idea of what’s happening. At their heart, these are two very different sets of customers: those using voice, SMS and USSD services with 2G phones and those using data to access services with smartphones.

MNOs are pushing for cheaper smartphones at US$30-40. This is a familiar song but one that is hard to sing. There have been initiatives going back as far as 2012. Even if the vendors can produce this ‘holy grail’ there are other significant barriers. Countries with 80% literacy will take to a phone of this sort like a duck to water but those with 30-50% literacy will struggle. I have been criticised for pointing this out but it’s a hard barrier and it exists. Below this one lies the less daunting but equally as real barrier, technology illiteracy. There must be voice-activated or visual ways of helping the user but again these have been discussed but not yet fully realised. The end result is a two-speed mobile user market that will persist for some time.

With customers using data, they will be encouraged to use self-service apps to manage their top-ups, getting loyalty points and communicate with the company. Very little data exists on the current success of this development but it will eat into more African ‘bricks-and-mortar’ approach to customers. Happy young, digital natives vs less happy older, less educated 2G customers?

But for the MNOs the message is clear. Data is their future business and in time all their company and network decisions will come to reflect this reality. But as one industry insider told me: ““With next gen investments you need to have cash to plough in. With fibre, there’s a lot of costs.”

**Expensive mobile data vs household data**

The challenge with mobile data is that it is sold in expensive, pre-paid bundles that still encourage fear of running out. Yes, I know that in many countries the rates are a great deal cheaper than they were but living the digital life on mobile data would be extravagant and beyond the means of many Africans.

Africa may be mobile first but other devices do exist. A new generation of ISPs are delivering affordable, uncapped broadband using wireless or fibre to lower income households. (See: [https://russellsouthwood.substack.com/p/african-isps-aimed-at-lower-income?r=4tqb](https://russellsouthwood.substack.com/p/african-isps-aimed-at-lower-income?r=4tqb) ) The MNOs are taking the higher-income customers and developing significant broadband businesses. These include: Kenya (Safaricom’s 301,000 household broadband customers overtaking Wananchi’s 250,000+), South Africa (Vodacom 198,000 broadband customers, by wireless and broadband) and Senegal (Sonatel’s 614,000 residential broadband customers in Senegal, 280,000 in Mali, total 900,000 across group). Vodacom is acquiring a 30% stake in Maziv, the owners of broadband operator Vumatel and Dark Fibre Africa. In this light, Axian’s purchase of Wananchi makes perfect sense. Another MNO competitor in this field is GVA (Vivendi’s broadband and streaming op in Africa), which had 76,000 customers in Cote d’Ivoire in 2024.

**Wholesale – Building the plumbing for the new infrastructure**

Putting together an effective fibre network for a digital future is both costly and sometimes complicated. Up until about 2010, network decisions were largely made with voice as the priority. After that, up until the present day, the decisions have mixed voice and data priorities, with the latter coming into sharper focus with 4G and 5G. What base stations now need to deliver is high throughput data in key locations and an acceptable amount in less widely used locations. All in all, this requires a great deal more fibre that has to be built directly to the base stations. This comes with a price tag and a lower rate of return than its voice equivalent back in the day.

For MNOs, wholesale used to be a low volume, high margin business, which along with roaming was a nice source of returns. Nowadays, it a low margin, high volume business and the winnings go to those who can generate the highest volumes. In the more competitive markets, wavelengths and dark fibre are available if the volume has or will be sold. If you’re MTN, you’re already sitting on significant volumes across local, national and international. If you’re another operator, your hand looks less good and your margins less impressive.

*The question at every point is: are you just looking after your own demand (and customers) or can you extend that volume lead by selling to third parties?* All the major players have enterprise sales companies but with even corporate bandwidth looking much more like a commodity, enterprises are probably looking for something more than just bandwidth purchases.

MTN has chosen to sell to third parties by rebranding its former subsidiary MTN GlobalConnect as the rather more ambitious Bayobab. It can then sell its unused capacity to others and attract investment for doing so. This approach is a way of making the hill of CAPEX less steep to climb.

In a different way, MTN and Airtel Africa have an agreement to share mobile network infrastructure, including fiber, in Uganda and Nigeria, with potential expansion to other markets like Congo-Brazzaville, Rwanda, and Zambia. The agreement’s main goals are to improve network efficiency, reduce costs, and expand coverage to more customers, especially in rural areas. In January 2025, Orange and Vodacom formed a new joint venture focused specifically on rural areas. It will build, own, and operate solar-powered mobile base stations in underserved rural areas of the DRC. Agreements on network sharing are easier to sign than to operate but there is a good logic here.

MNO data centres used to be largely for their own purposes with some enterprise customers. As outlined below, the future includes cloud infrastructure, greater use of APIs and, (something no-one really understands but is very important) artificial intelligence. Bharti Airtel launched its data centre arm Airtel Nxtra and fundraised for it. It has two large projects being built in Kenya and Nigeria. Axian launched Stellarix which offers data centres in Tanzania, Cote d’Ivoire, Senegal, and Madagascar. Safaricom has signed with IXAfrica, both to take care of its future data centre needs and to be AI-ready. MTN has built a data centre in Lagos and has been in talks to deliver AI-capability. In public, with its Project Eva, it is the only MNO that has created data sets ready for AI training. These MNO data centres join a crowded market where demand goes to the patient, willing to take a long-term position. Orange has boosted the capacity of the data centres it has where its opcos operate.

**Mobile money – the financial Wizard of Oz**

Mobile money was almost an accidental product for MNOs but is now a vital part of the income streams of the four major players: Airtel (16.8% of revenues), MTN (28.5%, higher percentages for some opcos), Orange (20.4% but for EMEA, Africa not broken out), and Vodacom (11.6% overall but 42% of Safaricom revenues). Other MNOs have similar contributions on smaller volumes: Africell (US$1.7 million in DRC on Afripay), Axian (18%), and Econet (14%). Axian Group has set up a Digibank and Fintech Cluster and bought into an Ivorian Insurtech and Jumia Technologies.

There is a green hill far away on which stands a unified African digital payments system. But at present, there is only a great deal of fragmentation. MTN and Orange had the Mowali interconnection agreement to connect their mobile money users in 2018 but it has not lived up to its promise. MNOs have agreements with individual banks, who both interconnect with MNO customers and run the underlying banking infrastructure for the services but things are not really joined up. Low credit card use means that the underlying system does not provide the sort of backbone found elsewhere. It’s all complicated but not insoluble.

All of this is very complicated and involves many different parties. There will be a tug-of-war between three different sets of people – the banks, the MNOs and fintechs – who all need each other to make the changes. Mobile operators are not banks, with their higher standard of regulation and wider range of functions. Even Orange Bank’s modest progress in Africa is based on partnerships with local banks. MNOs have opened up a wider portfolio of services to their customers – including loans, saving and insurance – but they are product-takers rather than product-makers.

The quality of the platforms that connect the different parts of the payment systems matter. The mobile money platforms of Africa’s operators have much improved from their early days but were not really designed to sit at the core of an interconnected payments system. Therefore, not surprisingly, all the major mobile operators have made moves to transition their systems to the cloud: Airtel (AWS and Comviva), MTN, Orange (using TenCent to enrich its systems) and Vodacom’s M-Pesa. At the launch of the latter, Safaricom said it wanted to create a comprehensive digital ecosystem, promising among other things, faster API responsiveness. It wants to become “a central hub for everyday digital transactions in Kenya *and beyond*.” (*my italics*)

In the future, they will be dealing with those two different streams of customers: the 2G USSD service users and the app-based, digital natives, overwhelming younger than the former. They may feel they ‘own’ the customers but in a multi-channel, data world, that includes payment through social apps like Facebook and WhatsApp, this is by no means guaranteed. Competition is already coming from companies like Wave in francophone Africa that offers more competitive transfer fees and ‘free’ banking. All this will vary by country as some have no fintechs and a very low percentage of banking customers.

*That question posed above recurs: are you just looking after your own customers or can you extend that volume lead by selling to customers of other MNOs?* M-PESA Ethiopia has literally just rolled out M-PESA Lehulum, a mobile money platform that allows users to access digital financial services regardless of their telecommunications provider. This is a desperate play from an opco currently fighting a losing battle for market share. But it contains the recipe for the future. If one version of M-Pesa (or Momo or Orange Money or whatever) is app-based and digital, the user no longer has to have an account with the MNO, once all the usual KYC is completed. It becomes an untethered platform that can then interconnect far more easily, using APIs and cloud. It will then reproduce the ‘network effects’ that voice interconnection did back in the day.

**Leading the digital life**

What I have dubbed the digital life is the smallest area of revenue contribution (2-10% where it is broken out) for all the four power players and much less visible for others. Some of the best and brightest minds have been working on this but the results are not yet there. From the users’ perspective, their digital life is driven by four things, which are in descending order: social media and news, video streaming, music streaming and gaming. These are also the four things that are currently driving consumer demand for data and causing the revenue shift from voice to data.

This is a very competitive area for the MNOs that brings them right up against the hyperscalers that only five years ago were their sworn competition. Social media? Facebook has 200+ million users against MTN’s Ayoba with 35 million. The latter is a terrific achievement with interesting potential but it still feels like Yahoo or Hotmail was to Googlemail back in the day. Video streaming? YouTube is at the broad end of the pyramid and Netflix and Showmax at the top end, passing TikTok somewhere along the road. Music streaming? Hard to say but since no-one’s shouting about their data, it’s unlikely to be large. Gaming? Plenty of casual low-value USSD games but no success at the top of the pyramid where money might actually be made. Nobody really associates an MNO with any of the four streams driving demand except as a delivery platform.

As with mobile money, the four power players are product-takers rather than product makers. MTN has various tie-ups including Mdundo for music streaming and Viu for video streaming in South Africa. The pattern is the same across all MNOs because these are complicated businesses to be in and someone else usually does them better. This lack of success is in part down to the fact that the youthful digital natives do not have a plentiful supply of disposable income.

*There goes that question again: are you just looking after your own customers or can you extend that volume lead by selling to third parties?* Using those who have bank accounts (20-30%) across a range of the wealthier countries, it may just be that there are not enough of them just amongst one MNO’s customer base. Again, the app-based platform that can address all MNO customers might be more interesting.

The reasonable concluding question must be: is the digital life core MNO business? In the early days, it was sensible to encourage the development of these services but now, why not just provide a platform that solves the payment problems and is a gateway to valued services?

**Leading rather than following – the innovation challenge**

In the last decade or so, Sub-Saharan African MNOs have been followers rather than leaders in a number of key developments: data centres, artificial intelligence, financial products (with one or two exceptions), data-based calling, household broadband, cloud services and the digital life. To be fair, Millicom and MTN did buy into Jumia but it always appeared like a ‘side-bet’ rather than something fundamental to future growth. It failed to deliver on its promises but that’s the point about innovation – it’s gnarly, complicated and sometimes doesn’t work.

Orange and Safaricom have ‘vehicles’ that invest in start-ups. But most MNOs have programmes for start-ups that are simply ways of being seen to be doing something virtuous. In essence, they are performative. However, I can find no instances (and please tell me I’m wrong) of MNOs identifying successful start-ups as the basis for expansion in their key areas. Contrast this with African banks who have either bought into fintechs, set up their own fintech arms or are working with fintechs. MTN gave greater freedom of this kind to Ayoba but once more, it’s in the ‘exceptions’ column. Again, to be fair, innovation is hard for publicly quoted companies answerable to shareholders on a quarterly basis.

Therefore, there needs to be a change in attitude by the MNOs where they begin to understand that although they still loom large in the market, not everything is about them. A more collegiate approach on same of the key developments – the payments infrastructure is case in point – will generate commercial opportunities that everyone will share.

**My view**

MNOs are now data companies. They may only be half the way there at the front of the queue but the direction of travel is clear:

• To solve the two-track market dilemma – 2G handsets vs smartphones – the MNOs who have the scale need to get serious about solving the problem. The answer? A US$30-40 smartphone that is either voice-activated in local languages or has a largely visual OS or both.

• The answer to the question: are you just looking at your own/demand/subscribers or third parties/other MNO’s subscribers?, is the idea that MNOs create app-based services that subscribers from any network can access. Mobile money is an obvious one but there are others. APIs and cloud-based infrastructure will allow interconnection with everybody who can supply a service that can operate on the platform.

• The same principle applies to services like wholesale and data centres. These need to be arms-length businesses that have neutrality for third parties to trust them.

• The challenge of fibre CAPEX is something everyone has to take responsibility for. It needs more ideas like JVs to build national networks and network sharing agreements. Think about how international cables have been operated.

• The digital life has not been a success for anyone. There may be two explanations: 1) It’s too early, give it time, or 2) this is not core business, leave it to others. If it’s too early, why are others succeeding? MNOs have a distribution platform and they can either ‘curate’ these choices in ways that brings user satisfaction and money to them or let the market in its Darwinian way winnow out the less successful on their platforms.

• MNOs need to start understanding that it’s no longer all about them and play nice. Except in their core business, all the choices they have will need a much more collegiate approach to solving them. To repeat, creating an effective pan-African payments structure will need banks and fintechs. Understand when you need the skills and power of others.
