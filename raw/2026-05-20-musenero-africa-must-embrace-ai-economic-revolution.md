Africa must embrace AI as an economic revolution

Ugandan minister: Africa must embrace AI as an economic revolution

Ugandan Minister Dr. Monica Musenero Masanza says governments must stop seeing AI as a technology and view it instead as a platform for economic transformation.

7 Min ReadEric Latiff, host of Fixing The Nation on NTV and Uganda's Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation, Dr. Monica Musenero Masanza. (Source: Gitex Kenya)

Uganda's Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation, Dr. Monica Musenero Masanza believes that African governments and policy makers need to shift their mindset from seeing AI as just a technology and rather view it as a driver for economic development.

She was speaking at the AI Everything Summit, part of Gitex Kenya, taking place in Nairobi.

"AI, which initially was seen as one of the technologies of the fourth industrial revolution, has grown and swallowed all the others," she told delegates at the conference.

"Industrial revolutions, although they present with the frontline technology, they are actually an economic revolution. They redefine how people and nations do their business, how they develop themselves, and how they grow," Masanza added.

"This is the first industrial revolution that we are participating in as a continent. The first three passed us by…" she said. "This is Africa's industrial revolution. It is going to shift everything, and we need to understand that."

She admitted that because Africa lagged a bit behind when it came to AI, the continent has so far only consumed AI rather than focusing on its economic opportunity.

"We've seen AI as technology, and we are pushing to invest in it as a technology. We have not yet actively positioned it as the new platform for economic and socioeconomic transformation. We have not yet embraced it that way," Masanza said.

She said African governments need a mindset shift away from just being consumers of technology to understand that AI "is the new soil in which we are cultivating our wealth."

"We are still looking at AI as a technology – and so are investors and technology companies – but we have not yet looked at it as something that is the new carpet below us, and that is what I would like us to challenge ourselves [with] now," Masanza said.

"We need to understand AI as an economy, and every economy benefits people, depending on how they capture the value," she added.

Africa's AI market is on track to surge from US$4.5 billion in 2025 to US$16.5 billion by 2030, according to data from Mastercard's AI in Africa report.

However, Masanza said that African nations need to question how to truly capture value from AI and "we need to understand the sentiment: Where is the money?"

"Don't sit at an AI summit or conference and leave without knowing where the money is. Because this is about money. Who is getting the best money in the end? When the world is divided into economic segments, whether you are a developing nation or a middle-income country, it's about where the money flows, and so our goal must be to tip that balance so that [AI] money flows into Africa" she stated

"Yes, we have a lot of [AI] activity ongoing, but right now Africa is only retaining 1% of the global wealth in AI," Masanza claimed.

Dr. Monica Musenero Masanza, Uganda Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation. (Source: Uganda Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation)

She believes this is something that needs to change. She gave the example of the Uganda's automotive industry saying that the first car was sold in in the country in 1904, but only very recently did the country begin making money from car manufacturing.

"We cannot allow ourselves to do this with AI, because this is our revolution," she reiterated.

Ambassador Philip Thigo, special envoy for technology in the office of the President of Kenya, agreed that Africa's role in AI must be articulated as an investment agenda.

"AI is not about ICT; AI is literally AI Everything. The continent still lags behind in AI infrastructure, connectivity, and energy, but we possess everything around artificial intelligence – whether it is green minerals, data, talent, emerging compute infrastructure, modern innovations, or the opportunities around investment and creating new industries," he said.

The Ambassador said that Kenya's Silicon Savanna has come of age.

"This next chapter is no longer just about the mobile revolution that began in 2006 and 2007, but about the potential to sustain that momentum through globally competitive AI infrastructure, innovation ecosystems, investment and new industries. Africa is open for business, open for investment and ready to shape the future of artificial intelligence," Thigo added.

Ambassador Philip Thigo, special envoy for technology in the office of the President of Kenya, speaks at the AI Everything Summit in Nairobi, Kenya. (Source: Gitex Kenya)

"Building capacity on AI development and entrepreneurship is important for countries not to be only consumers of AI products and services, but also producers and contributors to the digital economy," agreed Dr. Lourinho Chamane, chairman of the National Institute of Information and Communication Technologies (INTIC) in Mozambique.

"AI is no longer a technology shift; we are talking about a new geopolitical and economic restructuring," said Trixie LohMirmand, CEO of the global organizer of Gitex.

"East Africa has already shown the world how it can leapfrog industries through digital innovation, and now has the opportunity to become a globally competitive AI and infrastructure powerhouse by building sovereign capabilities across cloud, compute, talent, investment and research," LohMirmand added.

Better metrics needed to track AI impact

Minister Masanza also called for better economic metrics to track the actual impact of AI on African economies.

"We drive the narrative that it is automatically going to shift us, but how are you going to determine whether AI makes a farmer wealthy?" the Minister asked.

She lamented some recent meetings she had with the agritech sector in Uganda.

"Everybody was so excited, [speaking] about how many farmers had been onboarded, and then I ask them, are the farmers making more money? And nobody could answer that," Masanza said.

"We must develop metrics in the African context. We have a lot of informal businesses. We cannot use the same metrics as developed nations, which are so formal in everything. We need the metrics that capture that tell us whether AI is building our economies or if it is, in a subtle way, filtering away that wealth and income," she explained.

Masanza highlighted the need for more formal dialogues about AI and said her department is setting up a think tank to develop better metrics to monitor the development of the AI economy.

"We cannot delegate thinking in the era of AI," she said.

Masanza said it is important that the people who determine the economic policies need to be AI literate, and they need to be at the table for AI conversations.

"If they are not part of the conversation, then for us, we are building our science when the economists, the people who determine the economic construction of the continent, are in offices planning for a yet undeveloped continent," she said.

"But you cannot have AI and be underdeveloped because it itself as a technology requires a certain level of development," Masanza continued.

She said critical people like finance ministers and central bank governors need to be in the room at events like the AI Everything Summit.

"They have raised money, they have to go to World Bank and borrow money to build infrastructure to do this. But they do not see [AI] as the new economy which is coming," she said.

"We must bring the people who determine the economic policies, the monetary policies on board. They must be part and parcel of these [AI] discussions," she put forward.

She also advocated for public servants "to completely retrain ourselves" to embrace AI.

"Training experts in fields such as health, agriculture, justice and tourism is very important to facilitate the inclusion of AI in their day-to-day activities," INTIC's Chamane agreed.

"Domain experts are fundamental not only in preparing youth to develop algorithms, but also in designing and developing small language models that support AI for local languages as part of digital inclusion," he added.
