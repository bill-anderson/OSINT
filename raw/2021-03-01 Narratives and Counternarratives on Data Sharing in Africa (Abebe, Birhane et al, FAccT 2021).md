**Abebe, Aruleba, Birhane, Kingsley, Obaido, Remy & Sadagopan (2021), *Narratives and
Counternarratives on Data Sharing in Africa*** (ACM FAccT '21). Full text:
`raw/2021-03-01 3442188.3445897.pdf`.

- Argues that conversations about accessing/sharing **African data** are too often driven by
  **non-African stakeholders** deploying a **"deficit narrative"** (blaming lack of education,
  training, resources), which distorts the real complexity of the continent's data ecosystem.
- Uses **fictional personas built from interviews with African data experts** to surface
  **counternarratives** and identify recurring barriers and **inequities in the distribution of
  data-sharing benefits**.
- Roots the friction in **power imbalances from the legacies of colonialism, ethno-centrism and
  slavery**, disinvestment in trust, unacknowledged extractive practices, and **Western-centric
  policies ill-suited to the African context** — then discusses avenues for more equitable data
  sharing.
- Structural literature for the `colonialism`/`sovereignty` reading of African **open data** and
  **cross-border data exchange**; pairs with Birhane's [[2020-08-21 Algorithmic Colonisation of Africa]].
