Interoperability costs cut while 113 agencies operate payments systems

By Guardian Correspondent , The Guardian Published at 11:14 AM Apr 28 2026 Photo: File Payment illustration

THE government has significantly reduced the cost of interoperability transfers—moving funds to mobile phone wallets from bank accounts or vice versa.

Khamis Mussa Omar, the Finance minister, detailed these interventions in the National Assembly yesterday when responding to Kinondoni MP Tarimba Abbas, noting that transaction fees are capped at 5,000/- for any amount exceeding 500,000/-.

“As of March 2026, a total of 113 service providers have been licensed to operate payment system services across the country,” he said, affirming that this influx of providers ensures that consumers have a variety of choices, pushing companies to offer competitive pricing and better service quality to retain their customer base.

For smaller amounts falling below this threshold, a tiered guided cost limit to ensure that low-income earners are not disproportionately affected by service fees, he said, insisting that beyond direct price controls, the government expects that market competition will drive down costs.

The Bank of Tanzania (BoT) has been aggressively licensing new players to break monopolies in the electronic payment sector, he said, affirming that BoT has launched a multi-faceted strategy to ensure mobile money services remain affordable for all.

By slashing electronic transaction fees, reinforcing payment infrastructure and streamlining regulatory frameworks, digital financial inclusion will be accelerated, easing economic burdens on everyday users, he said.

The MP had demanded why the government had not yet issued a rigid schedule of indicative prices for mobile money deductions to shield consumers from high costs.

The minister pointed at achievements during 2024 and 2025, where the BoT moved to cap the maximum cost for single transactions in the Tanzania Automated Clearing House (TACH) and the instant payment system at 2,000/-.

This move was designed to prevent exorbitant charges on high-value transfers that previously discouraged formal digital banking, where the momentum was pursued last year with a strategic focus on the Tanzania Instant Payment System (TIPS).

The central bank remains vigilant, collaborating with private payment providers to periodically evaluate the cost of living versus transaction expenses, he stated. While the goal is to make transactions as cheap as possible, “the state will not take measures that could compromise the underlying stability, liquidity or cybersecurity of the national payments ecosystem,” he added.
