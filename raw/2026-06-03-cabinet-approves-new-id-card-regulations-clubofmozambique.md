# Mozambique: Cabinet approves new ID card regulations, notes gains in access to safe water

The government on Tuesday updated the Regulations on Civil Identification and the National Identity Card of Mozambican citizens, approved under Law No. 6/2022 of 20 June. Briefing journalists, the spokesperson for the 15th Session of the Council of Ministers, Ussene Isse, explained that the new regulations are intended to ensure a more secure issuance of National Identity Cards and to standardise the process nationwide.

“The regulations establish rules governing the collection, processing, management and preservation of personal identification data relating to Mozambican citizens, in order to create their civil identity, ensure the secure issuance of the National Identity Card and standardise procedures followed by Civil Identification officials across the country,” Isse explained.

During its 15th Session, the government also approved the 2026 Second Voluntary National Review Report on the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). The first report was presented in 2020.

In general terms, the Minister of Health also explained that the second report shows significant progress, particularly in the social sector, including an increase in the national rate of access to safe water to 62.3%.

“In 2025, although critical inequalities in access to water remain, there was an increase in the percentage of the population with access to safe water, from 81.9% in 2020 to 87.9% in 2025 in urban areas, and from 42.1% in 2020 to 48.5% in 2025 in rural areas. The national sanitation rate stands at 21.4%,” Isse highlighted.

According to the minister, the proportion of the population using adequate sanitation services increased from 59.2% in 2020 to 67.3% in 2025 in urban areas, and from 15.6% in 2020 to 21.4% in 2025 in rural areas. However, the government said sanitation challenges remain significant, and it is therefore working to minimise their negative impact on the population.

“We are going to drastically reduce water-borne diseases, such as cholera, which is an epidemic affecting our country. We are going to reduce cases of malaria, because malaria is a disease that is closely linked to the environment and environmental sanitation,” the minister said.

In the economic sphere, the real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) growth rate recovered from negative to positive growth between 2020 and 2023, followed by a decline in 2024 and a forecast recovery in 2025. This trend was also reflected in real GDP per capita.

“In the context of responsible production and consumption, the mining and energy sector has carried out activities to disseminate good mining practices. These activities intensified until 2023, declined in 2024 and resumed growth in 2025,” the source explained.

According to the minister, access to energy increased from 32% to 35.8% between 2020 and 2022. The share of renewable energy in the energy mix rose to 84%, before recording a slight decline to 78.5% in 2025, reflecting reduced investment in the sector.

In the area of governance, Isse reported that the percentage of districts with judicial courts increased from 84% to 97% during the period between 2019 and 2025.

There are 17 Sustainable Development Goals, with a global deadline for achievement by 2030. Progress is assessed continuously at local, national and global levels, culminating in the United Nations’ universal review of the agenda.
