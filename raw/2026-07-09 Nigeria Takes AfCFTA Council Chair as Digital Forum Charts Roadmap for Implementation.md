***The African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) has entered a new phase of implementation following the conclusion of the 18th Meeting of the Council of Ministers Responsible for Trade in Abuja, where Nigeria assumed the Chair of the Council’s Bureau, while the AfCFTA Digital Trade Forum in Lagos charted a roadmap for advancing Africa’s digital single market***

Family photo of Ministers and Officials During the 18th Meeting of the AfCFTA Council of Ministers, Abuja, Nigeria

Held against a backdrop of growing geopolitical uncertainty, rising protectionism and intensifying global competition for investment and supply chains, the meeting marked a decisive transition from negotiating the AfCFTA’s legal framework to delivering tangible economic outcomes for African businesses and citizens. The implementation agenda was further reinforced by the conclusion of the second **AfCFTA Digital Trade Forum in Lagos**, where stakeholders adopted Eight Calls to Action to operationalise Africa’s digital market.

The Council meeting followed a major milestone reached during the 39th Ordinary Session of the African Union Assembly of Heads of State and Government in February 2026, which adopted key Annexes to the Protocol on Intellectual Property Rights, significantly advancing the AfCFTA’s Phase II negotiations.

While negotiations continue on a limited number of outstanding legal instruments, including an Annex to the Investment Protocol and a remaining Annex to the Intellectual Property Rights Protocol, Ministers agreed that the continent’s immediate priority is implementation.

Opening the Council Meeting, **AfCFTA Secretary-General, H.E. Wamkele Mene**, described the current phase of the Agreement as one focused on converting legal commitments into commercial opportunities for Africans.

*“We have therefore entered a new phase of the Agreement, where the emphasis shifts from negotiating legal frameworks to accelerating implementation,”* **he** said.

Mene noted that implementation is already gaining momentum, with more than 12,000 Certificates of Origin issued under the Agreement as of March 2026. He also highlighted a gradual shift in intra-African trade towards manufactured goods and agri-food products, signalling growing industrial diversification across the continent.

He identified the establishment of the AfCFTA Intellectual Property Office as a strategic priority for strengthening Africa’s innovation ecosystem, while reaffirming the objective of achieving full market liberalisation by 2030.

Reflecting on Egypt’s tenure as outgoing **Chair of the Council’s Bureau, Hon. Dr. Mohamed Farid Saleh** stressed that the Agreement’s success must now be measured by its real economic impact.

*“The success of the Agreement can no longer be measured solely by the number of protocols adopted or legal frameworks established. Rather, it must be measured by our collective ability to translate these achievements into tangible economic realities,”* **he** said.

Assuming the Council’s Chairpersonship on behalf of Nigeria, the **Honourable Minister of Industry, Trade and Investment, Dr. Jumoke Oduwole,** outlined four priorities for the Bureau’s tenure: accelerating implementation, strengthening regional value chains, unlocking the potential of digital trade, and expanding access to finance for women-led enterprises, youth entrepreneurs and micro, small and medium-sized enterprises (MSMEs).

*“Let us move from ambition to execution; from commitments to measurable outcomes; and from fragmented markets to one integrated and prosperous African market,”* **she** said.

Consistent with its implementation agenda, the Council advanced the Ministerial Regulation on Preferential Market Access for Women and Youth in Trade, adopted key reports from its subsidiary bodies, endorsed recommendations to strengthen Africa’s competition framework, and considered the 39th AU Assembly’s decision to negotiate a Protocol on Industrial Policy and Development while deepening the continent’s collective engagement with external trading partners.

A Cross-Section of Officials at the AfCFTA Digital Trade Forum, Lagos, Nigeria

The implementation agenda adopted in Abuja was immediately reflected in the second **AfCFTA Digital Trade Forum**, jointly organised by the AfCFTA Secretariat and the Government of Nigeria in Lagos under the theme, **“Digital Trade for a Connected African Market.”**

Bringing together policymakers, regulators, entrepreneurs, investors and development partners from across Africa, the Forum focused on translating the recently adopted Protocol on Digital Trade into practical action across digital payments, cross-border data flows and e-commerce.

With Africa’s digital economy currently valued at approximately US$180 billion and projected to reach nearly US$712 billion by 2050, participants emphasised that the Protocol’s success will depend not on its adoption alone, but on effective implementation capable of transforming businesses and improving livelihoods.

The Forum adopted **Eight Calls to Action** to accelerate Africa’s digital market, including fast-tracking implementation of the Digital Trade Protocol, expanding digital infrastructure, paperless trade, cybersecurity, digital inclusion, investment, public-private partnerships and interoperable cross-border payment systems.

The AfCFTA Secretariat highlighted ongoing implementation through the **AfCFTA Digital Inclusion and Entrepreneurship Programme (ADIEP)**, which, in partnership with Google, is training 7,500 SMEs across 19 African countries. It also announced an MoU with Quest Ghana Limited to establish a Digital Trade Corridor, while highlighting PAPSS’s role in simplifying intra-African payments.

Mene described digital trade as a catalyst for expanding opportunities for women, youth and rural communities, while Dr. Jumoke Oduwole reaffirmed Nigeria’s commitment to implementing the Digital Trade Protocol. Both the Council Meeting and the Forum concluded with a shared resolve to translate AfCFTA commitments into trade, industrialisation and inclusive economic growth.
