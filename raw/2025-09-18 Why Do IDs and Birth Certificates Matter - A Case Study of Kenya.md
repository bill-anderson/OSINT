Human Rights Research Center case study on legal-identity exclusion in Kenya. In **March 2025** Kenya removed the 100 KSh first-time ID fee; the national ID was once called "kipande" (a colonial-era term). Millions — especially youth, women, children and marginalized communities in arid/remote regions — face exclusion from services for lack of birth certificates and IDs.

The **Maisha Namba** project was rolled out to provide universal identifiers at birth, but faces low citizen participation and privacy concerns (data handled by privately-contracted IT firms; ~10.6bn KSh spent by 2022). Data points: only **34%** of registered children have a birth certificate and **15%** of under-ones (KDHS 2022); **~5 million** Kenyans lack a national ID and UNHCR records ~9,800 stateless; birth registration is lowest in arid/semi-arid lands (Mandera, Turkana, Wajir). Legal identity gates access to finance (2m+ excluded per FSD; 91% of adults have ID per the World Bank), education, health (SHA), voting and labour markets, with disproportionate exclusion of Nubians, ethnic Somalis and nomadic communities. Recommendations: lower the ID-issuance age, mobile registration clinics, privacy-enhancing technologies (PETs), and institutional accountability.
