Identity seems universal. Every society has developed ways to answer the same basic questions: Who are you? How do I know you are who you claim to be? And what rights or responsibilities come with that identity?

Yet the answers to those questions have never been universal.

Across cultures, identity is understood, established, and verified in different ways. It may be rooted in family lineage, place of birth, community recognition, religious affiliation or a combination of these. The institutions that establish identity—and the information considered necessary to prove it—reflect generations of history, culture, law, and social context.

Digital public infrastructure (DPI) inherits these assumptions. Every successful national identity system is built around a particular understanding of identity, embedded within local institutions and legal frameworks. India’s India Stack, Brazil’s open banking framework, Kenya’s eCitizen portal, and Rwanda’s Irembo are all nationally designed systems that reflect local realities and serve domestic needs.

The challenge begins when identity needs to travel.

Across Africa, digital integration is becoming an economic imperative. Initiatives such as the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) envision a future in which people, businesses, payments, supply chains, and services move more seamlessly across borders. Realizing that vision requires identity to become portable—to be recognized, verified, and trusted beyond the jurisdiction in which it was originally established.

But if identity is inherently local, how can identity data be translated across borders? And what does it mean to govern identity data responsibly across jurisdictions?

These questions sit at the heart of my research fellowship. Over the coming months, I will explore what responsible cross-border identity data governance should look like: how countries can enable trusted identity flows without compromising sovereignty, individual rights, or the diversity of identity systems that have evolved across the continent.

For now, let’s examine the data governance challenges that arise when identity needs to travel.

## The data sufficiency problem: When is someone considered "identified"?

One of the most immediate challenges is that jurisdictions rarely agree on what constitutes acceptable proof of identity.

A driving license may be sufficient to open a bank account in one country but insufficient in another. National identity cards are mutually recognized for travel between Kenya, Rwanda, and Uganda, yet those same credentials may be inadequate to establish identity for financial services across those borders, where passports, residence permits, or additional documentation are required.

These differences are not arbitrary. They reflect varying levels of trust in foreign-issued credentials, differing assessments of the strength of another country’s identity infrastructure, and domestic regulatory requirements. The result is that the same individual, carrying the same identity documents, may be fully recognized in one jurisdiction and effectively invisible in another.

The first challenge, then, is one of mutual recognition and trust.

## The data adequacy problem: Does the required information even exist?

Even where countries trust one another’s identity systems and documents, they often collect fundamentally different information. A country that does not record proof of address in its foundational identity registry cannot provide that information for a cross-border transaction that requires it. A system that stores names in a single field cannot easily interoperate with one that separates given names and family names into distinct attributes. Some countries collect biometric identifiers that others intentionally exclude.

These differences reveal a deeper challenge: interoperability is not only about recognizing credentials but also about whether the underlying data can satisfy another jurisdiction’s legal or operational requirements.

One avenue this fellowship will explore is whether countries could publish standardized identity schemas while continuing to maintain their own national identity systems. Rather than attempting to standardize identity itself, jurisdictions could issue purpose-specific, locally compliant credentials that answer only the questions required by a receiving country. But this raises another question: what happens when the originating country simply does not possess the information needed to create those claims?

Not every interoperability problem has a technical solution. Some are fundamentally questions of governance.

## The sovereignty problem: Who governs identity once it leaves its country of origin?

The deepest challenge is not about technology or data models—it is about authority.

When identity data crosses borders, whose laws apply? Who is responsible if data is misused? Who determines whether information may be shared, for what purpose, and under what safeguards? Can countries participate in regional identity ecosystems without compromising political sovereignty or weakening domestic data protection?

These questions are particularly salient across Africa, where many countries are simultaneously strengthening their domestic privacy regimes while being asked to participate in increasingly ambitious cross-border digital initiatives. Regional frameworks provide important foundations, but implementation remains uneven, and many of the hardest governance questions remain unresolved.

## Although these challenges are emerging rapidly within digital public infrastructure, they are not entirely new.

For decades, the financial sector has had to establish trust across borders while operating under different legal systems, regulatory environments, and risk frameworks. International card networks, correspondent banking, remittances, bulk settlement systems, and mobile money have all developed mechanisms for verifying identity, managing risk, allocating liability, and enabling cooperation between institutions that remain subject to different national laws.

The financial services sector represents the most mature sector operating at the intersection of cross-border identity, trust, and data governance, making it a valuable laboratory for understanding which principles can—and cannot—be applied more broadly.

My research fellowship will examine those lessons critically. Which governance models have enabled trusted cross-border data sharing? Which assumptions have limited inclusion or reinforced inequalities? And what principles should be carried forward as governments build the next generation of interoperable digital public infrastructure?

Ultimately, the goal is not simply to make identity more portable. It is to understand how countries can enable trusted cross-border identity while respecting the legal traditions, cultural diversity, and political sovereignty that shape identity in the first place.

The answers will matter not only for finance, but for education, healthcare, social protection, labor mobility, and every public service that will comprise a digitally connected Africa.
