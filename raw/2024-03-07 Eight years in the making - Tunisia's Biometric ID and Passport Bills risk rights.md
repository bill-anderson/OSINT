In a decision Access Now says risks the rights of millions, on March 6 [2024] the Tunisian Parliament officially adopted two controversial bills related to the Biometric ID and Biometric Passport. The decision marks the culmination of a campaign led by Access Now and civil society partners in Tunisia since 2016, advocating against a mandatory biometric ID system without robust data protection and privacy safeguards.

Since 2016, civil society had demanded robust human rights guarantees, but many demands from eight years earlier remain unaddressed. Kassem Mnejja, MENA Campaigner at Access Now, said the group's commitment to advocating for the rights and privacy of every Tunisian citizen "remains unwavering." Marwa Fatafta, MENA Policy and Advocacy Director, called the vote "a sad end to eight years of civil society campaigning for privacy and data protection," and warned that "pursuing digital transformation without prioritizing people's fundamental rights is both risky and counterproductive."

Access Now points to the outdated 2004 Data Protection Law, coupled with a lack of clarity on how authorities will use, store, and protect citizens' sensitive information against abuse or unlawful access. Despite persistent campaigning, Parliament ignored civil society recommendations to address loopholes in the Biometric ID draft law.

The Biometric ID Bill first emerged in August 2016 when the Ministry of Interior (MoI) proposed replacing traditional identity cards with mandatory chip-enabled biometric ones for all citizens aged 15 and above. After setbacks, the MoI revived the initiative in late 2023. On November 30, 2023, the ministry's fourth draft law secured approval during a ministerial council session, then advanced to Parliament's Committee on Rights and Freedoms on December 25, 2023. Deliberations, which lacked public consultations, gave the Biometric ID and Passport Laws a green light on February 28, 2024, and both were adopted by an overwhelming majority vote on March 6. One day before the vote, MPs introduced an amendment to prohibit the creation of a centralized biometric database by the MoI, but it was dismissed.

Access Now said it will continue to monitor implementation and rollout of the new Biometric ID law and engage with Tunisian authorities and stakeholders on privacy and human rights.
