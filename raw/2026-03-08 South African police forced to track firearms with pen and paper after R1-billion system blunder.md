The South African Police Service (SAPS) has racked up a bill exceeding R1 billion over a botched contract for a biometric firearm management system.

The service provider, Forensic Data Analysts (FDA), switched off the system in 2019 after years of non-payment, and the cost has ballooned ever since.

SAPS lost a Pretoria High Court case in December 2025, where Judge Sulet Potterill ordered the police to pay FDA R644 million for the system’s intellectual property.

A further R120 million was awarded for maintenance and support, with interest continuing to accumulate on both amounts.

According to [the Sunday Times](https://www.sundaytimes.timeslive.co.za/politics/2026-03-07-how-ex-top-cop-khehla-sitoles-arms-fiasco-cost-sa-r1bn/), that interest alone is costing taxpayers approximately R1 million per week as SAPS appeals the ruling.

The dispute centres on three computer systems FDA built for SAPS: the Firearm Permit System (FPS), the Property Control and Exhibit Management System (PCEM), and an intelligence tool called VA-AMIS.

The FPS handled biometric processing for all firearm permit applications in South Africa, tracking weapons issued to officers and booked into police custody.

The PCEM managed forensic exhibits and evidence across the police service, while VA-AMIS supported DNA testing and investigative analysis for the Hawks and Crime Intelligence.

These systems were provided by Forensic Data Analysts, Investigative Software Solutions, and Solve Forensics, which the judge collectively referred to as FDA for ease of reading.

When FDA switched off the systems in 2019, police stations nationwide reverted to manual pen-and-paper processes for firearm administration.

The Sunday Times [report](https://www.sundaytimes.timeslive.co.za/politics/2026-03-07-how-ex-top-cop-khehla-sitoles-arms-fiasco-cost-sa-r1bn/) e [d](https://www.sundaytimes.timeslive.co.za/politics/2026-03-07-how-ex-top-cop-khehla-sitoles-arms-fiasco-cost-sa-r1bn/) that more than 3,000 police firearms have gone missing since the systems went dark, with authorities unable to track weapons.

Police portfolio committee chair Ian Cameron said the situation showed how procurement failures end up costing the public enormous sums.

“This is money that should have been used to strengthen policing capacity, improve investigative capability and support frontline officers, not to pay for avoidable administrative and contractual failures,” Cameron said.

The court also found that SAPS had continued operating FDA’s systems in violation of the interdict, and that the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) had illegally extracted FDA’s source code.

According to Potterill’s ruling, which cited past court cases, SITA had been attempting to develop systems to replace FDA’s.

Potterill noted in her ruling that FDA had to retrench 70 employees due to non-payment by SAPS.

## The Presidential Guesthouse deal

Judge Sulet Potterill

The court judgment laid bare a years-long saga of broken promises that began under former police commissioner Khehla Sitole.

Sitole was fired by President Cyril Ramaphosa in 2022 after a breakdown in his relationship with then-police minister Bheki Cele.

He stopped paying FDA’s licensing fees in late 2017, despite the police continuing to use the systems. SAPS continued using FDA’s systems despite court interdicts barring them from doing so.

For his defiance of the court order, Sitole was found in contempt of court. The crisis peaked on 31 January 2020, when FDA sent a sheriff to physically remove its systems from SITA’s premises.

Sitole urgently arranged a meeting at the Sefako Makgatho Presidential Guesthouse in Pretoria, where a government Lekgotla was underway.

Former National Treasury director-general Dondo Mogajane was brought in to negotiate, establishing that SAPS had budgeted R500 million for the acquisition.

Mogajane brokered a deal at R460 million for the intellectual property, plus R120 million for two years of maintenance and support.

Sitole signed a letter confirming the settlement. Two hours later, he withdrew it after Cele objected to the agreed price.

Cele cited a Government Technical Advisory Centre report that valued the systems at just R88 million, far below the negotiated figure.

## Fair value and broken promises

Bheki Cele, former Minister of Police

The withdrawal triggered a fresh round of negotiations. National Treasury appointed independent expert Don MacRobert to determine a fair value.

MacRobert valued the three systems at R560 million, consistent with four of six independent valuations obtained during the process.

Sitole accepted the MacRobert valuation in writing and instructed SITA to proceed with the acquisition, but payment never came.

SAPS raised successive objections about patents, procurement processes, and risk assessments that the court ultimately found were without merit.

Judge Potterill found Sitole’s testimony unreliable and contradictory, noting conflicting claims he made under oath across different proceedings.

In one incident, he claimed the systems belonged to SAPS, while in another, he acknowledged knowing that FDA owned the copyright.

“The fact that the national commissioner of police ignored contempt of court orders reflects a total disregard of the judicial system,” Potterill said.

The judge ordered SAPS to pay the full R644 million plus R120 million for support, with interest running from the date of breach.

Current police commissioner Fannie Masemola has petitioned the Supreme Court of Appeal, meaning the interest bill continues to grow while SAPS operates without the digital systems.
