In Harare and Bulawayo, white Starlink terminals are visible everywhere, even bolted to taxi roofs. Starlink landed in Zimbabwe in late September 2024; by Q1 2025 over 30,000 terminals were active nationwide, per POTRAZ. Never Ncube, CEO of Dandemutande (Starlink's authorised reseller), said there is "increased adoption by individuals, businesses, schools—even public institutions," used as primary and secondary links.

Behind the urban uptake lies a divide over who gets left behind. John Arufandika, a digital transformation strategist at Aptiva AI, noted that while Starlink's low-latency tech is a game-changer for reach and speed, "cost remains a barrier as hardware purchases and subscription fees are priced in USD leaving many rural users still struggling to afford." In rural Zimbabwe, where over 60% of the population lives, the standard hardware kit costs $350 (the portable Mini $200), with monthly subscriptions from $30 paid in US dollars — beyond the reach of most rural households, 76% of whom live below the poverty line. Rural access "so far, is mostly limited to missionary schools and donor-funded initiatives."

The arrival is also stirring unease among local ISPs: Starlink launched nationwide in September 2024, but local ISPs only received permission to offer similar satellite services in January 2025, giving the US disruptor a significant head start.
