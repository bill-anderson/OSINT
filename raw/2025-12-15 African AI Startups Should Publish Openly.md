Welcome back to *Digital Cognoscente*! Through my extensive engagement in AI and policy conversations across Africa, along with my experience in academic publishing, I’ve become curious about how institutions and organizations on the continent can increase their scholarly research output in AI.

For this edition, I’m providing brief commentary on why AI startups developing (non-)commercial products within Africa should invest in efforts to publish their research in academic venues.

Over the past half-decade, the number of AI startups operating in Africa has **[increased](https://www.afrilabs.com/new-ai-insights-afrilabs-publishes-two-groundbreaking-reports-funded-by-the-gates-foundation/)** substantially. However, my **[prior research](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-08215-3_3)** in this domain has shown that it is unclear what types of AI methodologies these startups employ and how they specifically classify their products as AI. Now that the global AI startup landscape has evolved since the launch of ChatGPT in November 2022, the issue of **[AI snake oil](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691249131/ai-snake-oil)**, where AI-enabled tools claim overstated capabilities or exhibit poor performance, has become significantly more prominent. Given the challenges of AI in exacerbating bias, along with its rapid adoption that has led to a lack of robust governance frameworks, verifying AI functionality becomes even more crucial within African contexts.

While there are many ways to increase transparency in the African AI landscape, this approach only works if researchers and developers **first** release AI models open-source (not open-weight — here’s a **[good explanation](https://opensource.org/ai/open-weights)** of the difference) and **second**, publish information detailing model performance through standard machine learning evaluation metrics and benchmarking. While red teaming is still relatively early in implementation across the African AI landscape, conducting these exercises on AI models would also significantly move the needle towards improving the science of AI safety in Africa. While African AI startups can publish model information and performance independently (e.g., on their websites, Hugging Face, ArXiv), which many AI companies around the world do in practice, it is also essential that this information be published in peer-reviewed journals and conference venues.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ctd7!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4db45b01-c4c9-4820-a019-794fbc3f1f21_1500x200.png)

By going through the peer-review process and publishing results openly, AI startups in Africa can receive vital feedback that can help strengthen AI model development within their organization and also reveal key deficiencies in their respective business models. Prestigious conferences, such as NeurIPS, ICML, KDD, ACL, and ICLR, may be inaccessible at first, but startups can work their way up by submitting to workshops within these conferences. There are also AI research conferences in Africa, like **[Deep Learning Indaba](https://deeplearningindaba.com/2025/)**, **[Pan Africon AI](https://panafriconai.org/)**, and the **[Southern African Conference for Artificial Intelligence Research](https://sacair.org.za/)**, which are excellent alternatives.

Attending these conferences (even without publishing) can also be a valuable way for AI startups in Africa to market their products, connect with leading researchers, forge new business partnerships, and potentially increase investment opportunities. An example of an African AI startup that has performed well is **[Intron Health](https://www.intron.io/)**, a startup focused on enhancing speech recognition for African accents to develop AI applications across various domains. They have recently **[published](https://www.intron.io/research/)** papers at EMNLP, Interspeech, and a workshop at ICLR.

While I have many perspectives on how African academic institutions can increase their AI research output, I find that actively publishing in academic venues remains relatively niche for AI startups operating in Africa. I also acknowledge that publishing academic research can be very expensive and out of reach for researchers, especially those in Global Majority countries, where they must navigate hurdles including open-access fees, conference registration costs, and visa complications. This, however, may necessitate the need for new models of AI publishing across Africa and Global Majority countries that account for these nuances and support a thriving research ecosystem.
