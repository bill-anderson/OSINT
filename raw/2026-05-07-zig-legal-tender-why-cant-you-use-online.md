An opinion-analysis piece on why Zimbabwe's gold-backed ZiG currency, though legal tender, struggles in the digital economy. In the February 2026 Monetary Policy Statement, RBZ governor John Mushayavanhu raised ZIPIT and mobile money transaction limits from ZiG 8,000 to ZiG 13,000 per transaction (monthly cap ZiG 50,000) to promote ZiG usage, and introduced larger denomination notes (10, 20, 50, 100, 200 ZiG). Yet ZiG cards often fail at online checkout while USD Visa/Mastercards "breeze through."

The backbone of online payments runs through gateways such as Paynow, Pesapal and DPO Group. Paynow (the leading gateway) supports Visa, Mastercard, EcoCash and Zimswitch, and ZiG card payments should flow through Zimswitch — but in practice the experience is bumpier. Most local e-commerce operators do not configure ZiG card acceptance as default, setting up USD flows for "revenue certainty." Merchants paid in ZiG face settlement delays (Paynow settles local Visa/Mastercard T+2, foreign T+3), with no public breakdown for ZiG settlements — opacity that pushes merchants to default to USD. Many ZiG-denominated cards are not automatically enabled for online transactions, requiring separate activation, and some get routed to bank USSD prompts mid-checkout.

On fees, from April 1, 2026, POS charges are capped at 1.5% for local and international cards with no minimum, but the author stresses "a directive is not implementation" — the RBZ governor himself acknowledged limited enforcement capacity.
