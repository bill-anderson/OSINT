# Kofi Woods Leads National ID Card Initiative

Liberian Observer, July 9, 2025

The government is at the verge of resuscitating the issuance of the National Biometric Identification System (NBIS — the National ID Card), and President Joseph Nyuma Boakai has named his National Security Advisor, Atty. Samuel Koffi Woods, to lead the initiative. Per a presidential memo dated July 5, Woods will chair the steering committee overseeing implementation, scheduled to be rolled out on April 13, 2026.

The steering committee includes members from the Ministries of Internal Affairs, Foreign Affairs, and Post and Telecommunications, the Liberia Telecommunications Authority, the National Identification Registry (NIR), the Central Bank of Liberia (CBL), the National Elections Commission (NEC), the Senior Economic Advisor, and the Presidential Delivery Unit. The committee's main objectives are to develop and execute the biometric ID system to enhance national security, facilitate access to government services, and promote economic development, using biometric technology with offline verification capabilities.

The appointment follows delays in the rollout of Executive Order No. 147, which Boakai signed on April 14, 2025, making it mandatory for all Liberian citizens and foreign residents living in the country for 90 days or more to register for a biometric national ID or foreign ID card — required to access banking, telecommunications, healthcare, education, and immigration services. On June 16, 2025, the government extended the initial registration deadline from August 1 to August 31, 2025, with NIR Executive Director Andrew Peters citing high turnout and processing delays.

However, the government subsequently announced the suspension of National ID Card issuance and the enforcement of EO 147. The Ministry of Information, Cultural Affairs and Tourism (MICAT), with the CBL and NIR, cited "administrative reasons," instructing public social service providers to comply. The decision came amid public dissatisfaction with technical delays, long queues, and reports of irregularities at registration centers, especially in rural and remote areas. Commercial banks, mobile operators, and government service providers were instructed to relax requirements and accept alternative forms of identification.
