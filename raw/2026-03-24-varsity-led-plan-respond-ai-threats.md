Dar es Salaam. As the world accelerates into the age of artificial intelligence (AI), Tanzania is making a calculated bet: that its universities will produce the expertise needed not only to harness the technology, but also to manage its risks. At the centre of this strategy is a growing recognition by policymakers that technological progress comes with real threats, including job displacement, data privacy concerns and the misuse of digital tools.

The issue came to the fore during a visit by the Parliamentary Committee on Education, Culture and Sports to the University of Dar es Salaam's College of Information and Communication Technology (CoICT), where MPs sought to understand the role of these institutions in ensuring the benefits of AI are realised. Speaking during the visit, the Minister for Education, Science and Technology, Prof Adolf Mkenda, outlined both the promise and the risks. "Technology, especially AI, has many benefits for society and the economy. But it also brings challenges that we must prepare for early," he said.

Among the risks he highlighted is the growing use of AI to generate misleading content such as fake images and videos, though he stressed a deeper concern lies in the labour market. "This technology could lead to the loss of some jobs as machines take over tasks once done by humans. But it also creates new opportunities that require highly specialised skills," he acknowledged.

A digital policy analyst based in Dar es Salaam, Mr Juma Omar, noted: "The future of AI is not just about technology. It is about governance, ethics and security. Universities must now train students who understand all these dimensions." Experts say Tanzania's institutional experience gives it a strong foundation but caution that the scale of the AI challenge requires faster and more coordinated action.
