NIRA is pledging to strengthen privacy and security measures around Uganda's biometric national ID database after recent reports of a data breach. A BBC investigation claimed that data from the National Identification Registry (NIR) was shared illegally by some unscrupulous NIRA staff to a criminal ring. Local media (ChimpsReports) reported the alleged breach is linked to a sex-trafficking network with presence in Dubai.

NIRA said that while it awaits further evidence from the BBC, it has opened an investigation in collaboration with the Personal Data Protection Office and the Uganda Police Force. "Once the investigations are concluded, any persons found culpable will be subjected to both disciplinary action and criminal prosecution," the release said.

NIRA assured Ugandans its commitment to protecting the security, privacy and integrity of the NIR "remains unwavering," noting the register "plays a critical role in service delivery, financial inclusion, and national security." Safeguards cited include the Registration of Persons Act and the Data Protection and Privacy Act, plus technical and institutional measures; staff meetings begin with a data-protection recap.

Privacy advocates such as Unwanted Witness expressed deep concern and called for "fully independent investigations." Ugandan authorities have also faced accusations of using the national ID system as a surveillance tool. Separately, the nationwide ID drive has seen more than 10 million ID cards renewed (up from 5 million in July), with government pushing birth registration — a prerequisite for ID issuance — via on-site registration at health facilities.
