Safaricom has strengthened its position in Kenya's telecommunications sector, expanding its fixed internet market share to 35.5 per cent and approaching the one-million subscriber milestone. According to the Communications Authority of Kenya's (CA) sector statistics report for the third quarter of the 2025/2026 financial year, the telco added more than 83,000 fixed broadband customers between December 2025 and March 2026.

This pushed its total subscriber base to 941,501, up from a 34.9 per cent market share in the previous quarter.

The growth widens the gap between Safaricom and its primary terrestrial competitors. Jamii Telecommunications Limited (Faiba) currently holds 20.1 per cent of the market, followed by Wananchi Group (Zuku) at 11.1 per cent and Poa Internet at 10.7 per cent.

This rapid expansion is the result of a deliberate structural shift. Safaricom Group CEO Peter Ndegwa recently noted that the company realized it had primarily been serving high-income, well-organized estates, leaving a significant segment of the market unexploited. By achieving a 50 per cent reduction in fixed broadband installation costs, Safaricom has overhauled its deployment model.

This infrastructure cost reduction has directly enabled initiatives like WiFi Bamba, which brings affordable home fibre connectivity to lower-income communities, including Nairobi's Mukuru Affordable Housing Project.

The company is also rethinking how customers access and pay for data. Safaricom is currently piloting tokenisation models that allow users in fibre-ready zones to purchase short-term, high-speed access on demand. Furthermore, the company recently retired its legacy 5 Mbps entry-level tier, which leadership noted no longer met modern digital demands, and established 15 Mbps as the new baseline at no additional cost to existing customers.

This strategic upgrade aligns with broader national trends; the CA report highlights that the majority of fixed internet subscriptions in Kenya now fall within the 10 to 30 Mbps band due to its optimal balance of reliability and affordability.

Despite the rapid adoption of terrestrial fibre, the overall national market remains largely untapped. While more than eight million Kenyan households are connected to the national electricity grid, total fixed internet subscriptions currently stand at just 2.66 million.

As terrestrial networks push into new neighbourhoods, the competitive landscape is simultaneously expanding into orbit. The latest CA data reveals that Elon Musk's Starlink has officially broken into Kenya's top ten fixed internet providers. Driven by a surge in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite adoption, the satellite internet segment recorded an 11.4 per cent quarterly growth, allowing Starlink to capture a 0.9 per cent market share with nearly 25,000 active subscriptions.

To support the surging data demands across both fibre and satellite platforms, Kenya's underlying infrastructure is expanding. The country's international internet bandwidth grew by 16.4 per cent to 28,130.3 Gbps during the quarter, largely propelled by submarine cable operator SEACOM expanding its capacity by 53.3 per cent to 10,500 Gbps. Consequently, total utilized bandwidth in the country increased by 3 per cent to 17,758.8 Gbps.

For Safaricom, the fixed broadband push is proving highly lucrative. In its financial year ending March 2026, the segment saw a 32 per cent surge in subscribers and a 12 per cent increase in revenue. Supported by hardware partnerships with Huawei for Fibre-to-the-Home (FTTH) solutions, and anchored by a dominant 89.1 per cent market share in the mobile money sector via M-Pesa, Safaricom is steadily turning its fixed internet infrastructure into a central pillar of its digital ecosystem.
