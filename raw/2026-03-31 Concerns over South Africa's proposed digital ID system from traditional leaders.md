South Africa’s plans to introduce a digital ID system are being positioned as a step towards modernising identity management, but the proposal is already facing resistance, particularly from traditional leaders who say key questions remain unanswered.

Leaders affiliated with Amadumisa, under the banner of the United Kingdoms of South Africa (UCoSA), have raised concerns about the impact such a system could have on ordinary people, especially those in rural and under-resourced communities.

They argue that the government should consider holding a referendum before rolling out the digital ID, to give citizens a direct say. Nkosi Ncedisizwe Welani said Amadumisa's primary concern is to protect communities from potential fraud and risks that could arise from a fully digital system.

“In a country like South Africa, where fraud is already a daily reality, we cannot ignore the risks that come with digitising identity. We are not rejecting the idea outright, but we believe people must be consulted first,” he said.

Mr Welani added that Amadumisa has been engaging traditional leaders across the country through UCoSA, with discussions now turning towards the possibility of a national referendum.

“We believe a referendum will allow the voices of ordinary people to be heard. It should not be something imposed on communities, especially when there are still gaps and uncertainties. Yes, we have seen countries like China where digital IDs are working, but our local realities are very different,” he said.

Traditional leaders also raised accessibility concerns, warning that a digital system could exclude those without smartphones or reliable internet access.

“There are people in deep rural areas who cannot afford smartphones. If alternative measures like microchips are introduced, as some have suggested, that raises serious privacy and even health concerns. Our people need clarity on these issues,” said Mr Welani.

He stressed that without proper education and consultation, the system could disadvantage the most vulnerable.

“These are communities that are already struggling. Many do not fully understand what digital ID means, and yet they will be expected to adapt to it,” he said.

The leaders said they are preparing to formally engage the government, particularly the Department of Home Affairs, to seek clarity and ensure communities are properly informed.

The department has, in recent months, indicated that it is moving towards a future built on digital identity, including a more integrated population register and stronger civil registration systems - a direction that aligns with global trends where identity systems are increasingly seen as critical infrastructure.

However, Chief Mzwandile Aaah! Zanoxolo Mfazwe said the process must begin with education and consultation.

“People need to understand what a digital ID is and how it will affect them before anything is implemented. As traditional leaders, we also want to engage the government directly before taking the matter back to our communities,” he said.

For now, the call from traditional leaders is clear: No rollout without proper consultation, and no system imposed without the voices of the people being heard.
