*The $4.5m minors-enrolment budget is flagged by the article itself as apparently SEPARATE from the UNICEF pilot — preserve that hedge.*

Tanzania's National Identification Authority (NIDA) says it is working out how to implement an infant biometric registration system for children below the age of five, with a pilot expected in July.

Edson Guyai, director of ID Management at NIDA, disclosed the plan at the ID4Africa 2025 AGM. He said the pilot has UNICEF support and is an integral part of the new Universal ID or Jamii Number digital ID project, which prioritises registration of citizens from birth. "Upon birth, a child will receive both a birth certificate and a National ID number and document," Guyai said.

Because of challenges in finding the right biometric solution for under-five children, NIDA opted for the interim measure of linking the child's identity to the mother's National ID. "Starting in July, we will launch a pilot program for infant biometric registration, in collaboration with UNICEF, in selected regions. We are also upgrading ICT infrastructure, reforming our registration processes, and reviewing our legal frameworks to align with these new goals," Guyai said. The country has also budgeted $4.5 million for a seemingly separate pilot focused on enrolling minors for legal identity.

On overall coverage: "We have successfully registered over 25 million individuals. According to our legal framework, the eligible age for registration is 18 years and above. Based on the 2022 national census, the total population aged 18 and above stands at over 31.5 million. This means we've covered about 81 percent of the eligible adult population." NIDA has personalised over 21.2 million IDs and issued more than 20.1 million cards. Registration extends to citizens, legal residents, refugees and stateless persons within the country's borders.

On the identity ecosystem: "So far, we have integrated with over 124 institutions, both public and private. Through ID verification services, we've generated approximately $45.4 million. We charge $0.18 per transaction and additional fees for card replacement and legal resident registration."

Guyai said the Jamii Number aligns with national policies for digital inclusion and economic growth, supports regional and global integration, enhances security and advances social protection programmes. Challenges include legal constraints, financial shortfalls, accessibility due to geography, and a lack of foundational documents. NIDA credits progress to political commitment, sustainable financing models, stakeholder engagement, and use cases such as SIM card registration, which he said boosted ID registration by about 25 percent.
