# Econet AI is here, and it's more than those Yamurai ads we've been seeing

Written by Leonard Sengere, 17 April 2026

Econet has officially launched something called Econet AI.

Now, if your first reaction is: "Ah, more of those AI things they've been pushing… Yamurai, EcoChat and all that…" You're not alone.

They've been talking about AI for a while now, mostly through assistants and chatbots that, let's be honest, haven't exactly taken over people's lives.

So it's easy to hear "Econet AI" and assume this is just more of the same.

But from what was shared at the launch, this looks like them trying to do something a bit bigger.

## So what is Econet AI actually?

The simplest way to think about it is this: Econet wants to move from just selling connectivity to helping businesses actually use AI.

That includes:

- tools like Yamurai AI
- business solutions like OmniContact AI
- and services where they help companies figure out how AI fits into their operations

So it's not just apps. It's tools + consulting + platforms.

They even talked about working directly with CEOs and leadership teams, not just IT departments.

## The part that actually matters: AI compute

This is where things get interesting.

One of the big points they made at the launch was simple: If you don't have AI compute, you can't really build AI.

And according to Econet AI, they now have access to that compute through Cassava Technologies.

If you've been following, Cassava has been making big moves in AI:

- talking about an AI factory
- rolling out GPU infrastructure
- building partnerships across Africa

So hearing that Econet AI can tap into that is a big deal.

## But is that infrastructure in Zimbabwe?

From what we understand so far:

- Econet AI does have access to AI compute
- but it's not clear that this compute is physically in Zimbabwe

In other words, you can use the power but the machine might be somewhere else. That's still useful.

It means businesses here can build and run AI solutions without waiting for massive local infrastructure to be built.

But it's not quite the same as having huge GPU clusters sitting in a data centre in Harare. At least not yet.

(And to be fair, they did talk about a commitment to bring more of that capability into Zimbabwe over time.)

## What accessing that kind of power looks like today

To see why this matters, it helps to look at what things are like right now.

We've heard from local developers trying to access high-performance computing resources for AI work, and the costs can run into tens of thousands of dollars. Yes, I'm talking about the High Performance Computer at the University of Zimbabwe.

Not even for something crazy, just to get a little access.

And it's not always flexible either. Instead of something like pay-as-you-go, you're often quoted for large chunks of compute.

For a student or small developer, that basically meant we had no HPC at the UZ.

So when Econet AI talks about giving access to AI compute through Cassava, that's where it starts to make sense.

## What else are they bringing?

There were also mentions of:

- data centres and cloud services
- a sovereign cloud for government and institutions
- and work on local language AI, including making Yamurai sound more Zimbabwean

That localisation angle is actually interesting.

Global AI tools don't always get local languages and context right. Try speaking to Gemini or ChatGPT in Shona, their accents are hilarious, they sound Chinese for some reason.

## So where does this leave Zimbabwe?

If you've been following Cassava's AI plans, it has felt like most of the serious infrastructure is happening elsewhere:

- South Africa
- Kenya
- Nigeria

Which made it seem like Zimbabwe might be getting left behind.

Econet AI changes that picture a bit.

We may not have massive AI infrastructure sitting locally yet, but through Econet AI, there's now a simple way to actually use that infrastructure.

You can't complain about that.
