## Verdict: RESOLVED on scope and dates — the two claims describe two sequential phases of one programme, and Claim B's headline is wrong. INCONCLUSIVE on the size of the alternative-identification cohort (no national figure published).

There is no genuine conflict between the sources; there is a conflict between Claim B's **headline** and Claim B's own **body**, and the body is correct. The brief's own assessment ("one slipped-and-widened programme, B's headline overreaches B's body") was right, and the primaries now settle it.

**Both start dates happened; they are different measures.** The 5 May 2025 measure was not cancelled and did not slip into September. It commenced and was actively enforced: on 30 April 2025 SASSA confirmed that May payments would be delayed for beneficiaries "registered using alternative forms of identification rather than the standard 13-digit South African ID number," who had to present at offices or face suspension and eventual lapsing (Letsatsi, 2025-04-30). What slipped was a *separate, wider* rollout — the Beneficiary Biometric Enrolment (BBE) programme, planned for the start of FY2025/26 (April 2025), delayed by union negotiations, piloted from the start of that financial year, and launched nationally on 1 September 2025. Matlou's "we hit a snag" refers to the BBE programme, not to the 5 May alternative-ID measure. The brief's inference that "the May date slipped" is therefore incorrect.

**Existing 13-digit-ID recipients are still not required to enrol merely by being beneficiaries.** Claim A's carve-out survives 1 September 2025 intact, and it was restated on the record the day before the launch. Acting CEO Brenton van Vrede told the Portfolio Committee on Social Development on 27 August 2025: *"We are not saying everyone must come in and be biometrically enrolled. We want to make sure that every new grant application from the first of September has biometric enrolments."* The Minister's own written reply to Parliament a year later characterises the 1 September 2025 event as "the implementation of compulsory biometric enrolment **for new applicants**" (NW1941, 2026-05-18). What changed on 1 September is that the *new-application* trigger stopped depending on ID type — from that date every new application needs biometrics, not only those using alternative identification.

**"Updated grant applications" means a triggered event, not a standing obligation.** Three consistent primaries define the covered population as new applicants + beneficiaries in a review cycle + beneficiaries changing personal details. Matlou to the Portfolio Committee, 17 September 2025: the enrolment figure "includes new applicants, people undergoing reviews and those coming to SASSA offices to change their personal details." An existing recipient on a 13-digit ID who neither applies afresh, nor is called for review, nor changes their details, is not required to enrol.

**But the carve-out is narrowing, and the review channel is a wide door.** Two things qualify the reassurance. First, stated intent: SASSA acting CIO Jabulani Makondo told Parliament on 18 June 2025 that "the bigger plan is to make biometrics compulsory for all applicants, whether you are trying to access the programme with or without identification." Second, scale of the review trigger: in Q3 FY2025/26 SASSA income-tested approximately 6 million Child Support Grant recipients, and 67,868 grants were suspended and 11,213 lapsed in that quarter for failure to verify (NW1941). "Only on review" is not a small exception when reviews are run at that volume. Even so, the aggregate refutes "all beneficiaries": 997,379 beneficiaries were biometrically enrolled at local offices between September 2025 and 11 March 2026 — roughly 5% of the 19,049,235 grant recipients recorded as at end-January 2026 (NW1941, 2026-05-18; recipient total from reply NW641).

**Cohort size — genuine provenance gap.** No national count of beneficiaries on alternative identification could be found in any primary. The only located figure is provincial and five years stale: 5,050 recipients using SASSA-generated "quad 7" numbers in Gauteng (SASSA Gauteng spokesperson Nandi Mosia, 2021-10). Question 4 of the brief cannot be answered from the public record as it stands.

**Correction to the brief's framing of the litigation.** The brief states the scope question "is precisely what is being litigated at the Supreme Court of Appeal." That does not hold. The SCA matter (hearing set for 25 August 2026) is the government's appeal in *Institute for Economic Justice and Another v Minister of Social Development and Others* [2025] ZAGPPHC 29 (23 January 2025), which concerns SRD-grant exclusion barriers — online-only application, bank verification, government-database checks, the means-test threshold and the budget cap. It is not a challenge to the biometric enrolment programme, which post-dates the High Court judgment. The two are thematically adjacent (both about verification-driven exclusion) but legally distinct.
