# Research: Nigeria's 2008 market loss in dollars, NGX market cap, institutional trading volume

## Verdict: RESOLVED on all three claims — and Claim 1 was misframed: ₦6.96tn is not the loss, it is the end-December-2008 closing market capitalisation. Neither $55.03bn nor $5.03bn is correct, because both convert the wrong quantity.

The brief asked which of two dollar conversions of "₦6.96 trillion lost" was right. The
contemporaneous CBN source shows the premise is wrong. ₦6.96tn is a **stock** (the closing value of
the market) that TechCabal has reported as a **flow** (the loss). The actual March-to-December 2008
loss was **₦5.55 trillion**. This is a stock/flow conflation, not a dropped digit — and it is why the
wiki's decision to compile naira figures only and hold the dollar values was the right one, though for
a different reason than assumed: the naira figure itself was mislabelled.

## Claim 1 — the 2008 crash loss (material)

**Source of record:** A. A. Sere-Ejembi (Assistant Director, Statistics Department, Central Bank of
Nigeria), "Nigerian Stock Market Reflection of The Global Finance Crisis: An Evaluation", *CBN
Bullion* Vol. 32 No. 4, December 2008. This is a **contemporaneous CBN publication**, written as the
crash was still unfolding, drawing on the monthly bulletins of the **SEC**, the **NSE** and the CBN's
Trade and Exchange Department — precisely the regulator/exchange provenance the brief called for.

Its market-capitalisation findings, verbatim:

> Similar to the movement observed in the ASI, Market Capitalization at the NSE peaked at N12.50
> trillion in February 2008 (Figure 3). The trend maintained a reversal from March till the market
> closed at N6.96 trillion at end-December 2008.
>
> Between March 2008 and the end of year, the market has lost N5.55 trillion in market capitalization,
> amounting to 44.4 per cent.

So:

| Quantity | Value | As of |
|---|---|---|
| Peak market capitalisation | ₦12.50tn | February 2008 |
| Closing market capitalisation | **₦6.96tn** | 31 December 2008 |
| **Loss, March → end-December 2008** | **₦5.55tn (−44.4%)** | 31 December 2008 |

₦12.50tn − ₦6.96tn = ₦5.54tn, internally consistent with the stated ₦5.55tn. The ₦6.96tn figure that
TechCabal presents as the loss is the **closing market cap** — the same number, in the same role, in a
second CBN-hosted source, which paraphrases then-Governor Sanusi: *"equity market capitalization
declined by 32.4 per cent from N10.3 trillion at year-end [2007] to N6.96 trillion at the close of
2008."*

**Corroboration of the loss figure's order of magnitude** (secondary, not relied on): Punch (2019)
and multiple academic accounts repeat "investors lost about N6.96tn", showing the mislabelling is
**not TechCabal's invention** — it is a long-propagated error in Nigerian financial press and academic
literature, in which the closing market cap has been recycled for over a decade as the loss. This is
exactly the failure mode the brief anticipated ("the kind of round number that propagates uncorrected
for years once repeated"), and it is worse than suspected: the propagated number is not merely
mis-converted but mis-defined.

Note the peak figure itself is not perfectly settled across sources — CBN Bullion says ₦12.50tn
(February 2008); other accounts cite ₦12.6tn or an all-time-high ₦13.5tn in March 2008, and the loss
measured to the January/March 2009 trough (₦4.5–4.6tn) is correspondingly larger (~₦8tn). The wiki
should therefore state the **window** with the figure, not a bare "2008 crash loss".

### Dollar conversion

Both TechCabal values are rejected. Converting the **correct** naira loss:

| Basis | Rate | ₦5.55tn ≈ |
|---|---|---|
| End-Dec 2008 official rate | ~₦135.4/$ | ~$41.0bn |
| 2008 period-average official rate (World Bank) | ~₦118.6/$ | ~$46.8bn |

The article's stated "then exchange rate of ₦126.48/$" matches neither the end-2008 nor the 2008
average official rate cleanly (it sits between them, near the 2007 average of ~₦125.8). **Recommendation:
the wiki should carry the naira figure and not a dollar figure at all.** A loss measured across a
nine-month window during which the naira moved from ~₦117/$ to ~₦135/$ has no single honest dollar
value, and any figure chosen would be a rate-selection artefact. If a dollar figure is ever needed,
state the rate and date inline.

## Claim 2 — NGX market capitalisation ($113.79bn vs $113.02bn)

**Not a factual dispute.** Both are conversions of the same naira market capitalisation at slightly
different FX rates and/or capture moments. NGX's own weekly market report puts equities market
capitalisation at **₦157.057 trillion as at Friday 17 July 2026** (ASI 243,462.13), the trading week
immediately before TechCabal published on 18 July.

- ₦157.057tn ÷ $113.79bn implies ₦1,380.2/$
- ₦157.057tn ÷ $113.02bn implies ₦1,389.6/$

Both implied rates are plausible naira quotes days apart; the ~0.7% gap is FX drift, not a
market-cap disagreement. The prose value and the widget value are reconcilable and neither is "wrong".

**Reconciled reading:** carry **₦157.057tn as at 17 July 2026**; a dollar figure is ~$113–114bn at
~₦1,380–1,390/$, and should be written with its rate if used at all.

## Claim 3 — institutional trading volume ($4.06bn)

**Settled directly by the primary, and TechCabal is wrong.** The NGX *Domestic & Foreign Portfolio
Investment Report* for May 2026 gives the **2026 year-to-date (January–May)** figures:

- Domestic **retail**: ₦2.85974 trillion
- Domestic **institutional**: ₦4.06178 trillion

These match the article's ₦2.86tn and ₦4.06tn exactly, confirming both the source and that these are
**YTD cumulative** figures, not May-month figures (May alone: retail ₦0.72627tn, institutional
₦1.03337tn — a distinction the article does not make and the wiki should).

The report states its own conversion rate: **₦1,373.25 to $1 (NAFEM), as at 31 May 2026**. At that rate:

| Figure | Naira | Correct USD @ ₦1,373.25/$ | TechCabal said |
|---|---|---|---|
| Retail YTD | ₦2.85974tn | **$2.08bn** | $2.07bn ✓ (implies ~₦1,382/$) |
| Institutional YTD | ₦4.06178tn | **$2.96bn** | $4.06bn ✗ |

Confirmed: the institutional dollar value is the **naira figure carried into the dollar column** — the
hypothesis in the brief. The retail conversion is sound.
