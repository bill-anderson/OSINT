# Sudan: AML/CTF and data-protection legal framework

## AML/CTF — established, from the announcer of record

**Anti-Money Laundering / Combating the Financing of Terrorism Law of 2014.** Passed by the National
Assembly in its 26th sitting of the 9th cycle on **18 June 2014** (20 Sha'ban 1435 AH), signed by the
President, in force from the date of signature. Repealed the AML/CFT Law of 2010, with regulations
made under the 2010 law saved until repealed or amended.

- Arabic text on the Central Bank of Sudan's own site: https://cbos.gov.sd/node/480
- English translation PDF: https://alsalama.sd/home/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/Sudan-AMLCFT-Law-english.pdf
- UN ESCWA Arab Legal Portal entry (June 2014): https://alp.unescwa.org/legislations/anti-money-laundering-and-counter-terrorism-financing-law

**Structure of the regime** (from the statute's own section headings): control and supervision bodies
(s.4); obligations of financial and non-financial institutions; the **Financial Information/Inquiries
Unit** (the FIU, established at the central bank); the **National Committee for Combating Money
Laundering and Terrorism Financing**, a body corporate seated in Khartoum state, under the President's
supervision and chaired by the deputy minister of justice (s.21, s.23–29); offences of money laundering
(s.35) and terrorism financing (s.36); confiscation; General Criminal Court jurisdiction. Power to make
implementing regulations is given to the Minister, the National Committee, the Unit, the supervision
bodies, the prosecutor general and customs, each in its own field.

**Implementing directive, central bank.** **Circular No. 8/2014** — "Regulatory and Supervisory
requirements for the Institutions under the Supervision of Central Bank of Sudan on AML/CFT", made under
Article 44 of the 2014 Law and Article 8-2 of the Banking Business Act 2004; cancelled and replaced
Circular 2/2014. Requires risk assessment updated bi-annually, CDD, and application to local and foreign
branches and majority-owned (≥50%) subsidiaries, stricter-of-the-two rule where host-country rules differ.
https://cbos.gov.sd/en/content/circular-no-82014-regulatory-and-supervisory-requirements-institutions-under-supervision

**Predecessor instruments** also on the CBoS site: AML/CFT Act 2010 (from the Money Laundering and
Terrorism Financing Bill 2009, a Provisional Order), which repealed the Money Laundering (Combating)
Act 2004. CBoS's own "Sudan Efforts on AML/CFT" report lists the surrounding statutes: Criminal Act 1991,
Counter Terrorism Act 2001, the 2014 Act, Human Trafficking Act 2014.

**One date discrepancy, resolved in favour of the primary.** A 2025 academic paper
(https://doi.org/10.22495/clgrv7i3p6) states the Act "shall come into effect as of May 16, 2014". The
statute's own attestation clause and the Eversheds Sutherland guide both give **18 June 2014**
(promulgation/signature). The primary text governs; the May date appears to be an error.

**So: the parked KYC lead's claim was correct.** Sudan does have an AML/CTF Act of 2014, and the central
bank and the FIU do issue directives under it. It is now sourceable from the announcer of record.

## Data protection — the finding is an absence

**Sudan has no comprehensive personal-data-protection statute and no data-protection authority.**
Multiple independent lines converge and none contradicts:

- ALT Advisory's Data Protection Africa factsheet: "NO DATA PROTECTION LAW / DATA PROTECTION AUTHORITY
  NOT APPOINTED" — https://dataprotection.africa/sudan/ (page dated 2022-05-23)
- Global Voices Advox, Mohamed Suliman, 2019-11-05: "Sudan has no data protection authority and no data
  protection law that regulates the collection, storage and use of personal data by governments and the
  private sector."
  https://advox.globalvoices.org/2019/11/05/the-right-to-privacy-in-sudan-a-call-to-enact-a-data-protection-act/
- Teresia Munywoki, "Privacy in Displacement: Data Protection for Refugees in Sudan", JIPIT 4:1 (2024),
  https://doi.org/10.52907/jipit.v4i1.505 — "it lacks a comprehensive data protection legislation";
  recommends enactment of a law and establishment of a DPA.
- Konrad-Adenauer-Stiftung comparative study of North African data-protection law: Libya and Sudan have
  not issued data-protection regulations, while Algeria, Egypt, Mauritania, Morocco and Tunisia have
  comprehensive laws. https://www.kas.de/documents/265308/0/Data+Protection+Laws+Northern+Africa.pdf

**What exists instead — a sectoral patchwork:**
- Constitutional privacy guarantee: Article 54 (some sources cite 55) of the 2019 Constitutional Charter
  for the Transitional Period.
- **Electronic Transactions Act 2007** — s.9 confidentiality of e-signature data and information given to
  certification bodies; duty on processing-system providers to protect information; s.28 penalties for
  unauthorised disclosure. https://cbos.gov.sd/sites/default/files/Electronic_Transactions_act_2007.pdf
- **Informatic Offences (Combating) Act 2007** — unauthorised entry to sites/systems with intent to
  disclose or alter personal data; interception offences.
  https://cbos.gov.sd/en/content/informatic-offences-combating-act-2007
- **Telecommunications and Postal Regulation Act 2018**, Article 74 — permits interception and
  surveillance on order of a prosecutor or specialised judge.
- Cybercrime amendments: 2020 (Law No. 14 of 2020, in the Miscellaneous Amendments package —
  https://redress.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/2-Sudan-Amendments-July-2020-English-REDRESS-translation.pdf),
  2022, and a **2025 amendment approved by the Council of Ministers in October 2025** (reported
  https://asdaasudania.com/13/65846/).
- A **draft Personal Data Protection Bill (c. 2018)** was never enacted. A draft Arabic bill is held on
  CYRILLA (https://cyrilla.org/entity/87twtuqb2lm) — but caution: the fetched table of contents refers to
  "مصالح الحكومة اللبنانية" (the Lebanese government's interests) at Article 27, so that particular file
  appears to be a Lebanese draft, possibly mis-filed or used as a template. **Not verified as Sudanese.**

**Regional instrument.** Sudan **signed** the AU Convention on Cyber Security and Personal Data Protection
(Malabo) on **15 March 2023** and has **not ratified or deposited** it (AU status list, February 2026).

**Institutional development that is not a law.** The 5 November 2025 prime-ministerial decree created the
**Sudanese Data and AI Authority** (هيئة البيانات والذكاء الاصطناعي) alongside the Digital Transformation
Authority and the Cybersecurity Authority. Its remit is described as regulating national data management
and governance — but it is a **government data-management body under a ministry, not an independent
data-protection supervisory authority**, and it was created by decree, not under a data-protection statute.
The wiki already holds this (2025-11-07 source). A cybersecurity law was also stated to have been passed
("إجازة قانون الأمن السيبراني", minister, 31 Jan 2026) — text not located.

**Contemporary Sudanese commentary confirms the gap is live and salient.** Al-Taghyeer / Sudan Akhbar,
2026-07-03, "بلا قانون يحميها.. بياناتنا المصرفية في مهبّ محوّلات خاصة" — argues explicitly that the
central bank imposing data-protection conditions in a licence is categorically different from a national
data-protection statute giving citizens justiciable rights, and that Sudan has the former and not the
latter. https://www.sudanakhbar.com/1806715
