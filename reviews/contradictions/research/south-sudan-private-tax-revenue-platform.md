# South Sudan's privately-operated revenue platform: identified

The platform has a name, a contract, a date, a fee structure and a UN investigative record. It is
**Crawford Capital Ltd**, and the primary of record is the **UN Commission on Human Rights in South Sudan,
"Plundering a Nation", A/HRC/60/CRP.5, 16 September 2025** — which devotes a titled section to it
("Collections outsourced to Crawford Capital Ltd.", p.48).

## What it is — established

- **Legal name:** Crawford Capital Ltd. **UK-registered.** Operational/payments arm: **CapitalPay**
  (also UK-registered as Capital Pay Ltd and Capital Pay Software Solutions Ltd).
- **Contract:** **November 2019**, with the **Ministry of Information, Communication Technology and Postal
  Services** (then minister **Michael Makuei Lueth**), as **exclusive provider of e-Government Services**.
- **Procurement:** **no competitive tender** on the public record, despite the Public Procurement and
  Disposal of Assets Act 2018. The UN Commission found the procurement was endorsed by key ministries and
  by the **National Security Service's Internal Security Bureau** — DG at the time **Akol Koor Kuc** wrote
  personally in support, proposing Crawford work with the intelligence service's ICT department because the
  company was creating large databases and integrating online services. **This is the data-governance
  centre of gravity: the security service sponsored the contract on database grounds.**
- **Scope:** trade permits, visas/e-visas, tax payments, customs clearance, work permits, and the
  **Electronic Crude Oil Accreditation Permit** — the digital gateway every barrel of exported crude passes
  through.
- **Terms (UN Commission finding):** under the 2019 agreement Crawford **retains a 75 per cent profit share
  of taxes it collects**; the remaining 25 per cent goes to the Ministry. Extended in **2024** to an
  **unlawful levy on tax-exempt humanitarian agencies**, which forced suspension of critical food aid.
  A separate 2023 stream: a **0.3 per cent of cargo value** electronic crude-oil accreditation fee, split
  between Crawford and the Ministry — the Commission estimated Crawford's share at **US$9.6m–11.5m annually**.
- **Where the money sits (the governance crux):** the Commission found the SSRA "complicit in allowing
  representatives of Crawford to **collect and hold revenues in company bank accounts**, diverting further
  income from government budgets" — i.e. **public revenue held outside the national treasury**. It also
  found collectors profit from the parallel exchange-rate gap (collected in USD, reported in SSP).
  **Crawford denies this** (below).
- **SSRA's own conduct:** the Authority withholds a percentage of collections for operating expenses — a
  practice due to end by 2023 and never to exceed 2 per cent; by 2024–25 it was withholding **14.5 per
  cent**, with evidence of irregular withdrawals from retention accounts as recently as January 2025.
  *(This detail appears in secondary investigative reporting summarising the UN report; verify against the
  full PDF before putting a figure on a page.)*
- **UN legal characterisation:** SSRA complicity "in clear violation of articles 9 and 10 of the **UN
  Convention against Corruption**".

## Crawford's rebuttal — dated, and it must be carried

Press statement **22 June 2026** (via Sudans Post): the 75/25 split "does not apply to government revenue
itself, but only to a separate convenience fee charged on selected digital services"; the company says it
earns a **2 per cent "platform service fee"**; "Government revenue remains Government revenue"; funds sit in
**regulated trust or collection accounts** under South Sudan's **Electronic Money Regulation 2017**, subject
to reconciliation, audit and central bank oversight, "not private operating accounts". It claims monthly
non-oil revenue rose from ~**US$648,000 (2020)** to ~**US$28m (2025)**. It **did not address the oil-sector
allegations**. The statement was issued by an **unnamed CEO** — itself notable.

## Government's position — dated

**14 May 2026**: separate statements from the **SSRA** (Office of the Commissioner General, Juba) and the
**Ministry of ICT and Postal Services** defending the arrangement as lawful, transparent, and endorsed by
the **Council of Ministers**. SSRA claims digital systems generate ~**130bn SSP monthly**, ~**1 trillion SSP
over eight months**, peaking **April 2026**. *(Caution: SSP figures are near-meaningless for comparison
given the parallel-rate gap and monetary financing — do not put them on a page as a performance measure
without that caveat.)* Neither statement acknowledged the 75/25 split, the humanitarian levies, or the
private-account finding.

## The Adut Salva Kiir Mayardit link — NOT established by the primary

The wiki's existing claim associates the platform with **Adut Salva Kiir Mayardit**, the president's
daughter. **The UN Commission's published summary does not name her.** It says Crawford "is owned and run by
family members of national political elites" and locates the on-paper ownership in the full report.
Secondary investigative reporting (Kenya Insights 2026-05-27; Amnons 2026-06-13) names:
majority shareholder **Garang Mayom Kuoc Malek (68%)**, son of a former deputy minister; **Jeremy Gisemba**,
a Kenyan businessman (26%); founding equity among children of political families; co-founders previously
formed **Air Afrik Aviation Ltd** with the president's son **Mayar Salva Kiir** (2013). Those outlets
describe the structure as "Adut Salva Kiir's private treasury" — **characterisation, not documented
shareholding.**

**Recommended handling:** state the ownership the primary supports ("owned and run by family members of
national political elites"; named shareholders per investigative reporting, attributed), and mark the
specific Adut Salva Kiir attribution **as reported but not established by the UN Commission's published
findings**. The full A/HRC/60/CRP.5 PDF documents the on-paper ownership structure and would settle it —
see acquisitions.

## Related but distinct — do not conflate

**SSRA + NCA + Ensoft/N-Soft**, signed **8–9 October 2025** in Juba: a **six-year Build-Operate-Transfer**
agreement for **telecom revenue assurance** — real-time visibility of telecom sector revenue. Vendor takes
**3.56 per cent** of revenue collected through the system; **96.44 per cent** to government (SSRA + NCA);
NCA hosts and oversees; full transfer to government after six years; no fixed contract value. Signed by
SSRA Commissioner-General **Simon Akuei Deng**, NCA DG **Gieth Kon Mathiang**, and the vendor's VP of Sales
(**David Meseguer**, per One Citizen Daily). **This is a different platform, a different sector and
radically different terms** (3.56% + BOT transfer vs 75% + no transfer) — the contrast is itself the
analytical point. **Vendor name unresolved: Eye Radio says "Ensoft", One Citizen Daily says "N-Soft".**

Also note the SSRA appears under two names in sources — **South Sudan Revenue Authority (SSRA)** and
**National Revenue Authority (NRA)** (its eTax portal is at etax.nra.gov.ss). Commissioner-General is given
as **Simon Akuei Deng** (Oct 2025) and **William Anyuon Kuol** (Jan 2026, per the wiki) — a leadership
change or a naming inconsistency worth pinning.
