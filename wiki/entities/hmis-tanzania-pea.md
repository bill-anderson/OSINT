# A political economy analysis of strengthening health information system in Tanzania

Peer-reviewed article by **Kuwawenaruwa et al.**, *BMC Medical Informatics and Decision
Making* **23:245 (2023)**. A political-economy study of Tanzania's health information system
(HIS) based on **17 key-informant interviews** conducted **April–May 2022**. Cited here as
literature; a dated fact drawn from it becomes a source citing this page. Part of the wiki's
HMIS/[[dhis2]] evidence cluster (see [[dpi.mis]]).

## Argument

- Tanzania's HMIS ecosystem is highly **fragmented** — some **18 separate systems** in
  operation — creating interoperability and duplication problems.
- **[[dhis2]]** was rolled out to **all district councils in 2013** and now carries **>90%
  of routine data flows**, the de-facto backbone of the national HIS.
- **Power mapping:** the **government** is the dominant actor, but **long-relationship
  donors** are the most influential — notably via the pooled **Health Basket Fund** — a
  dependence with clear sovereignty implications ([[sovereignty]]).
- **Barriers to interoperability** ([[dpi.exchange]], [[gov.standards]]): weak staff
  capacity, constrained financing, **closed-source lock-in**, weak political will, and
  variance in coding/standards across systems.
