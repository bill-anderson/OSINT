Rwanda's **National Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) Strategy** is a government framework for making the country's public digital systems interoperable — so that identity, payments and data-exchange layers function as shared public goods that any government or private actor can build on. It was unveiled at Rwanda's first **DPI Day** on 9 March 2026, organised by the [[rwanda-information-society-authority]] (RISA) with Access to Finance Rwanda and the Ministry of ICT and Innovation.

## What it mandates / addresses

The strategy responds to a specific legacy problem: after a decade of digital progress, many of Rwanda's platforms were built independently and cannot talk to one another. RISA CEO Antoine Sebera framed DPI as "roads and electricity for the digital economy" — a shared foundation letting services move faster across the whole country. The strategy is explicitly positioned as groundwork for AI: stronger data-exchange systems and interoperable platforms are meant to give AI access to larger volumes of reliable, high-quality data across sectors, supporting Rwanda's ambition to be a regional AI hub.

Core pillars are the three canonical DPI layers — digital identity, payments, and data exchange — plus an inclusion mandate. UNDP resident representative Fatmata Lovetta Sesay stressed safeguards so women, rural communities and marginalised groups are not excluded; Access to Finance Rwanda's Jean Bosco Iyacu highlighted reusable "shared technologies" that innovators can build on to expand financial inclusion.

## Status and building blocks (as of 2026-03)

- Builds on the **Rwanda Centre for Digital Public Infrastructure**, established 2025, directed by Sharon Umunyana; the Centre supports foundational-system development and technical capacity-building.
- A **digital identity system** rollout was planned for **June 2026** (cited as an early DPI step alongside [[nida-rwanda]]'s existing ID work).
- The interoperable payment platform **e-Kash** was cited as an operational DPI building block; it was **officially launched 5 December 2025** (powered by [[rswitch-rwanda]]; see [[ekash]]).
- DPI Day included technical training on DPI architecture, interoperable payments and data-exchange frameworks, certifying a growing community of practitioners.

## External assessments (analysis)

- **Phased, sequenced DPI as the contrast case (Adebowale, 2026-06/07).** In Anjola Adebowale's
  critique of Nigeria's CBN circular, Rwanda is held up as the counter-model: regulation tied to a
  **multi-year national strategy**, infrastructure built **before** the rule, and a **regulatory
  sandbox established in 2022** by the National Bank of Rwanda — i.e. sequencing over deadline-driven
  mandates. Attributed to Adebowale. Source:
  [[2026-06-19 Strategy Ahead of Infrastructure, Part II The Opportunity and the Case for Sequencing]].
- **Top marks for policy clarity (Kariuki, 2025-08).** Samora Kariuki's six-country DPI-maturity
  rubric scores Rwanda a perfect mark for **policy clarity** (high-coverage national ID + the Irembo
  e-gov portal), while noting its data-exchange layer is still nascent. One analyst's rubric,
  attributed to Kariuki. Source:
  [[2025-08-04 93 - Ecosystem Debt or Generative Platforms A Case for Digital Public Infrastructure]].

Officials repeatedly stressed that DPI cannot be built by any single institution — it requires coordination across government, regulators, private sector, civil society and development partners, and success depends on removing institutional silos (state minister Yves Iradukunda).

The strategy reads through a **sovereignty** lens: Rwanda is widely cited as a DPI leader that has retained sovereign control over core systems via open-source digital public goods (see [[dpga]] on DHIS2/Mojaloop in Rwanda).
