The **DPI Map** ([dpimap.org](https://dpimap.org/)) is a global dataset and reference tool documenting the prevalence of digital public infrastructure worldwide. Maintained by **[[ucl-iipp]]** (UCL Institute for Innovation and Public Purpose), it launched in **October 2024** as the first systematic attempt to map DPI globally, and has become a reference point for researchers, implementers and policymakers.

## What it covers

The Map catalogues three foundational DPI components — **digital identity, digital payments, and data-exchange systems** — and analyses the presence of attributes such as interoperability and privacy among them. It feeds the annual **State of DPI Report**.

### Global baseline (2025 State of DPI Report, launched 16 Jan 2026)
- **64** digital IDs
- **97** digital payment systems
- **103** data-exchange systems

## Methodology & codebook

The Map's counts rest on a documented **"Measuring DPI" framework**: **6 attributes** plus
outcome indicators (**38 for digital ID, 20 for payments, 20 for data exchange**); the v1.0
release collects only the measurable "must-have" indicators, applying explicit
**"DPI-likeness" counting rules** (e.g. payments must be central-bank-operated and real-time).
Underlying data is drawn from government websites and press releases, cross-checked against
World Bank / UN / regional-development-bank sources and validated by country implementers. The
framework is set out in **Eaves, D. & Rao, K. (2025), "Digital Public Infrastructure: a
framework for conceptualisation and measurement," UCL IIPP Working Paper WP 2025-01** (cited
but not yet a page here).

The separate **codebook** defines the classifying variables — **23 for digital ID, 22 for
payments, 18 for data exchange** — distinguishing system- from sub-system-level indicators and
cross-referencing each to the Measuring DPI indicators. These reinforce the single-category
caveat below: variables focus on national/federal, publicly-governed systems and do not encode
architecture. Sources: [[2026-07-12 Codebook - DPI Map]], [[2026-07-12 Methodology - DPI Map]].

## How we use it

The DPI Map is the standing quantitative baseline for cross-country DPI comparison in this wiki. Caveats worth carrying: it currently treats digital ID as a single category, without distinguishing architectures (centralised vs federated, biometric vs verifiable-credential, government- vs user-controlled) — a limitation flagged by its own community. It does not yet capture subnational variation, cost data, or the relationship between DPI and the broader digital-public-goods ecosystem ([[dpga]]).

## Community context

At the 16 January 2026 launch (288 registered participants), representation was skewed: Europe 37, North America 15, Asia 7, **Africa only 6**, Latin America & Caribbean 2 — a notable under-representation of the regions living with these systems. The community's single **biggest concern** (11 of 33 respondents) was ensuring DPI does not **exclude vulnerable populations**; other top concerns were measuring whether DPI actually works, and balancing innovation against privacy/security. For 2026, participants prioritised research into real-world adoption and inclusion outcomes.

Engagement is via the DPI Map **Community of Practice**, open dataset use, and country-level data contribution.
