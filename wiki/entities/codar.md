# Codar

Nigerian professional-training company selling practical digital-skills courses — software
development, cybersecurity, data analytics, cloud computing and, increasingly, AI — through an
online learning platform and physical learning hubs. A portfolio venture of
**[[kwe4-africa-venture-studio]]**. Founded by **Sokefun David**, **Williams Olusegun**,
**Oduoye John** and **Asaolu Emmanuel**.

> **Provenance.** Everything the wiki holds on Codar comes from a **TechCabal Partner / Press
> Room placement** — a sponsored, first-hand company announcement, not independent reporting.
> Admissible as a company statement under Source admissibility; its operational figures rest on
> no filings or third-party audit the wiki holds, and its self-descriptions are recorded as such.

## What matters now

- **$1.5m round closed (2026-07-17).** Raised through a **combination of equity and debt**
  ([[codar-funding-round-2026]]) to expand AI programmes, enhance the online platform, open new
  learning hubs and widen scholarships in underserved communities. *Investor names, valuation,
  the equity/debt split and the stage are all undisclosed.* See [[finance.new]],
  [[capacity.training]].
- **Reach, self-reported (as of 2026-07-17).** **>5,000 active learners**; **>70,000 students
  reached** cumulatively through programmes and initiatives. *Company-reported; no start date is
  given for the cumulative figure, and no completion, certification or placement rate is
  disclosed — so "reached" is not evidence of trained.*
- **Stated direction.** Multilingual learning, "internationally relevant certification pathways",
  mentorship and industry partnerships aimed at AI engineering, data analysis, machine learning
  and software-development roles. *Company plan, not delivered capability.*

The company describes itself as "one of Africa's fastest-growing professional training
companies" — a **self-description** the wiki does not adopt.

## Why it matters here

Codar sits in the private, fee-and-scholarship layer of Africa's AI-skills build-out, beside the
hyperscaler-funded programmes tracked on [[capacity.training]] (Google, Microsoft, Huawei) and
the state pipelines (NITDA's [[dl4all-nitda]], 3MTT). The distinguishing feature is that it is
**venture-financed rather than donor- or vendor-financed** — the skills gap treated as a market
rather than a development programme, which changes who the training is ultimately accountable to.
