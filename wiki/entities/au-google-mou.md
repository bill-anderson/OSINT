# AU Commission–Google MoU (AI & digital capacity)

- **Parties:** The **[[african-union|African Union Commission (AUC)]]** and
  **[[google|Google]]**.
- **Signatories:** Commissioner **Lerato D. Mataboge** (AUC, Infrastructure and
  Energy) and **Charles Njenga Murito** (Google, Regional Director for
  Sub-Saharan Africa, Government Affairs and Public Policy).
- **What:** A Memorandum of Understanding to advance AI and digital
  transformation across Africa.
- **When / where:** **2026-02-17**, AU Headquarters, Addis Ababa.
- **Anchoring frameworks:** The **[[au-continental-ai-strategy|AU Continental AI
  Strategy]]** and the Digital Transformation Strategy for Africa (2020–2030).
- **Status:** Signed MoU (non-binding); binds no member state.

## Priority areas / commitments

Priority areas: development of AI, digital and **cloud infrastructure**; talent
and skills development; research and innovation; support for startups and MSMEs;
and policy/governance frameworks for responsible AI. Murito framed it as a shift
from "digital access to digital agency."

Google's complementary commitments:

- Train **3 million students and teachers** in AI-related skills **by 2030**.
- Free access to AI tools including **Gemini Pro** and **NotebookLM**, with
  **Amharic** and other local-language support.
- An **AI-readiness training programme for African public officials**, delivered
  in partnership with **[[apolitical]]** — targeting **50,000 public officials in
  Ethiopia alone**, with a stated goal of expanding across the continent
  alongside the AUC and [[uneca|UN Economic Commission for Africa]].

## Sovereignty critique

The MoU drew a substantial named-analyst critique from [[alice-munyua]] ("The
Gatekeepers Part XII," 2026-03-05):

- **CLOUD Act exposure:** Because the infrastructure is owned and managed by a US
  corporation, African institutional data is argued to fall under the US
  **[[cloud-act|CLOUD Act]]** (18 U.S.C. § 2713, 2018) — US authorities can
  compel Google to produce data held anywhere, regardless of data-residency
  clauses. The press release used "sovereign" four times but (per Munyua) in no
  binding clause; the MoU establishes no data-localisation requirement, audit
  right, or exit clause.
- **Structural dependency:** The AU's 2025 programme budget was **77.5% funded by
  external donors** — the same governments (EU, Germany, UK, US/USAID and others)
  whose foreign-policy interests the AUC would need to negotiate against.
- **The Apolitical vector:** Officials trained through a Google.org-embedded
  curriculum are argued to be conditioned toward Google's frameworks when they
  later write procurement specs and regulations ("epistemic," not merely
  infrastructural, dependency).
- **Historical precedent:** Munyua likens it to the 2012 Chinese-built AU HQ (fit
  out by [[huawei]]), whose systems were reported by Le Monde Afrique in 2018 to
  have exfiltrated data nightly to Shanghai for five years — after which the AU
  nonetheless expanded its Huawei partnership (2019).

The two sources agree on the facts of the signing; the CIO Africa report presents
it as a capacity-building partnership, while the Gatekeepers piece reads it as
"the formalisation of African exposure." Tagged for analysis.
