# From "digital divide" to "digital equality": Unpacking the digital inequality paradox (Gillwald, GISW 2025)

A named-authorship chapter by **[[alison-gillwald]]** ([[research-ict-africa]] / University
of Cape Town) in the **Global Information Society Watch (GISW)** special edition
*WSIS+20: Reimagining Horizons of Dignity, Equity and Justice for Our Digital Future*
(Association for Progressive Communications, 2025). Captured as a standing reference entity
(`instrument`) — named scholarly authorship, lit-review material likely to be cited
structurally on digital inequality and data justice. The full chapter PDF is the underlying object.

## Argument

- Reads twenty years of **WSIS** and the **Global Digital Compact (GDC)** through an
  **intersectional inequality lens from a "Majority World" perspective**: the "New World
  Information Order" ambitions of the MacBride (1980) and Maitland reports were never
  redressed and have been amplified by globalised, platformised communication.
- Frames the **"digital inequality paradox"** — that expanding access has coincided with
  deepening, not narrowing, structural inequality — arguing supply-side connectivity metrics
  obscure demand-side exclusion (affordability, skills, meaningful use).
- Situates **datafication and platformisation** within "surveillance capitalism" (Zuboff)
  and the more contested **"data colonialism"** (Couldry & Mejias), and calls for a
  **global digital public goods framing** for the governance of digitalisation and
  datafication.
- Positions WSIS/GDC as flawed but "our only hope" of the global cooperation needed to
  redress widening digital inequality and **data injustice**.

## How to use / cite
