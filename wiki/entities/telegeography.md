# TeleGeography

**TeleGeography** is a US-based telecommunications market-research firm and the
principal standing **data asset** for the submarine-cable and international-
bandwidth domain. Its work is the underlying dataset for most cable analysis in
this wiki. We consult it for cable ownership, routes, landing points, capacity,
and bandwidth-demand figures.

## Key assets

- **Submarine Cable Map** (submarinecablemap.com) — a regularly updated,
  CC BY-SA 4.0 interactive map of all active and planned cables. It listed
  **622 systems as of October 2024** (the base for the [[cable-wars-abels-babic-2026]]
  dataset), and ~550 active/planned per the Silicon Canals essay (2026-03-02).
- **Content Providers' Submarine Cable Holdings list** — a reference list
  maintained "for nearly a decade," grown from **20 cables in 2017 to 60+**
  (table last updated March 2026). It shows [[google]] owning/part-owning ~35
  systems (including sole ownership of Equiano, Curie, Dunant, Umoja and others),
  [[meta]] part-owning [[2africa-cable]] and sole-owning Anjana and Project
  Waterworth, plus Microsoft and Amazon holdings.
- **Transport Networks** and **IP Networks** research services — subscription
  data on long-haul networks, capacity, traffic and pricing.
- **Africa telecommunications map** — a Feb 2025 edition depicting **77 cable
  systems connected to Africa** (cited via the Carnegie paper).

## Headline figures we cite

- Submarine cables carry **>95–99%** of intercontinental internet traffic;
  satellite is ~1% (TeleGeography "mythbusting").
- Global used international bandwidth **surpassed 6.4 Pbps at year-end 2024** —
  tripling 2020→2024 at a **32% CAGR**, though annual growth is decelerating
  (45% in 2020 → 29% in 2024).
- **Africa is the fastest-growing region at >40% CAGR** demand growth; the source
  elsewhere cites Africa's international-bandwidth market growing 44% CAGR
  2019–2023.
- **Content/cloud providers** (Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon) accounted for
  ~**three-quarters** of all bandwidth demand in 2024 (≥80% on trans-Atlantic,
  trans-Pacific and intra-Asian routes) — having overtaken internet-backbone
  carriers as the largest capacity users in **2017**; content-network capacity
  nearly quadrupled 2019–2023 to 3.6 Pbps.

Author of the cited TeleGeography pieces: **Alan Mauldin** (research director).
