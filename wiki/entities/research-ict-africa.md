# Research ICT Africa (RIA)

African digital-policy research think tank (Cape Town–based), a recurring evidentiary
authority on ICT markets, access and regulation across the continent. Hubbed as a standing
reference organisation whose analyses are cited as evidence.

## Activity in this wiki

- **South African data & cloud / sovereignty analysis.** In the BRICS-book chapter, RIA is
  cited repeatedly: on the **MTN + Vodacom 78% mobile-market duopoly**; on the failures of
  the **SA Connect** broadband policy; and — critically — warning that the 2021 **Draft
  National Policy on Data and Cloud**'s localization stance could obstruct the cross-border
  data flows needed for **AfCFTA** trade, and adopts a "Russian" data-storage approach
  (mandatory in-country copy). See [[ZAF]], [[gov.policy]], [[dpi.exchange]],
  [[south-africa--gov-protect]]. Source:
  [[2025-01-01 South African Digital Sovereignty at the Crossroad of Securitization and Development (Chapter 4) - Digital Sovereignty in the BRICS Countries]].
- **Gillwald analyses (2026).** RIA director **Alison Gillwald** authored two analyses in
  this wiki: on the geopolitical rupture and AI governance (South Africa's diluted 2025 G20
  presidency), and a [[club-de-madrid]] case study on governing digital public goods
  (regional DPI as the Global-South pathway). Sources:
  [[2026-02-17 From Davos to New Delhi, Rupture of Global Order Tests AI Governance]],
  [[2026-06-01 CdM-APD-2026-Governing-Digital-Public-Goods]].
- **Nigeria digital-ID access data (2026).** In reporting on Nigeria's NIN regime, RIA's
  finding that **72% of Nigerian adults lack a smartphone** anchors the critique that
  confining NIN corrections to a smartphone self-service portal excludes most citizens. See
  [[NGA]], [[dpi.id]], [[include.divides]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-07 Nigerians Groan Under Digital Identity Regime That Turns Basic Rights into Privileges]].
- **ICT4D evidence base (Russell Southwood, 2026-04-16).** Southwood's ICT4D
  retrospective names RIA (alongside DataReportal) as a standing evidence base for
  the persistence of **two-speed markets** and the digital divide — the empirical
  counterweight to the "leapfrogging" myth. His companion policy piece
  (2026-06-25) draws on the same body of work. See [[include.divides]],
  [[capacity.research]]. Sources:
  [[2026-04-16 The Long View - What ICT4D did for Africa and why that deep past matters for today]],
  [[2026-06-25 Regulating for Africa's future digital economy – A draft agenda to speed things up]].
