The **Digital Public Goods Alliance (DPGA)** is a multi-stakeholder initiative that promotes **digital public goods (DPGs)** — open-source software, open data, open AI systems and open content that meet the **DPG Standard** — as core building blocks of digital public infrastructure (DPI). It maintains the DPG Standard and registry and advocates for open, sovereign approaches to DPI, including through its **50-in-5** campaign.

## Research / evidence work

In 2026 the DPGA Secretariat published **"Building Open Digital States: Country Case Studies on the Impact of DPGs for DPI"** (authors Gordon LaForge and Akash Kapur, with Jon Lloyd and Max Kintisch). The report responds to a gap: interest in DPG-based DPI has grown fast but rigorous evidence of real-world impact has been thin. It offers a **four-dimension value framework** for DPG adoption:

- **Economic** — cost savings, reduced vendor lock-in, competition, domestic-market stimulation.
- **Social** — expanded access to health, identity and payments for underserved populations; SDG progress.
- **Governance** — state capacity, interoperability, data-driven decisions, anti-corruption.
- **Political** — digital sovereignty, strategic autonomy, ability to shape one's own digital future.

Plus **ecosystem effects** (trust, local capacity, innovation spillovers) that are hardest to measure but most durable.

## Country cases (headline findings)

- **Rwanda** — long-term investment in DPGs such as [[dhis2]] and Mojaloop. DHIS2 raised health-data completeness from **88% to 95%** and timeliness from 60% to 90%+; maternal mortality **fell over 50%** (476 → 203 deaths per 100,000 live births, 2010–2019); 77%+ full COVID vaccination coverage using DHIS2-integrated certificates and national-ID linkage. Rwanda exports DHIS2/Mojaloop expertise regionally and is recognised as a regional-to-global DPI leader.
- **Philippines** — [[mosip]] and Mojaloop cut vendor dependence; up to 95% reduction in the total cost of an interoperable instant-payments system; PhilSys enabled up to 8.3m additional Filipinos to open bank accounts.
- **Kyrgyzstan** — the X-Road–based Tunduk platform saved citizens an estimated **21 million hours and 1.7 billion soms ($19m) in 2024 alone**, while serving as an anti-corruption tool and reinforcing sovereign data control.

## Cross-cutting lessons

The report argues DPGs are institution-building tools, not just cheaper software; that their impact is "as much political as social or economic" (sovereignty and autonomy are among the most-sought benefits); and that incremental rollout, capacity-building, aligned mandates and crisis moments (COVID) drive lasting impact. The material reads strongly through a **sovereignty** lens.
