# Communications Authority of Kenya

The Communications Authority of Kenya (CA) is Kenya's telecommunications and ICT
regulator, responsible for licensing operators, managing spectrum, and setting
technical standards for the country's networks. It regulates the licensed
terrestrial spectrum on which Kenyan mobile operators — market leader
[[safaricom]], [[airtel-africa]] and Telkom Kenya — run their 3G, 4G and 5G
services.

## Why it matters here

As of early 2026 the CA sits at a globally significant regulatory frontier: it
has begun a **technical review of Airtel's application to deploy [[starlink]]'s
Direct-to-Cell technology** in Kenya (audit opened as reported 2026-03-10). The
central question is spectrum interference — whether Low Earth Orbit (LEO)
satellite signals beamed directly to handsets will disrupt existing terrestrial
3G/4G/5G networks, since Direct-to-Cell uses the same licensed frequencies that
ground operators rely on.

The CA is specifically examining whether satellite transmissions are strong
enough to connect handsets without "drowning out" local cell towers, and is
seeking to define **strict geographic and power boundaries** for satellite
transmissions so that satellite coverage can operate within terrestrial rules
without degrading quality of service for millions of existing users. Its
determination is being framed as a potential **global precedent** for how
satellite and mobile signals coexist.

The audit was expected to conclude **by mid-2026**. If the CA approves, Airtel
plans a phased rollout beginning with basic SMS/text messaging before expanding
to high-speed data. The Airtel–Starlink Direct-to-Cell arrangement is part of a
broader rollout across [[airtel-africa]]'s 14 African markets, so the CA's ruling
carries weight beyond Kenya's borders.

This positions the CA as a de facto standards-setter on LEO-to-handset
coexistence — a role that touches infrastructure capacity (backhaul for
hard-to-reach towers), spectrum governance, and the terms on which foreign
satellite operators access African mobile subscribers.

Beyond the LEO-to-handset question, the CA also licenses new satellite entrants: in
**January 2026** it granted **[[spacecoin]]** a transmission licence for satellite
IoT/connectivity in Kenya, one of a set of pilot agreements for the blockchain-integrated
satellite operator. See [[data.satellite]], [[infra.connect]]. Source:
[[2026-01-13 Spacecoin strikes major deals to launch satellite internet]].

## In the sovereignty critiques (Munyua, 2025–26)

Alice Munyua's "Gatekeepers" analyses situate the CA within Kenya's fragmented
digital-governance institutions: "The Double Standard" (2025-11-04) frames the continental
internet-governance capture around [[smart-africa]]'s **[[caiga]]** and [[afrinic]] — the community
side of governance the CA (as national regulator) sits adjacent to — while "Gatekeepers XIV"
(2026-05-19) reads the [[kenya-ai-bill-2026]] as an EU-AI-Act transplant that would route AI
oversight through a presidentially-appointed Commissioner rather than existing regulators like the
CA. See [[gov.regional]], [[gov.legislate]]. Sources:
[[2025-11-04 The Double Standard Why Are Internet Governance Rules Different for Africa]],
[[2026-05-19 GATEKEEPERS XIV]].
