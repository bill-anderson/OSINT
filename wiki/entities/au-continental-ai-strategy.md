# African Union Continental AI Strategy

- **Issuing body:** [[african-union|African Union]] (adopted at continental
  level).
- **Primary document now held (July 2024).** The full text — *Harnessing AI for Africa's Development and Prosperity* — is in `raw/`; prior citations here were secondary reporting. Source: [[2024-07-01 Continental AI Strategy]].
- **Adopted:** **July 2024**.
- **Scope:** Pan-African (all 55 member states); a framework for the ethical and
  responsible development and governance of AI on the continent.
- **Status:** Active; serves as the headline reference framework invoked to
  anchor AU-level AI partnerships.

## What it is

The Continental AI Strategy is the AU's flagship framework for AI governance,
intended to provide a shared continental approach to the ethical and responsible
development, deployment and governance of AI. It sits alongside the **Digital
Transformation Strategy for Africa (2020–2030)** in the AU's digital-policy
architecture, and is the instrument explicitly cited as the anchor for the
[[au-google-mou|AUC–Google MoU]] (17 Feb 2026).

## Contested provenance (sovereignty / colonialism critique)

The strategy is the subject of a pointed sovereignty critique by analyst
[[alice-munyua]] ("The Gatekeepers Part XII," 2026-03-05), who argues it was
**not substantively authored by Africa**:

- Drafting and expert consultation were led by researchers affiliated with the
  **Global Center on AI Governance**; **UNESCO** provided what its own liaison
  officer called an "entry point" into the AU AI Working Group, with technical
  support beginning before the conceptual framework was drafted.
- An independent consulting expert to the AU on the strategy simultaneously held
  a fellowship at the **Brookings Institution's** Center for Technology
  Innovation.
- The document is said to be **architecturally modeled on external regulatory
  frameworks** — the EU AI Act, the ASEAN Guide to AI Governance and Ethics, and
  the 2024 Santiago Declaration — rather than grounded in African legal,
  philosophical or indigenous-knowledge traditions.
- Strathmore University research (cited in the same piece) warns of
  **fragmentation risk**: countries "adopting established approaches, such as
  those from the European Union, without considering local contexts."

Munyua frames the strategy, via Abeba Birhane's mapping of **algorithmic
colonialism**, as "a local institutional vehicle" for importing Western
conceptual categories rather than a counter to that dynamic. This is a
**named-analyst polemic**, tagged for analysis, not a neutral description of the
instrument.
