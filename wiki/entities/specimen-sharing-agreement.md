The **Model Specimen Sharing Agreement** is a U.S.-government template treaty-adjacent instrument, an **annex to the bilateral MOUs** signed under the [[america-first-global-health-strategy]] (AFGHS). It governs the sharing of biological specimens, samples, sequencing data and related data on "novel and emerging infectious diseases with epidemic or pandemic potential" between the U.S. Government and partner (largely sub-Saharan African) countries.

**Issuing body:** U.S. Government (State Department, as an AFGHS MOU annex). **Status:** model/template text circulated to partner-country governments during MOU negotiation as of late 2025; full text reproduced publicly by analyst Emily Bass (21 Nov 2025). Signed versions were being pushed for high-priority SSA countries around World AIDS Day (1 Dec 2025) / by 12 Dec 2025.

## What it mandates (key terms, per the full text)

- **Rapid one-way sharing:** on U.S. request, the partner country must **initiate sharing specimens and related data within 5 days** (or share sequencing data on a public database).
- **Onward sharing:** the country **consents to the U.S. sharing** its specimens/data with **up to 10 non-U.S.-Government U.S. entities** ("U.S. Recipients").
- **No reciprocity:** the U.S. has **no obligation** to share data or specimens back; countries get no access to, or co-authorship on, research based on materials they provide.
- **Weak benefit clause:** if a countermeasure is developed "**primarily**" from a country's specimens, the U.S. "shall prioritize" that country's request only **immediately behind U.S. domestic need**, and "make best efforts" to supply it at prices at or below what the U.S. pays — "subject to availability of funds." No technology-transfer or IP-sharing provisions. Bass documents how "primarily," "prioritize" and "best efforts" are loophole-laden and non-binding.
- **Duration:** **25 years** from entry into force. Terminable on one year's notice, but termination "shall not affect the use by the U.S. Government of any specimens and related data previously shared."
- **Enforcement asymmetry:** failure to fulfil sharing commitments "could result in changes in U.S. Government planned assistance," MOU discontinuation, or termination (MOU §4.7 linkage) — i.e., specimen-sharing is tied to aid conditionality.

## Why it matters / how it reads

Both sources read this instrument through **sovereignty and colonialism** lenses. Bass frames it as unilateral extraction ("this is not how sharing works") that could fragment multilateral pandemic governance (the WHO Pandemic Agreement and its pending **PABS** annex), noting the U.S. itself withdrew from the WHO. The Pambazuka analysis by **Ahmed Olayinka Sule** ("America First, Africa Last") situates it in a wider AFGHS critique: 14 African countries signed AFGHS bilaterals worth ~**$16bn**; only **Kenya's** framework is public (signed 4 Dec 2025); a **Kenyan court suspended** part of it on data-privacy grounds after a Consumer Federation of Kenya petition. Sule stresses further asymmetries (Kenya audits up to 5% of its facilities for the U.S., 7-day outbreak detection, 1-day U.S. notification; no reciprocal U.S. obligations) and that the framework declares itself "**not an international agreement**" giving no legal recourse. He invokes a lineage of medical extraction (Tuskegee, *Medical Apartheid*) and calls for a collective AU-level renegotiation.

Munyua ("The Gatekeepers Part V," 2025-12-15) folds this specimen/genetic-sequence sharing into her
"new scramble" reading of the [[us-kenya-health-framework]] — 25-yr access to specimens and genetic
data for 5-yr funding, benefit-sharing left "to be determined." Source:
