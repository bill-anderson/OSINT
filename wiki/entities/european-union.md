The **European Union (EU)** is a supranational political and economic union of
member states that acts in African digital and data cooperation both as a
standards-setter (its GDPR is a global reference model for data protection — see
[[nigeria-data-protection-act-2023]]) and as an infrastructure financier through
the **Team Europe** approach and the **[[global-gateway]]** strategy. This page
tracks the EU as an *actor* in African digital/data governance; blocs are treated
as entities in this wiki, not places.

## Nigeria: the €290m Global Gateway package (Mar 2026)

The clearest recent instance of EU action is the **[[eu-nigeria-global-gateway-290m]]**
package — €290m across seven operations (digitalisation €131m, agriculture €86m,
migration €16m, plus health), announced 24 March 2026 at the **8th EU–Nigeria
Ministerial Dialogue** in Abuja under the Global Gateway strategy. Key EU
principals named:

- **Kaja Kallas** — High Representative for Foreign Affairs and Security Policy /
  Commission Vice-President, who co-chaired the dialogue and framed enhanced
  partnership with Nigeria "in the current geopolitical context."
- **Jozef Síkela** — Commissioner for International Partnerships, stressing that
  Global Gateway investments benefit Nigerians "but also create new opportunities
  for Europe."
- **Modestus Chukwulaka** — spokesperson of the EU delegation to Nigeria and
  ECOWAS.

The digital tranche co-finances (with the [[ebrd]]) a 90,000 km fibre roll-out,
secure interoperable DPI and data-exchange systems, and workforce training under
Nigeria's 3MTT programme — placing the EU directly inside Nigeria's DPI build-out.

## Team Europe and the digital-services track

The EU also acts through **Team Europe Initiatives** that pool member-state
capacity. In Nigeria, Finland leads the **Team Europe Initiative on Support to
Digital Public Services** (implemented by Finland's HAUS with Estonia's
[[estdev]]), the framing behind the bilateral [[nigeria-finland-digital-mou]].
This shows the EU operating as an umbrella under which member states run
digital-cooperation programmes aligned to a shared strategy.

## Framing

EU engagement is explicitly **geopolitical** (`geopol.eu`): investment is
presented as strategic partnership and infrastructure competition, aligned to
Nigeria's Renewed Hope agenda. Its instruments — Global Gateway finance, Team
Europe coordination, and the GDPR standards template — recur across African
data-governance stories and warrant continued tracking.

## The EU AI Act as extraterritorial standard

Nagadya ("Europe Wrote The World’s Most Advanced AI Law," 2026-05-18) argues the **[[eu-ai-act]]**
already reaches African businesses through its **"where the output lands" market-placement test**:
an African firm whose AI output is used in the EU falls in scope regardless of where it operates.
She dates the Act in force 2024-08-01, prohibited-practice bans from 2025-02-02 (workplace emotion
recognition, untargeted facial-recognition scraping, government social scoring) and full high-risk
enforcement 2026-08-02, and urges Africa to **extend** rather than copy the risk-based model. Munyua
by contrast reads Kenya's wholesale transplant of the Act (the [[kenya-ai-bill-2026]]) as importing a
stack Kenya did not build. See [[geopol.eu]], [[gov.legislate]], [[tech.ai]]. Source:
[[2026-05-18 Europe Wrote The World's Most Advanced AI Law. The EU AI Act]].

## EU/German technical assistance in Kenya's data policy (2026-06)

Rose Mutiso flags the EU (with German TA) behind Kenya's **Draft National Data Governance Policy**
(May 2026), which mirrors the **EU Data Governance Act** yet would license aggregated public data for
revenue — reuse that GDPR and German informational self-determination would bar at home. A
"wouldn't-fly-at-home" dependency critique. See [[KEN]], [[geopol.eu]], [[kenya-data-marketplace]].
Source: [[2026-06-08 Kenya Wants to Sell Your Data. But It's Complicated]].

## EU–Kenya Digital Partnership (2026-06)

The EU deepened its bilateral digital engagement with Kenya on two tracks. A **KES 15.3bn** commitment
under the **EU–Kenya Digital Partnership** targets digital infrastructure, connectivity and innovation —
including **€37m for the Blue Raman submarine cable** (Djibouti–Somalia–Kenya–Tanzania), boosting
regional bandwidth. And PS **[[john-tanui]]** held talks with an EU/Germany/Estonia/France delegation
(GIZ, ESTDEV, Expertise France) under the **Digital Transformation Center Kenya (DTC II)** framework —
digital jobs/BPO, AI & data-governance frameworks, and DPI/DPGs — explicitly working toward an **EU
Adequacy Decision** to enable cross-border data flows. A Brussels-effect alignment play (cf. the
[[kenya-ai-bill-2026]] EU-AI-Act transplant and the data-marketplace critique above). See [[KEN]],
[[geopol.eu]], [[dpi.exchange]], [[kenya--infra-connect]]. Sources:
[[2026-06-09-kenya-153bn-eu-digital-boost-techafricanews]],
[[2026-06-18-kenya-eu-digital-cooperation-ai-dpi-techafricanews]].

## Central African Republic — financing the revenue stack (2026-04)

In one of Africa's most fragile states the EU's digital footprint is on the **fiscal** side. Through
delivery agent **[[expertise-france]]**, a **€17.5m** four-year EU programme (**€8.5m** in its "Justice
pour l'Ouest 1" component) funds **Pata Polélé**, the platform digitising CAR's *mini-recettes* — the
administrative fees levied across some twenty ministries — replacing cash and manual *quittances* with
digital receipts and mobile-money settlement. A harmonisation workshop ran **14–16 April 2026** in Bangui
with **Justice, Finance and Interior** as pilot ministries; the system was built by a Tunisian expert with
CAR's **Office National de l'Informatique (ONI)**, framed as skills transfer. Alongside the EU-funded
**MODERO** civil-registration programme, this puts Brussels on both CAR's identity and revenue stacks.
See [[CAF]], [[caf--dpi-govtech]], [[dpi.pay]]. Source:
[[2026-04-18-pata-polele-digitalisation-paiements-ndjonisango]].
