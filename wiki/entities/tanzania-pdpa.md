# Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA), Tanzania

Tanzania's **data-protection statute** — **Personal Data Protection Act, Chapter 44, Revised
Edition 2023** — **passed by the National Assembly on 27 November 2022** and **in force from 1
May 2023**, supplemented by the **Personal Data Protection (Collection and Processing)
Regulations, GN No. 449C of 2023**. It establishes the **[[pdpc-tanzania]]** (Personal Data
Protection Commission) as supervisory authority, and broadly tracks the EU **GDPR** — but
diverges from it in ways that matter, set out below.

## Provisions

- **Sec. 3 — territorial scope.** Applies to controllers and processors operating within
  Tanzania and, **in certain circumstances, extraterritorially** to those outside the country
  processing data of individuals or entities in Tanzania.
- **Sec. 2 / Art. 4(3) of the Constitution — the union limit.** The Act applies to Tanzania
  Mainland **and Zanzibar, but in Zanzibar only to 'Union Matters'** — a genuine jurisdictional
  boundary inside a single data-protection regime, and one that echoes the mainland-only reach
  of the civil-registration picture held on **[[rita-tanzania]]**.
- **Sec. 15 — registration.** Entities must register as **data controllers or processors** with
  the Commission; **the final registration deadline expired 30 April 2025**.
- **Sec. 5 — processing obligations.** Lawful, fair and transparent processing for a defined
  purpose; data minimisation; accuracy, with rectification or erasure without delay.
- **Sec. 27(3) — mandatory DPO.** Requires appointment of a **data protection officer**.
- **Sec. 27(5) — breach notification.** The controller must notify the Commission of any security
  breach affecting personal data **without undue delay**.
- **Sec. 31 et seq. — cross-border transfers.** Require **adequate safeguards or express
  consent**.
- **Penalties (regulation 63 of the Collection and Processing Regulations).** A fine from **TZS
  100,000 (~USD 41) to TZS 5,000,000 (~USD 2,030)**, or **imprisonment up to five years**, or
  both. *(USD conversions as of 2026-02.)*

## Where it departs from the GDPR

Two divergences are load-bearing, and each is a **named analyst's reading**, not a settled fact:

- **No adequacy-decision mechanism (Malekela and Jurisic, Kluwer Arbitration Blog, 2025-05-07).**
  **Unlike the GDPR, the PDPA adopts no adequacy decision for third countries**; instead each
  **controller must itself assess** whether a third country's framework ensures adequate
  protection. The **Minister for Communication** (**[[micit-tanzania]]**), in consultation with
  the **[[pdpc-tanzania]]**, may issue regulations specifying where transfers are **not
  permitted** — so the state's control over data flows runs through a ministerial prohibition
  power rather than a published adequacy list. The authors note these restrictions may conflict
  with arbitration rules prioritising procedural efficiency. See [[sovereignty]]. Source:
  [[2025-05-07 The Tanzanian Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) and its applicability in Arbitration Proceedings]].
- **Breach notification is not risk-conditioned (Clyde & Co, 2026-02-09).** **Unlike the GDPR —
  where notification is required only if the breach is likely to risk data subjects' rights and
  freedoms — sec. 27(5) does not condition notification on harm or risk**: a breach becomes
  notifiable **once a security incident affecting personal data has occurred**. A strictly
  broader trigger than the GDPR's. Source:
  [[2026-02-09 Notification obligations arising from personal data breaches in Tanzania]].

## Undefined terms, and a gap in the regime

- **"Security breach" is undefined** in the Act; **[[clyde-and-co]]** read it as any incident
  resulting in unauthorised access to, disclosure of, loss of or compromise of personal data,
  accidental or intentional.
- **"Without undue delay" is undefined**; comparable regimes in neighbouring jurisdictions are
  read as roughly **48 to 72 hours**.
- **No obligation to notify data subjects.** The Act requires notification to the Commission
  only; Clyde & Co advise informing affected subjects where harm is likely as **good practice**,
  not law. The regulator learns of a breach; the people whose data it was need not.
- The controller is primarily responsible; processors must promptly inform the controller. Where
  one entity is both, it bears full responsibility for detecting, managing and reporting
  directly. The Commission provides a **prescribed notification format** (details of the
  controller/processor; breach summary; assessment; notifications and communications;
  root-cause analysis; technical and organisational measures; areas for improvement; supporting
  documentation; declaration). Source:
  [[2026-02-09 Notification obligations arising from personal data breaches in Tanzania]].

## Activity in this wiki

- **Statutory basis for the CDPO programme.** **Sec. 27(3)**'s mandatory-DPO requirement is what
  the **[[pdpc-tanzania]]** / Open University of Tanzania **Certified Data Protection Officer
  (CDPO) Programme** (2026-07-14) exists to serve: a statute that mandates DPOs across the
  economy creates the demand for a national certification pipeline. The training programme is the
  compliance-capacity answer to this section. See [[capacity.training]], [[gov.protect]].
- **Imported by the financial regulator (2025-05-23).** The **[[bank-of-tanzania]]**'s amended
  Financial Consumer Protection Regulations (**GN No. 298 of 2025**) require providers to protect
  consumer financial and personal information **in accordance with the Personal Data Protection
  Act** — a BOT-side cross-reference into the PDPA regime. See
  [[tanzania-financial-consumer-protection-regulations]], [[dpi.pay]]. Source:
  [[2025-06-27 Key Highlights of the Bank of Tanzania Financial Consumer Protection Amendment Regulations GN 298 of 2025]].
- **Reach into arbitration (2025-05-07).** Given the broad definition of "data processing",
  arbitral institutions are to be considered controllers/processors, and **arbitrators**
  themselves correspond to the **Sec. 3** definition of a data controller. Tanzanian arbitral
  institutions **TIArb** and **TIAC** **lack explicit data-protection rules or practice
  guidance** — an unremarked compliance gap in a statute whose registration deadline has already
  passed. Source:
  [[2025-05-07 The Tanzanian Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) and its applicability in Arbitration Proceedings]].
