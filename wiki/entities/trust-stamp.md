**Trust Stamp** is a biometric identity firm whose core product is **AI-powered
proprietary biometric tokenization** — technology that converts biometrics into
privacy-preserving tokens for identity verification, fraud prevention and
financial inclusion. It appears in this wiki as an external identity vendor
seeking government-level footholds in West African digital-trust and DPI
programmes.

## Nigeria engagement (Feb 2026)

Nigeria's [[nitda]] held **strategic discussions** with Trust Stamp officials in
Abuja (Trust Stamp news release 2026-02-17; reported by Biometric Update
2026-02-24) to explore government-level collaboration on a **digital trust
framework** for the country. NITDA expressed interest in the company's biometric
tokenization technology, which is **already used by a Nigerian mobile network
operator for SIM-swap fraud prevention**.

- **Status: talks only** — no signed deal. Both parties agreed to pursue
  technical consultations to draft a plan for concrete collaboration.
- NITDA Director General **[[kashifu-inuwa]]** tied digital trust to Nigeria's
  goal of a **trillion-dollar digital economy by 2030**, arguing trust is a
  critical foundation for digital-economy adoption and that a framework would
  safeguard citizens' personal data. He framed NITDA's role as providing a
  "supportive regulatory and policy framework" for secure identity verification,
  especially for the SMEs that make up much of the digital economy.
- Trust Stamp **Vice President Jonathan Pasha** said the company was open to
  collaborating with the Nigerian government to support its digital
  transformation, citing utility for advanced identity verification, fraud
  prevention and financial inclusion.

## Regional footprint

Trust Stamp is already engaged in West Africa beyond Nigeria: **Ghana** brought
in Trust Stamp (September 2025) to build **tokenized digital ID infrastructure**
— hence the `GHA` place tag. This positions the firm as a cross-border vendor of
tokenized-biometric identity to African governments.

## Significance

The engagement links external biometric-identity vendors to Nigeria's DPI and
data-protection agenda (see [[nigeria-data-protection-act-2023]] and the broader
NIN debate under [[nimc]]). It also sits alongside NITDA's parallel work — draft
technical standards for DPI and a digital-literacy drive targeting 95% by 2030
(`capacity.literacy`) — as part of building the "digital trust" layer Inuwa
regards as prerequisite to the 2030 digital-economy target.
