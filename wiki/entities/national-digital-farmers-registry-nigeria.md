The **National Digital Farmers Registry** is a Nigerian federal initiative,
officially launched in 2025 by the **Federal Ministry of Agriculture** (Minister
Abubakar Kyari), intended to be a single unified platform linking farmers'
[[nimc]] National Identity Numbers (NIN) to their farmlands so that, when a
farmer applies for support, the system already "knows" him and his fields. Its
stated goals are to eliminate ghost beneficiaries and ensure targeted delivery
of inputs, extension services, credit and insurance. As of the ministry's 2025
statements the registry was **NIN-anchored and accessed through the NIN**.

## The disharmonisation problem it is meant to solve

The registry is the wiki's anchor case for Nigeria's fragmented agricultural-DPI
landscape, documented in HumAngle field reporting (published 2026-03-03). To
access most federal or sub-national agricultural interventions a farmer must
hold: a valid NIN; a phone number linked to that NIN; a bank account linked to a
[[cbn]]-managed BVN; and a registration in a state or federal farmer database.
If any link fails, the whole process typically collapses.

These systems operate in silos: NIN sits with [[nimc]], BVN with the [[cbn]],
and SIM registration with the mobile network operators. The reporting follows
rice farmer **Bala Abubakar** of Gurin, Adamawa State, who failed NIN and then
BVN verification when trying to register for **NIRSAL** (Nigeria Incentive-based
Risk Sharing System for Agricultural Lending) inputs; another farmer, Sani
Bukar, hit "verification failed" trying to access support under **GEEP** (the
Government Enterprise and Empowerment Programme) despite a NIN-linked phone.
State **Agricultural Development Programme (ADP)** databases conflict with
federal records — farmers are "not synced." The agriculture ministry itself
acknowledged **duplications and inconsistencies** in farmers' records.

Structural gaps compound this: as of 2024 only about **38%** of Nigerians were
online, with rural areas far behind; the NIN registry had enrolled over **120
million** people but many rural Nigerians remain unenrolled.

## Interventions in train

- [[nimc]] is expanding enrolment centres under a [[world-bank]]-supported
  programme aiming to register up to 150 million Nigerians, with mobile NIN vans
  reaching rural markets.
- The World Bank approved (October 2025) a **$500m Building Resilient Digital
  Infrastructure for Growth (BRIDGE)** project to lay **90,000 km** of fibre over
  five years, expanding the national backbone from 35,000 km to 125,000 km — the
  same fibre roll-out the [[eu-nigeria-global-gateway-290m]] package and the
  [[nigeria-finland-digital-mou]] also attach to.

## Expert prescriptions

Digital-identity expert **Frank Akabueze** argued the NIN can be central but
alternative pathways must exist: offline registration by trusted cooperative
agents with later sync; voter-card numbers as interim IDs; portable biometric
devices for extension workers; harmonisation of the NIMC / BVN / SIM silos under
a data-sharing legal framework (with privacy safeguards); and biometric
deduplication to clean spelling mismatches and duplicate records. He urged the
registry to connect to the NIN and verify against existing records such as the
national farmers' census.

## Comparators (from the same reporting)

India's **Agri Stack** (Aadhaar-linked unique farmer ID tied to land records;
offline Aadhaar verification added after e-KYC exclusion problems); Kenya's 2023
national digital farmer registry (Huduma-linked, hampered by connectivity/low
smartphone ownership); Rwanda's **Smart Nkunganire** e-voucher system
(geotagged-plot-based input coupons); and Ethiopia's **NAFIR** roadmap using the
[[fayda-ethiopia-national-id]] ID against an 18-million-plot land registry.
