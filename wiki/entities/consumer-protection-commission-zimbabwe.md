# Consumer Protection Commission (Zimbabwe)

**Zimbabwe's consumer-protection regulator, established under the Consumer Protection Act
Chapter 14.44** — and, on the held material, an unusual thing: a consumer body enforcing
**access to electronic payments** as a legal right, against merchants who won't offer
them. *(Not to be confused with Zambia's similarly-named body; this clip arrived
mis-routed on the name collision.)*

## Activity in this wiki

- **Electronic payment as a consumer right (2024-10-23).** The CPC condemned businesses
  operating **without Point of Sale machines**, holding that denying consumers the ability
  to pay electronically is an **unfair trade practice**. Its legal basis:
  **Consumer Protection Act Chapter 14.44, s.51(h)**, which mandates the consumer's right
  to **choose their preferred mode of payment**. See [[dpi.pay]], [[gov.legislate]].
  Source:
  [[2024-10-23 Electronic Transactions A Consumer Right - Consumer Protection Commission]].
- **The mandate to accept e-payments.** Backed by the **Finance Act 2018**, which amended
  **s.10 of the Bank Use Promotion and Suppression of Money Laundering Act (Ch 24:24)** to
  require businesses to facilitate electronic payments — by POS machine or by mobile phone
  and computer systems. Source: as above.
- **Enforcement blitz: 80% non-compliance (as of 2024-10).** An ongoing CPC enforcement
  drive found **80% of the 27 service stations inspected (~22) had no POS machine**. The
  Commission issued **Compliance Notices under s.68** of the Consumer Protection Act, and
  said the drive would expand to other sectors. Note the sample: **27 inspections in one
  sector** — an enforcement finding, not a national compliance rate. See
  [[include.access]]. Source: as above.
- **Public reporting.** The CPC called on the public to report businesses failing to offer
  electronic payment options. Source: as above.

## Context: the charges problem

The push toward e-payments met **public outcry over excessive transaction charges**, which
the source says **derailed public confidence** in the payment method, with experts urging
government to reduce them. The [[reserve-bank-of-zimbabwe]]'s exemption of sub-US$10
transactions from bank charges (**effective 1 September 2024**) is the policy response held
here. See [[reserve-bank-of-zimbabwe]].
