# ZeroBionics

Kenyan assistive-robotics startup co-founded in **2021** by **[[norah-kimathi]]**, building
**AI-powered humanoid robots that translate spoken language into sign language in real time** for
deaf students in classrooms. Began as a robotic hand assembled from recycled plastic in a
Strathmore University workshop.

*Naming note: the held source uses "ZeroBionic" and "ZeroBionics" interchangeably. The wiki uses
**ZeroBionics**; no authoritative registration document is held.*

## What it does

- **The teaching loop.** A semi-autonomous humanoid — head, face, chest, hands and a screen — sits
  in the classroom. A remote teacher who **does not know sign language** delivers a lesson over a
  network (the founder's example: an instructor at Machakos Institute of Technology teaching a
  robotics concept **over 2G**); the robot renders it as sign language. Students respond through
  the screen and the system relays it back as audio. **Speech to sign and sign to speech**, so
  neither party needs the other's language.
- **AF1 ("Africa's First").** The current humanoid, **released and in school testing as of
  2026-07-17**. Earlier iterations were two robotic hands built from recycled tyres; the founder
  says testing showed young students needed a machine that "looked like them", prompting the move
  to a humanoid form.
- **Recycled-plastic casing as a cost strategy.** Collected waste plastic for the outer casing
  **cut costs by over 60%** — the stated response to a market of marginalised, off-grid schools
  that could not afford humanoid robots priced in the hundreds of thousands of dollars. The
  company reports it can now build **ten units for the cost of five previously**.
- **A maker space.** After buying 3D printers and finding others in the ecosystem could not access
  such equipment, ZeroBionics opened its office partly as a shared maker space for robotics
  founders and builders.

## Why it matters here

Three threads the wiki tracks converge in one small company. **Accessibility as data
infrastructure**: the product's long-term asset is a **sign-language dataset** the founder intends
to open-source — which would make it shared African assistive-AI infrastructure rather than a
proprietary product, and raises the consent and governance questions any such dataset carries
(unaddressed in the held source). **Frugal engineering against the compute-and-cost exclusion**
tracked on [[tech.ai]] — recycled casing and 2G delivery are design responses to the same
affordability constraint that prices African institutions out of imported systems. And
**accessibility treated as a right rather than a late-stage add-on**, the framing Kimathi
contrasts with box-ticking inclusion. See [[include.access]], [[tech.innovate]],
[[kenya--tech-ai]].
