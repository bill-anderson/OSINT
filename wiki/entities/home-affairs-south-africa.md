The **Department of Home Affairs (DHA)** is the South African government body responsible for civil registration, national identity and immigration. It is the custodian of the **National Population Register** and the issuing authority for the **Smart ID Card**, the green ID book it is phasing out, birth and marriage certificates, and — through the [[south-africa-eta]] — visas and travel authorisations. Under minister **Leon Schreiber** (of the Government of National Unity), the department has made digital transformation the centre of its programme, branded **"Home Affairs @ home"**, with the stated ambition that every South African can access DHA services via phone, computer or a trusted partner institution, and eventually without carrying physical documents at all.

## Smart ID bank partnership

On **9 March 2026**, at a launch in **Orange Farm**, DHA opened its **first live bank-branch Smart ID partnership**, with **Capitec Bank**, letting citizens obtain Smart ID and passport services inside bank branches instead of travelling to Home Affairs offices (2026-03-09 launch). Schreiber framed this as "the beginning of the end of long queues." Key facts as of that date:

- For more than a decade the bank partnership was limited to just **30 branches** nationwide, in a country of over **63 million** people — deemed far too small a footprint.
- Roughly **16 million** South Africans still rely on the insecure **green ID book**, which the minister called one of the most defrauded identity documents on the continent; ID fraud costs the country billions of rand per year.
- The partnership rests on a **secure digital gateway** connecting DHA systems to banking partners via the **Online Verification Service** (upgraded in 2025), using **biometric verification**. DHA stresses it **remains the custodian** of the National Population Register; banks integrate but do not hold the register.
- Rationale extends to financial-crime compliance: strengthening the identity system is cited as key to South Africa exiting the **FATF grey list**.
- Enabled by high financial inclusion — over **80%** of South African adults have a bank account. Modernised DHA branches and mobile offices continue to serve those without accounts.

The minister positioned the model as a template for a "new operating model for government services" in which public institutions and trusted private partners jointly expand access. See [[sa-digital-id-regulations]] for the regulatory framework and [[mymzansi]] for the wider single-digital-entry-point programme that DHA services plug into.

## Smart ID delivery and the population register (2026)

- **Record 4,002,964 smart IDs issued in 2025** (+17% on 2024's 3,427,468; ~1.3m more than 2022+2023 combined) — the highest annual delivery in DHA history, credited to repairing the **Online Verification Service (OVS)** and improved population-register uptime (as of 2026-01-29). Minister **[[leon-schreiber]]**; smart cards produced by Government Printing Works. Source: [[2026-01-29 Over 4 million smart ID cards issued in 2025]].
- **OVS verification-fee court fight (as of 2026-01-27).** DHA raised the OVS ID-verification fee from **15c to ZAR 10** per real-time check (~6,500%; ZAR 1 off-peak batch), effective 1 July 2025, with revenue earmarked for an "Intelligent Population Register" and future digital ID. The **[[act-south-africa]]** (Association of Communications and Technology; incl. [[vodacom]], [[mtn-group]]) filed a High Court review calling it a "regressive tax"; TymeBank opposed, Capitec will absorb it. Schreiber says the old fee left the register failing >50% of the time vs <1% now. This is the same OVS whose repair drove the smart-ID record above — the register-funding model is itself contested. See [[south-africa--dpi-id]]. Source: [[2026-01-27 SA's Telcos, Banks At War With Govt Over 6,500% ID Verification Fee Hike]].
- **Digital-ID policy still pending (as of 2026-01-28).** The national digital-ID policy remains under development — Cabinet approval then public comment outstanding — so rollout will take several more years despite ministerial statements punting end-2026. See [[sa-digital-id-regulations]]. Source: [[2026-01-28 Bad news about South Africa's newest ID book plan]].
