The **South African Police Service (SAPS)** is South Africa's national police force. In the OSINT record here it appears as the losing party in a landmark **govtech-procurement failure**: a decade-long dispute over a biometric firearms and evidence-management system that has left SAPS liable for more than **R1 billion** and forced firearm administration back onto **pen and paper**.

## The Firearm Permit System dispute

The systems at issue were built for SAPS by the contractor collectively referred to in court as **FDA** (see [[forensic-data-analysts]] — comprising Forensic Data Analysts, Investigative Software Solutions and Solve Forensics):

- **Firearm Permit System (FPS)** — biometric processing of all firearm permit applications, tracking weapons issued to officers and booked into police custody;
- **Property Control and Exhibit Management System (PCEM)** — forensic exhibits and evidence;
- **VA-AMIS** — DNA-testing and investigative-analysis intelligence tool for the Hawks and Crime Intelligence.

Former national commissioner **Khehla Sitole** stopped paying FDA's licensing fees in late **2017** while SAPS kept using the systems. FDA **switched the systems off in 2019** over non-payment; police stations nationwide reverted to **manual pen-and-paper** firearm administration. Since then **more than 3,000 police firearms have gone missing**, with authorities unable to track them. A settlement brokered at the **Sefako Makgatho Presidential Guesthouse** in Pretoria (peaking around **31 January 2020**, when FDA sent a sheriff to physically remove its systems from [[sita]]'s premises) was signed by Sitole then withdrawn two hours later after then-minister **Bheki Cele** objected, citing a Government Technical Advisory Centre valuation of just R88 million against negotiated figures of R460m+ (independent expert Don MacRobert later valued the three systems at R560m).

## The R1 billion ruling

In **December 2025**, Judge **Sulet Potterill** of the **Pretoria High Court** ordered SAPS to pay FDA **R644 million** for the intellectual property plus **R120 million** for maintenance and support, with interest running from the date of breach. Per the Sunday Times, that interest alone costs taxpayers about **R1 million per week** while SAPS appeals. The court also found SAPS operated FDA's systems in violation of an interdict, held Sitole in contempt of court, and found that [[sita]] had **illegally extracted FDA's source code** while trying to build replacement systems. Current commissioner **Fannie Masemola** has petitioned the Supreme Court of Appeal, so the interest bill keeps growing while SAPS runs without the digital systems. Portfolio-committee chair **Ian Cameron** called it a procurement failure diverting money from frontline policing (all as of the 2026-03-08 report).
