# Jess Auerbach Jahajeeah

**Jess Auerbach Jahajeeah** is an anthropologist studying submarine cables as
**colonial and knowledge infrastructure** in Africa. She is affiliated with the
Department of Anthropology at **North-West University** (and, per a 2024
interview, an associate professor at the **University of Cape Town Graduate
School of Business**), and holds an **Iso Lomso fellowship at STIAS** (the
Stellenbosch Institute for Advanced Study). She matters here as a leading expert
voice framing African subsea connectivity through **digital sovereignty and
digital colonialism**.

## The book project

Auerbach is writing an ethnography of an undersea cable — variously titled
**"Capricious Connections: the politics of knowledge in the South Atlantic and
Indian Ocean islands"** (and referenced as *Capricious Connections* more broadly).
It traces a cable system stretching from **Fortaleza, Brazil → Angola → South
Africa → across the Indian Ocean via Mauritius → India and Malaysia**, drawing on
fieldwork in **Angola, Brazil, Mozambique and Mauritius**. The book is held
pending publication; treat its arguments as forthcoming scholarship, not settled
fact.

## Key positions

- **Infrastructure is not neutral.** She notes there are far fewer cables in the
  South Atlantic than the North, and that the **City of London has four times the
  data-centre capacity of southern Africa**.
- **Cables retrace empire.** Modern fibre routes overlay 19th-century copper
  telegraph lines and, in one case, the **2012 transatlantic cable runs almost
  exactly over the transatlantic slave-trade route**. Her crew once dredged up a
  section of **Victorian-era cable** while grappling for a modern fibre.
- **The digital divide is about more than cables.** At the Sangano landing
  station in Angola (run by Angola Cables), a school a few hundred metres away
  still lacks electricity — connectivity without infrastructure, skills or
  frameworks remains abstract.
- **Sovereignty risk.** As private firms (Alphabet/[[google]], [[meta]], Huawei)
  displace public–private cable financing, poorer countries "have little choice
  but to accept the terms and conditions of wealthy corporate entities" — a
  danger for African digital sovereignty in a "high-stakes battle between China,
  the United States and... increasingly, Russia."

## Fieldwork detail we cite

In **late 2023** Auerbach spent time aboard the **Léon Thévenin**, the ship
whose crew maintains most of Africa's undersea network, during a repair operation
**off the coast of Ghana**. She gave expert commentary to The Conversation Africa
on the **14 March 2024 four-cable outage** (Nigeria, Côte d'Ivoire, Liberia,
Ghana, Burkina Faso and South Africa worst hit), stressing that single-cable
countries like Sierra Leone and Liberia go fully offline when their one cable
breaks, and that satellite carries only ~1% of global traffic.
