# The Case for "Open Access" Communications Infrastructure in Africa: the SAT-3/WASC cable (Jagun et al., 2008)

## Argument

A foundational piece of African connectivity-policy literature making the case for
**"Open Access"** to submarine-cable infrastructure. It uses the **SAT-3/WASC**
cable as the illustrative failure case: a **consortium-owned, monopoly-controlled**
cable whose closed, "commercially confidential" governance kept bandwidth scarce
and prices high across sub-Saharan Africa. The paper argues that how infrastructure
is **built, owned and operated** — not just whether it exists — determines
development outcomes, and calls for open-access principles (non-discriminatory
wholesale access at cost) in current and future builds.

## Key facts we draw from it

- **SAT-3/WASC/SAFE**: a **14,350 km** fibre pair from **Sesimbra, Portugal** to
  **Melkbosstrand, South Africa**, passing nine African countries (landings at
  Dakar, Abidjan, Accra, Cotonou, Lagos, Douala, Libreville, Cacuaco, Melkbosstrand).
  Commissioned 1999; **commercial service April 2002**. Design capacity 120 Gbps
  (SAT-3/WASC) + 130 Gbps (SAFE).
- **Consortium of ~36 investors from ~35 countries**; largest shareholders TCI/AT&T
  (12.42%), France Télécom (12.08%), VSNL (8.93%), Nitel (8.39%). Reported total
  cost ~US$595m (some sources cite up to US$650m). African members included Angola
  Telecom, Camtel, Ghana Telecom, Sonatel, Telkom SA and others.
- **Chronic under-utilisation**: by 2003 the cable was upgraded to only 40 Gbps
  (a third of design capacity) yet actual usage was estimated at **<3%** of design
  capacity — despite acute bandwidth scarcity — because monopoly owners restricted
  and over-priced access.
- **Infrastructure gap framing**: in 2004 ~88% of world bandwidth sat in developed
  regions; a European/North American had ~570 more bits/inhabitant of bandwidth
  than an African. The briefing's "concentric circles of monopolistic barriers"
  diagnoses layered gatekeeping.
- **Four-country case study**: Angola, Cameroon, Ghana, Senegal (research initiated
  Nov 2006) — documenting how competition levels, wholesale bandwidth cost, retail
  call and internet prices moved (or failed to move) with SAT-3 access. Notes
  next-generation cables **SEACOM, EASSy and TEAMS** were then projected for
  2009/2010 as competitive alternatives.

## Why we cite it

The canonical statement of the **open-access argument** in African cable policy,
and a primary reference for the **SAT-3/WASC monopoly-pricing** history that later
hyperscaler and open cables (Equiano, 2Africa) are measured against. Its four case
authors are themselves notable African connectivity analysts.
