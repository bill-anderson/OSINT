# US CLOUD Act (2018)

- **Full name:** Clarifying Lawful Overseas Use of Data Act — the **CLOUD Act**.
- **Citation:** 18 U.S.C. § 2713.
- **Issuing body / jurisdiction:** United States federal law, **enacted 2018**.
- **Scope:** Global reach over data held by US technology companies, regardless
  of where the data physically resides.

## What it does

The CLOUD Act requires American technology companies to **produce data held
anywhere in the world** when a US court or law-enforcement agency demands it —
**without the knowledge or consent** of the country where that data originated.
Its governing principle is that **jurisdiction follows ownership, not location**:
a data-residency clause, however well worded, does not change which country's
courts govern the infrastructure. The Electronic Frontier Foundation, cited by
[[alice-munyua]], characterised it on passage as "a sweeping expansion of
extraterritorial surveillance authority that no bilateral MOU can override."

## Why we cite it

The CLOUD Act is the central **data-sovereignty concern** raised against African
deals with US hyperscalers. In "The Gatekeepers Part XII" (2026-03-05), Munyua
argues that the **[[au-google-mou|AUC–Google MoU]]** places African institutional
data and "the digital futures of 1.5 billion people" on infrastructure owned by a
US corporation and therefore subject to the CLOUD Act — meaning US authorities can
access it without involving African courts, regulators or governments, whatever
data-residency language the MoU contains.

It functions in the wiki as the reference instrument invoked whenever a US-cloud
arrangement (Google, [[amazon-web-services]], [[microsoft]], [[oracle]]) is
weighed against African data-protection and sovereignty claims. Contrast with
localisation approaches such as [[nitda]]'s Nigerian data-hosting mandates, which
Munyua cites as a partial counter-model.
