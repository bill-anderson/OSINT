# African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA)

AU continental free-trade framework — 54 member states, ~1.3bn people, combined GDP
>$3tn; the largest free-trade area by participating countries since the WTO. Filed as an
**initiative entity** (a bloc-level framework), per the "blocs are entities" rule.

## Activity in this wiki

- **2nd Digital Trade Forum (2026-07-07, Lagos).** State parties committed to **expedite
  operationalization of the Protocol on Digital Trade** (adopted by the AU Assembly in
  2024), closing with **eight calls to action**: ratify/domesticate the protocol; speed up
  DPI; move to paperless, harmonized cross-border trade; build trust via cybersecurity,
  consumer protection and responsible data governance; expand inclusion/skills; mobilize
  investment; deepen PPPs; and strengthen **secure, interoperable cross-border payments**
  ([[papss]]). SG **[[wamkele-mene]]**: digital trade formalises the economy and boosts
  growth. A common framework harmonizing **e-signatures, mutual recognition of digital ID
  and interoperable payments** ([[gov.standards]], [[dpi.id]], [[dpi.pay]]) is the route to
  a functioning digital single market. Source:
  [[2026-07-07 African nations recommit to operationalize AfCFTA digital single market framework]].
- **The governance gap (2026-07, P.M. News / Okorie).** Commercial integration is
  outrunning **regulatory** integration: incompatible national data-protection and IP
  regimes ([[gov.protect]], [[gov.legislate]]) create cross-border legal exposure that is
  "the silent factor killing deals before they close." Governance has become a
  **valuation/defensibility** metric in investor due diligence. See [[dpi.exchange]].
- **DPI as AfCFTA enabler (2026-07, Equity/AfricaNenda).** Cross-border payments, trusted
  digital ID and secure data exchange positioned as infrastructure "supporting the
  implementation of AfCFTA." See [[equity-africanenda-gates-dpi-partnership]].
- **Cross-border ID governance (2026-07).** A Warwick/Alan Turing study proposes AfCFTA as a **supranational governance layer** for cross-border digital-ID interoperability (which fails at governance, not tech). Separately, the **US–AUC Strategic Investment Working Group** ([[us-auc-strategic-investment-working-group]]) anchors US–Africa investment to AfCFTA. See [[gov.standards]], [[gov.regional]]. Sources: [[2026-07-03 Study finds governance, not technology, is blocking digital ID interoperability]], [[2026-01-28 Joint Statement of the United States and African Union on the Launch of a Strategic Investment Working Group]].

- **DPI as the operational layer for AfCFTA (2026, UNDP).** UNDP's continental DPI paper
  argues DPI's core rails — digital ID, cross-border payments, trusted data exchange — are
  what translate AfCFTA commitments into "operational reality," citing the **AfCFTA Protocol
  on Digital Trade and IP** endorsed at the **February 2025 AU Summit** as the first
  comprehensive continental legal framework for the digital economy, and pegging the AfCFTA
  opportunity at **US$450bn**. See [[dpi.exchange]], [[gov.regional]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-01 Digital Public Infrastructure in Africa - UNDP]].

- **"Building the same road twice" (2026-07).** The AfCFTA Secretariat's **ADAPT**
  cross-border data-exchange system launched within **five days** of Smart Africa's SADX —
  the Data Landscapers duplication critique pairs the two competing rails as wasteful
  overlap ([[smart-africa]]). See [[dpi.exchange]], [[gov.regional]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-28 Building the same road twice]].
- **Google MoUs (2026-07).** Google's MoUs with the AfCFTA Secretariat / **ADIEP** feature
  in the Data Landscapers "Google in Africa" audit. See [[dpi.exchange]], [[finance.mou]].
  Source: [[2026-05-20 Google in Africa]].
- **Nigeria takes the Council Chair (2026-07-09, Abuja).** At the 18th Council of Ministers,
  **Nigeria** took the Council Bureau Chair from **Egypt**; Trade Minister **Jumoke Oduwole**
  set four priorities (implementation, regional value chains, digital trade, MSME/women-and-
  youth finance). The parallel 2nd Digital Trade Forum pegged Africa's digital economy at
  **~$180bn now → ~$712bn by 2050** and reaffirmed the Eight Calls to Action on the
  **[[afcfta-digital-trade-protocol]]**; **ADIEP** (with [[google]]) is training **7,500 SMEs
  across 19 countries**, an MoU with Quest Ghana will build a Digital Trade Corridor, and
  [[papss]] was cited as the payments rail. See [[gov.regional]], [[dpi.pay]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-09 Nigeria Takes AfCFTA Council Chair as Digital Forum Charts Roadmap for Implementation]].
- **700 data centres needed (2026-04-08, GITEX Africa, Marrakech).** SG [[wamkele-mene]] said
  delivering AfCFTA digital trade needs **>700 data centres** (Africa has ~220, ~1% of global
  commercial capacity), pegging the single market at **$3.6tn**, the digital economy at
  **$721bn by 2050**, and DC capacity tripling to **~1.2 GW by 2030**. See [[infra.store]],
  [[infra.capacity]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-08 Africa's digital trade push requires 700 data centres, says AfCFTA chief]].
- **ADLI cohort on the Digital Identity Annex (2026-05-27).** DIAL's Africa Data Leadership
  Initiative partnered with the AfCFTA Secretariat for a test-and-learn cohort (6 countries,
  3 trade corridors) to operationalise the **Digital Identity Annex** of the
  [[afcfta-digital-trade-protocol]] (AU-adopted Feb 2025). See [[dpi.id]], [[gov.standards]].
  Source:
  [[2026-05-27 Africa's economic future hinges on breaking down silos. This ADLI cohort advances integration]].
- **PAPSS–Onafriq West Africa trade rail (2026-02-04).** [[onafriq]] and [[papss]] launched a
  CBN-approved six-month pilot (from 1 Dec) for wallet-based outbound payments **Nigeria→Ghana
  settled fully in Naira** with no hard-currency conversion — explicitly "supporting the
  operationalisation of the AfCFTA mandate." PAPSS spans 160+ banks / 400m+ accounts across 19
  countries; Onafriq brings 1bn+ mobile wallets. [[cbn]]'s Policy Insight Series 2025 casts
  regulatory passporting (bilateral pilots with Ghana/Kenya/Senegal/South Africa) as the concrete
  ECOWAS/AfCFTA financial-integration test. See [[dpi.pay]], [[gov.regional]]. Sources:
  [[2026-02-04 PAPSS, Onafriq launch new trade rail for West Africa]],
  [[2026-02-06 Nigeria just raised the bar for West African Fintech]],
  [[2022-08-30 Pan-African Payment and Settlement System drives Africa's banking integration]],
  [[2022-05-11 Pan-African Payment and Settlement System]].

- **Payment-rail integration deepens (2026).** [[pesalink]] joined [[papss]] as a
  Technical Connectivity Provider (80+ Kenyan institutions into PAPSS's 160+ banks),
  and [[bank-of-ghana]] pressed for a payments overhaul — intra-African transfers
  cost **7–10%** (vs ~3% globally), **>80%** routed via correspondent banks abroad at
  ~**$5.3bn/yr** — both cast as AfCFTA single-market enablers. See [[dpi.pay]],
  [[gov.regional]]. Sources:
  [[2026-03-02 Pesalink and PAPSS link networks for African payments]],
  [[2026-02-05 Bank of Ghana Calls for Payment System Overhaul to Unlock AfCFTA Trade]].
