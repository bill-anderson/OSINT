**Authors:** Stefaan G. Verhulst and Begoña G. Otero. **Year:** 2026. **Venue:** working paper (SSRN, abstract id 6907425). Full title: *"The Triad of Tech Sovereignty and Europe's Package: Dependency, Openness, and Agency."*

## What it argues

The paper is an early assessment of the European Commission's **Technological Sovereignty Package** (IP/26/1187, presented **3 June 2026**) — comprising the Chips Act 2.0, the Cloud and AI Development Act, an Open Source Strategy, and a Strategic Roadmap for Digitalisation and AI in Energy, aiming to triple EU data-centre capacity over 5–7 years, expand sovereign compute, and promote open-source alternatives.

Verhulst and Otero read the Package through their **Triad of Tech Sovereignty**, arguing the sovereignty contest unfolds across three interlinked dimensions:

1. **Dependency** — the weaponisation of structural dependency, where reliance on foreign infrastructure becomes strategic vulnerability. Europe's Package addresses this **strongly** (chips, cloud, compute, Eurostack).
2. **Openness** — the weaponisation of openness, where access and interoperability become mechanisms for **extraction** (LLMs harvesting the digital commons, data enclosure, cultural sovereignty, "digital colonialism"). The Package gives this **growing but partial** attention.
3. **Agency** — systemic asymmetries where those most affected have least say. Here the Package is **underdeveloped or misallocated**: decision-making is relegated to national administrations with insufficient mechanisms for participation, redress or enforcement.

Their central claim: viewing sovereignty purely as **infrastructure ownership** misses the core issue — *who has the power to shape, govern and challenge* these systems. Since concentration of power "is a choice, not an inevitability," merely transferring concentrated power from foreign to domestic hands solves the geopolitical problem while reproducing the democratic one.

## The proposal

Grounded in the principle of **digital self-determination**, they propose a **"stewardship architecture"**: a structured set of engagements and oversight mechanisms, each matched to a specific challenge and paired with enforceable oversight, built largely from **existing EU law** — data collaboratives and data-sharing agreements that operationalise a community's **social licence**, plus market-structure tools (Digital Markets Act, Article 102 TFEU, merger control, procurement conditionality). The aim is to prioritise **agency over ownership** as the true measure of digital sovereignty.

## Why we cite it

This is a rigorous, named-scholarly framing of **digital sovereignty** that, though EU-focused (**geopol.eu**), travels directly to African debates: its dependency/openness/agency triad, its warning against "solving one asymmetry by creating another," and its data-extraction/openness critique map onto recurring themes in this wiki (data colonialism, sovereign compute, who governs DPI).
