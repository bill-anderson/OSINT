The **Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA)** is South Africa's digital visa-screening and travel pre-authorisation platform, operated by the [[home-affairs-south-africa]] and positioned by minister **Leon Schreiber** as the **cornerstone of the country's visa reform**. Accessible at **eta.dha.gov.za**, it was launched in an initial phase ahead of the **G20 Leaders' Summit** hosted by South Africa, and is intended to become the single gateway for all tourist visas — ultimately ending parallel processing channels, including at diplomatic missions abroad.

## Why it exists

The ETA is the structural response to a **Special Investigating Unit (SIU)** probe of visa administration between 2004 and February 2024, which found what Schreiber called "up to twenty years of maladministration and malfeasance": corruption thriving in a manual, paper-based system with wide official discretion. Dismissals are underway and thousands of fraudulent student visas are being revoked. The ETA's design goal is to remove discretionary human intervention at critical points to close those gaps.

## How it works

The platform combines several technologies (as of the 2026-02-24 report):

- **Machine learning** to verify the authenticity of documents such as passports;
- **Facial recognition**, comparing the applicant's live selfie against their official photo;
- **Rules-based automated decision-making** — approvals/rejections made **without human review**.

By official figures it had already **rejected more than 30,000 applications** that failed to meet tourist-visa requirements. The whole process is completed on a smart device (scan passport, take a selfie); eligible applications are processed **within 24 hours**.

## Scope and roadmap

At this stage the ETA applies to nationals of **China, India, Indonesia and Mexico** aged 18+ on ordinary passports (≥6 months' validity), entering via **OR Tambo (Johannesburg), Cape Town, or Lanseria** international airports. It is valid **12 months** and permits stays of **up to 90 days**, with a Section 11(1) visitor visa issued on arrival; admission remains subject to inspection by the **Border Management Authority (BMA)**. Government plans to expand it to all visa categories (notably student visas) and to extend **facial recognition at airports and land borders** in cooperation with the BMA and [[sars]]. Home Affairs intends to link the ETA to a **smart population register** and a future **biometric digital ID** (see [[sa-digital-id-regulations]]). The automated, biometric, no-human-review design — and its reach into a national identity register — carries clear data-sovereignty and surveillance implications, hence the sovereignty lens.
