**WAXAL** (from the Wolof word for "speak") is an open-source **voice/speech dataset for African languages**, built to seed the development of speech AI — automatic speech recognition (ASR), text-to-speech (TTS), voice assistants and dictation tools — for languages long starved of training data. It was released by **[[google]] Research Africa** and announced **2 February 2026**, available free on Hugging Face under an open license.

## What it is

- **21 African languages**, including Acholi, Akan, Dagaare, Dagbani, Dholuo, Ewe, Fante, Fulani, Hausa, Igbo, Ikposo, Kikuyu, Lingala, Luganda, Malagasy, Masaaba, Nyankole, Rukiga, Shona, Soga, Swahili and Yoruba.
- **Over 11,000 hours** of speech data drawn from **nearly 2 million individual recordings**.
- Of which ~**1,250 hours of transcribed speech** for ASR and **20+ hours of studio recordings** for TTS voice synthesis.
- Developed over **three years**. Google frames it as reaching **100 million+** speakers.

## Who built it (and who owns it)

WAXAL was collaboratively produced with leading African institutions, who **retain ownership** of the corpora they collected while releasing them openly:
- **Makerere University** (Uganda) and the **University of Ghana** — led data collection for a combined **13 languages**. (Makerere's Joyce Nakatumba-Nabende: "For AI to have a real impact in Africa, it must speak our languages and understand our contexts.")
- **Digital Umuganda** (Rwanda) — led **five** major languages.
- Studio partners **Media Trust** and **Loud n Clear** for high-quality voice recordings.
- **African Institute for Mathematical Sciences (AIMS)** — multilingual data for future releases.

The retained-ownership, open-license model is the notable governance feature: it is explicitly framed as building "on their own terms, in their own languages" (Aisha Walcott-Bryant; see [[aisha-walcott-bryant]]).

## Why it matters / how we use it

Sub-Saharan Africa has 2,000+ languages but only a handful have NLP-ready resources; data scarcity is the main barrier to voice AI on the continent. WAXAL is a standing reference asset for African speech-tech and language-inclusion questions. It sits alongside efforts like Nigeria's **N-ATLAS** (2025) open-source model for Yoruba, Hausa, Igbo and Nigerian English. Intended downstream uses stress low-literacy access to health, education and agriculture services.
