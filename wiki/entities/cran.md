# Communications Regulatory Authority of Namibia (CRAN)

Namibia's telecommunications regulator, established under the Communications Act, which
licenses operators, manages spectrum and enforces sector compliance.

## Activity in this wiki

- **Denied Starlink's licence (2026-03-24, Windhoek).** CRAN refused [[starlink]]'s
  combined ECS/ECNS service licence and its fixed-satellite spectrum licence, finding the
  LEO provider met only **three of six** statutory criteria. Starlink passed on competition,
  technical/financial capacity and frequency availability, but failed on **ownership and
  control**, **national defence and public security**, and **compliance history**. On
  ownership, Starlink's 100% foreign ownership breaches the **51%-Namibian-ownership** rule
  under section 46 of the Communications Act, and it obtained no ministerial exemption. On
  security, CRAN cited **data sovereignty** and national control, holding that wholly foreign
  ownership undermines its ability to exercise effective oversight. See [[NAM]],
  [[gov.protect]], [[sovereignty]]. Source: [[2026-03-26 Starlink blocked from operating in Namibia]].
- **Prior cease-and-desist (Nov 2024).** CRAN had already ordered Starlink to cease
  operating without a licence and ordered confiscation of all Starlink terminal equipment
  in-country; Starlink's failure to respond to the Authority's summons counted against it in
  the compliance-history assessment. Source:
  [[2026-03-26 Starlink blocked from operating in Namibia]].
- **Door open to compliant LEO providers.** CRAN affirmed it recognises the transformative
  potential of LEO satellites for rural and underserved connectivity and will continue to
  champion the technology — signalling that compliant operators may still be licensed.
  (Starlink claimed 98.6% public support in consultation and noted >30% of Namibians lack
  internet access.) CRAN noted it may **reconsider within 90 days**, on its own initiative
  or on formal petition, leaving Starlink a narrow window to revise its ownership structure
  (Starlink is now licensed in 27 African countries; the denial mirrors its South Africa
  licensing impasse over local-ownership rules). See [[infra.connect]], [[digital.rural]].
  Sources: [[2026-03-26 Starlink blocked from operating in Namibia]],
  [[2026-03-24 Namibia puts foot down against Musk's Starlink]].
- **Reconsideration rejected — denial upheld (2026-06-24).** CEO **Emilia Nghikembua** said CRAN received
  **624 reconsideration requests**; **622** failed the legal threshold and **2**, heard on merits, showed "no
  new evidence nor a material error" — so the **March denial stands** (Starlink still failing ownership,
  compliance and national-security criteria). She stressed the issue "has never been whether satellite
  technology is beneficial" but statutory compliance; CRAN noted a **fresh Starlink application on 8 June 2026**.
  CRAN also runs **[[nam-csirt|Nam-CSIRT]]** (see [[namibia--infra-cybersec]]). Sources:
  [[2026-06-24-namibian-regulator-rejects-starlink-again-connectingafrica]],
  [[2026-03-31-starlink-says-cran-denial-unjustified-namibiansun]].
- **4G-device tax breaks proposed (2026-02).** At an MTC Namibia tower launch in Kunene
  (Epupa), CRAN CEO **Emilia Nghikembua** proposed tax breaks and incentives to make
  4G-enabled handsets affordable, citing MTC 4G-tower utilisation of **under 50%** ("putting
  up the tower is not digital transformation ... It's an enabler"). See [[include.access]],
  [[include.divides]], [[NAM]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-02 Namibian regulator proposes tax breaks on 4G devices]].
