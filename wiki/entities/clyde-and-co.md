# Clyde & Co

**International law firm**, held here as the publisher of dated legal analysis on African data
protection regimes.

## Activity in this wiki

- **Tanzania breach-notification analysis (2026-02-09).** A briefing by **Tenda Msinjili, Naomi
  Kammu and Hadia Mgaya** on notification duties under the **[[tanzania-pdpa]]** and the Personal
  Data Protection (Collection and Processing) Regulations, GN No. 449C of 2023. Its load-bearing
  finding: **section 27(5) requires a controller to notify the Commission "without undue delay" and
  — unlike the EU GDPR — does NOT condition that duty on risk or harm to data subjects**, so a
  breach becomes notifiable once a security incident affecting personal data has occurred. The firm
  further notes that **"security breach" is undefined**, that **"without undue delay" is undefined**
  (comparable neighbouring regimes read it as roughly 48 to 72 hours), and that there is **no
  obligation to notify data subjects at all** — good practice only. Penalties under regulation 63
  run from **TZS 100,000 (approx. USD 41)** to **TZS 5,000,000 (approx. USD 2,030)**, or up to five
  years' imprisonment, or both (USD conversions as of 2026-02). See [[TZA]], [[pdpc-tanzania]],
  [[gov.protect]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-09 Notification obligations arising from personal data breaches in Tanzania]].

> **Attribute the readings to the firm.** The GDPR-divergence analysis is Clyde & Co's
> interpretation of an under-defined statute, not settled law.

## Why it matters analytically

Where a data-protection statute leaves core terms undefined, **practitioner briefings are often the
only place the ambiguity is named** — regulators rarely advertise the gaps in their own instruments.
That makes this class of source valuable for [[gov.protect]] precisely where official material is
silent, provided it is read as one firm's advice rather than as the law itself. Note the
complementarity with the practitioner analysis on [[tanzania-pdpa]]'s registration, territorial
scope and cross-border provisions: different sections of one statute, different firms, no overlap.
