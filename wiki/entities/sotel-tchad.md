# Sotel Tchad (Société des télécommunications du Tchad)

**Chad's state-owned fixed-line incumbent** — sold in **2010** to the Libyan state investment
vehicle **Lap Green**, then nationalised after the Libyan civil war, and reduced from a peak of
**~10,000 employees to under 500** (as reported 2026-02-16). Its fibre network is largely confined
to N'Djamena and parts of the south. It once held protective monopolies on national and
international networks; these were removed in response to public discontent over the price and
quality of internet services.

Sotel is simultaneously the vehicle through which the government announces new network build, and
— in Russell Southwood's analysis — a **"stranded asset"** that has failed at the one thing
[[TCD]] most needs from it.

## Activity in this wiki

- **The unsolved problem: international fibre.** Southwood ([[russell-southwood]]) argues the
  fundamental issue neither Sotel nor the government has solved is **a reliable international
  fibre link**. Landlocked Chad faces civil unrest in five of its six neighbours; the historic
  route runs through Cameroon, which is unreliable and costly in wholesale terms because of the
  **Camtel monopoly** — so much so that the wholesale fibre price in Chad is *a large percentage
  of the final retail internet price*. See [[infra.connect]], [[tech.industry]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-16 Waking the dead - Slow road to extinction for Africa's former telco incumbents 11]].
- **SudaChad concession.** Frustrated by Sotel's inability to maintain or build links, the
  government granted a **20-year PPP concession** to a new entity, **SudaChad Telecom**, to build,
  operate and maintain a link from the Sudanese border to Cameroon in the west. Camtel and Sudatel
  eventually joined the entity, but **no progress has been made**. The plan secured a commitment
  of **20.5 billion CFA** from the [[european-union]] and the [[african-development-bank]].
  Source:
  [[2026-02-16 Waking the dead - Slow road to extinction for Africa's former telco incumbents 11]].
- **The Airtel corridor and the sovereignty reversal (April 2025).** The government signed an
  agreement with Sotel and [[airtel-africa]] to open a **second N'Djamena–Mberé fibre corridor**;
  the following month Camtel committed to building the connecting section on its side of the
  border. The government then **had second thoughts and sought to revise the contract terms** for
  reasons of *"national sovereignty"* and strategic interest. Southwood: the government's aim is
  unclear, but likely concerns its capital contribution being too small to guarantee control, and
  Airtel's desire to manage the link. See [[sovereignty]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-16 Waking the dead - Slow road to extinction for Africa's former telco incumbents 11]].
- **Rehabilitation of the same corridor (2026-02).** Ecofin separately reports the
  **N'Djamena–Mberé fibre link rehabilitation** under way, to diversify international connectivity
  routes via Cameroon, alongside planned regional links to Niger, Nigeria, Algeria, Libya and
  Egypt. Source: [[2026-02-13 Chad Plans 50 Telecom Sites, 50 km of Fiber in N'Djamena]].
- **The N'Djamena build (announced 2026-02-11).** The government announced **50 new telecom
  sites** in N'Djamena and **50 km of fibre-to-the-home**, to be delivered *through* Sotel — with
  **no timeline, financing or coverage detail** provided by Minister [[boukar-michel]]. See
  [[infra.connect]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-13 Chad Plans 50 Telecom Sites, 50 km of Fiber in N'Djamena]].
- **Privatisation, repeatedly announced.** The government first announced its intention to
  reprivatise Sotel in **2014** and has re-announced several times. In **February 2025** the CEO
  announced the process again, offering a **60% stake**: *"The selected advisory bank will be
  responsible for the operator's valuation process, proposing a sale strategy and facilitating
  contacts with potential buyers."* **There is no sign of the advisory bank having been
  appointed** (as of 2026-02-16). Source:
  [[2026-02-16 Waking the dead - Slow road to extinction for Africa's former telco incumbents 11]].
- **The 5G / Salam Mobile ambition.** In **2024** the minister responsible for telecoms visited
  [[huawei]] in China and requested funding for a **5G platform** that Sotel might use to relaunch
  its former mobile service, **Salam Mobile** — sold rhetorically as a route to more competition
  and better prices. See [[geopol.china]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-16 Waking the dead - Slow road to extinction for Africa's former telco incumbents 11]].
  *Context that reporting omits: a Salam network core was already inside a China-financed programme.*
- **Sotel is a build target of [[pmice]], not just a bystander to it.** The China Eximbank loan's
  stated scope included **200 sites for Sotel (2020-05-08)**, and PMICE covers modernising **Groupe
  Sotel through installation of the network core of its mobile arm, SALAM (2025-01-10)** — under
  [[huawei]] as contractor. So the 2024 ministerial approach to Huawei for a 5G platform to relaunch
  Salam Mobile was made to **the vendor already contracted to install Salam's core**. **No source
  held reports how many of the 200 sites were delivered, or whether the SALAM core was
  commissioned (as of 2026-07-20).** See [[geopol.china]], [[infra.connect]]. Sources:
  [[2020-05-08-ciomag-tchad-pret-eximbank-chine-tic]],
  [[2025-01-10-agenceecofin-tchad-centre-donnees-national-finalisation]].

## Reading it

Southwood's verdict, recorded as a named analytical view: in public-purpose terms Sotel is
*"clearly a failure as a vehicle to drive a solution to the country's need for reliable,
cost-effective, redundant fibre links"* — the need for a PPP special-purpose vehicle proves it. In
for-profit terms, he sees a case only *"perhaps as a wholesale and retail fibre operator,"* caught
in a chicken-and-egg trap: that business case cannot work without the international links Sotel
has not built. He notes the [[tchad-connexion-2030]] funding shows the government **has** the
capital to solve this, and concludes: *"maybe the Government finds performative rhetoric easier
than actually getting stuff done."* Source:
[[2026-02-16 Waking the dead - Slow road to extinction for Africa's former telco incumbents 11]].
