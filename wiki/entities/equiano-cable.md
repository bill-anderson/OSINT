# Equiano subsea cable

**Equiano** is a submarine cable **independently built and wholly owned by
[[google]]**, running down the Atlantic coast of West Africa from **Sesimbra,
Portugal** to **Cape Town, South Africa**. Announced in 2019 (Google's third
intercontinental and 14th submarine-cable investment), it is named after the
Nigerian-born abolitionist Olaudah Equiano, part of Google's alphabetical naming
of its wholly-owned cables (Curie, Dunant, Equiano, Firmina, Grace Hopper). It
uses space-division multiplexing (SDM) to reach a capacity of **144 Tbps** —
which [[equiano-mainone-nwankwo-2025]] reports exceeds the combined capacity of
all previously existing cables serving Africa, and roughly 18× MainOne's 8 Tbps.

## Landings and the disintermediation model

Equiano first landed at **Lomé, Togo** (March 2022), then **Lagos, Nigeria**
(April 2022), followed by **Swakopmund, Namibia**, **Cape Town, South Africa**
and **Sesimbra, Portugal**. Crucially, Google does **not** land the cable itself:
it owns and controls the undersea trunk in international waters but partners with
local **landing partners** (in Nigeria, WIOCC/OADC, terminating at the OADC Lagos
data centre) to handle country-specific regulation and terrestrial connection.
[[equiano-mainone-nwankwo-2025]] frames this as deliberate **disintermediation** —
Google keeps regulatory and physical exposure minimal while capturing the
strategic international-bandwidth layer, its real goal being cloud-service
access and user/data acquisition (the "Next Billion Users") rather than direct
cable profit.

## Capacity partners

In ~March 2022, days after the Togo landing, **[[liquid-c2]]** (Liquid
Intelligent Technologies, an [[econet-wireless]]/[[cassava-technologies]]
subsidiary) acquired a **fibre pair on Equiano** carrying up to **12 Tbps**,
planning to interconnect Equiano's landing stations east-to-west across its
100,000+ km African fibre network to create a new Asia–Africa–US IP route.

## Impact claims and framing

A Google-sponsored assessment (Africa Practice / Genesis Analytics, 2021)
projected Equiano would cut Nigerian retail internet prices by **21%**, improve
speeds ~6×, indirectly create ~1.6 million Nigerian jobs and add **$10.1 billion**
to Nigerian GDP by 2025; comparable figures were cited for South Africa (180,000
jobs) and Namibia (21,000) by [[jane-munga]]/Carnegie. Both scholarly sources
read Equiano critically — as forcing older cables into obsolescence and
entrenching Google's dominance across the digital-economy stack, with
[[equiano-mainone-nwankwo-2025]] noting the cable's outsized capacity "confers on
Google a superior position." Equiano is part of Google's **Africa Connect**
infrastructure programme, alongside [[umoja-cable]] and the Johannesburg Google
Cloud region.
