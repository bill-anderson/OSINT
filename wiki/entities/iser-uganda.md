# Initiative for Social and Economic Rights (ISER), Uganda

**A Ugandan economic and social rights organisation** — lead applicant in the
constitutional challenge to Uganda's mandatory national-ID requirement for access to
public services, dismissed by the High Court in June 2025.

## The case

***Initiative for Social and Economic Rights (ISER) & 2 Others v Attorney General &
NIRA*, HCT-00-CV-MC-0066-2022, High Court of Uganda — dismissed 10 June 2025.**

The applicants challenged the **mandatory requirement of a NIN or National ID
([[ndaga-muntu]]) under the [[registration-of-persons-act-2015]]** to access services
including **healthcare and social protection**, arguing the system disproportionately
excludes vulnerable populations including **older persons and women**.

**Amicus curiae** was filed by **[[cipesa]], [[article-19]] and [[access-now]]**, with
expert opinions from **Prof. Tom Fisher, Dr Reetika Khera and Prof. Philip Alston** — all
unavailing.

**The Court held, verbatim:** *"Uganda's ID system is not 'digital' because it operates
primarily offline. A person cannot be denied access simply because of lack of internet
connectivity."* It further held that the deponents *"appear to me to be few and isolated
cases"*, and that the applicants had **not adduced sufficient evidence of "actual and
systemic exclusion"** from [[sage-uganda]].

**The judgment itself is not held by this wiki (GAP-UGA-001)** — the account below is
advocacy analysis, not the primary.

## Activity in this wiki

- **DRAA's critique (2025-09-13) — attribute as advocacy analysis.** Writing for the
  Digital Rights Alliance Africa, **Michael Aboneka** argues the ruling legitimises a
  system that denies access to those without a National ID. His central rebuttal to the
  Court's "offline and non-digital" finding: the government's own **5-year Digital
  Transformation Roadmap, launched 17 August 2023**, shifts service provision to DPI —
  the National ID system, mobile money, **[[efris]]**, the Express Penalty Scheme, Digital
  Tax Stamps and Payment Registration Numbers — **all of which require authentication**,
  cutting directly against the finding. He further notes the **Ministry of Education**
  mandates learner registration on an **EMIS** portal requiring a NIN, excluding those
  without from enumeration, planning and budgeting; and that **[[sage-uganda]]** benefits
  only those on the register. He argues the Court relied heavily on respondents'
  submissions and offered **no alternatives** (baptism cards, voters' cards, residence
  identity cards) to remedy exclusion, deepening exclusion of rural communities, older
  persons, women and the digitally illiterate — and that Uganda's DPI lacks strong legal
  safeguards, comprehensive data protection and independent oversight, challenging its
  compliance with the World Bank's **ID4D principle of universal coverage**. See
  [[gov.legislate]], [[gov.protect]], [[include.access]]. Source:
  [[2025-09-13 Uganda's Digital ID Barrier Worsens as Court Delivers Blow to Access]].

## Contradiction — open, not resolved

The same piece cites **18 million (about 40%) of the population** as lacking a National ID.
**Resolved (2026-07-20): this is NIRA's own figure, not the author's estimate** - ED Rosemary
Kisembo told COSASE on 2024-07-01 that against a census population of 45.9m, "we haven't
registered about 18.1 million individuals... most of those unregistered are **children under 17**."
The finding is one of **framing**, not fabrication: a whole-population statistic deployed in an
adult-access argument. Against the **23,154,716 adults aged 18+ (2024-05-09)** the adult gap is
~**2.0m, ~9%**.
