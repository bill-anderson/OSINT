# Initiative for Social and Economic Rights (ISER), Uganda

**A Ugandan economic and social rights organisation** — lead applicant in the
constitutional challenge to Uganda's mandatory national-ID requirement for access to
public services, dismissed by the High Court in June 2025.

## The case

***Initiative for Social and Economic Rights (ISER), The Unwanted Witness (U) Ltd & Health
Equity and Policy Initiative Ltd v Attorney General & NIRA*, Miscellaneous Cause No. 0086 of
2022, High Court of Uganda at Kampala (Civil Division), Wamala J — dismissed 10 June 2025,
each party bearing its own costs.**

*(**Citation corrected 2026-07-20.** This page previously carried **HCT-00-CV-MC-0066-2022**,
the form used in secondary coverage and by the applicants themselves. The ruling is headed
**Miscellaneous Cause No. 0086 of 2022**; the court's own number governs.)*

ISER was **first applicant**, with [[unwanted-witness]] second and **Health Equity and Policy
Initiative Ltd (HEAPI)** third. Proceeding under **Article 50** of the Constitution and the
**Human Rights (Enforcement) Act**, they challenged use of the National Identification Register
as primary data source and mandatory means of identification under **ss.65(1)(j) and 66** of the
[[registration-of-persons-act-2015]] for **[[sage-uganda]] benefits**, **public health services**
and **proof of nationality**. They filed **32 affidavits**, 21 from named residents of Amudat,
Kumi, Nebbi, Buliisa, Busia, Kayunga and Namayingo.

**Amici corrected.** The Court admitted **two** sets of amici curiae: **Prof. Philip Alston**,
and **[[cipesa]] & 2 Others**. **Dr Thomas Fisher** (Privacy International), **Dr Reetika Khera**
and **Diana Gichengo** were **deponents for the applicants, not amicus experts**. This page
previously recorded a single amicus by [[cipesa]], [[article-19]] and [[access-now]] "with expert
opinions from" those three — two distinct roles conflated. *([[article-19]] and [[access-now]] are
not named in the ruling.)*

**What the Court actually held.** On mandatoriness the applicants **won the point**: *"In view of
the head note and the express provision of section 66 of the Act, it is undebatable as to whether
the provision is mandatory or not."* On exclusion they lost on evidence — the deponents *"appear
to me to be few and isolated cases that could be handled through proper follow up, guidance and
assistance rather than as constituting evidence of a failed national ID system."* On the nature of
the system, verbatim: *"I am convinced that the Ugandan national ID system cannot be categorized as
a digital ID system since in majority of cases it operates offline. A person cannot be denied
access simply because of lack of internet connectivity."* That finding rested on **unrebutted**
State expert evidence — *"No contrary evidence was adduced by the applicants."* On health, the
Court found **no legal requirement** exists at all, health not being listed in s.66(2).

**GAP-UGA-001 closed (2026-07-20): the judgment is held.** Source:
[[2025-06-10-iser-v-attorney-general-nira-uganda-digital-id-ruling]]. The account below is
attributed advocacy analysis and is no longer the wiki's route to the ruling's content.

## Activity in this wiki

- **DRAA's critique (2025-09-13) — attribute as advocacy analysis.** Writing for the
  Digital Rights Alliance Africa, **Michael Aboneka** argues the ruling legitimises a
  system that denies access to those without a National ID. His central rebuttal to the
  Court's "offline and non-digital" finding: the government's own **5-year Digital
  Transformation Roadmap, launched 17 August 2023**, shifts service provision to DPI —
  the National ID system, mobile money, **[[efris]]**, the Express Penalty Scheme, Digital
  Tax Stamps and Payment Registration Numbers — **all of which require authentication**,
  cutting directly against the finding. He further notes the **Ministry of Education**
  mandates learner registration on an **EMIS** portal requiring a NIN, excluding those
  without from enumeration, planning and budgeting; and that **[[sage-uganda]]** benefits
  only those on the register. He argues the Court relied heavily on respondents'
  submissions and offered **no alternatives** (baptism cards, voters' cards, residence
  identity cards) to remedy exclusion, deepening exclusion of rural communities, older
  persons, women and the digitally illiterate — and that Uganda's DPI lacks strong legal
  safeguards, comprehensive data protection and independent oversight, challenging its
  compliance with the World Bank's **ID4D principle of universal coverage**. See
  [[gov.legislate]], [[gov.protect]], [[include.access]]. Source:
  [[2025-09-13 Uganda's Digital ID Barrier Worsens as Court Delivers Blow to Access]].

## Contradiction — open, not resolved

The same piece cites **18 million (about 40%) of the population** as lacking a National ID.
**Resolved (2026-07-20): this is NIRA's own figure, not the author's estimate** - ED Rosemary
Kisembo told COSASE on 2024-07-01 that against a census population of 45.9m, "we haven't
registered about 18.1 million individuals... most of those unregistered are **children under 17**."
The finding is one of **framing**, not fabrication: a whole-population statistic deployed in an
adult-access argument. Against the **23,154,716 adults aged 18+ (2024-05-09)** the adult gap is
~**2.0m, ~9%**.
