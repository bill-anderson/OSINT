# UN Independent International Scientific Panel on AI

The UN Independent International Scientific Panel on AI, established under Resolution A/RES/79/325 and framed as an "IPCC for AI," comprises 40 experts. It matters here for the notable African representation shaping global AI science advice.

## Profile
- Eight of the 40 members are African: Vukosi Marivate (South Africa; African-language NLP), Rita Orji (Nigeria), Adji Bousso Dieng, Joyce Nakatumba-Nabende, Tegawendé Bissyandé, Girmaw Abebe Tadesse, Mennatallah El-Assady and Awa Bousso Dramé.
- Situated against the African AI communities Masakhane and Deep Learning Indaba.
- Distinct from the UN High-Level Advisory Body on AI.

## Activity in this wiki
- **Preliminary Report presented in Geneva (2026-07).** The Panel presented its first **Preliminary Report** — an evidence-based assessment co-chaired by **Yoshua Bengio** and **Maria Ressa**, established under GA resolution 79/325 — at the inaugural **Global Dialogue on AI Governance** (Geneva, 9 Jul 2026, alongside AI for Good and WSIS Forum 2026). The **[[african-union]]** Commission (Lerato Mataboge) met the panel's African scientists (Marivate, Tadesse, Orji, El-Assady) to align continental scientific expertise with the AU's push for a coordinated African position on global AI governance. See [[tech.ai]], [[gov.standards]], [[XAF]]. Sources: [[2026-07-01 Independent International Scientific Panel on AI - Preliminary Report 2026]], [[2026-07-09 African Union pushes for unified African voice at Global AI Governance Dialogue]].

- **Independence critique (2026-07).** Researchers at Umeå University's AI Policy Lab argue the
  Preliminary Report **does not establish the Panel's independence** — neither from industry nor
  from states: funders are named (Germany, Japan, Spain, the Omidyar Network Fund) but with no
  amounts, conditions or member interest-declarations; the report **smooths over disagreement**
  ("no member endorses every point") despite a mandate to document consensus *and* dissent; and it
  leans on **developer-produced evidence** it elsewhere flags as a structural weakness. An IPCC
  comparison is drawn (state "line-by-line" filtering of the Summary for Policymakers). A wider TPP
  survey of the 2026 UN AI for Good Summit frames the same tension — 193 states, US/EU/China
  rivalry, and the Global South's demand for real power over AI rules rather than "a performative
  seat." See [[gov.standards]], [[capacity.research]]. Sources:
