# Network Startup Resource Center (NSRC)

The **Network Startup Resource Center (NSRC)** is a US-based (University of
Oregon, Eugene) non-profit that has provided pro-bono engineering assistance and
training to developing-country network operators since it was formalised in 1992
(NSF-supported; roots in late-1980s volunteer networking in southern Africa). In
this wiki it is a standing **historical-reference and connectivity-data asset**
for the deep history of internet connectivity in Africa.

## What the asset is

The **"Stepping Stones to the Internet in Africa"** page (nsrc.org) pairs a
narrative history with an **interactive connectivity map** (hosted at
pages.uoregon.edu) tracing, country by country, the milestone moments of African
connectivity and the transitions between messaging technologies. On the map's
left panel it surfaces archival records of the original emails and "first ping"
data exchanged between NSRC and African internet pioneers.

## Key datable facts we draw from it

- **First full TCP/IP internet connection to Africa: October 1991** — Tunisia to
  France; **South Africa** followed in **November 1991** (to Portland, Oregon).
- **Eritrea was the last African country to connect** via TCP/IP, in **November
  2000**.
- Pre-internet stepping-stone protocols: **FidoNet** and **UUCP** store-and-
  forward messaging in the 1980s–1990s. The **first known FidoNet email from
  Africa** was sent **October 1988** by Prof. Pat Terry (Rhodes University, South
  Africa) to NSRC founder Randy Bush. Not every country followed FidoNet→UUCP→
  TCP/IP; later adopters often leapt straight to TCP/IP.
- The **Association for Progressive Communications (APC)** helped establish
  FidoNet nodes across the continent — connecting this asset to the
  [[jagun-sat3-open-access-2008]] APC line of work.
- **End-2022: ~600 million Africans online (~43% of the population)**, ~60% via
  mobile.

NSRC also maintains related open assets referenced here — an African
undersea-cable history (manypossibilities.net) and a terrestrial-fibre map
(afterfiber.nsrc.org). Use NSRC for pre-2000 connectivity chronology and
first-connection dates.
