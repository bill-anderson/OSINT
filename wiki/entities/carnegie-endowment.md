# Carnegie Endowment for International Peace

The **Carnegie Endowment for International Peace** is a US-based foreign-policy
think tank. In this wiki it is profiled as a producer of **analytical policy
research on African digital infrastructure and governance**, most directly
through the work of its Africa technology-policy analyst **[[jane-munga]]**.

## Why we cite it

Carnegie's April 2025 paper **"Beneath the Waves: Addressing Vulnerabilities in
Africa's Undersea Digital Infrastructure"** (Jane Munga, 2025-04-03) is a
reference-quality analysis of subsea-cable resilience for Africa. Its key
findings, which the wiki draws on:

- **77 cable systems** land in Africa, spread across **37 of 38 coastal
  countries** (Eritrea the exception); **Egypt, South Africa, Djibouti, Nigeria
  and Kenya** are the regional hubs with the most landings.
- The **March 2024** four-cable outage (WACS, ACE, MainOne, SAT-3) knocked ten
  African countries offline; **Nigeria lost over $590 million in four days**.
  A May 2024 East African outage hit EASSy and Seacom.
- Repairs cost **$1–3 million per cable**; fewer than ~100 repair ships exist
  worldwide and **only three serve Africa** (only the *Léon Thévenin* is
  Africa-based).
- Recommends **redundancy** (route/landing-station diversification, cross-border
  circuits, interconnections) and **harmonised governance** at national, regional
  and continental levels, with the [[african-union]] championing a continental
  framework and drawing on the [[international-cable-protection-committee]] (ICPC)
  and the ITU/ICPC advisory body co-chaired by [[bosun-tijani]].

Carnegie also maintains the **Africa Technology Policy Tracker** (a regulatory
database referenced in the paper) and has published companion analyses on subsea
cables in Southeast Asia and Europe. The paper is read through a **digital-
sovereignty** lens: Africa needs to "think and act as one" to secure the physical
foundation of its digital economy.
