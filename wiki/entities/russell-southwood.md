# Russell Southwood

Veteran analyst of African telecommunications, ICT and the digital economy, and one of the longest-standing independent voices covering the sector. Founder of **Balancing Act** and **Smart Monkey TV**, he built an evidence base and reporting franchise (including the long-running *Balancing Act* newsletter and the AfricaCom-era analyst circuit) that tracked the continent's mobile revolution from its earliest days. He now publishes analysis through his Substack.

He is the author of **_Less Walk, More Talk_** (Wiley), on the Celtel team that pioneered pan-African mobile, and **_Africa 2.0_** (Manchester University Press, 2022), a history of the internet and mobile in Africa.

## What he argues in this wiki

Southwood writes state-of-the-market and structural analysis rather than event reporting. Recurring theses across his pieces here:

- **MNOs are becoming data/holding companies.** As data revenue overtakes voice in the leading markets (Ghana, Kenya, Nigeria, South Africa), operators are spinning off towers, data centres, wholesale (MTN's Bayobab) and mobile money, while losing "digital life" verticals to the hyperscalers. He urges neutral, arms-length, app-based, interoperable platforms plus JV/network-sharing to bear fibre and AI CAPEX.
- **Telecoms regulators must repurpose.** Born of 1990s liberalisation, they are no longer the central market institution and should reorganise around digital infrastructure as a national "nervous system" — via single/sub-regional data-sovereignty markets, licence passporting, data-rollover mandates, market-based infrastructure rights, spectrum reform and sandboxes.
- **The ICT4D "deep past" still shapes the present.** Development actors, liberalisation and mobile were transformative, but network effects re-created monopolies and incumbents extracted rents from landing stations — a two-speed market that persists.
- **AI is a demand "wild card."** He reframes Africa's AI debate around demand: consumer inference makes Africa a product-taker, local training is nascent and data-constrained, and supply-side GPU deals risk weak anchor-tenant demand (e.g. Microsoft–G42 Kenya).
- **Former state incumbents are stranded assets** undone by failures of vision, capital, skills and patronage.
