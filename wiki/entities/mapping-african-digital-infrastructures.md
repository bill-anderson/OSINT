# Mapping African Digital Infrastructures (RIA study, 2022)

A single **[[research-ict-africa]]** study, funded by a 2021 digital-infrastructure grant,
investigating how African participation in the development, maintenance and application of
global open-source digital infrastructure can be enhanced. The study comprised a literature
review, a quantitative study, a qualitative study and an open-source policy case study, and
was published across **three blog parts** (the three sources cited above). Named authors:
**Senka Hadzic, Sarah Kiden** (Part 1), **David Johnson** (Part 2), **Amreesh Phokeer**
(Part 3). Read with a **[[colonialism]]** lens — the Global-North origin and orientation of
digital infrastructure is the study's framing problem.

## Scope and findings

- **Part 1 (qualitative):** barriers to African-led open-source projects — funding
  (reliance on donations/personal funds), motivations, skills, and francophone/language
  diversity; flagship projects (iNethi, Village Telco, Grassroots Economics, Ushahidi,
  Masakhane, OpenUp, Crane Cloud, OSCA, Translate.org) concentrated in **South Africa and
  Kenya**.
- **Part 2 (quantitative GitHub analysis):** very low but rising African contribution;
  **Nigeria #1** by contributing users (2019–2020), then ZAF, Egypt, Kenya.
- **Part 3 (Internet standards):** negligible African participation in **IETF** standards
  development, driven by technical, financial ("hallway talk"/travel funding) and language
  barriers; **[[internet-society-africa-history]]** (ISOC) IETF-Africa sponsors
  participation.

## Key reference figures (captured — do NOT promote into concept/place pages)

- Africa was **2.3%** of active GitHub users in 2021 vs **31.5%** for North America
  (GitHub Octoverse 2021); contributing-author share grew ~0.5% (2010) → ~2.7% (2020).
- African authors accounted for **~0.26%** of published RFCs (15 RFCs + 9 drafts of 9,170
  total, as of 2022-01-04).
- Average Kenyan developer age ~23.
