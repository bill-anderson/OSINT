The **Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 (NDPA)** is Nigeria's primary personal-data
protection statute. It establishes data-processing principles and obligations and
creates the **Nigeria Data Protection Commission ([[ndpc]])** as the regulator,
superseding the earlier NITDA-administered Nigeria Data Protection Regulation
(NDPR). Its enactment is described by commentators as a "watershed moment" for
Nigeria's digital economy.

- **Issuing body / status:** Act of the National Assembly, 2023; in force. Official
  text is hosted by CERT-NG.
- **Regulator:** the [[ndpc]] (see accountability critique below).
- **Implementing guidance:** the NDPC's **General Application and Implementation
  Directive (GAID) 2025**, issued 20 March 2025, plus the 2024 "Updated Guidance
  Notice on Registration of Data Controllers and Data Processors of Major
  Importance."

## What it mandates

The NDPA requires that personal data be processed on a **lawful basis** and in
line with core principles — fairness, transparency, **purpose limitation** and
**data minimisation**. Consent must be **freely given** and cannot be validly
obtained under coercion. It treats certain categories as **sensitive personal
data** (including data revealing political affiliation), demands appropriate
technical and organisational security measures, imposes **breach-notification**
duties to the regulator within set timelines (and to affected individuals in
high-risk cases), and expects **Data Protection Officers** and **Data Protection
Impact Assessments (DPIAs)** for high-risk processing. The NDPC is empowered to
promote compliance, receive complaints, investigate, issue compliance orders and
apply administrative penalties. Limited exemptions exist, but political and
electoral activity is not automatically exempt.

## Live application: NIN and party e-registration (2026)

A Guardian Nigeria legal analysis (El-shaddai Ikeh, Data Protection & Technology
Lawyer, 2026-02-02) used the NDPA to interrogate the **APC's** nationwide
membership **e-registration** (launched ~23 Jan 2026), which requires a valid
[[nimc]] NIN, alongside allegations that some Enugu State civil servants were
pressured to submit NINs tied to salary/employment (the APC denied coercion).
The piece frames the NIN not as "just another field" but a persistent identifier
that keys a person across systems — raising lawful-basis, free-consent,
sensitive-data (political affiliation), transparency, security and
purpose-limitation questions under the Act, and pointing to NIMC's consent-based
**NINAuth** authentication as a lower-retention alternative to mass NIN storage.

## Live application: INEC privacy-policy suit (2026-07)

[[digital-rights-lawyers-initiative]] (DRLI) and lawyer Solomon Etisang sued [[inec]] at the
Federal High Court, Yenagoa, for failing to publish a website privacy policy — pleading
breaches of NDPA **ss.24(1)(a), 24(3) and 27(1)** and **s.37** of the Constitution, ten
months after an FIJ investigation exposed the gap (and following a May-2025 voter-record
leak). An early test of whether the Act's controller obligations bind public bodies. Source:
[[2026-07-10 FIJ Showed That INEC Broke Data Law. Digital Rights Lawyers Sue the Commission 10 Months Later]].

**Cross-border-transfer tension (2026-07).** Analysts note the Act's cross-border-transfer
provisions sit awkwardly with the [[cbn]]'s June-2026 payment-data-localisation directive
(data in-country by 1 Jan 2027): domestication is not the same as protection, and forced
onshoring raises its own compliance questions. Source:
[[2026-07-10 CBN's Data Localisation Directive Puts Nigerian Fintechs In A Cloud Dilemma]].

## Accountability critique: "the next frontier" vs. a weakened regulator

Two 2026 commentaries frame the Act's limits:

- **Data protection as economic infrastructure.** Specialist **Samiat Akande**
  (ThisDay, 2026-03-04) argues data protection is now "the next frontier" for
  Nigeria's digital economy — a "trust deficit" issue where "innovation without
  protection is exploitation." She credits the NDPA and NDPC but stresses that
  **legislation alone does not guarantee trust**: it needs institutional capacity
  and independence, real (not box-ticking) private-sector compliance, public
  literacy, and meaningful enforcement, with the EU's **GDPR**
  cited as the standard-setting model. (See [[european-union]] for GDPR as
  external template.)
- **Accountability gaps.** Dataphyte reporting (Adeyemi Okediran, 2026-02-27)
  notes the Act "created a regulator, then weakened it" by placing the [[ndpc]]
  under **ministerial control** — a regulator that "needs ministerial clearance
  before acting" is criticised as not independent (lawyer Habeeb Eyinade). It
  situates the NDPA amid wider digital-rights strain: the 2021 seven-month
  Twitter/X suspension (LCCI estimated over ₦10.72tn cost), a Freedom House
  *Freedom on the Net 2024* "partly free" score of 59/100, the NITDA Code of
  Practice (48-hour takedown; 13.5m+ accounts deactivated by mid-2025), the
  Cybercrimes Act 2015 s.24 used against journalists, and a 2024 alleged exposure
  of up to **43 million** NIN records (Paradigm Initiative) that produced
  litigation but no transparent breach-notification — underscoring the gap
  between the Act on paper and enforcement in practice.
