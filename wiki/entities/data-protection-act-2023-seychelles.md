# Seychelles Data Protection Act, 2023 (Act 24 of 2023)

**Seychelles' omnibus data-protection statute**, enforced by the
[[information-commission-seychelles]] as the designated authority (s.5). It **repeals the Data
Protection Act 2003** — one of the earliest such attempts in Africa, but a law that, in TechHive
Advisory's words, was "enacted but never implemented in practice." The 2023 Act is the first
Seychelles privacy law with an actual enforcer, an expanded set of data-subject rights and a
cross-border transfer regime. See [[gov.protect]], [[gov.legislate]], [[SYC]].

## Enactment chain — settled from the gazetted text

The gazetted primary is now held, and it resolves what two secondary accounts had left looking
like a conflict. The **two December 2023 dates are two different events**, not competing reports
of one:

- **Passed by the National Assembly — 5 December 2023.** Certified by Mrs Tania Isaac, Clerk to
  the National Assembly ("I certify that this is a correct copy of the Bill which was passed by
  the National Assembly on 5th December, 2023").
- **Presidential assent — 19 December 2023.** The signature block reads *"I assent / Wavel
  Ramkalawan, President, 19th December, 2023"*. This is what TechHive Advisory reported as
  "enactment" (source: [[2024-06-04 Review of Seychelles DPA]]), following the
  [[dict-seychelles]] public consultation on the Bill opened in **August 2023**.
- **Coming into operation — 22 December 2023.** The gazette header carries the enacting date
  **"[22 December 2023]"**. This is the date Appleby correctly attached the transitional period
  to (source: [[2024-03-25 Data Protection Law Now In Force In Seychelles]]).
- **Citation — Act 24 of 2023**, published in the Supplement to the Official Gazette.

## Transitional period (s.56) — elapsed

**s.56(1)** verbatim: a data controller or processor "that was or is processing any personal data
before the coming into operation of this Act and does not conform to the provisions of this Act,
shall, within a period of **18 months from the coming into operation** of this Act, ensure that
its activities conform". **s.56(2)** makes failure to comply **an offence**.

The clock therefore runs from **commencement (22 December 2023)**, not assent — expiring
**22 June 2025**. As of **2026-07-20** that window is long past: Seychelles controllers and
processors are **subject to the Act in full**, not sitting in a transition. **No source held here
reports what happened at expiry** — whether compliance was achieved, extended, or simply not
enforced. **s.55** repeals the former Data Protection Act (Cap 57).

## Key provisions

- **Scope (s.3) — and the territorial gap.** Applies to processing by automated or
  semi-automated means, and to data forming part of a filing system, **within the territory of
  Seychelles**. Appleby reads it as covering individuals and public and private entities that
  collect, process or store personal data in Seychelles, excluding criminal investigation by
  relevant authorities, national-security matters, and purely personal processing. **TechHive
  Advisory's analyst criticism:** unlike laws with clear extraterritorial application, s.3
  "diverges" — the territorial scope suggests the Act **may not** reach controllers and
  processors outside Seychelles, and the provision is similar to the repealed Act's. *(Attributed
  analysis, not a settled reading.)* See [[sovereignty]], [[dpi.exchange]].
- **Principles and consent.** GDPR-adjacent: explicit consent before collection and processing;
  duties to inform data subjects of the purpose and to ensure data quality, security and
  confidentiality.
- **Data-subject rights.** To be informed, access, rectify, erase; restrict or object to
  processing; and demand **compensation for unlawful processing**.
- **Retention (s.16).** Data held only as long as needed, then deleted, archived or anonymised
  (masking, pseudonymisation, encryption, or removal of PII).
- **Accountability (s.21).** Data protection by design, accurate records of processing
  activities, formal contracts with processors and third parties.
- **Sensitive personal data (s.22).** Prohibited except on consent or specified grounds
  (employment/social-protection law, vital interests, non-profit processing, legal claims,
  public interest, archival purposes, or covering misconduct/inadequate behaviour — the last
  **not clearly defined**).
- **Children's data (s.23).** A child is anyone **under 18**; parental/guardian consent
  required. **No mechanism** for verifying a child's age or the validity of third-party consent.
- **Breach notification (ss.43-44).** **72 hours** after discovering a breach to notify the
  supervisory authority, extension possible with an explanation for the delay. Controllers must
  promptly notify data subjects where a breach is expected to negatively affect a significant
  number of them. The Act specifies **neither** the extension time frame **nor** the
  subject-notification deadline. See [[infra.cybersec]].
- **Safe custody (s.33).** One or two designated persons responsible for compliance,
  **non-delegable**, contact details of at least one made public.
- **Logging and blocking (ss.38-39).** Logs of consultations and disclosures, open to the
  Commission on request; access blocked to data subject to rectification or erasure.
- **Data protection officer (ss.45-46).** Required where core activities involve regular,
  systematic **large-scale monitoring** or large-scale processing of special categories.
  Appleby frames the trigger as organisation size and nature of processing.
- **Cross-border transfers.** Measures regulating transfer of personal data outside Seychelles;
  entities must adhere to specified safeguards and mechanisms. See [[dpi.exchange]],
  [[gov.standards]].
- **Other controller duties (Appleby).** Publish the type of information held; adopt a privacy
  policy covering processing and transfer; conduct **Data Protection Impact Assessments** for
  high-risk processing.
