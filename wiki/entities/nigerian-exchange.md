# Nigerian Exchange (NGX)

Nigeria's principal securities exchange, and — for this wiki — the **statistical authority on retail
participation** in Nigerian capital markets through its *Domestic & Foreign Portfolio Report*.

## As a data source

The **Domestic & Foreign Portfolio Investment Report**, prepared monthly by **[[ngx-regulation|NGX
Regulation Limited]]** from returns polled from 100% of Active Trading Licence Holders, is the underlying
series behind the retail-investing figures the wiki holds. The **May 2026 edition is now held directly**
(as at 2026-05-31), so the figures below rest on the primary rather than trade-press accounts of it.

- **Domestic retail investors traded ₦2.85974 trillion of equities, January–May 2026 (cumulative
  year-to-date)**, up **138.76%** on the same period a year earlier (₦1.19775 trillion, Jan–May 2025).
  The YTD framing matters: **May 2026 alone was ₦0.72627 trillion** retail. (NGX, as at 2026-05-31.)
- **Institutional investors traded ₦4.06178 trillion** over the same Jan–May 2026 period — still the
  larger share; **May 2026 alone was ₦1.03337 trillion**, or **59% of domestic activity that month**.
  (NGX, as at 2026-05-31.)
- At the report's own **₦1,373.25/$ (NAFEM, 31 May 2026)**, those YTD figures are **$2.08bn** retail and
  **$2.96bn** institutional. The "**$4.06 billion**" that circulates in trade-press accounts is the naira
  figure carried unconverted into the dollar column — **not** a competing measurement.
- **Retail investors accounted for 36.22% of all trading activity** (NGX data, May 2026).
- **Domestic investors were 87.67% of total transactions YTD (₦6.9215tn) against foreign 12.33%
  (₦0.9734tn)** as at 2026-05-31 — foreign participation down from **29.17%** in Jan–May 2025. Over the
  longer run the report puts domestic transactions up **160.83%** (₦3.5560tn in 2007 → ₦9.2747tn in 2025)
  and foreign up **329.87%** (₦0.6156tn → ₦2.6475tn). (NGX, as at 2026-05-31.)
- **Market capitalisation ~$113.8bn** (as reported 2026-07-18), against a population of over 200
  million; the Johannesburg Stock Exchange, serving just over 60 million people, was put at roughly
  **$1.46 trillion** on the same date. *(The held source gives the NGX figure twice, as $113.79bn in
  prose and $113.02bn in a data widget. The 2026-07-20 reconcile found this is **not a factual dispute**:
  both are the same naira market capitalisation converted at slightly different FX points. The underlying
  naira series sits in the NGX weekly market report, which the wiki **does not hold as of 2026-07-20**.)*
- Nigerian equities **returned 67% in dollar terms in 2026 to date** (as of 2026-07-18), reported as
  the best performance among the 92 exchanges Bloomberg tracks, ahead of South Korea.

## Digital access

The exchange launched **NGX Invest in 2024** to make public offerings more accessible to retail
investors — the exchange's own move toward the app-based access that platforms like **[[bamboo]]**,
**[[cowrywise]]** and **[[meristem-securities]]** built independently. Broker league position is now
being reshaped by those platforms: **Bamboo reported overtaking CardinalStone as NGX's largest broker
by weighted market share in April 2026** (11.13%, 542,582 trades in the month — company figures).

## Governance friction

The unresolved retail complaint on file is **dividend delivery**, which sits with **registrars**
rather than the exchange or brokers: inconsistent recording of shareholder names delays payment, and
investors direct the complaints to their brokers ([[richmond-bassey]], 2026-07-18). A registry
data-quality problem presenting as a market-confidence problem.

## Historical baseline — the 2008 crash

As the **Nigerian Stock Exchange** (the pre-2021 predecessor name), the market's crisis-era record is
documented by the **[[cbn]]**'s own contemporaneous account, drawing on SEC/NSE/CBN monthly bulletins:
the All-Share Index peaked at **65,653.4 (February 2008)** and closed at **31,450.8 (December 2008)**;
market capitalisation peaked at **₦12.50tn (February 2008)** and closed at **₦6.96tn (31 December
2008)** — a loss of **₦5.55tn, or 44.4%, between March and end-2008**. The widely repeated claim that
"investors lost ₦6.96tn" mistakes the **closing capitalisation** for the loss; the error recurs in
Nigerian press and academic writing and should be read that way wherever it is met. No dollar figure is
carried: the naira moved ~₦117/$ to ~₦135/$ across the window, so any single conversion is a
rate-selection artefact. Source:
[[2008-12-01-cbn-bullion-nigerian-stock-market-global-financial-crisis]].
