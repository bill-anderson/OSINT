# Alison Gillwald

**Alison Gillwald** is the executive director of **[[research-ict-africa]]** (RIA) and an adjunct professor at the University of Cape Town's Nelson Mandela School of Public Governance. She is one of the most-cited named analysts in the wiki's domain — a recurring critical voice on Southern Africa's digital-inequality, data-governance and digital-policy debates, whose demand-side survey work (the [[after-access]] programme) and published commentary frame much of the region's evidence base.

## Analytical position

Across a body of published commentary and RIA research, Gillwald argues that supply-side ICT statistics systematically **understate the digital divide**, that meaningful access is determined by **education and income** more than by network coverage, and that hype-driven policy agendas — especially the **"Fourth Industrial Revolution" (4IR)** framing promoted by the World Economic Forum and consultancies — divert scarce public resources away from the connectivity, affordability, skills and institutional basics that inclusion actually requires. She reads this dependence on external "best-practice" blueprints through a sovereignty/colonialism lens.

## In this wiki (historical framing sources)

These are dated op-eds/policy commentary; treat their figures as **as-of** their publication, not current state:

- **2019 (4IR hype critique).** Argued SA's WEF/Accenture-driven 4IR fixation — including unexplained claims of **R5tn value + 4m jobs** — distracts from structural inequality. Sources: [[2019-08-20 South Africa is caught in the global hype of the fourth industrial revolution]], [[2019-10-03 South Africa must harness technology in a way that helps fix its problems]], [[2019-10-03 Addressing SA's extreme digital inequality]].
- **2020 (data-pricing interventions).** Argued the Competition Commission's prepaid "poverty-premium" removal and a "lifeline" free-data package would have limited effect because they ignore device cost and demand-side barriers. Source: [[2020-03-03 Digital equality South Africa still has a long way to go]].

Note: the 2019 pieces cite a "~60 million SA Facebook users" Cambridge Analytica figure that is **erroneous** and is not propagated onto wiki synthesis pages.
