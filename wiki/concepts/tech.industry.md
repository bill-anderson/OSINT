# ICT Industry (`tech.industry`)

Structure and power dynamics of the technology industry — here, the cloud oligopoly and
Africa's emerging data-centre operators.

## Key material

- **Platform power and "data colonialism" (2026 H1).** CIGI argues Africa is positioned to lead on
  regulating US tech giants that dominate its markets (Google ~90% search, Meta ~85% social) with little
  local reinvestment; Foresight Africa and CIGI both invoke the "raw data mine"/"data colonialism"
  framing, and data-centre localisation (Nigeria's demand that Google/Microsoft/Amazon host locally) is a
  live sovereignty battleground. Sources:
  [[2026-02-01 Why Africa Should Lead on Governing Global Tech Giants CIGI PB226]],
  [[2026-02-18 Foresight Africa 2026]],
  [[2026-03-01 Tech Diplomacy and Avenues for Digital Development in Africa CIGI PB232]]. See [[gov.regional]], [[infra.store]].

- **Concentration, quantified ("Parting Clouds," Eaves & Bester / [[camp]]).** Three
  hyperscalers hold ~**66%** of global public cloud (85% in Canada); costs of
  concentration include lock-in, opaque pricing, gatekeeping of adjacent markets,
  innovation capture, outage risk, and geopolitical coercion. The evidenced version of
  the commodification argument. Source: [[2025-01-01 Eaves commodification]].
- **The African operator layer (2026-06).** A domestic industry is forming —
  [[cassava-technologies]] (ADC), [[mtn-group]], [[airtel-africa]], IXAfrica, Rack Centre,
  Teraco — though **>half** of continental capacity is still in South Africa. MTN's
  naira-billed local cloud is a competitive challenge to hyperscaler pricing. Source:
  [[2026-06-23 Africa's Data Centre Boom Where The New Facilities Are Being Built]].
- **Ownership vs agency (Triad).** [[the-govlab]] reframes the industry question as
  distribution of power, not ownership. Source: [[2026-06-09 The Triad of Tech Sovereignty and Europe's Package Dependency, Openness, and Agency]].
- **Public-sector delivery capacity ([[sita]]).** The industry story has a state-capacity
  flip side: South Africa's central ICT agency cannot reliably procure/deliver. Source:
  [[2026-07-07 South Africa's state IT agency is slowing government tech rollout]].

- **Global (2025, Eaves).** A handful of US hyperscalers — **AWS, Microsoft Azure,
  Google Cloud** — control the digital substrate. The **"original sin of cloud"**: when
  Azure launched (2008) with storage incompatible with AWS **S3** (2006), lock-in
  became a feature, not a bug. Chokepoints are **IaaS** and **PaaS**; migration is
  costly (reconfigure data, rewrite code, retrain staff), producing vendor and career
  lock-in. Eaves warns the reflexive fix — backing a domestic telco/"national champion"
  — merely swaps one lock-in for another. See [[geopol.usa]], [[gov.standards]].

- **AfCFTA as the demand thesis (2026-07).** At the 2nd AfCFTA Digital Trade Forum (Lagos,
  Jul 2026) Africa's digital economy was valued at **~$180bn now, rising to ~$712bn by 2050**;
  the Forum's Eight Calls to Action foreground digital infrastructure, interoperable payments
  ([[papss]]) and cross-border data — the continental market signal the industry is building
  toward. See [[afcfta]], [[gov.regional]], [[dpi.pay]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-09 Nigeria Takes AfCFTA Council Chair as Digital Forum Charts Roadmap for Implementation]].
- **Investment rebound, concentrated (2025 data).** Partech: African tech VC **$4.1bn in
  2025 (+25%)**, the strongest since 2022 — but debt reached 41% of capital, the **Big Four
  (KEN/ZAF/EGY/NGA) took 72%**, domestic investors rose to 45%, and the seed pipeline keeps
  contracting. [[african-development-bank]]'s **[[nafa]]** ([[sidi-ould-tah]]) supplies the
  "financial sovereignty" macro-frame. See [[finance.new]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-26 The AfDB's push for African financial sovereignty, and a tech investment rebound]].
- **Operator & channel layer thickening (2026-04).** Hyperscaler capacity —
  [[amazon-web-services]]' Cape Town region, Microsoft — is increasingly intermediated by a
  domestic channel: **[[axiz]]**, [[equinix]]'s SA distribution partner, packaging
  AI/hybrid-cloud/data-sovereignty demand for local partners, alongside emerging local clouds
  (e.g. [[kasi-cloud]]) and pan-African carriers such as [[orange]]. SA still anchors the
  market and acts as the continental gateway. See [[infra.store]], [[ZAF]]. Sources:
  [[2026-04-28 Axiz Welcomes Equinix Investment, Citing Boost to Africa's Digital Infrastructure]],
  [[2026-07-11 AWS in Africa]].
- **MNO state-of-market — from telcos to holding companies (Russell Southwood, 2025-12–2026-06).**
  Southwood's recurring analysis reframes the African operator layer: as **data revenue
  overtakes voice** (by end-FY2025 for Airtel; Vodacom calls prepaid voice a "managed
  decline"), the four "power players" — **[[mtn-group]], [[airtel-africa]], [[orange]],
  [[vodacom]]** — are becoming **data/holding companies**, spinning out towers, data
  centres, wholesale ([[mtn-group]]'s Bayobab brand) and mobile money as arms-length
  businesses, adopting network-sharing/JVs to bear fibre and AI CAPEX, and struggling in
  "digital life" verticals against the hyperscalers. Below them sit a "second division"
  ([[axian-telecom]], [[econet-wireless]], Africell, Globacom), the state incumbents
  ([[ethio-telecom]]) and — his sharpest case — the **former fixed-line incumbents as
  "stranded assets"** (Telkom Kenya, Sotel Tchad, Gamtel, Camtel, SCPT), undone by absent
  vision, capital, skills and patronage/corruption, with only Camtel profitable and only via
  protected wholesale/landing-station monopolies. His ICT4D long view roots this in the
  2000s liberalisation wave that mobile transformed but whose network effects re-created
  monopolies. See [[infra.connect]], [[tech.ai]], [[infra.store]]. Sources:
  [[2025-12-04 Is it a bird Is it plane No, Jim, it's an African mobile operator, but not as we know it 9]],
  [[2026-02-16 Waking the dead - Slow road to extinction for Africa's former telco incumbents 11]],
  [[2026-04-16 The Long View - What ICT4D did for Africa and why that deep past matters for today]].
- **Payments margins collapse to a utility (Samora Kariuki, 2026-02-23).** A parallel
  structural read of the *payments* industry: as low-cost central-bank instant-payment rails
  become utilities (Brazil Pix, India UPI, Africa's IPS at **$1.98T** in 2024), processing
  margins collapse and durable value migrates to credit, data and compliance layers — the
  same "infrastructure commoditises, value moves up the stack" dynamic. See [[dpi.pay]],
  [[finance.new]]. Source: [[2026-02-23 111 - Winning But Losing - The Payments End Game]].
- **Vendor vertical-integration at the top (Anjola Adebowale, 2026-06-05).** Adebowale reads
  Microsoft Build 2026 as **[[microsoft]]** assembling a full-stack, vertically-owned AI
  platform (own MAI models, tuning, agents, quantum, hardware) — the industry's concentration
  logic extended from cloud into models and silicon, with Africa's risk being engagement on
  others' terms. See [[geopol.usa]], [[infra.store]]. Source:
  [[2026-06-05 Microsoft Build 2026 What Caught My Attention and What It Means for Africa]].
- **Research base (resource).** The **[[diode-network]]** (Univ. Manchester GDI; Heeks et al.)
  curated bibliography on the digital economy and digital labour in the Global South is the
  standing literature shelf for the industry's political economy — gig/platform labour,
  digital enterprise in Africa, latecomer industrial policy. Filed `needs-review`; entries
  promoted to `publication` only on citation. See [[capacity.research]]. Source:
  [[2017-04-18 Publications]].

- **The satellite-broadband entrant field widens (2026-01).** The LEO industry in Africa is
  diversifying beyond [[starlink]]: [[amazon-leo]] (formerly Project Kuiper) won a 7-year Nigeria
  landing permit, and blockchain-integrated operator [[spacecoin]] secured a Kenyan transmission
  licence (continuing under an existing NGA licence) — two more players in the satellite-industry
  layer. On the terrestrial side, [[mtn-nigeria]]'s 2025 wrap paired a return to profit
  (PAT ~$525m, 9M to Sep 2025) with **9,218 fibre-cut incidents**, underscoring the operating
  risk in the West-African market. See [[infra.connect]], [[data.satellite]], [[NGA]], [[KEN]].
  Sources: [[2026-01-15 Amazon Leo secures landing permit in Nigeria]],
  [[2026-01-13 Spacecoin strikes major deals to launch satellite internet]],
  [[2026-01-23 MTN Nigeria fibre network takes a hit]].
