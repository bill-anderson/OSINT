# US / hyperscaler activities (`geopol.usa`)

US government and US-headquartered hyperscalers shaping the global (and African) digital
substrate.

## Key material

- **Data-governance diplomacy (2026-07, Kenya).** The US Embassy engaged Kenya on its
  **Draft Data Governance Policy (2026)** and cross-border data flows — shaping
  partner-state rules as "trusted digital economy" alignment (soft standard-setting
  alongside the harder TPD/Pax Silica track). See [[KEN]],
  [[us-department-of-state]], [[gov.policy]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-10 Kenya and U.S. Discuss Draft Data Governance Policy to Strengthen Digital Economy]].
- **Health data as a funding condition — the AFGHS (2026-02/03).** Under the
  **[[america-first-global-health-strategy]]** (which replaced USAID and restructured
  PEPFAR), the US signed **26 bilateral health MOUs** with African governments, each
  bundling a **Specimen-Sharing Agreement** (physical pathogens/genetic sequence) and a
  **Data-Sharing Agreement** (US access to national surveillance and lab systems, **10 years
  post-termination**), attached to five-year declining funding with **no benefit-sharing**
  guarantee, bypassing the WHO Pathogen Access and Benefit-Sharing (PABS) framework the US
  had already rejected. Kenya signed first (**$2.5bn/5yr** framework, Rubio–Mudavadi,
  2025-12-04) via the **[[us-kenya-health-framework]]**, but its High Court **suspended** it
  (Omtatah petition, injunction 2025-12-19) as conflicting with the Data Protection Act 2019
  and Digital Health Act 2023 — [[jane-munga]] (Carnegie) reads it as an "early warning" that
  domestic data law can constrain US health diplomacy. **Zimbabwe rejected** it (**~$350–367m**;
  Mnangagwa; also objecting to folding in critical minerals; the two figures conflict — flag),
  **[[ZMB]]** pushed back, **[[RWA]]** cut a bespoke $228m deal (naming Zipline/Ginkgo), and
  **[[NGA]]**'s $5.1bn MOU carried Christian-healthcare conditionality. Africa CDC's "Health
  Security and Sovereignty Agenda" (Nov 2025) — Kaseya: "we want to own our data in Africa" —
  sits against 18 members signing. The **14** signed bilaterals total **~$16bn**
  ([[kff-afghs-mou-tracker]]; Uganda $2.3bn, Kenya $2.5bn, Nigeria $5.1bn, Ethiopia $1.47bn,
  Mozambique $1.8bn, plus [[CIV]], [[CMR]], [[LSO]], [[SWZ]], [[MDG]], [[SLE]], [[LBR]],
  [[BWA]], [[RWA]]). The now-public **model [[specimen-sharing-agreement]]** (MOU annex)
  confirms the asymmetry: the country shares specimens/sequences **within 5 days** of a US
  request, consents to onward-sharing with **up to 10 non-USG US entities**, over a **25-year**
  term, with **no reciprocity** and only a "best efforts / subject to funds" pledge on any
  resulting countermeasure; the Kenya framework adds one-directional audit rights (US may audit
  5% of Kenyan facilities), 7-day outbreak detection, 1-day US notification, and states it "is
  not an international agreement" — so signatories have **no legal recourse**. Kaseya also cites
  "huge concerns regarding data, regarding pathogen sharing" and **declined an observer role**
  citing sovereignty; **Zimbabwe** pulled out of a **$367m/5yr** deal (corroborating the higher
  figure) and **Zambia** pushed back. The clearest case of US aid conditioned on sovereign
  health/genomic data. See [[gov.protect]], [[dpi.mis]], [[gov.regional]], [[KEN]],
  [[ZWE]], [[UGA]], [[SEN]]. Sources:

- **US frontier models as "sovereign" stacks (2026-07).** In South Africa, [[anthropic]]'s
  Claude (via [[trendai]]) is deployed as *sovereign AI* under local law — US capability
  localised to satisfy data-residency rules, the demand-side mirror of the CLOUD-Act ceiling
  ([[2026-06-08 The Promise and Limits of African Data Sovereignty]]). See [[ZAF]], [[tech.ai]].
- **DFC infrastructure finance in Africa (2026-06).** The **[[us-dfc]]** put **$300m**
  into Africa Data Centres ([[cassava-technologies]]), framed *explicitly* as "a
  counterweight to Chinese infrastructure financing" — data-centre capital as geopolitics.
  See [[geopol.china]], [[infra.store]]. Source:
  [[2026-06-23 Africa's Data Centre Boom Where The New Facilities Are Being Built]].
- **AI export push (2026-07).** The **[[american-ai-exports-program]]**
  ([[us-department-of-commerce]]) promotes **full-stack** US AI systems abroad (78
  applications) with federal financing/advocacy/expedited licensing — the supply side of
  Africa's AI-dependency risk ([[tech.ai]]). Source:
  [[2026-07-07 Commerce Statement on Application Conclusion, Next Steps of the American AI Exports Program]].
- **Hyperscaler dependence as the thing being unwound (2026-07, Nigeria).** The [[cbn]]
  localization directive exists because Nigerian financial data still sits largely on
  **AWS, Azure, Google Cloud and IBM**; the proposed "smoothest path" — inviting those same
  US providers to deploy **local cloud regions** — keeps the platforms American while
  nationalising only the infrastructure layer. See [[infra.store]], [[nigeria--gov-protect]].
  Source: [[2026-07-07 CBN's local data order puts Nigeria's data centres to test]].
- **Vendor "sovereign cloud" (2026-07, Kenya).** [[oracle]] pitches sovereign-cloud
  infrastructure to Kenya's government — the US-vendor framing of digital sovereignty. See
  [[KEN]], [[infra.store]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-07 Kenya's State Department of ICT and Digital Economy discusses digital partnership with Oracle]].

- **Structured AI diplomacy toward Africa (2026-07 batch).** Carnegie ([[jane-munga]])
  maps the US instruments — **Technology Prosperity Deals** ([[technology-prosperity-deal]]),
  **[[pax-silica]]**, the [[american-ai-exports-program]] — and argues **no African state
  has yet been included**, proposing Kenya as entry point. At continental level the
  **[[us-auc-strategic-investment-working-group]]** ([[us-department-of-state]] + AU) and the
  **Chad** bilateral track show the "trade/investment not aid" posture. Hyperscalers scale on
  the ground: **[[google]]** exceeded its $1bn Africa target; **[[microsoft]]** placed
  graduates in SA government and pursued the stalled Kenya AI DC with **[[g42]]**;
  **[[meta]]** funds Nigeria's data-protection regulator; the **[[uae]]** is courted as Gulf
  bridge. See [[tech.ai]], [[technology-prosperity-deal]]. Sources:
  [[2026-06-23 A Kenya Technology Prosperity Deal Could Help Washington Secure Durable AI Partnerships with Africa]],
  [[2026-01-28 Joint Statement of the United States and African Union on the Launch of a Strategic Investment Working Group]],
  [[2026-07-01 Chad and US Explore Partnerships on Satellite Connectivity and Cybersecurity]].
- **The CLOUD Act ceiling (2026-06).** [[2026-06-08 The Promise and Limits of African Data Sovereignty]]: US-parent cloud firms stay reachable by US courts wherever
  African data sits — the hard limit under hyperscaler dependence. See [[infra.store]],
  [[gov.protect]].
- **The AU–Google MoU as continental CLOUD-Act exposure (2026-02).** Alice Munyua's polemic argues
  the Feb-2026 **[[au-google-mou]]** — advancing "Africa's sovereign AI" — places the institutional
  data of 1.5bn people on [[google]] infrastructure reachable under the **[[cloud-act]]** (18 U.S.C.
  § 2713), where jurisdiction follows ownership not server location, so no data-residency clause
  overrides it. Google's cable+cloud dominance (Equiano, Umoja, the Johannesburg region) is the
  substrate; an Apolitical/Google.org curriculum trains 50,000 Ethiopian officials in Google's
  frameworks. Set against the AU's **77.5% donor-funded** budget, it is US platform power dressed as
  African sovereignty. See [[tech.ai]], [[ETH]], [[XAF]], [[colonialism]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-05 THE GATEKEEPERS PART XII]].
- **Global (2025, Eaves).** US hyperscalers (AWS/Azure/Google — see [[tech.industry]])
  control cloud infrastructure, "compounded by a US government increasingly willing to
  wield its digital industries as leverage." Macron: *"There is no such thing as happy
  vassalage."* The Pentagon's **JEDI** ($10bn) was the most ambitious attempt to commodify
  hyperscaler cloud; the hyperscalers spent heavily on lobbying/lawsuits to kill it and now
  push "multicloud" language without real interoperability. The `colonialism` reading:
  dependency on foreign digital infrastructure as vassalage, most acute for states that
  cannot afford alternatives.

  exclusivity, freeing it to sell across [[amazon-web-services]] (Bedrock) and Google Cloud
  (Azure primary through 2032) — the US stack African "sovereign AI" deployments sit on is
  itself reshuffling, and India's [[g42]]/[[uae]] Cerebras deal shows governments seeking
  non-US compute paths. See [[tech.ai]], [[geopol.gulf]]. Sources:

- **Hyperscaler footprint — and its absence (2026-04/05).** [[microsoft]]'s **$329m** SA
  expansion ([[microsoft-sa-2026-expansion]], atop $1.2bn) hardens [[ZAF]] as the hyperscaler
  gateway; yet **no hyperscaler runs a full cloud region in [[NGA]]** — capital routes to
  Kenya/SA over power, tax and 25-year-old telecom policy ([[ncc]] is rewriting it), leaving
  Africa's biggest market an "access point" to foreign regions. See [[infra.store]],
  [[infra.energy]]. Sources:

- **Sovereignty enforcement turns on US firms (2026-07, Nigeria).** Nigeria directed the
  **[[fccpc]]** to probe [[meta]]/[[google]]/X/GenAI for anti-competitive practices (after the
  2025 $222m Meta penalty, under appeal), while the [[cbn]] onshoring mandate (by 2027-01-01)
  targets financial data still on [[amazon-web-services]]/Azure/Google Cloud — NIBSS logged
  ~$702bn e-transactions in 2024. Sovereignty asserted against, and through, US platforms.
  See [[gov.protect]], [[nigeria--gov-protect]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-10 Nigeria tests data sovereignty as foreign tech firms facing probe]].
- **Sovereign enclaves against the CLOUD Act (2026-04).** [[smart-africa]] + Swiss
  **[[realtyme]]** ([[smart-africa-realtyme-partnership]]) launched a "national protection
  framework" of state-controlled communication enclaves positioned against the **US Cloud
  Act** — pitched as "digital decolonisation" countering both Silicon Valley and Chinese
  stacks. See [[XAF]], [[infra.cybersec]], [[geopol.china]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-16 Smart Africa, RealTyme join forces for data sovereignty]].
- **Satellite revenue leakage — the SpaceX/Starlink question (2026-05).** [[starlink]] is
  authorised in **≥25 African countries** (~66k NGA users, >67k ZWE) under far lighter
  obligations than local telcos (Senegal: **$150k licence vs >$50m for 5G**); SpaceX's
  $1.75tn-valuation S-1 frames "bridging the digital divide," but revenue, data and control
  flow offshore. Namibia rejected Starlink (Mar 2026); SA issued a cease-and-desist yet is
  mulling law changes. See [[infra.connect]], [[include.access]], [[gov.policy]]. Sources:
  [[2026-05-26 Africa risks losing billions to satellite internet operators, report says]],
  [[2026-05-22 View How SpaceX is making a quiet bet on Africa]].
- **A second US LEO entrant — Amazon–Globalstar (2026-04).** [[amazon-web-services]]'s
  parent is in advanced talks to buy **[[globalstar]]** for **~$9bn**
  ([[amazon-globalstar-acquisition]]), fast-tracking **Project Kuiper** (rebranded "Leo")
  against [[starlink]]. Kuiper has just **~180 satellites** vs Starlink's **7,000+**, but
  Globalstar's existing spectrum/partnerships in **[[ZAF]]/[[RWA]]/[[MOZ]]/[[GAB]]** would
  hand Amazon a shortcut into markets Starlink spent years entering (Apple's 20% stake is the
  sticking point; deal not finalised). US satellite competition now runs two-deep over African
  skies. See [[infra.connect]], [[finance.new]], [[digital.rural]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-14 Amazon's $9bn satellite deal set to benefit Africa]].
- **Hyperscalers go overland (2026-03).** After the subsea era (Meta's 2Africa, Google's
  Equiano), **[[google]]**, **[[meta]]**, **[[amazon-web-services]]** and **[[microsoft]]**
  are now building **ultra-high-speed terrestrial fibre across Africa's interior** with local
  players (Yangtze Optics Africa Cable's Durban factory illustrative) — extending US control
  of the connectivity substrate from coast to hinterland. See [[infra.connect]], [[XAF]].
  Source: [[2026-03-16 Why Google, Amazon and Meta are going overland in Africa]].
- **US–China compute duopoly and its African blind spot (2026-03/04).** US and Chinese
  firms operate **>90% of the world's AI data centres** (Oxford); Africa and South America
  have almost none, and AI adoption grew ~**2x faster** in wealthy vs low/middle-income
  countries last year (Microsoft Research) — the concentration the frugal-AI movement
  (Cambridge's Frugal AI Hub, in talks with **[[KEN]]/[[NGA]]**) answers. And even where US
  models are available they can be **unfit**: [[nasa-harvest]]'s [[catherine-nakalembe]] found
  Western-trained AI **could not recognise local crops** in western Kenya, forcing her team to
  collect 5m+ images to map cropland across Kenya/Uganda/Senegal — a "digital colonialism"
  warning that US AI supply is not neutral. See [[tech.ai]], [[include.divides]],
  [[geopol.china]], [[colonialism]]. Sources:
  [[2026-04-02 Nations priced out of Big AI are building with frugal models]],
  [[2026-03-12 Western AI models 'fail spectacularly' in farms and forests abroad]].
- **Dollar stablecoins as monetary-sovereignty risk (2026-04/07).** SSA moved **$205bn**
  through digital rails (Jul 2024–Jun 2025, +52%), much via USD stablecoins outside
  central-bank reach; SARB's Kganyago warned of lost monetary sovereignty and Nigeria launched
  the regulated cNGN. The [[visa]]–M-Pesa–[[onafriq]] DRC stablecoin pilot
  ([[visa-mpesa-onafriq-drc-stablecoin-pilot]]; *low-authority source — verify*) puts US
  payment rails and USD-pegged tokens at the centre of African cross-border flows. See
  [[dpi.pay]], [[geopol.gulf]], [[COD]]. Sources:
  [[2026-04-23 Africa's $205 Billion Digital Shift Is Redefining Money, and Central Banks Are Losing Control]],
  [[2026-07-10 Visa and M-Pesa Launch Blockchain Stablecoin Pilot in DR Congo]].
- **The EU mirror (2026-06).** Tech Policy Press / AI Now: even wealthy Europe's "Sovereign
  AI" keeps funnelling value to US hyperscalers (DeepL→AWS, Mistral→Nvidia) — a live
  comparator for Africa's dependency debate. See [[geopol.eu]], [[tech.industry]]. Source:

- **The anti-data-sovereignty cable (2026-02, State Dept).** An internal
  **[[us-department-of-state]]** cable (18 Feb 2026, signed **Rubio**) directs US diplomats
  worldwide to **monitor and actively counter** data-sovereignty and data-localization laws —
  branding them threats to AI, cloud and "global data flows," naming the EU's **GDPR** as
  archetype and pushing the US-led Global Cross-Border Privacy Rules Forum as the "trusted"
  alternative. With the Brazil Section 301 probe and the WTO e-transmission-duty moratorium
  push (opposed by India / **[[ZAF]]** / Indonesia), it is a doctrinal campaign to keep sovereign
  regulators off American platforms — direct context for every African data-localization debate
  here. See [[gov.protect]], [[gov.legislate]], [[geopol.eu]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-12 How Trump's Data Regulation Crackdown Undermines Digital Sovereignty]].
- **Tech Corps — the human arm of the AI-export push (2026-03).** White House S&T adviser Kratsios
  launched **[[tech-corps]]** at the India AI Impact Summit: thousands of US technical volunteers
  offering "last-mile" help to deploy American AI in partner-nation health/education/agriculture —
  to steer countries onto the US stack and away from Chinese equivalents, extending the
  [[american-ai-exports-program]] and America's AI Action Plan. Analysts read it as an
  "on-the-margin" answer to China's decade of embedded S&T diplomats and DeepSeek's cheap diffusion.
  See [[tech.ai]], [[geopol.china]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-02 The U.S. Launches a Peace Corps for AI to Compete With China]].
- **Frontier-AI diplomacy vs domestic pressure — Anthropic–Rwanda (2026-02/03).** [[anthropic]]
  signed its **first multi-sector African government deal** ([[anthropic-rwanda-mou]]) — Claude
  across Rwanda's health, education and public sector — even as, at home, the US **"Department of
  War"** designated the firm a "supply-chain risk" and pushed it to drop military guardrails, which
  Amodei refused: a US company exporting AI capability to African governments while resisting its
  own government's demands. See [[RWA]], [[tech.ai]], [[capacity.training]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-04 U.S.-based AI firm Anthropic bags first multi-sector government deal in Africa despite policy pressures at home]].
- **Zambia's leaked MOU — the exploitation charge (2026-02).** The leaked Zambia draft
  ([[zambia-us-health-mou]]) crystallised the AFGHS critique: **$1.012bn over 5yr** (down from a
  $1.5bn figure and below prior PEPFAR levels) demanding **40,000 new health workers + $400m** in
  Zambian co-spend, a **10-year** health-data-access clause and a **25-year** pathogen-data pledge
  (the longest any country has faced), with US-embassy confirmation funding was **contingent on
  copper/cobalt mining access**. Health minister Muchima denied the mining link on 15 Feb and was
  **fired three days later**; Zambia then requested "revisions" and Hichilema reframed aid cuts as a
  chance to "take care of our own affairs." Kaseya: "We cannot accept not owning our data." See
  [[gov.protect]], [[dpi.mis]], [[ZMB]], [[colonialism]]. Sources:
  [[2026-02-25 US accused of 'shameless exploitation' over proposed Zambian health aid deal]],
  [[2026-02-26 Zambia rejects U.S. health aid over mining partnership ties]].
- **US fintech vs DRC — the PayServices suit (2026-01).** Idaho-based **[[payservices]]** sued the
  DRC and senior officials in US federal court over a collapsed **$72m** state banking/payments
  project ([[payservices-drc-banking-deal]]), seeking up to **$400m** (some filings cite $4bn) and
  alleging obstruction after it refused bribe demands; Kinshasa calls the signed documents a
  non-binding MoU. A US company using American courts against an African state over a DPI contract —
  an investor-risk and sovereignty flashpoint as Kinshasa courts Washington on minerals/security.
  See [[dpi.pay]], [[dpi.govtech]], [[COD]], [[finance.new]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-19 US fintech sues DR Congo for $400m over failed banking deal]].
