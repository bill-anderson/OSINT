# Digital divides (`include.divides`)

Who is left out of connectivity and digital services — by gender, disability, age,
geography, and increasingly by **access to compute/intelligence**.

## Key material

- **[[MUS]] Mauritius — bringing Rodrigues into the digital strategy (2026).** Mauritius is extending its AI /
  cyber / digital-transformation programme to its **autonomous outer island of Rodrigues**: a cyber working
  framework (Mauritius-based team + dedicated cyber/AI representative), the **Rod IT** youth-awareness project,
  a **Technopark**, and **MauPass / KOREK** super-app rollout via Mauritius Post agents — anchored by the
  Rodrigues Regional Assembly's **"Rod-Digital Vision"** roadmap (6 May 2026). A rare, concrete sub-national
  digital-inclusion move. See [[mauritius--infra-cybersec]], [[MUS]], [[tech.ai]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-29-rodrigues-port-mathurin-ai-cybersecurity-mission-lexpress]].
- **[[MWI]] Malawi — schools offline, no budget line (2026-04).** UNICEF/Giga's **School Connectivity
  Landscape Analysis** found **85% of Malawi's 9,000 schools** have limited or no internet and **~900 have
  none** (electricity and devices the main barriers) — yet the 2026/27 budget carries **no dedicated
  school-connectivity allocation** (the World Bank is separately connecting 200+ schools; DMAP targets 2,000).
  The distribution edge of a country whose wholesale bandwidth price already collapsed. See
  [[malawi--infra-connect]], [[infra.connect]], [[capacity.training]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-17-school-connectivity-unicef-giga-report-malawi24]].
- **DPI, shutdowns and civic space: exclusion by design and by governance (2026 H1).** Pollicy's
  Afro-feminist analysis argues DPI in Uganda/Kenya/South Africa is state-centric and reproduces gendered
  and minority exclusion; Access Now records **313 shutdowns in 52 countries in 2025** (Tanzania the worst
  African offender, 8; a five-day election blackout), with ECOWAS-Court/ACHPR/ICC pushback; ARTICLE 19
  flagged surveillance and cybercrime-law misuse in Kenya and Rwanda at the ACHPR; and Bloggers of Zambia
  tracked new interception-enabling cyber laws before the 2026 polls. Sources:
  [[2026-01-01 Systemic Barriers Gendered Consequences An Afro-feminist Analysis of DPI]],
  [[2026-03-01 Rising Repression Meets Global Resistance Internet Shutdowns in 2025]],
  [[2026-05-15 African Commission Highlighting pressures on civic space in Kenya and Rwanda]],
  [[2026-04-01 State of Internet Freedom in Zambia 2026 6th Edition]]. See [[gov.protect]], [[dpi.id]].

- **ID as a gate that excludes (2026 H1).** Mandatory SIM-registration and national-ID requirements
  exclude the undocumented: CMU-Africa finds **>1 in 3** interviewees failed ID registration on the
  first attempt and many bank under a **third-party ID**; Caribou documents refugees in Kenya cut off
  from health cover (SHA) and mobile money, and tens of thousands of citizens denied IDs via
  double-registration. Sources:
  [[2026-01-01 My Money, Your Name Challenges and Workarounds in ID-Required Mobile Money in East Africa]],
  [[2026-02-01 Digital Identity and Migration Struggles for Equitable Technology Governance]]. See [[dpi.id]], [[include.access]].

- **The AI divide (2026-06, *The Sun* / Ademola).** A new axis of exclusion: "the digital
  divide is no longer about access to the internet; it is about access to intelligence."
  Africa hosts **<1%** of global HPC; institutions pay 25–40% more for cloud compute;
  **8%** of SA SMEs use AI (vs 29% EU); <5% of Nigerian universities can train modest ML
  models. Risks a bifurcation within societies (elite universities/corporates vs everyone
  else) and continental "algorithmic dependency." See [[tech.ai]], [[capacity.research]].
  Source: [[2026-06-17 Priced out of the future Confronting the rising cost of AI in Africa]].

- **[[ZMB]] Zambia (2025).** [[dzap]] names notable disparities in internet access for
  **women, persons with disabilities and older people**, and rural coverage gaps
  (un-upgraded cell towers, absent fixed lines). It targets improved internet
  experience for **3.7 million women** and carries a dedicated gender annex. The PAD
  flags that **gender-disaggregated ICT data is largely absent**, with a baseline study
  planned. Ties to [[infra.connect]], [[dpi.pay]] and [[capacity.training]].

- **Inclusion as programme rationale (2026-07).** Two new items foreground inclusion as
  the stated goal: **Namibia's** Smart City programme pairs AI/skills with "digital
  inclusion" and workforce readiness ([[NAM]], [[china-namibia-smart-city]]); **Ethiopia's**
  Fayda registration JV is justified by digital and financial inclusion and expanded
  service access ([[ETH]], [[safaricom-ethiopia-fayda-jv]], [[dpi.id]]). Both frame
  foundational infrastructure as the on-ramp for the excluded — the counter-narrative to
  the AI-divide bullet above. Sources:
  [[2026-07-08 China funds Namibia digital infrastructure]],
  [[2026-07-06 Safaricom Ethiopia partners National ID Program to expand digital identity registration]].

- **"Digital-first must not become digital-only" — the democratic divide (2026-07, ZAF).** SA
  communications minister **[[solly-malatsi]]** ([[dcdt-south-africa]]) warned that digitising
  government ahead of **affordability, connectivity and digital literacy** builds "a premium
  service layer for the already included," not a digital state — "a promise made only to the
  connected is not a promise to the nation." He distinguishes theoretical coverage from
  **meaningful connectivity** (affordable, regular use on capable devices), and escalates the
  stakes to democracy: "if only the connected receive real-time, credible information, the
  digital divide becomes a **democratic divide**," and gauging public sentiment from "the
  loudest online voices" risks "mistaking visibility for representativity." Prescribes low-data,
  multilingual and **offline** channels so [[mymzansi]] includes "the citizen who has been
  excluded, unheard, unseen, offline, priced out, or spoken for." A sitting minister voicing
  the inclusion-first critique. Its **supply-side complement**: the DCDT/Treasury removal of the
  **9% smartphone luxury excise** (sub-R2,500 devices), GSMA-measured at **+1.1m** entry-level
  units in 11 months ([[gsma-sa-smartphone-tax-reform-2026]]) — affordability as a divide-closing
  lever. See [[ZAF]], [[include.access]], [[dpi.govtech]], [[tech.industry]]. Sources:
  [[2026-07-13 Digital-first must not become digital-only, says Malatsi]],
  [[2026-07-10 SOLLY MALATSI  Removing smartphone tax means access to opportunity]].
- **The satellite/affordability divide (2026-05).** Reframes the divide as *cost*, not coverage: LEO
  providers like [[starlink]] (authorised in ≥25 countries) can cut deployment cost in remote areas,
  but retail affordability still tracks local income and regulation. The Africa CEO Forum/Askya
  report warns that offshore "revenue leakage" could make local operators *retreat* from unprofitable
  rural and low-income areas — widening the divide satellite was meant to close. See [[include.access]],
  [[data.satellite]]. Sources: [[2026-05-22 View How SpaceX is making a quiet bet on Africa]],
  [[2026-05-26 Africa risks losing billions to satellite internet operators, report says]].
- **Exclusion by design — SA digital ID (2026-05).** Mark Burke's critique of South Africa's draft
  Identification Regulations warns the DPI could deepen divides: **Regulation 49(5)** only says
  implementation must not "unreasonably exclude" people without devices or internet, with **no**
  concrete alternative-channel or digital-literacy mandates — so an officially "optional" digital ID
  becomes practically compulsory for the rural, older and low-income citizens least able to use it.
  See [[ZAF]], [[dpi.id]], [[sa-digital-id-regulations]], [[popia]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-29 Opinion South Africa's Digital Identity Regulations A Governance Crossroads]].
- **The widening mobile-money gender gap (2026-04).** Women in LMICs were **36% less likely** than
  men to own a mobile-money account in 2024 — *up* from 30% in 2021, so the gap is **widening**. AI
  credit-scoring trained on this data inherits the gap structurally (thinner histories → lower scores
  → thinner future data), and fraud models risk flagging first-time rural or women users as
  anomalous. See [[dpi.pay]], [[tech.ai]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-16 3 Reasons Mobile Money Programs Have a $1.7 Trillion Problem]].
- **The compute/intelligence divide, measured (2026).** Two standing references anchor the AI-divide
  thesis: [[global-index-on-responsible-ai]] (GIRAI, from [[global-center-on-ai-governance]], lead
  author Rachel Adams) finds that where AI frameworks are active only **45%** of Global-South cases
  show implementation (vs 55% overall), and just **22%** of Southern cases are covered by *binding*
  rules vs 58% in the North — reframing low Southern scores as *development gaps*, not ambition gaps.
  The [[diode-network]] scholarship (Univ. Manchester GDI) frames the same dynamic historically as
  "digital economies at global margins" and "digital extractivism." See [[capacity.research]],
  [[tech.ai]]. Sources: [[2026-07-11 Global-Index-on-Responsible-AI-2026.pdf]], [[2017-04-18 Publications]].
- **Mobile-money adoption gaps, anchored — GSMA 2026.** Beyond the widening gender gap above, the
  [[2026-03-01 GSMA State of the Industry Report on Mobile Money 2026]] ([[gsma]]) locates the divide in
  devices and skills: SSA smartphone ownership only **~33%** of adults (cost the main barrier),
  women **~14%** less likely to use mobile internet, and stark digital-financial-literacy gaps
  (Ethiopia: 60% of women vs 54% of men "don't know how to use it"). As services move from USSD
  to super-apps/QR, the gap risks hardening into "two-speed" inclusion. See [[dpi.pay]],
  [[include.access]]. Sources: [[2026-03-01 GSMA State of the Industry Report on Mobile Money 2026]],
  [[2026-03-24 Mobile Money in Africa Strong Growth, but Adoption Still Falls Short]].
- **The compute divide, sharpened — frugal AI (2026-04).** US + Chinese firms operate **>90% of
  AI data centres** (Oxford); Africa and South America have almost none, and generative-AI
  adoption grew ~**2x** faster in wealthy than in low-income countries last year (Microsoft
  Research). The frugal-AI response (Cambridge hub, in talks with [[KEN]]/[[NGA]]; sub-$50
  hardware, open-weight models) reframes the divide as one of *compute access and ownership* —
  "will access to major AI models become similar to access to oil?" See [[tech.ai]],
  [[capacity.research]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-02 Nations priced out of Big AI are building with frugal models]].
- **The rural infrastructure divide, at the frontline — Ethiopia eCHIS.** A study of Ethiopia's
  electronic community health information system ([[echis-ethiopia-study]]) shows how connectivity
  and power gaps exclude the rural frontline: Ethio Telecom covers ~85% 2G / 66% 3G / just **4%
  4G**, and only **9%** of rural residents have electricity (29,950 power banks distributed as a
  workaround). Health Extension Workers travel hours to sync data or charge tablets, run parallel
  paper+electronic records, and fear theft-liability — the connectivity/power divide made concrete
  inside a flagship DPI. See [[dpi.mis]], [[digital.rural]], [[ETH]]. Source:
  [[2023-06-07 Barriers, facilitators and motivators of electronic community health information system use among health workers in Ethiopia]].

- **[[GHA]] Ghana — the usage gap and its rural face (2025).** GSMA's Digital Africa Summit report puts
  Ghana's mobile-internet **usage gap at 62%** despite 99% 4G coverage, with device affordability the
  binding constraint (smartphone = 27% of monthly GDP/capita; 76% for the poorest 40%). A ground-level
  UTAUT study of Wenchi headteachers finds a sharp **urban–rural divide** in school-management ICT use.
  See [[GHA]], [[digital.rural]], [[capacity.training]]. Sources:
  [[2025-09-03 New GSMA report at Digital Africa Summit Ghana - digital reforms could add $3.4bn to the economy by 2030]],
  [[2025-11-26 Bridging the digital divide - ICT adoption among Ghanaian headteachers (Wenchi)]].
- **The demand-side measurement tradition — RIA (framing).** Two strands of
  [[research-ict-africa]] literature landed this batch as *framing*, not fresh figures. The
  **[[after-access]]** qualitative gender study ([[NGA]], [[GHA]], [[UGA]], [[ETH]], 2026) reads
  the divide as gendered and intersectional — cost, low digital literacy, security fears and
  social norms compounding to exclude rural women and women with disabilities (see
  [[capacity.literacy]]). And a set of Alison Gillwald ([[alison-gillwald]]) op-eds — **dated to
  2019–2020** — argue South Africa's 4IR/AI hype diverts resources from the connectivity, skills
  and affordability basics that actually close inequality (historical context only; one widely
  quoted "~60m SA Facebook users" figure is an error and is not carried here). Figures stay on
  the underlying study/source pages. See [[include.access]], [[tech.ai]], [[ZAF]]. Sources:
  [[2026-02-02 After Access qualitative study of gender and digital technologies Nigeria report]],
  [[2019-10-03 South Africa must harness technology in a way that helps fix its problems]],
  [[2019-08-20 South Africa is caught in the global hype of the fourth industrial revolution]],
  [[2019-10-03 Addressing SA's extreme digital inequality]].

- **Two-speed markets and the offline-first gap (2026 analyst batch).** Russell
  Southwood's ICT4D long view argues liberalisation and mobile were transformative
  but **network effects re-created monopolies** (M-Pesa, MoMo) and left persistent
  **two-speed markets** — puncturing the "leapfrogging" myth — with his policy
  agenda proposing **data-rollover** mandates and **zero-rating** as divide-closing
  levers ([[2026-04-16 The Long View - What ICT4D did for Africa and why that deep past matters for today]],
  [[2026-06-25 Regulating for Africa's future digital economy – A draft agenda to speed things up]]).
  Rebecca Mbaya's account of building the TAIS Knowledge Map flags the
  **offline-first / connectivity gap** as a live constraint on documenting and
  including African AI innovators ([[2026-03-25 On Documenting African AI Knowledge What Becomes Possible]]).
  See [[include.access]], [[tech.innovate]].

- **[[SDN]] Sudan — the offline majority in wartime (early 2025).** **71.3% offline** — 14.6m internet
  users, **28.7%** penetration — as conflict compounds exclusion. See [[infra.connect]], [[SDN]]. Source:
  [[2025-03-03 Digital 2025 - Sudan (DataReportal)]].
- **[[RWA]] Rwanda — penetration up, cost still the barrier (2025-06).** ITU put internet penetration
  at **38%** mid-2025 (from **34.4%** Jan-2024, DataReportal), with an **urban 57% vs rural 19%** split
  and affordability the primary barrier. See [[infra.connect]], [[digital.rural]], [[RWA]]. Source:
  [[2025-06-12 Rwanda hits 38% internet penetration, but cost still keeps millions offline]].
- **[[NGA]] Nigeria — the mobile gender gap (2024).** The GSMA **Mobile Gender Gap Report 2024**
  identifies affordability, skills, safety and access as the key barriers keeping Nigerian women
  offline. See [[dpi.pay]], [[NGA]]. Source:
  [[2024-08-19 GSMA outlines solutions to Nigeria's mobile gender gap (Mobile Gender Gap Report 2024)]].
- **The least-connected frontier — Sierra Leone and South Sudan (2024–25).** **[[SLE]] Sierra Leone**
  sits at **20.7%** internet penetration (DataReportal, Jan 2025) with a wide usage gap, and
  **[[SSD]] South Sudan** ranks **192/193** on EGDI (2024) among the world's least-connected states. See
  [[infra.connect]], [[include.access]], [[SLE]], [[SSD]].
