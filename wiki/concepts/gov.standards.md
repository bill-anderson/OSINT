# Standards (`gov.standards`)

Technical and de facto standards as instruments of policy and sovereignty.

## Key material

- **Open-source standards in practice (2026-06, [[mosip]]).** India's DPI export shows
  the standards thesis working: countries that took the conventional tender-and-vendor
  route "ended in lots of trouble" (lock-in, cost), while open-source **MOSIP** (30
  engagements, 14 rollouts; [[MAR]] first) lets adopters own and switch. See
  [[geopol.india]], [[dpi.id]]. Source: [[2026-06-17 They're taking DPI to the world - The Times of India]].
- **Agency over ownership (2026-06, Verhulst & Gonzalez Otero, "Triad").** A second
  independent argument that sovereignty is about *who can shape/govern/challenge* systems,
  not ownership — openness can be weaponised too. Converges with Eaves. See [[geopol.eu]],
  [[the-govlab]]. Source: [[2026-06-09 The Triad of Tech Sovereignty and Europe's Package Dependency, Openness, and Agency]].
- **The competition evidence base ("Parting Clouds").** Eaves & Bester ([[camp]]) detail
  why concentration persists and how interoperability + procurement can break it. See
  [[2025-01-01 Eaves commodification]], [[tech.industry]].
- **Open standards in a greenfield build (2026-07, Sierra Leone).** The Sierra
  Leone–[[bhutan-ndi]]–[[sign-foundation]] MoU commits to **open-source** technologies and
  **W3C verifiable credentials**, with governance designed so national institutions can
  "**sustainably manage and evolve**" the platform — the standards-as-agency thesis
  applied to a new national ID, via **South–South** transfer (Bhutan → Sierra Leone). Ties
  to [[zambia--dpi-id]] and [[mosip]]. Sierra Leone's **National Data Strategy** (2025) is likewise
  aligned to the **AU Data Policy Framework**. See [[SLE]], [[dpi.id]], [[gov.policy]],
  [[sierra-leone-digital-id-mou]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-08 Sierra Leone partners with Bhutan, Sign on digital identity platform]].

- **Open-source as state policy (2026-06, Zambia).** Zambia's Cabinet **directed
  ministries to adopt open-source** technology, with an open-source-built **NSDI** portal —
  the standards-as-vendor-independence thesis enacted as procurement policy
  ([[smart-zambia-institute]], [[data.open]]). Reinforces the [[zambia--dpi-id]] thread.
  Source: [[2026-06-17 Zambia's govt leans towards open-source]].
- **Standards aren't the bottleneck — governance is (2026-07).** The Warwick/Turing
  interoperability study finds mature *technical* standards still fail without
  **governance and assurance** — a direct counterpoint/complement to the standards thesis:
  interoperable rails need interoperable *institutions* (via **[[afcfta]]**). Source:
  [[2026-07-03 Study finds governance, not technology, is blocking digital ID interoperability]].
  Meanwhile **[[mosip]]** hardened its compliance regime (Fime lab accreditation):
  [[2026-06-09 MOSIP accredits Fime for biometric device testing]]. The EU's Open Source
  Strategy ([[geopol.eu]]) is the wealthy-bloc version of the same bet:
  [[2026-06-03 How the EU's Tech Sovereignty Package Finally Puts Open Source to the Test]].
- **Global (2025, Eaves).** Core thesis of [[david-eaves]]' Tech Policy Press piece:
  **agency comes from interoperability, not ownership**. Governments should shape the
  cloud market the way they standardised **railway gauges** (Britain 1846; US Pacific
  Railroad Act 1863) and electricity — using **procurement power** to force **de facto
  standards** (e.g. mandate S3-compatible storage — [[infra.store]]). "De facto
  standards almost always beat untested de jure standards"; Europe's Gaia-X/Data Act
  ([[geopol.eu]]) failed partly by relying on new, voluntary standards without buying
  power. The Pentagon **JEDI** contract came closest to commodifying cloud but was
  killed by hyperscaler lobbying.

- **Classification frameworks as reference instruments (new).** Standards also include the
  *classification* vocabularies that structure how institutions describe their own work. The
  **[[world-bank-theme-taxonomy]]** (June 2025) — the wiki's first `instrument` entity — is
  the controlled vocabulary the World Bank uses to theme its operations (incl. [[dzap]],
  [[dmap]], [[nigeria-id4d]]); useful for reconciling Bank project language against this
  wiki's own [taxonomy](../taxonomy.md). Data-protection statutes as reference objects sit
  here too — e.g. **[[popia]]** (SA), on an EU-adequacy model. Sources:
  [[2025-06-01 World Bank Theme Taxonomy]].
- **Internet-infrastructure standards (2026-07, Africa).** [[smart-africa]] and [[afnic]]
  signed an MoU to raise the technical standard of African **ccTLD** operators and harden
  **DNS** security/resilience — standards-as-agency applied to the continent's own
  domain-name infrastructure and internet governance. See [[infra.connect]],
  [[smart-africa-afnic-mou]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-09 Smart Africa and AFNIC Partner to Strengthen Africa's Internet Infrastructure]].
- **MOSIP standards & compliance (2026, reference explainer).** The Biometric Update MOSIP
  report details standards development, the MOSIP Marketplace and vendor accreditation as
  the mechanism that makes open-source ID *switchable* — the anti-lock-in bet at scale
  ([[mosip]], [[dpi.id]]). Source: [[2026-05-01 Understanding-MOSIP-Biometric-Update-report]].

- **Harmonisation as a continental standard (2026).** The **[[afcfta-digital-trade-protocol]]**'s
  **Digital Identity Annex** (AU-adopted Feb 2025) is the continental instrument turning
  standards-as-agency into policy: state parties must **harmonise technical regulations**, adopt
  **common technical specifications** for interoperable digital ID, and **jointly assess** each
  other's systems. The **[[adli]]** cohort ([[dial]]) operationalises it across six countries — a
  reminder that the binding layer is *institutional* interoperability, not just protocol. See
  [[gov.regional]], [[dpi.id]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-27 Africa's economic future hinges on breaking down silos. This ADLI cohort advances integration]].
- **Maturity assessment as a reusable standard (2024→2026, DIAL).** DIAL's **[[digital-id-assessment]]**
  — a **12-category** self-assessment scoring a digital-ID system's design, architecture and
  processes — has been adopted by **Ethiopia's NIDP** as an **official monitoring mechanism** for
  **[[fayda-ethiopia-national-id]]** (first used early 2025, re-run 2026; **>42m** registrations,
  **130+** agency integrations, 90m-by-2028 target). Standards as continuous, benchmarkable
  improvement rather than one-off certification. See [[ETH]], [[dpi.registry]],
  [[capacity.research]]. Sources: [[2024-01-16 Digital ID]],
  [[2026-05-12 Strengthening Fayda Ethiopia's improvement journey through the Digital ID Assessment]].
- **Cross-border technical standards in practice (2026-04, SADX).** The **[[sadx]]** technical
  mission with **[[RWA]]**, **[[BEN]]** and **[[GHA]]** worked the concrete standards layer —
  **public-key-infrastructure trust chains, API contracts, certificate management and incident
  response** — for a "functional, federated and decentralised" data-exchange architecture: the
  interoperability standards that make sovereign cross-border verification workable. See
  [[dpi.exchange]], [[infra.cybersec]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-27 Smart Africa drives cross-border data interoperability through SADX Technical Mission]].
- **MOSIP as the de facto ID standard (2026-05).** MOSIP now holds **MoUs with 29 countries** and
  underpins national ID in **Ethiopia, Uganda and Sierra Leone** (**[[MAR]] Morocco** the first
  implementer); **[[TGO]] Togo** has issued 6m unique numbers on it, while Ghana's non-MOSIP Ghana
  Card reaches ~20m (~90%). Architecture choice (centralised vs federated vs decentralised) is
  shaped by trust, governance and local context — common building blocks, divergent national paths.
  See [[mosip]], [[dpi.id]], [[gov.protect]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-28 Why decentralized digital identity looks different in every country]].
- **The multilateral standards frame (2025, WBG).** Two World Bank reference instruments anchor the
  DPI-standards debate: the **[[wbg-dpi-white-paper]]** (Mar 2025) centres three core DPIs — digital
  ID + e-signatures, digital payments, data exchange — on **open standards** and
  federated/decentralised models; and **[[world-development-report-2025]]** ("Standards for
  Development") frames standards as a **"quiet catalyst,"** with a dedicated spotlight on standards
  and DPI. Normative scaffolding the African instruments above align to. See
  [[world-bank-theme-taxonomy]], [[gov.policy]].
- **Benchmarking responsible AI (2026, GIRAI).** The **[[global-index-on-responsible-ai]]** (2nd ed;
  [[global-center-on-ai-governance]]) scores **135 countries** on whether responsible-AI commitments
  reach people in practice — **global average 35/100**, with a two-tier split (**22%** of Global-South
  cases covered by *binding* instruments vs **58%** in the North). It reframes low Southern scores as
  **development gaps**, not ambition gaps — a standards-and-measurement lens on AI governance. See
  [[tech.ai]], [[gov.policy]], [[gov.protect]].
- **Harmonising ID technical specifications — WURI (2026-03).** The **[[wuri-programme]]**
  ([[ecowas]] + World Bank) validated a **Regional and Cross-border Interoperability Strategy** and
  assessed the **harmonisation of technical specifications** among member states toward mutual
  recognition of ID credentials — standards-as-integration for a West African identity ecosystem
  (~300m citizens). See [[dpi.id]], [[gov.regional]], [[XWA]], [[TGO]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-30 ECOWAS, World Bank Advance West Africa Digital Identity]].
- **GIS data standards by regulation — South Africa (2026-04).** **[[icasa]]**'s draft
  rapid-deployment regs mandate a national infrastructure database on **prescribed technical
  standards** — the **WGS84** coordinate reference system and interoperable file formats (ESRI
  Shapefile, GeoJSON, GeoPackage) — for all operator geo-data submissions. A de jure data standard
  imposed via the regulator (fines up to R1m). See [[gov.legislate]], [[data.open]],
  [[infra.connect]], [[ZAF]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-12 Icasa moves to mandate national infrastructure database]].
- **The case for international HMIS register standards (2021, 5-country study).** A multi-country
  study ([[rhis-recording-burden-5country]]; Ghana, Mozambique, Nigeria, Tanzania + Cambodia) found
  PHC facilities juggling on average **34 registers** and **35 monthly reporting forms**, with
  recording eating ~**one-third of consultation time** and reporting **4–15h/worker/month** — and
  concludes there is an "imperative need to invest in **international standards of facility-based
  registers and reporting forms**" for comparable, quality-driven data ([[dhis2]]/HMIS). Standards as
  a fix for the data-collection burden underlying [[dpi.mis]]. See [[data.statistics]],
  [[capacity.training]], [[GHA]], [[MOZ]], [[TZA]]. Source:
  [[2021-09-13 The burden of recording and reporting health data in primary health care facilities in five low- and lower-middle income countries]].
- **Enterprise-architecture standards for government — Nigeria (2026-04).** **[[nitda]]**'s takeover
  of the **[[ngea]]** (Nigeria Government Enterprise Architecture; hosted by [[galaxy-backbone]]) sets
  a **common architecture standard** so MDA systems are interoperable by design — de facto
  interoperability standards enforced through central IT governance. See [[dpi.govtech]],
  [[dpi.exchange]], [[NGA]]. Source: [[2026-04-07 NITDA Assumes Control Of NGEA Infrastructure]].
- **GovTech maturity as a standardised measure (2026-01, World Bank [[gtmi]]).** The **2025 GovTech
  Maturity Index** scores states on **48 indicators across four pillars** — core government systems,
  online services, digital citizen engagement and GovTech enablers (legal frameworks, institutional
  capacity, skills) — explicitly reweighted from "portals launched" toward **interoperability and
  adoption**. **[[TZA]] Tanzania** re-entered Group A ("Extensive") with **[[KEN]]/[[RWA]]/[[UGA]]/[[EGY]]**;
  the index functions as a de facto benchmark disciplining East African digital states. Standards as
  measurement. See [[dpi.govtech]], [[gov.regional]], [[XEA]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-26 Tanzania's GovTech success raises bar for East Africa's digital states]].
- **Regional customs interoperability in production — SIGMAT (2026-01).** [[ecowas]]'s **[[sigmat]]**
  (Interconnected System for the Management of Goods in Transit, live since 2019, presented at the WCO
  2026 Technology Conference) is the working standards layer for cross-border customs: **structured
  interoperability testing**, end-to-end message-exchange simulations between administrations, and a
  secure, scalable **e-Hub** — harmonising and dematerialising transit workflows along regional
  corridors. De facto interoperability standards proven in operation, not on paper. See [[dpi.exchange]],
  [[gov.regional]], [[XWA]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-30 ECOWAS PRESENTS 'SIGMAT' AT THE WORLD CUSTOMS ORGANIZATION 2026 TECHNOLOGY CONFERENCE IN ABU DHABI   Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS)]].
- **Safeguards as a design standard — Lesotho (2026-01).** Lesotho is applying the
  **[[universal-dpi-safeguards-framework]]** (UNICEF + Co-Develop) to the redesign of its
  **[[nissa-lesotho]]** social registry (data on >90% of households): a principles standard for building
  DPI that is **fair, inclusive and safe from the outset** — preventing exclusion of the
  undocumented/disabled/remote and hardening how children's data is collected and used. Rights-protective
  safeguards treated as a reusable standard, not an afterthought. See [[dpi.registry]], [[gov.protect]],
  [[include.access]], [[LSO]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-16 Lesotho's Digital Leap How Child-Centred Systems are Reshaping Social Protection]].
- **Certification stacks as trust infrastructure — Ghana (2026-01).** Ghana's **[[margins-id-group]]**
  illustrates standards-as-market-access: its ICPS arm is the **only West African facility holding ISO
  14298** (government-grade security printing) and is **Mastercard/Visa/Europay-certified** to make
  payment cards (it produces the **[[ghana-card]]**), while the group holds **ISO/IEC 27001:2022**,
  **ISO 22301** and **ISO/IEC 20000**, and subsidiary MIDSA carries CREST and Ghana CSA credentials. As
  ID programmes outrun regulation, verifiable certification becomes the currency of trust — and Margins
  positions it for **AfCFTA-interoperable** systems and SDG 16.9. See [[dpi.id]], [[infra.cybersec]],
  [[gov.protect]], [[GHA]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-28 Margins ID Group emerges as Africa's leading provider of certified digital identity solutions]].
- **An evidence base for AI governance — the UN Scientific Panel (2026-07).** The **UN Independent
  International Scientific Panel on AI** ([[un-ai-scientific-panel]]) presented its **Preliminary Report**
  at the inaugural Global Dialogue on AI Governance (Geneva); the **[[african-union]]** Commission
  coordinated with its four African members (Marivate, Tadesse, Orji, El-Assady) to feed African evidence
  into an emerging global standards process anchored in the Global Digital Compact. Scientific consensus
  and measurement as the scaffolding beneath AI norm-setting. See [[tech.ai]], [[gov.regional]],
  [[global-index-on-responsible-ai]], [[XAF]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-09 African Union pushes for unified African voice at Global AI Governance Dialogue]].

- **ISO 20022 as the payments standard — Nigeria's NPS (2025-11).** **[[nibss]]**'s **National
  Payment Stack** ([[national-payment-stack]]) is built **ISO 20022-compliant** — its first live
  transaction (PalmPay→Wema Bank, 7 Nov 2025) ran on the global messaging standard, with KYC via
  BVN/TIN/RC and multi-currency/cross-border support. Standards adoption as the enabler of Nigeria's
  unified, interoperable payment rail (and NIP rated "Mature" on AfricaNenda's spectrum). See
  [[dpi.pay]], [[NGA]]. Source: [[2026-02-02 How Strategic  Shift of NIBSS Is  Rewiring Nigeria's Payments System]].
- **African participation in internet standards — IETF (2022, RIA).** RIA's
  **[[mapping-african-digital-infrastructures]]** study (Part 3) quantifies negligible African
  contribution to **IETF Internet standards** — ~15 RFCs + 9 drafts from African authors, ~**0.26%**
  of 9,170 RFCs (as of Jan 2022) — driven by technical, funding and language barriers, with ISOC's
  IETF-Africa ([[internet-society-africa-history]]) the main remediation channel. The standards-making
  layer where African agency is thinnest. See [[XAF]], [[capacity.research]]. Source:
  [[2022-06-12 Mapping African Digital Infrastructures (Part 3) Understanding the motivations and challenges of African contributions to the Internet standards development]].
- **An African co-chair in global infrastructure standard-setting (2026-07-10).** Against that
  thinness, the **[[international-advisory-body-on-submarine-cable-resilience]]** — the ITU–ICPC body
  whose final report was approved at WSIS Forum 2026 — was **co-chaired by Nigeria's
  [[bosun-tijani]]**, with summits in **Abuja (2025)** and Porto (2026). Its seven priorities are
  soft-law recommendations, not binding standards: government–industry coordination, streamlined
  permitting, risk monitoring, route diversity/redundancy, preparedness, **the needs of vulnerable
  regions** (LDCs/SIDS), and climate integration. The interesting fact for this concept is the
  **seat**, not the text — an African state in the chair rather than the audience. See
  [[infra.connect]], [[gov.regional]], [[NGA]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-10-itu-icpc-advisory-body-cable-resilience-report]].
