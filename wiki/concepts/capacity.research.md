# Research institutions (`capacity.research`)

Universities and research capacity as the frontier most exposed to compute-cost
exclusion — and to the resulting knowledge and "intellectual sovereignty" gap.

## Key material

- **An AI centre of excellence in a fragile state (2026-07-20, [[SOM]]).** **Somali National University**
  launched the **Somali National Artificial Intelligence Centre (SNAIC)** in Mogadishu as a Centre of
  Excellence for AI research, education, innovation and capacity building, with a stated mandate to
  develop **locally driven AI solutions** across machine learning, NLP, data science, computer vision,
  robotics and AI ethics, applied to education, health, agriculture and governance. Rare and thin — the
  held source carries **no budget, staffing, partner or timeline figures**, so nothing here is a
  capability claim. Its value is as a datapoint at the far end of the compute-exclusion gradient this
  page tracks: the institution-building step happens well before the compute question is reachable. See
  [[tech.ai]], [[capacity.training]], [[tech.innovate]], [[SOM]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-20-somali-national-university-ai-centre]].

- **Open science as research-capacity policy (2026-07-20, [[ZAF]]).** South Africa's
  **[[dsti-south-africa|DSTI]]** reports adopting a **National Open Science Policy** aligned to the
  **2021 UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science**, and points to the **[[african-open-science-platform]]**
  hosted by the **[[national-research-foundation-south-africa|NRF]]** as its continental arm for regional
  collaboration, policy development, capacity building and infrastructure. Openness as research
  *capacity* rather than as transparency. **Adoption date and text are not established** in the held
  source. The UNESCO **first monitoring cycle** findings quoted alongside it are a reference study —
  cited, not absorbed. See [[data.open]], [[gov.regional]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-20-sa-open-science-unesco-gina]].

- **Research capacity inside a revenue authority (2026-07-17, [[ZMB]]).** The
  **[[zambia-revenue-authority|Zambia Revenue Authority]]** signed an **MoU and an NDA** with the
  **[[international-centre-for-tax-and-development|ICTD]]** ([[zra-ictd-mou]]) covering research,
  capacity building and innovation — CG **Dingani Banda** framing it as building "a fully data-driven
  tax administration," ICTD ED **Giulia Mascagni** around **domestic resource mobilisation**. A useful
  variant of the research-institutions pattern: not a university or a think tank standing outside the
  state, but an external research body **embedded in a line agency's own data**. What the NDA covers is
  undisclosed. See [[finance.mou]], [[dpi.govtech]], [[zambia--dpi-govtech]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-17-zambia-revenue-authority-ictd-deal]].

- **An African university as AI deployer, not just trainer (2026-07-17, [[MWI]]).** The **Malawi
  University of Science and Technology** commissioned an **AI fertigation** system (automated irrigation
  + fertiliser dosing from live crop data) and an AI-assisted climate-resilient tree nursery at its
  **Thyolo** campus, led by **Dr Jabulani Nyengere** (geoinformatics), under **TagDev 2.0** — the
  *Transforming African Agricultural Universities* programme. It is a small, concrete counter-example to
  the priced-out-academia entry below: the constraint bites hardest on **frontier-model compute**, while
  applied, sensor-scale AI remains within reach of a national university and can be pointed straight at a
  domestic problem. See [[tech.ai]], [[digital.rural]], [[capacity.training]], [[MWI]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-17-malawi-must-ai-fertigation]].
- **Priced-out academia (2026-06, *The Sun* / Ademola).** African universities are
  "among the hardest hit": a single medium LLM training run (~£150k) can exceed a
  department's annual research budget. In **Nigeria**, <5% of universities can train even
  modest ML models; in **South Africa**, only 8% of SMEs use any AI vs 29% in the EU.
  **Rwanda** and **Kenya** have piloted AI health diagnostics but scaling nationally is
  financially prohibitive. Risk: African scholars become "consumers rather than creators"
  of AI knowledge, undermining intellectual sovereignty. See [[tech.ai]],
  [[include.divides]], [[capacity.training]]. Source:
  [[2026-06-17 Priced out of the future Confronting the rising cost of AI in Africa]].

- **Measuring AI-governance capacity — GIRAI (2026).** The South-Africa-based
  [[global-center-on-ai-governance]] (Rachel Adams) produces the [[global-index-on-responsible-ai]]
  (GIRAI), the fullest cross-country measure of whether responsible-AI commitments reach people:
  the 2nd Edition scores 135 countries (global average **35/100**), finding implementation lags most
  in the Global South (**45%** vs 55% where frameworks are active) and a two-tier gap in *binding*
  rules. An African-led node countering the Northern framing of AI standards; its founder argues
  sovereignty "cannot mean total independence from global AI supply chains" but can mean stronger
  control over data, procurement and local skills. See [[tech.ai]], [[gov.policy]]. Sources:
  [[2026-07-11 Global-Index-on-Responsible-AI-2026.pdf]],
  [[2026-05-21 Pushing back from Big Tech Africa's hard road to AI sovereignty]].

- **Statistical-research capacity under strain — PARIS21 (2026).** [[paris21]] and Open Data Watch's
  discussion paper [[2026-07-11 data-systems-crossroads]] diagnoses converging pressures on national statistical
  offices — **2024–25 donor funding cuts**, AI disruption, expanding mandates and eroding trust — and
  frames a "fork in the road" between optimisation and transformation of data systems. Bears directly
  on whether African research and statistical institutions can produce their own evidence base. See
  [[data.statistics]]. Source: [[2026-07-11 data-systems-crossroads.pdf]].

- **Local research institutions as DPI builders (2026-05).** Uganda's Government Systems Prototype
  Showcase and gazetted **National Innovator Registry** explicitly invite *research institutions*,
  innovation hubs and local firms to submit working DPI/digital-ID prototypes for government
  procurement — building domestic capability (its ID runs on [[mosip]]; >28m records migrated) rather
  than importing systems. See [[UGA]], [[dpi.id]], [[tech.innovate]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-22 Uganda launches prototype program for digital ID and public infrastructure]].

- **AI public goods for research (2026-05).** The [[gates-anthropic-partnership]] (**$200m/4yr**,
  [[gates-foundation]] + [[anthropic]]) funds shared **datasets, benchmarks and infrastructure** —
  free public goods — across health, education and agriculture, aimed at extending researchers' reach
  in LMICs (incl. work with IHME on the Global Burden of Disease). Whether this builds local research
  capacity or deepens dependence on frontier-lab tooling is the open question. See [[tech.ai]],
  [[finance.mou]], [[XGL]]. Source: [[2026-05-14 Making AI work for more people]].

- **Local data as research sovereignty — agriculture (2026-03).** [[nasa-harvest]]'s
  [[catherine-nakalembe]] found Western-trained AI could not recognise local crops in western
  Kenya, so her team collected **5m+ images in two weeks** to build African cropland models
  across [[KEN]]/[[UGA]]/[[SEN]] — a concrete case that models must be *built with*, not
  parachuted into, the Global South, or they entrench "digital colonialism." Digital-farming is a
  ~$30bn (2024)→$84bn (2034) market big tech is racing for. See [[tech.ai]], [[data.satellite]],
  [[include.divides]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-12 Western AI models 'fail spectacularly' in farms and forests abroad]].
- **Frugal AI and the compute-exclusion frontier (2026-04).** With US + Chinese firms operating
  **>90% of AI data centres** (Oxford) and Africa/South America almost none, the Cambridge
  **Frugal AI Hub** — building smaller open-weight models on sub-$50 hardware for data
  sovereignty — is in talks with **[[KEN]] Kenya and [[NGA]] Nigeria** officials. Research
  capacity is reframed around *efficiency and ownership*, not frontier compute. See [[tech.ai]],
  [[tech.innovate]], [[include.divides]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-02 Nations priced out of Big AI are building with frugal models]].
- **Africa's thin share of AI research (2026-03).** [[google-deepmind]]'s AI Research Foundations
  rollout ([[google-ai-research-foundations]]) states the stakes bluntly: Africa is 1/5 of world
  population but only **~1.8% of global AI-research output** (SCImago). The curriculum (with UCL,
  AIMS and Nigeria's FATE Foundation) aims to widen who *produces* AI knowledge, not just who
  consumes it. See [[capacity.training]], [[tech.ai]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-17 Broadening advanced AI education across Africa]].
- **The health-data-systems scholarly cluster (2021–2026).** A body of HMIS research anchors the
  "locally-owned data systems" argument: [[hisp-centre]]/[[dhis2]] (Univ. Oslo) documents that
  after 2025 US/USAID cuts turned off donor-run systems (PEPFAR's DATIM; Kenya's EMR access),
  **locally-owned national DHIS2 systems stayed online** — DHIS2 runs in >90 countries (75
  national), touching ~3.2bn people, though a Feb-2025 survey found only **33.3%** of reported
  systems fully operational post-cuts. A BMC review of 12 African countries finds **11 of 12**
  aligned HMIS to donor rather than national strategies (Malawi the exception), and an [[ETH]]
  Ethiopia HMIS-data-use index quantifies facility-level use. The through-line: governance and
  local ownership, not tools, decide whether African research and statistical institutions can
  produce their own evidence base. See [[dpi.mis]], [[data.statistics]], [[gov.standards]].
  Sources: [[2025-04-03 Now is the time to invest in locally-owned health data systems]],
  [[2021-08-04 Health information systems in developing countries case of African countries]],
  [[2023-05-11 Data for local decision-making, not a mere reporting requirement development of an index to measure facility-level use of HMIS data  Published in Journal of Global Health Reports]].

- **RIA's research programme as evidence infrastructure (2022–2026).** **[[research-ict-africa]]**
  is the batch's most active African research institution: its **[[after-access]]** demand-side
  survey programme, the **[[mapping-african-digital-infrastructures]]** study of African open-source
  and IETF-standards contribution, and thematic reports on information integrity and NSOs-as-data-
  custodians. The research-capacity through-line: African evidence produced by African institutions
  (figures live on those pages, not here). See [[GHA]], [[TZA]], [[BEN]], [[data.statistics]].
- **DPI measurement methodology as a research reference (2026-07).** The **[[dpi-map]]** (**[[ucl-iipp]]**)
  published its "Measuring DPI" framework and codebook — the counting methodology (6 attributes;
  ID/payment/data-exchange indicators, resting on Eaves & Rao 2025) the wiki cites when interpreting
  cross-country DPI figures. **[[data-for-policy]]** (Cambridge *Data & Policy* journal) is a further
  data-for-governance research node captured this batch. See [[XGL]], [[gov.standards]]. Sources:
  [[2026-07-12 Codebook - DPI Map]], [[2026-07-12 Methodology - DPI Map]],
  [[2022-05-30 Mapping African Digital Infrastructures (Part 1) A qualitative analysis of open-source contributions]].

- **Research transparency and documentation (2026 batch).** Two named-author pieces
  press for building African AI *knowledge* infrastructure. Chinasa Okolo urges
  African AI startups to **open-source models and publish** benchmarked/red-team
  results in peer-reviewed venues (NeurIPS/ICML/ACL; Deep Learning Indaba,
  SACAIR) to counter "AI snake oil," noting cost/access barriers for Global
  Majority researchers and maintaining an open **Harvard Dataverse** dataset on
  African AI startups ([[2025-12-15 African AI Startups Should Publish Openly]]).
  Rebecca Mbaya's **TAIS Knowledge Map** documents ~**53** interviewed African AI
  innovators across six thematic clusters, while reflecting on the contradiction
  of mapping African AI sovereignty with non-African tools
  ([[2026-03-25 On Documenting African AI Knowledge What Becomes Possible]]).
  Russell Southwood's ICT4D retrospective names [[research-ict-africa]] among the
  standing African evidence bases for the digital-divide record
  ([[2026-04-16 The Long View - What ICT4D did for Africa and why that deep past matters for today]]).
  See [[tech.innovate]], [[data.open]], [[include.divides]].
