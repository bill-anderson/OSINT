# Access to services (`include.access`)

Whether DPI actually reaches people — financial inclusion and access to public and
private digital services as the test of infrastructure's value.

## Key material

- **The inclusion gap (2026-07, TechAfrica / AfricaNenda–Equity–Gates).** Despite mobile
  money's spread, **~400 million** Africans remain excluded from formal financial
  services. The partnership's pitch is that interoperable DPI (payments + ID) plus
  private-sector execution can close that gap across **20–30 markets**. Gates'
  Nanjira Sambuli: "building DPI is only a first step. The real measure of success is
  whether people use it to improve their lives." See [[dpi.pay]], [[dpi.id]],
  [[include.divides]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-07 Equity, AfricaNenda and Gates Foundation Partner to Advance Digital Public Infrastructure Across Africa]].
- **ID as service-access enabler (2026-07, Nigeria).** [[nimc]] frames the NIN's next
  phase as moving it from a "mere identity tool" to one that **enables and simplifies
  access** to public and private services under "one person, one identity." Access is the
  stated payoff of the coverage push (136M → 180M). See [[dpi.id]], [[NGA]],
  [[nigeria--dpi-id]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-09 Nigeria seeks support to operationalize new digital ID law as NIN tops 136M]].

- **Reach vs affordability, this batch (2026-04–06).** Several items sharpen what "access" means:
  - *Coverage push.* China Industrial Bank is backing **≥1,000** of Nigeria's [[nucap]] rural towers
    (3,700 planned; target >20m underserved), extending the network edge ([[NGA]], [[digital.rural]]).
    Source: [[2026-06-04 Chinese bank backs Nigeria towers project]].
  - *Affordability, not coverage.* The Africa CEO Forum/Askya framing (via Semafor) argues Africa's
    divide is "less about whether networks exist and more about whether people can afford to use
    them." [[starlink]] improves network-level affordability in remote areas, but retail prices still
    hinge on local regulation and income, and a licensing asymmetry (Senegal: **$150k** vs **>$50m**
    for a 5G licence) risks "revenue leakage" that could deter rural buildout. See [[include.divides]],
    [[data.satellite]]. Sources: [[2026-05-22 View How SpaceX is making a quiet bet on Africa]],
    [[2026-05-26 Africa risks losing billions to satellite internet operators, report says]].
  - *Access via language & interface.* Nigeria's [[govguide-nigeria]] (Meta Llama; English/Hausa/
    Igbo/Yoruba) targets rural, low-literacy citizens for public-service access — though rural
    broadband and device gaps cap uptake ([[dpi.govtech]], [[tech.ai]]). Source:
    [[2026-05-22 Nigeria launches GovGuide, an AI chatbot for public service access]].
  - *Access via ID.* In Nigeria the **NIN is now mandatory for all social services** ([[nimc]]),
    making digital ID the gateway to grants, banking and telecoms — inclusion and gatekeeping at once
    ([[dpi.id]]). Source: [[2025-02-13 Digital ID necessity increases in Nigeria despite lingering challenges]].
  - *The fee counter-current.* As governments fold mobile-money rails into **tax collection** (Kenya,
    Nigeria, Senegal), added transaction fees threaten to push low-income and informal users (~90% of
    SSA employment) back toward cash — access won, then priced back out ([[dpi.pay]], [[finance.new]]).
    Source: [[2026-05-29 Africa's Mobile Money Networks Are Becoming Tax Infrastructure]].
  - *Skills as access.* The [[liquid-c2]] / [[microsoft]] solar-powered mobile digital lab (North
    West SA) extends AI and Power-Platform training into an underserved community, building on the
    Mthatha centre (515 trained, 149 placed) ([[ZAF]], [[capacity.training]]). Source:
    [[2026-05-27 Liquid, Microsoft unveil mobile digital lab for North West youth]].

- **The adoption gap, measured — GSMA 2026 (2025 data).** The
  [[2026-03-01 GSMA State of the Industry Report on Mobile Money 2026]] ([[gsma]]) quantifies where DPI
  stops reaching people: SSA smartphone ownership only **~33%** of adults (cost the top barrier),
  women **~14%** less likely to use mobile internet (885m women unconnected, ~60% in SSA/South
  Asia), and digital-financial-literacy gaps (Ethiopia: 60% of women "don't know how to use it").
  As the sector shifts from USSD to super-apps and QR, the device- and skills-poor risk
  confinement to the most basic uses. See [[dpi.pay]], [[include.divides]], [[capacity.literacy]].
  Sources: [[2026-03-01 GSMA State of the Industry Report on Mobile Money 2026]],
  [[2026-03-24 Mobile Money in Africa Strong Growth, but Adoption Still Falls Short]].
- **Closing the device gap by tax — SA smartphone excise (2025-04, evidence 2026, ZAF).** The policy
  response to the ~33% device-ownership problem above: effective **1 April 2025**, South Africa's
  **[[dcdt-south-africa]]** / National Treasury removed the **9% ad valorem luxury excise** on
  entry-level smartphones **priced below R2,500 (~USD 150)**. The **[[gsma-sa-smartphone-tax-reform-2026]]**
  evidence report (primary now held) measures the effect: entry-level unit-sales growth flipped
  **−7.9%→+6.2%**, entry-level volumes **+80%** and feature-phone volumes **−87%** (Mar 2025→Feb
  2026, a substitution to smartphones), and market value **+15%** (**R2.7bn→R3.1bn/mo**) — affordability
  gain without lost value. Minister **[[solly-malatsi]]** popularised it (rounder "+1.1m units/11
  months") as treating the smartphone as an access-to-opportunity rail, not a luxury. See
  [[tech.industry]], [[include.divides]], [[gov.policy]], [[ZAF]]. Sources:
  [[2026-07-10 SOLLY MALATSI  Removing smartphone tax means access to opportunity]],
  [[gsma-sa-smartphone-tax-reform-2026]].
- **Access via interoperability and tax rails (2026-03/04).** Two cases show access widening
  through *usability*: M-PESA Ethiopia's [[ethswitch]] integration ([[safaricom]]) drove active
  users **+258% to 5.2m** by linking 30+ banks/wallets ([[ETH]], [[dpi.pay]]); and the
  [[kenya-revenue-authority]] WhatsApp ([[meta]]) filing chatbot targets the ~15m
  registered-but-inactive Kenyan taxpayers, meeting informal/youth users "where they are" —
  inclusion via familiar rails, though added fees can push low-value users back to cash. See
  [[dpi.pay]], [[gov.policy]], [[KEN]]. Sources:
  [[2026-03-30 M-PESA Ethiopia surges 258% as EthSwitch fuels growth]],
  [[2026-04-02 WhatsApp tax filing set to pull millions into Kenya's tax net]].
- **Rural reach — towers, satellite and free wifi (2026-04).** Universal-access schemes and
  satellite are extending the network edge: [[helios-towers]] completed **133 UCSAF sites** in
  Tanzania (~45% of its TZA sites rural), linking rural communities to financial, health and
  government services ([[TZA]], [[digital.rural]]); and [[ZWE]] Zimbabwe switched on **free public
  Wi-Fi** (409 hotspots, target 1,000) across schools/clinics/public spaces plus newly licensed
  [[starlink]] and a [[uae]]-backed coding drive, framing connectivity as the AI-readiness
  on-ramp. See [[digital.rural]], [[infra.connect]], [[data.satellite]]. Sources:
  [[2026-04-13 Helios Towers completes 133 UCSAF sites to expand rural connectivity in Tanzania]],
  [[2026-04-07 Zimbabwe switches on free wifi to power AI future]].

- **Reach and service-access, this ingest (2026-01–03).** New items sharpen the coverage-vs-use and
  access-to-services distinction:
  - *Free connectivity for public facilities.* [[NAM]] Namibia will give **schools and clinics** under
    towers built in the last two years **free internet for 7 years** (N$80m / ~$5m in nine RAN towers),
    while regulator **[[cran]]** proposed **tax breaks on 4G devices** to lift utilisation stuck **below
    50%** — the tower is "an enabler, not transformation" (Minister [[emma-theofelus]]). See
    [[digital.rural]], [[include.divides]], [[infra.connect]]. Sources:
    [[2026-01-27 Namibian schools, clinics to get free internet]],
    [[2026-02-02 Namibian regulator proposes tax breaks on 4G devices]].
  - *Satellite for the "white areas."* [[CIV]] Côte d'Ivoire's **[[orange-cote-divoire]]** launched
    **[[orange-sat]]** (with **[[eutelsat]]**) to reach households, businesses and public institutions
    beyond 4G/fibre — satellite as the access layer for education, health and e-administration in
    remote regions. See [[data.satellite]], [[digital.rural]]. Source:
    [[2026-01-21 Orange Côte d'Ivoire Launches Orange Sat Satellite Internet to Boost Nationwide Connectivity]].
  - *Regional service-access finance.* The World Bank's **$137m [[wardip]]2** (WARDIP phase 2 —
    Benin, Liberia, Sierra Leone) targets broadband for **>5m** citizens and digital public services
    for **>5m** more (~**10.6m** total), pairing connectivity with cross-border service delivery. See
    [[BEN]], [[LBR]], [[SLE]], [[XWA]], [[gov.regional]], [[finance.new]]. Source:
    [[2026-03-16 New $137M initiative to enable digital service access for millions in three ECOWAS nations]].
  - *ID as the access channel — Benin.* **[[anip-benin]]** reports **≥75%** of identification services
    were accessed via **digital platforms** in 2025 (10.5m services, 4m citizens; 37% portal / 30% ANIP
    app / 8% USSD; 25m eKYC verifications), with the [[seamfix]]-built ePass app and
    birth-registration-linked PINs widening the gateway — digital ID as the measured route to services.
    See [[BEN]], [[dpi.id]], [[data.statistics]]. Source:
    [[2026-02-02 Benin reports 75% of identification services accessed through digital platforms]].
  - *Restoring a basic service — South Sudan.* [[SSD]] South Sudan abolished cash at immigration
    offices for a **bank-linked digital payment system** for passports/national ID
    ([[south-sudan-national-revenue-authority]], EU-supported) after a booklet-supply crisis locked
    thousands out of travel — access restored, though with no interoperable national digital ID beneath
    it. See [[dpi.pay]], [[dpi.govtech]]. Source:
    [[2026-01-19 South Sudan introduces digital payment system for passports after supply crisis]].

- **Access below the smartphone line — voice AI + literacy (2026-01).** For users without
  smartphones, data budgets or reading fluency, voice/NLP AI is pitched as the access channel:
  [[viamo]]'s Ask Viamo Anything ([[ZMB]]) and [[mtn-group]]'s feature-phone Miss Baza assistant
  bring health, agriculture, finance and education services to the "voice era." The binding
  constraint here is [[capacity.literacy]] as much as coverage. See [[tech.ai]],
  [[include.divides]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-15 Leveraging AI to Bypass the Smartphone Barrier and Advance Digital Inclusion in Africa]].

- **Access as a regulatory agenda (Russell Southwood, 2026-06-25).** Southwood's
  regulatory reform piece frames several tools here as deliberate access levers a
  repurposed regulator should mandate: **data-rollover** provisions (already in
  Ghana, Nigeria, SA), **zero-rating** of government sites and free public Wi-Fi,
  **MTN "Open Time,"** and freeing spectrum (SA's "innovation spectrum," 900 MHz)
  for underserved areas — recasting affordability/usage measures as policy choices,
  not market outcomes. See [[include.divides]], [[infra.connect]], [[gov.policy]].
  Source: [[2026-06-25 Regulating for Africa's future digital economy – A draft agenda to speed things up]].

- **ID-access frictions — Sierra Leone and South Sudan (2024–25).** In **[[SLE]] Sierra Leone** a
  **$6–7** national-ID card fee plus low literacy gate access, while social-protection **digital
  payments** first reached **~35,000** households (2025). In **[[SSD]] South Sudan**, **17,400** returnee
  national-ID cards were issued via **EU/UNHCR** (2024-11). See [[dpi.id]], [[include.divides]], [[SLE]],
  [[SSD]].
