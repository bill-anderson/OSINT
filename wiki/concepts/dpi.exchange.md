# Data Exchange (`dpi.exchange`)

Interoperability layer that lets government and private systems share data — and, at the
cross-border scale, the legal enforceability of that exchange.

## Key material

- **[[MWI]] Malawi — a binding data-exchange regulation (2026-06).** Malawi proposed a **Government Data Sharing
  Regulation** to govern its **Boma Lathu** National Data Exchange Platform — the "missing piece," since MDAs
  still shared citizen data by email, USB and paper. It routes all requests through the **National Data Centre**,
  mandates **encryption**, sets a **common digital standard** for system-to-system exchange, and requires binding
  **Data Sharing Agreements** with usage limits and audit trails atop the DPA 2024 — a rare example of the
  *governance* layer of interoperability being codified, not just the plumbing. See [[malawi--dpi-govtech]],
  [[gov.legislate]], [[infra.store]]. Source:
  [[2026-06-11-government-data-sharing-regulation-boma-lathu-itweb]].
- **DPI as roadmap and sustainability problem (2026).** The **[[dpi-roadmap-playbook]]** (Gates
  consortium) and DPGA's *Building Open Digital States* frame DPI as three building blocks (ID, payments,
  data exchange) built on open-source DPGs for sovereignty; DIAL flags the **capex-funded, opex-unfunded**
  financing gap that kills national data-exchange platforms ([[adli]]); ODI
  reads DPI through the G20 troika and its contested, India-linked origins. CIPESA interrogates DPI's
  governance/power in East Africa; ODI's Nigeria study shows cross-border payments failing at the
  corporate-ID layer, not the rail. Sources:
  [[2026-03-01 DPI Roadmap Playbook]],
  [[2026-01-20 Building Open Digital States Country Case Studies on the Impact of DPGs for DPI]],
  [[2026-06-16 The financing gap for data exchange is well documented so why does it persist]],
  [[2026-05-29 Digital Public Infrastructure at the G20 tensions commitments and continuities]]. See [[dpi.pay]], [[gov.regional]].

- **From owning data to governing it (2026 H1).** Rwanda operationalised a public-sector
  **[[rwanda-national-data-sharing-policy]]** (open-by-default, Five Safes, institutions as stewards, NISR governance / RISA platform split) via the
  [[nisr]] Data Governance Unit; the draft **[[african-guidelines-data-access]]** pushes proactive
  disclosure and interoperable access continent-wide; and CIPESA flags weak cross-border interoperability
  of app-based **health** data. Sources:
  [[2026-05-07 What will determine the success of Rwanda’s data governance push]],
  [[2026-05-07 African Guidelines on Data Access ACHPR Res 620 draft]],
  [[2026-06-23 Towards Regulation of App-Based Health Data in Africa]],
  [[2025-06-09 Rwanda charts a new course for government data sharing]]. See [[data.open]], [[dpi.mis]].

- **Cross-border data under AfCFTA (2026-07).** As [[afcfta]] accelerates continental
  expansion, **incompatible national regimes** turn data-sharing into legal exposure — "a
  framework built for Lagos may create liability in Nairobi." Governance/enforceability
  is now a deal-gating **valuation** question. See [[gov.protect]], [[gov.legislate]],
  [[gov.regional]]. Source: [[2026-07-03 AfCFTA, Data Governance and the Hidden Legal Gaps Reshaping Africa's Digital Economy - P.M. News]].
- **Smart Africa Data Exchange federates cross-border verification (2026-04, SADX).**
  [[smart-africa]]'s **[[sadx]]** ran a Technical Assessment Mission in Kigali with
  **[[RWA]] Rwanda, [[BEN]] Benin and [[GHA]] Ghana**, working through **PKI trust chains,
  API contracts, cross-border verification-request processing and incident response** — a
  **"functional, federated and decentralised"** architecture that keeps national control
  while enabling trusted cross-border data sharing. See [[dpi.id]], [[gov.regional]],
  [[gov.standards]], [[infra.cybersec]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-27 Smart Africa drives cross-border data interoperability through SADX Technical Mission]].
- **[[BEN]] Benin — a live X-Road at national scale (2026-07).** Benin's **[[uxp-benin]]**
  exchange (built on **[[cybernetica]]**'s UXP, the commercial descendant of X-Road) had
  processed **138.2m requests** (32.7m in the last 30 days) across **73 members, 176
  information systems and 512 services** as of 2026-07-11, with identity ([[dpi.id]], ANIP),
  transport (ANATT) and tax (DGI) the busiest providers — the operational proof of what
  SADX is standardising regionally. See [[dpi.govtech]], [[data.statistics]]. Sources:
  [[2026-07-11 UXP Directory]], [[2026-07-03 Cybernetica]].
- **Estonia exports the interoperability model (2026-05).** **[[estdev]]** signed an MoU
  with [[smart-africa]] (Brussels) to build **interoperable, reusable DPI** across Smart
  Africa's 42 member states, explicitly to **reduce vendor lock-in** and strengthen
  cyber-resilience — pairing Estonia's X-Road/UXP heritage with continental programme
  architecture. Deal: [[estdev-smart-africa-mou]]. See [[geopol.eu]], [[finance.mou]],
  [[infra.cybersec]], [[XAF]]. Source: [[2026-05-28 Estonia backs Africa digital infrastructure]].
- **AfCFTA: data exchange as trade infrastructure (2026-04/07).** The 2nd AfCFTA Digital
  Trade Forum (Lagos) adopted **Eight Calls to Action** to operationalise the
  **[[afcfta-digital-trade-protocol]]** — cross-border data flows, interoperable payments
  ([[papss]]), paperless trade and cybersecurity — as Nigeria took the Council Bureau chair.
  Sec-Gen [[wamkele-mene]] frames localised compute (**700+ data centres**) and harmonised
  regimes as prerequisites for the digital single market; the DTP's **Digital Identity
  Annex** (AU-adopted Feb 2025) is being piloted through DIAL's **[[adli]]** cohort. See
  [[gov.regional]], [[dpi.pay]], [[dpi.id]]. Sources:
  [[2026-07-09 Nigeria Takes AfCFTA Council Chair as Digital Forum Charts Roadmap for Implementation]],
  [[2026-04-08 Africa's digital trade push requires 700 data centres, says AfCFTA chief]],
  [[2026-05-27 Africa's economic future hinges on breaking down silos. This ADLI cohort advances integration]].
- **Trusted data-sharing needs governance, not just pipes (2026-05, World Bank).** The
  World Bank's DPI / data-sharing work ([[wbg-dpi-white-paper]]), presented at ID4Africa
  2026, stresses that data exchange multiplies the attack surface and that under-resourced
  DPAs are the weak link; the **[[eac]]** has stood up a shared body for its eight member
  DPAs to align enforcement across borders. See [[gov.protect]], [[infra.cybersec]],
  [[XEA]]. Source: [[2026-05-16 World Bank, African DPAs outline formula for trusted digital identity, DPI]].
- **Health data is the hardest exchange layer (2026-04).** Interoperability is "the most
  critical and least resolved component" of African EHR: **OpenMRS, [[dhis2]]** and
  proprietary national systems run as **silos**, so data created in one is invisible in
  another. [[africa-cdc]] notes **>50% of facilities lack power or internet**, and duplicate
  testing burns **5–10% of diagnostic budgets**. See [[dpi.mis]], [[gov.standards]],
  [[dpi.id]] (patient-identity layer), [[ETH]], [[RWA]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-23 Health Data Without Continuity Why Electronic Health Records Still Have Not Scaled Across Africa]].
- **Secure data exchange as DPI pillar (2026-07).** The
  [[equity-africanenda-gates-dpi-partnership]] names "secure data exchange" alongside ID
  and payments as core continental DPI.
- **AfCFTA digital single market (2026-07-07).** The Digital Trade Forum framed
  **interoperable cross-border payments** ([[papss]]), mutual recognition of digital ID
  and harmonized e-signatures as the machinery for data and value to "move as freely as
  goods" — data exchange as trade infrastructure. See [[gov.regional]], [[afcfta]].
  Source: [[2026-07-07 African nations recommit to operationalize AfCFTA digital single market framework]].
- **Domestic interoperability (2026-07, Malawi).** Malawi's **Bomalathu** integration
  ([[dmap]]) explicitly targets **interoperability across ministries, departments and
  agencies** via a single portal — the within-government face of data exchange. See
  [[MWI]], [[dpi.govtech]]. Source: [[2026-07-07 Malawi integrates immigration e-services]].
- **Conceptual framing (Eaves & Rao, UCL IIPP).** Consented data exchange is one of the
  DPI "building blocks" in the measurement framework — see [[2025-01-01 dpi_conceptualisation_and_measurement]].

- **[[ZMB]] Zambia (2025).** **ZamConnect** is Zambia's data-integration / exchange
  platform — a core enabler in [[dzap]] Component 2 ("interoperable and secure
  platforms"), tying together digital ID ([[dpi.id]]), payments ([[dpi.pay]]) and
  e-services ([[dpi.govtech]]). Interoperability and open standards are explicit design
  goals, run through [[smart-zambia-institute]].

- **[[ETH]] Ethiopia — EthSwitch as the national interoperability switch (2026-03).** M-PESA
  Ethiopia active users surged **+258.5% YoY to 5.2m** (quarter to Dec 2025) after its **Oct 2025
  integration with [[ethswitch]]**, which links the wallet to **30+ banks and digital wallets**
  regulated by the National Bank of Ethiopia, enabling real-time wallet↔bank transfers and
  interoperable merchant payments via **EthQR** (50,000+ merchants). The national payment switch as
  the exchange layer that turns a walled wallet into interoperable rails. Entities [[safaricom]],
  [[ethswitch]]. See [[dpi.pay]], [[include.access]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-30 M-PESA Ethiopia surges 258% as EthSwitch fuels growth]].
- **[[ETH]] Ethiopia — a sovereignty-framed cloud exchange (2026-04).** [[wingu-africa]] launched
  the **Wingu Cloud Exchange (WCX)** in Ethiopia, a private-cloud platform for **in-country data
  residency** (compute, storage, Kubernetes, security) priced in local currency and offering
  **hybrid interconnect with Azure and AWS** — data-exchange plumbing built to keep sensitive data
  onshore while bridging to hyperscalers. First launched in [[TZA]] (Nov 2025); part of a $60m
  East-African expansion (also [[DJI]]). See [[infra.store]], [[gov.protect]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-01 Wingu Africa debuts cloud exchange platform in Ethiopia]].
- **[[NGA]] Nigeria — NGEA as the MDA interoperability backbone (2026-04).** **[[nitda]]** formally
  took over the **[[ngea]]** (Nigeria Government Enterprise Architecture) infrastructure — built over
  ~2.5 years with Korea's KOICA under e-Government Masterplan 2.0 — designed so government platforms
  are **interoperable for seamless data exchange across ministries, departments and agencies**,
  cutting duplication. Piloted at NIMC, Customs, Immigration and NITDA; hosted by
  **[[galaxy-backbone]]**. The within-government exchange bus. See [[dpi.govtech]], [[gov.standards]].
  Source: [[2026-04-07 NITDA Assumes Control Of NGEA Infrastructure]].
- **[[NGA]] Nigeria — NGDX, a verify-and-share data backbone (2025-08).** [[nitda]] unveiled the
  **Nigeria Data Exchange ([[ngdx]])** — built with the **[[european-union]]**, Finland and Estonia —
  to end the eight-siloed-database problem (NIN/BVN/NCC/NIS/FIRS/FRSC/CAC/INEC) with a verify-and-share
  backbone, targeting operational status by end-2025. Complements the [[ngea]] architecture above. See
  [[nigeria--dpi-exchange]], [[dpi.govtech]], [[dpi.id]]. Source:
  [[2025-08-28 Nigeria to launch National Data Exchange (NGDX) platform to boost digital economy]].
- **[[SEN]] Senegal — a National Interoperability Platform (2026-03).** Under the
  **[[new-deal-technologique]]**, Senegal is rolling out a **National Interoperability Platform** to
  break silos between ministries and enable **secure, real-time data exchange** behind online public
  services, paired with sovereign data centres/cloud to keep data in-country; the platform was
  first announced (2025-04) as a JICA-supported interministerial system, its first phase (end-2025)
  targeting faster social assistance. See [[dpi.govtech]],
  [[gov.protect]], [[infra.store]]. Sources:
  [[2025-04-22 Senegal launches interministerial interoperable data-exchange platform]],
  [[2026-03-25 Senegal Accelerates Digital Sovereignty with Rollout of Flagship Technology Initiatives]].
- **[[XWA]] West Africa — cross-border ID as exchange (2026-03).** The **[[wuri-programme]]**
  ([[ecowas]] + World Bank) validated a regional/cross-border interoperability strategy for **mutual
  recognition of ID credentials** across member states — identity as the cross-border data-exchange
  layer for free movement and services. See [[dpi.id]], [[gov.regional]], [[TGO]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-30 ECOWAS, World Bank Advance West Africa Digital Identity]].
- **[[XEA]] East Africa — harmonising the rulebook for exchange (2026-03).** The 4th EAC Regional STI
  Conference (Kigali) advanced a regional **AI Policy Roadmap** to harmonise shared rules and values
  across the eight-member bloc, Rwanda's Ingabire stressing East African data "stays in our hands" —
  the governance harmonisation that cross-border exchange presupposes. Entity [[eac]]. See
  [[gov.regional]], [[gov.standards]], [[tech.ai]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-31 EAC moves to create unified AI Sovereignty bloc]].

- **[[XWA]] SIGMAT — customs interoperability as regional exchange (2026-01).** [[ecowas]]
  presented **[[sigmat]]** (Interconnected System for the Management of Goods in Transit) at
  the WCO 2026 Technology Conference (Abu Dhabi). Launched 2019, SIGMAT dematerialises transit
  workflows and secures **electronic message exchange between member-state customs
  administrations** via a central **e-Hub**, with corridor interconnections now in full
  operational use and non-ECOWAS states expressing interest — customs transit as cross-border
  data exchange. See [[gov.regional]], [[gov.standards]], [[infra.capacity]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-30 ECOWAS PRESENTS 'SIGMAT' AT THE WORLD CUSTOMS ORGANIZATION 2026 TECHNOLOGY CONFERENCE IN ABU DHABI   Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS)]].
- **[[BEN]] Benin — SECUROUTE joins the national exchange (2026-01).** [[cybastion]] is
  partnering Benin's Ministry of Economy & Finance to extend infrastructure for **full
  interoperability with [[securoute]]**, the national road-enforcement data platform, so
  traffic and regulatory data exchange securely across police, transport and safety agencies —
  another provider plugging into Benin's [[asin-benin]]/[[uxp-benin]] exchange fabric. See
  [[dpi.govtech]], [[infra.cybersec]], [[gov.standards]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-27 Cybastion Advances Digital Government Solutions, Supports Benin's SECUROUTE Infrastructure Upgrade]].
- **[[LSO]] Lesotho — X-Road + MOSIP as the DPI stack (2026-02).** [[micsti-lesotho]] and the
  National University of Lesotho reviewed three systems: **X-Road** for secure
  ministry-to-ministry information sharing, **[[mosip]]** for biometric digital ID, and a
  Decision Support System for planning/budgeting — a government–academia collaboration standing
  up the interoperability layer. See [[dpi.id]], [[dpi.govtech]], [[dpi.mis]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-20 MICSTI AND NUL ADVANCED LESOTHO DIGITAL PUBLIC INFRASTRUCTURE]].
- **[[TZA]] Tanzania — GovESB as the exchange backbone (2026-01).** Tanzania re-entered the
  World Bank **[[gtmi]]** top tier (Group A) on the strength of its **Government Enterprise
  Service Bus (GovESB)** — the secure data-exchange spine linking ministries — alongside
  HCIMS, the Ajira portal, NeST procurement and GePG payments. The Bank's emphasis has shifted
  from portals to interoperability, adoption and outcomes; digital citizen engagement remains
  the weak pillar region-wide (Kenya, Rwanda, Uganda, Egypt also Group A). See [[dpi.govtech]],
  [[gov.standards]], [[XEA]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-26 Tanzania's GovTech success raises bar for East Africa's digital states]].
- **Cross-border identity — the hard exchange problem (2026-07, DIAL).** A [[dial]] fellowship
  analysis frames portable identity as three unsolved governance problems: **sufficiency**
  (jurisdictions disagree on acceptable proof), **adequacy** (registries collect different
  attributes), and **sovereignty** (whose law governs data once it crosses a border). National
  ID cards are mutually recognised for travel among **[[KEN]] Kenya, [[RWA]] Rwanda and
  [[UGA]] Uganda** yet often inadequate for cross-border financial services; DIAL floats
  standardised identity schemas issuing purpose-specific, locally-compliant credentials — the
  governance layer beneath [[afcfta]] identity portability. See [[dpi.id]], [[gov.protect]],
  [[gov.regional]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-10 Every country defines identity differently. Can digital public infrastructure bridge those differences]].
- **[[COD]] DR Congo — card–wallet interoperability (2026-01).** [[onafriq]] supported the
  Q4-2025 **Visa Pay** launch in DRC, bringing interoperability between mobile-money and card
  networks "at scale" in a market with 29m mobile-money accounts (34.6% penetration, Q4 2024)
  but weak interbank rails — an inter-scheme exchange layer in an early-interoperability
  market. See [[dpi.pay]], [[include.access]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-26 How Onafriq is growing digital finance amid uncertainty in DRC]].

- **Data exchange as the laggard DPI layer (analysis, 2025-08).** In Samora Kariuki's six-country
  DPI-maturity rubric, the **data-exchange** layer scores weakest almost everywhere — even joint
  leaders Ghana (40/60) and South Africa (39) have "weak Data Exchange," and India's TReDS /
  Account Aggregator model is held up as the target. He frames consented data exchange (with ID
  and payments) as the third rail of "generative" public platforms that closed systems lack. One
  analyst's framework, attributed to Kariuki. See [[dpi.pay]], [[dpi.id]], [[GHA]], [[ZAF]].
  Source: [[2025-08-04 93 - Ecosystem Debt or Generative Platforms A Case for Digital Public Infrastructure]].

- **[[GHA]] Ghana — why the exchange layer lags (case study, DIAL).** [[dial]]'s Ghana case study
  explains the weakness Kariuki scores: the national data-exchange hub **eGIF (2009) stalled** on
  funding and unclear mandates, and a private-sector-led ecosystem produced **fragmented, decentralized**
  government data. **Ghana.gov** — the fintech-enabled payments portal — became the de-facto exchange
  catalyst instead: a use-case-first path where a top-down interoperability framework failed. See
  [[GHA]], [[dpi.govtech]], [[dpi.pay]], [[ghana--dpi-pay]]. Source:
  [[2024-01-01 Ghana national data exchange case study (Digital Impact Alliance)]].

- **[[GAB]] Gabon — a government interoperability platform as a WB results indicator.** The
  **[[digital-gabon-project]]** funds secure public-sector data exchange; its results framework targets
  **5 government entities actively exchanging data on the interoperability platform** by June 2026 —
  at baseline (0) as of Dec 2024, one measure of the project's slow execution. See [[GAB]]. Source:
  [[2024-12-19-world-bank-digital-gabon-isr-seq7]].

- **[[STP]] São Tomé and Príncipe — an interoperability framework by law (2024).** São Tomé enacted a
  **National Framework for Interoperability** (Law 1/2024) to govern public-sector data sharing — the
  legal scaffolding for exchange ahead of the platform. See [[dpi.govtech]], [[gov.legislate]], [[STP]].
- **[[SLE]] Sierra Leone — an X-Road service bus in prototype (2024).** Sierra Leone is prototyping a
  **Government Service Bus** on X-Road via a Ministry of Finance PFM working group. See [[dpi.govtech]],
  [[gov.standards]], [[SLE]].
- **[[XEA]] East Africa — a regional cross-border data-sharing framework (2026-06).** The [[eac]], under
  the World Bank **[[eardip]]** project, validated a **secure cross-border data-sharing framework** in
  Dar es Salaam — common transfer standards preserving national regulatory authority — as core
  infrastructure for the Single Digital Market (mobile money, e-commerce, telemedicine, customs). At
  national level **[[BDI]] Burundi** put its electronic **Guichet Unique** (+ ABREMA) into production
  toward the [[asycuda]] customs system. See [[gov.regional]], [[gov.protect]], [[BDI]]. Sources:
  [[2026-06-25-eac-backs-secure-data-sharing-framework]],
  [[2026-02-19-burundi-guichet-unique-importation-wearetech]].
