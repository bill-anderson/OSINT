# Technical Capacity (`infra.capacity`)

The *hard limits* on Africa's digital build-out — grid power, fibre/subsea
bandwidth, and compute — and the contest over who controls them. Across the
2026-07 batch the binding constraint on ambition repeatedly turns out to be
**capacity**, not intent.

## Key material

- **Power as the ceiling (2026-07, DCD).** The **$1bn [[microsoft-g42-kenya-datacenter]]**
  stalled because its planned **1 GW** draw is a **third of Kenya's ~3 GW** grid;
  President **[[william-ruto]]** said serving it would mean cutting power to half
  the country. Kenya targets **10 GW by 2030** and seeks **$38bn** for
  energy/infrastructure. Capacity — not capital or willingness — killed the
  timeline. See [[infra.energy]], [[infra.store]], [[KEN]].
- **Bandwidth build-out (2026-06).** [[seacom]]'s new **Nairobi–Kampala**
  terrestrial route launches at **1 Tbps, scalable to 30 Tbps** with sub-50ms
  auto-reroute; the **[[powertel-paratus-fibre-corridor]]** (Botswana–Zimbabwe–Zambia)
  lights up **>10 Tbps-capable** DWDM; **[[telecom-namibia]]** takes **1 Tbps**
  of dedicated Equiano capacity via Angola. Regional backbone capacity is being
  laid ahead of demand. See [[infra.connect]], [[XEA]], [[XSA]].
- **Data-centre capacity as national infrastructure (2026-02, SA).** South
  Africa reframed data centres as **"as critical as electricity, ports and
  transport"** — with **55 DCs built** and **>R50bn** expected over three years,
  but the same power-and-grid constraint the rest of the continent faces. See
  [[infra.store]], [[gov.policy]], [[ZAF]].
- **The 1% problem.** Africa holds **~1% of global data-centre capacity**
  (What Google's cohort; sovereignty essay), forcing data offshore to be
  processed — the capacity gap that underwrites [[tech.ai]] dependency and the
  [[include.divides]] in compute. LEO satellites ([[starlink]]) add downlink
  capacity but on foreign-owned terms ([[2026-06-16 Who Owns Africa's Sky]]).
  Full hyperscaler cloud regions on the continent remain a handful (AWS's only
  African region is Cape Town), so most heavy compute is still served from
  abroad. See [[amazon-web-services]], [[2026-07-11 AWS in Africa]].
- **The 700-facility capacity target (2026-04, AfCFTA).** [[wamkele-mene]] cast
  **>700 data centres** (from ~220 today, ~**1%** of global commercial capacity)
  as the hard capacity precondition for AfCFTA digital trade. Installed IT
  capacity stands at ~**360 MW operational**, **238 MW under construction** and
  **656 MW planned** (early 2026), projected to **triple to ~1.2 GW by 2030** —
  the build needed before intra-African platforms, fintech and AI workloads can
  be hosted onshore. See [[infra.store]], [[afcfta]], [[dpi.exchange]], [[MAR]].
  Source: [[2026-04-08 Africa's digital trade push requires 700 data centres, says AfCFTA chief]].
- **Bandwidth built for AI (2025-09).** [[seacom]] 2.0's formal launch details a
  **48-fibre-pair** architecture engineered for high-capacity, low-latency AI
  workloads — networks are expected to carry **>10bn AI agents by 2030** — with
  landing stations reframed as **"AI communication nodes."** Backbone capacity is
  being laid explicitly ahead of the compute demand it will feed. See
  [[infra.connect]], [[XEA]], [[XSA]]. Source:
  [[2025-09-24 Unveiling Seacom 2.0 - ​ ​A new digital renaissance and subsea network to power the Indian Ocean Basin's digital and AI future]].

- **The human/production-capacity dimension (reference, 2022).** Capacity is not only megawatts
  and fibre but the ability to *build* the stack: the RIA study
  **[[mapping-african-digital-infrastructures]]** (a 3-part analysis, published here as reference
  literature) documents Africa's low-but-rising share of open-source and Internet-standards
  (IETF/RFC) contribution and the funding, skills and language barriers behind it. Captured on the
  publication page; figures not promoted here. See [[capacity.research]], [[tech.innovate]],
  [[gov.standards]], [[colonialism]]. Sources:
  [[2022-05-30 Mapping African Digital Infrastructures (Part 1) A qualitative analysis of open-source contributions]],
  [[2022-06-12 Mapping African Digital Infrastructures (Part 2) A quantitative analysis of open-source contributions]],
  [[2022-06-12 Mapping African Digital Infrastructures (Part 3) Understanding the motivations and challenges of African contributions to the Internet standards development]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: capacity is where sovereignty claims meet physics. A "sovereign
  cloud" still needs megawatts and fibre; whoever supplies the scarce capacity
  (grid, subsea landing, GPU) holds leverage — the throughline from
  [[microsoft-g42-kenya-datacenter]] to [[2026-06-16 Who Owns Africa's Sky]].
