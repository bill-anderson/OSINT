# Data protection (`gov.protect`)

Personal-data regimes across African jurisdictions — and the friction their
**fragmentation** creates for cross-border digital businesses.

## Key material

- **Enforcement is the frontier, not legislation (2026 H1).** The gap is now implementation, not
  statute: Unwanted Witness’s **Privacy Scorecard 2025** ([[privacy-scorecard-report]]) puts
  continental compliance at just **46%** (health sector 22%), and a 13-CSO coalition led by
  [[media-rights-agenda]] argues Nigeria’s crisis is one of **horizontal accountability** — the NDPA,
  NDPC and a 121m+ digital-ID system coexist with NINs sold for ₦100 and an INEC breach via leaked
  credentials. DPAs in Kenya, Nigeria and Tanzania ramped up enforcement in early 2026 (Kenya 110+
  decisions in 2025). Sources:
  [[2026-05-18 Africa’s Digital Economy Is Expanding Faster than Its Privacy Protections]],
  [[2026-06-22 MRA, Civil-Society Coalition Call on Nigerian Government to Strengthen Horizontal Accountability for Citizens Data]],
  [[2026-06-23 Bimonthly Update on Privacy in Africa (January & February 2026)]]. See [[gov.legislate]], [[dpi.id]].

- **Continental state of play — 44 laws, 38 authorities (end-2025).** The authoritative annual
  roundup ([[2026-01-11 The Year of the Teeth Data Protection in Africa Roundup, 2025, Projections for 2026]],
  [[digital-policy-alert]] × [[tech-hive-advisory]]) counts **≥44 African countries with
  data-protection laws** (~80% of AU members) and **≥38 fully operational DPAs**, leaving **6 with a
  law but no regulator** and **11 yet to legislate**; it projects **>50 laws** by end-2026. New laws
  came into force in **Djibouti** (Digital Code), **The Gambia** and **Botswana**;
  Algeria/Ghana/Kenya/Mauritius/Nigeria/Senegal are amending existing statutes.
  [[code-for-africa]] ([[2026-05-05 Africa's data protection laws began to bite in 2025]]) corroborates
  the same 44/38 figures and frames 2025 as the year enforcement moved from "paper law" to real
  "teeth." This anchors a count analysts reported variously as **44** (Adebowale) vs **36** (Mbaya) —
  the primary trackers give **44** *(bears on REV-001(b))*. See [[gov.legislate]], [[dpi.id]].
- **Fragmentation as a valuation problem (2026-07, P.M. News / Okorie).** As **AfCFTA**
  expands, incompatible national regimes — **Nigeria's Data Protection Act 2023**,
  **Kenya's Data Protection Act 2019** — turn "governance" into a deal-killer: "a data
  governance framework built for Lagos may create legal exposure in Nairobi." Regulators
  "are no longer passive"; investors now treat data-processing architecture and privacy
  compliance as a **defensibility** question in due diligence. See [[gov.legislate]],
  [[dpi.exchange]], [[gov.regional]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-03 AfCFTA, Data Governance and the Hidden Legal Gaps Reshaping Africa's Digital Economy - P.M. News]].
- **Data localization as sovereignty (2026-07, Nigeria).** The **[[cbn]]** directive
  (circular 2026-06-15) forces all financial institutions to host transactional data
  **inside Nigeria by 1 Jan 2027** — the sharpest African case of localization as
  sovereignty ("keeping sensitive transaction records within national jurisdiction becomes
  … national security"). The binding question is infrastructural, not legal. See
  **[[nigeria--gov-protect]]**, [[infra.store]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-07 CBN's local data order puts Nigeria's data centres to test]].
- **Local hosting for data control (2026-07, Malawi).** Malawi's Bomalathu integration
  ([[dmap]]) keeps immigration data on **locally hosted servers**, letting the department
  **retain ownership and control** — localization as sovereignty and secure public-sector
  data management. See [[MWI]], [[dpi.govtech]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-07 Malawi integrates immigration e-services]].
- **[[NGA]] enforcement widens — litigation and a big-tech probe (2026-07).** Civil-society
  lawyers ([[digital-rights-lawyers-initiative]]) sued [[inec]] for operating without a website
  privacy policy, an alleged **[[nigeria-data-protection-act-2023]]** breach — testing whether
  the Act binds public bodies. Separately, President Tinubu ordered the **[[fccpc]]** to probe
  [[meta]], Alphabet/[[google]], X and GenAI firms over uncompensated scraping of Nigerian news
  content — the competition-law flank to enforcement, citing NDPC's prior ₦7.2bn privacy
  penalties. A second analysis of the [[cbn]] directive warns domestication ≠ protection and
  flags NDPA cross-border-transfer tension. See [[gov.legislate]], [[nigeria--gov-protect]].
  Sources:
  [[2026-07-10 FIJ Showed That INEC Broke Data Law. Digital Rights Lawyers Sue the Commission 10 Months Later]],
  [[2026-07-10 Why Nigeria Is Investigating Meta, Google, X and AI Firms]],
  [[2026-07-10 CBN's Data Localisation Directive Puts Nigerian Fintechs In A Cloud Dilemma]].

- **Enforcement against big tech (2026-06, Nigeria).** Following a **2025 court-approved
  settlement**, **[[meta]]** now funds the **[[ndpc]]**'s two-year M-SIDP programme
  ([[ndpc-meta-settlement]]) — an African regulator compelling platform compliance. But
  [[2026-06-08 The Promise and Limits of African Data Sovereignty]] shows the ceiling: under
  the **2018 US CLOUD Act**, US-parent firms ([[microsoft]]/Azure via **[[g42]]** in Kenya)
  can be compelled to hand over data regardless of location, and Nigeria's **$220m FCCPC Meta
  fine** reportedly went unpaid. Jurisdiction asserted; enforcement uneven. See [[geopol.usa]],
  [[infra.store]]. Sources:
  [[2026-06-09 Meta backs NDPC in landmark privacy initiative]],
  [[2026-06-08 The Promise and Limits of African Data Sovereignty]].
- **Governance > technology (2026-07).** A Warwick/Turing study finds cross-border
  digital-ID interoperability fails at the **governance and assurance** layers (data
  protection among them), not technical standards, recommending supranational governance
  via bodies like **[[afcfta]]**. See [[dpi.id]], [[gov.standards]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-03 Study finds governance, not technology, is blocking digital ID interoperability]].
- **Adequacy vs localization — South Africa (2026-07).** SA runs an EU-style **adequacy**
  regime under **[[popia]]** (§72 cross-border transfers, §19 accountability; now
  enforcement-driven), while its 2021 **Draft Data & Cloud Policy** leans to localization
  and data-ownership claims **[[research-ict-africa]]** warns could obstruct AfCFTA data
  flows. AI sharpens the choice: the **[[trendai]]**/Anthropic deployment pitches a
  locally-governed SA data centre as POPIA-compliance-by-architecture. See
  **[[south-africa--gov-protect]]**, [[ZAF]]. Sources:
  [[2025-01-01 South African Digital Sovereignty at the Crossroad of Securitization and Development (Chapter 4) - Digital Sovereignty in the BRICS Countries]],
  [[2026-07-09 Anthropic's Claude to bolster data sovereignty in SA]].
- **Bilateral data-governance diplomacy (2026-07, Kenya).** Kenya and the US discussed
  Kenya's **Draft Data Governance Policy (2026)** and **secure cross-border data flows** —
  data governance as trade/investment enabler and vector of external policy influence
  ([[geopol.usa]]). See [[KEN]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-10 Kenya and U.S. Discuss Draft Data Governance Policy to Strengthen Digital Economy]].
- **Regulatory harmonisation — Nigeria (2026-07).** Minister **[[bosun-tijani]]** paused new
  internet-platform rules pending a unified national framework, convening a
  **[[ncc]]**/**[[nitda]]**/**[[ndpc]]** committee — data governance folded into wider
  digital-economy regulatory coordination. Source:
  [[2026-07-08 FG Halts Enforcement of New Regulations on Internet Platforms]].
- **Competition law as a platform lever (2026-07, Nigeria).** Abuja opened an **[[fccpc]]**
  probe (FG directive 2026-07-10, after a Nigerian Press Organisation petition) into alleged
  anti-competitive practices by **[[meta]]**, Alphabet/[[google]], X and generative-AI
  platforms — paired with the [[cbn]] onshoring mandate as a twin "data sovereignty" test of
  regulatory reach over foreign firms. The FCCPC's **2025 $222m Meta penalty**
  (consumer-protection breaches) is under appeal, distinct from the [[ndpc]] data-protection
  track. See [[gov.legislate]], [[tech.industry]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-10 Nigeria tests data sovereignty as foreign tech firms facing probe]].
- **Digital-ID as the rights frontier — South Africa (2026-05, Burke).** A critique
  of the DHA draft **Identification Regulations** ([[sa-digital-id-regulations]], gazetted
  2026-05-04) argues the framework concentrates power in the Director-General and under-protects
  the data subject: **no right to erasure** of biometric data, **implied consent**, **no citizen
  access logs or notification**, no selective disclosure — measured against GDPR, eIDAS 2.0 and
  Estonia's access-transparency model. Data protection as the contested governance layer beneath
  DPI. See [[dpi.id]], [[south-africa--gov-protect]], [[ZAF]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-29 Opinion South Africa's Digital Identity Regulations A Governance Crossroads]].
- **The DPA-capacity formula (2026-05, ID4Africa AGM).** A [[world-bank]]-led session put
  **data-lifecycle trust** at the centre of DPI, cataloguing **six data-storage risks** (vendor
  immaturity, single points of failure, function creep, cryptographic gaps, insider threat, absent
  redress). African DPAs are "frequently under-resourced": **[[MUS]] Mauritius** mandates chief data
  officers and runs ~**3,000 enforcement actions/yr, 99% resolved amicably** (now adding
  administrative fines); **[[BEN]] Benin's APDP** has a dedicated **AI policy** with strict
  purpose-limitation; **[[LBR]] Liberia's** data-protection bill is before its legislature; and the
  **[[eac]]** created a body for its **eight** national DPAs to align cross-border. See
  [[id4africa]], [[gov.regional]], [[UGA]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-16 World Bank, African DPAs outline formula for trusted digital identity, DPI]].
- **Payment data as a monetary-sovereignty battleground (2026-04→07).** Localization now reaches
  money itself: SARB's Kganyago warned (Oct 2025) that **dollar stablecoins** could cost African
  states **monetary sovereignty**, with **$205bn** moving through SSA digital rails Jul 2024–Jun
  2025 (+52%), much via USD stablecoins outside central-bank oversight. Nigeria's [[cbn]] answered
  with a 15-member task force and the regulated **cNGN**; meanwhile **[[visa]]** is settling
  **[[safaricom]] M-Pesa** top-ups in stablecoins in **[[COD]] DR Congo** via Visa Pay with **[[onafriq]]**
  ([[visa-mpesa-onafriq-drc-stablecoin-pilot]] — core mechanism corroborated on record; no jointly-announced
  pilot and USD peg inferred). Governing transaction data becomes inseparable
  from governing the currency. See [[dpi.pay]], [[geopol.usa]]. Sources:
  [[2026-04-23 Africa's $205 Billion Digital Shift Is Redefining Money, and Central Banks Are Losing Control]],
  [[2026-07-10 Visa and M-Pesa Launch Blockchain Stablecoin Pilot in DR Congo]].
- **Health data-sharing as the sharp new data-protection frontier (2026-02→03).** US bilateral
  health MOUs under the **[[america-first-global-health-strategy]]** (which replaced USAID/PEPFAR)
  make *health data* — sensitive under most African regimes — the hardest live test of these
  laws. Each reportedly bundles a **Data-Sharing Agreement** giving the US
  access to national surveillance and lab databases (10-yr post-termination) plus specimen/genomic
  sharing (25-yr), for five years of *declining* funding. **[[KEN]] Kenya** signed first
  (**[[us-kenya-health-framework]]**, ~$2.5bn, Rubio–Mudavadi 2025-12-04) but its **High Court
  suspended** the deal after the Omtatah / Consumer Federation petition (injunction 2025-12-19): a
  legal opinion found it breached the **Data Protection Act 2019** and **Digital Health Act 2023**
  (both classify health data as sensitive and require localisation absent reciprocal safeguards) and
  bypassed Parliament. **[[ZMB]] Zambia**'s draft MOU ([[zambia-us-health-mou]]) has the most
  extractive terms yet — **$1.012bn/5yr** for **10-yr health-data access** and a
  **25-yr pathogen-data pledge** (the longest of any signatory), financing reportedly tied to
  **copper/cobalt mining**; minister Muchima denied the mining link (15 Feb) and was fired
  three days later by President Hichilema, who frames the cuts as a chance to "take care of our
  own affairs" and has requested "revisions." The **[[odpc-kenya]]** and courts had
  already primed this by ordering **Worldcoin** to stop processing and delete ~**300,000**
  Kenyans' biometric data (2023 ODPC suspension, 2025 court ruling) — precedent that
  data-protection law can halt an external data-extractive project. **[[ZWE]] Zimbabwe rejected**
  its ~$367m deal as "clearly unbalanced" (Mnangagwa) and **[[ZMB]] Zambia** pushed back on the
  data clauses; **[[africa-cdc]]**'s Kaseya declined a US observer role, insisting "we want to own
  our data." Domestic data protection as a constraint on foreign policy, with a demonstration effect
  across the 22+ signatory states (Senegal's later MOU adds that data "remain governed by Senegalese
  law"). See [[geopol.usa]], [[dpi.mis]], [[finance.mou]], [[SEN]], [[ZMB]]. Sources:
  [[2026-03-27 Kenya's Health Deal Is a Stress Test for the America First Global Health Strategy]],
  [[2026-03-19 African Digital Health Data Is a Condition of Global Health Funding]],
  [[2026-02-25 Zimbabwe Turns Down U.S. Health Funding, Citing Sovereignty Concerns]],
  [[2026-02-25 US accused of 'shameless exploitation' over proposed Zambian health aid deal]],
  [[2026-02-26 Zambia rejects U.S. health aid over mining partnership ties]].
- **Washington's counter-offensive against data sovereignty (2026-02→03).** As African states build
  localisation regimes, the US works to roll them back. A State Department cable (**18 Feb 2026,
  signed Rubio**), classified as an *action request*, directs diplomats worldwide to monitor and
  **counter data-localization and data-sovereignty laws** — naming the EU's GDPR — and to promote
  the US-led **Global Cross-Border Privacy Rules Forum** as the "trusted" alternative
  ([[us-department-of-state]]). It sits inside a wider campaign (a Section 301 investigation against
  Brazil, anti-DSA lobbying, a WTO e-transmission duty-moratorium push opposed by India, South
  Africa and Indonesia). The bilateral **health MOUs** above are the same doctrine in practice: data
  sovereignty branded a barrier to American cloud/AI. See [[geopol.usa]], [[gov.policy]],
  [[gov.legislate]], [[XGL]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-12 How Trump's Data Regulation Crackdown Undermines Digital Sovereignty]].
- **The DPA/institutional build-out across smaller states (2026-01→02).** Even with the framework
  patchwork uneven, the *institutions* are being stood up: **[[COG]] Congo-Brazzaville** installed a
  **[[national-personal-data-protection-commission-congo]]** (~22 Jan 2026) — an independent body
  empowered to advise, warn and sanction, framed as "digital rule of law"; **[[SOM]] Somalia**'s
  Council of Ministers approved a **Regulatory Framework for the Public Data Protection Law** (early
  Feb 2026, [[somalia-ministry-communications-technology]]) to operationalise its existing statute;
  **[[MOZ]] Mozambique**'s **[[intic-mozambique]]** launched a pilot **data-breach reporting
  platform** (Feb 2026); and **[[MWI]] Malawi** used Data Privacy Week (Jan 2026) to declare
  "Privacy by Design" and its **[[malawi-data-protection-act-2024]]** "no longer optional."
  Enforcement capacity, not statute count, is the live question (cf. the ID4Africa formula above).
  See [[dpi.govtech]], [[gov.legislate]]. Sources:
  [[2026-01-22 Congo-Brazzaville intros data protection commission]],
  [[2026-02-06 Somalia adopts data regulatory framework]],
  [[2026-02-06 Mozambique intros data breach platform]],
  [[2026-01-28 Malawi positions data privacy as cornerstone of digital development]].
- **Cross-border identity as a data-governance problem (2026-07, DIAL).** A [[dial]] fellowship
  frames portable identity around three governance problems — **sufficiency** (jurisdictions
  disagree on what proves identity), **adequacy** (registries collect different attributes) and
  **sovereignty** (whose law applies once identity data leaves its country). National ID cards are
  mutually recognised for *travel* among **[[KEN]]/[[RWA]]/[[UGA]]** yet often inadequate for
  cross-border *financial services* — so AfCFTA-scale integration ([[afcfta]]) hits data protection,
  not technology, as the binding constraint (reinforces "governance > technology" above). See
  [[dpi.id]], [[gov.regional]], [[gov.standards]], [[XAF]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-10 Every country defines identity differently. Can digital public infrastructure bridge those differences]].
- **Chinese surveillance as data protection by its absence (2026-04).** An IDS "Smart City
  Surveillance in Africa" survey (11 countries) found Chinese firms — **[[zte]]**, **[[hikvision]]**,
  **[[huawei]]** — supplying facial-recognition and ANPR "safe city" systems in *every* country
  surveyed, >**$2bn** spent, typically via ~$250m China Eximbank loans bundling cameras + a national
  data centre + training ("integrated governance packages" creating institutional lock-in). Nigeria
  is largest (China Exim advanced $399m of $470m; 10,000 Lagos cameras). The data-protection story is
  what is *missing*: systems "repurposed to monitor political opposition… in the absence of adequate
  legal oversight" (Zimbabwe facial-recognition fears; Mozambique cameras in opposition strongholds),
  and — Georgetown's Jili warns — formal legal frameworks can *legitimise* rather than constrain
  surveillance — the missing layer beneath biometric mass surveillance. See
  [[infra.cybersec]], [[geopol.china]], [[tech.ai]], [[NGA]], [[ZWE]], [[ZMB]], [[SEN]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-04 Chinese surveillance rolled out in Africa ZTE, Hikvision, Huawei at the helm]].
- **Facial recognition — build the rules the tech lacks (Nagadya, 2026-06).** Building on
  Buolamwini's bias research (34.7% error on dark-skinned women), Nagadya catalogues large African
  biometric-surveillance spends with no legal safeguards: **Uganda $126m / 1,800 Kampala cameras**,
  **Nigeria >$470m**, **Kenya $219m**, across **11 African countries** — an **IDS (Institute of
  Development Studies) March-2026 report** finding *no adequate legal framework in any*.
  Counter-references: the **[[eu-ai-act]]** banned real-time public facial recognition for law
  enforcement (Feb 2025); Rwanda's AI policy carries a human-oversight requirement; Nigeria's CBN
  requires fintech AI explainability. She proposes pre-deployment accuracy testing on African faces,
  appeal mechanisms and tightened national-security exemptions. Surveillance is used against
  opposition in Uganda/Zimbabwe/Mozambique (complements the Chinese-surveillance bullet). See
  [[tech.ai]], [[infra.cybersec]], [[UGA]], [[NGA]], [[KEN]], [[ZWE]], [[MOZ]]. Source:
  [[2026-06-15 If The Technology Can't See Us Accurately, We Build The Rules That Make It]].
- **Kenya — health-deal litigation moved, and a data-selling policy (2026-06).** The
  [[us-kenya-health-framework]] litigation advanced: after the **[[consumer-federation-of-kenya]]**
  (COFEK) petition won a **High Court suspension** of the data-transfer component, the **Court of
  Appeal reversed** it (case continuing) — Nagadya frames it as data sovereignty in action, in an
  African data-localisation stack (**[[au-data-policy-framework]]** 2022, Kenya Cloud Policy 2025,
  Ghana's Draft Data Protection Bill 2025). Separately, Rose Mutiso reads Kenya's **Draft National
  Data Governance Policy (May 2026)** — behind the "Kenya wants to sell your data" headline — as a
  plumbing/interoperability reform with a small data-marketplace ([[kenya-data-marketplace]])
  component, flagging **weak anonymisation**, non-consent via mandatory **eCitizen**, and **EU/German
  TA** whose own GDPR/Data-Governance-Act frameworks would bar the same reuse at home. See [[KEN]],
  [[geopol.eu]], [[geopol.usa]], [[kenya--gov-protect]]. Sources:
  [[2026-06-08 Kenyan Citizens Took A $2.5 Billion Deal With America To Court To Fight For Their Health Data]],
  [[2026-06-08 Kenya Wants to Sell Your Data. But It's Complicated]].
- **[[NAM]] Data sovereignty as a licensing veto — Namibia (2026-03).** Namibia's **[[cran]]** denied
  **[[starlink]]** an operating and spectrum licence, meeting only 3 of 6 statutory criteria; two
  failures were governance ones — **ownership** (100% foreign, breaching the 51%-Namibian rule, no
  ministerial exemption) and **national security / data sovereignty**, CRAN holding that 100% foreign
  ownership undermines "jurisdiction, enforceability of compliance obligations, and… effective
  oversight." Data sovereignty as a hard gatekeeping tool against a foreign operator (cf. the same
  fight in [[ZAF]]). See [[infra.connect]], [[gov.legislate]], [[sovereignty]].
  Source: [[2026-03-26 Starlink blocked from operating in Namibia]].
- **[[ZAF]] Grassroots resistance to digital-ID data collection (2026-03).** Beyond the Burke
  critique above, South African **traditional leaders** (Amadumisa, under UCoSA) opposed the proposed
  digital-ID rollout, demanding prior consultation, public education and possibly a **referendum** —
  citing fraud risk, rural exclusion (no smartphones/connectivity) and privacy fears about
  biometrics/"microchips." Data protection contested from below, not just in the gazette. See
  [[dpi.id]], [[digital.rural]], [[include.divides]], [[south-africa--gov-protect]].
  Source: [[2026-03-31 Concerns over South Africa's proposed digital ID system from traditional leaders]].

- **The NDPA 2023 as frame — Nigeria's live data-protection debate (2026-02/03).** Nigerian analysis
  makes the **[[nigeria-data-protection-act-2023]]** (which created the **[[ndpc]]**) the reference
  point for a widening "trust deficit." A specialist (Samiat Akande) reframes protection as economic
  *infrastructure* — investors judge jurisdictions on data governance, so implementation, not statute,
  decides competitiveness, with GDPR the model to shape rather than follow
  ([[gov.legislate]], [[finance.new]]). But **accountability gaps** dominate: Dataphyte (Okediran)
  catalogues a fragmented digital-rights ecosystem — the 2021 seven-month Twitter/X suspension (est.
  up to ₦10.72tn cost), NITDA's 48-hour-takedown Code of Practice (>13.5m accounts deactivated by
  mid-2025), Cybercrimes Act s.24 used against journalists, an alleged 2024 exposure of **~43m NIN
  records** with no transparent breach-notification, and — the structural flaw — an NDPC **under
  ministerial control** ("a regulator that needs ministerial clearance is hardly independent"). The
  **[[nimc]]** NIN regime sharpens both: a Guardian analysis (Ikeh) warns that party membership
  **e-registration requiring a valid NIN** (the APC's nationwide drive, with alleged coercion of Enugu
  civil servants) turns political affiliation — sensitive data — into "surveillance data" and tests
  the NDPA's consent, purpose-limitation and DPIA duties; a THISDAY investigation shows citizens
  **blocked from correcting** NIN errors (a device-locked self-service portal, 72% of adults without
  smartphones per [[research-ict-africa]]) — a §24(1)(e) accuracy breach that "converts regulatory
  failure into revenue." Data protection as Nigeria's contested rights layer. See [[dpi.id]], [[NGA]],
  [[nigeria--gov-protect]]. Sources:
  [[2026-03-04 Data Protection is the Next Frontier for Nigeria's Digital Economy, Says Samiat]],
  [[2026-02-02 NIN, Party e-registration and the NDPA 2023 When politics meets privacy (and why Nigerians should care)]],
  [[2026-02-27 Who Protects Nigeria's Digital Citizens Unmasking Accountability Gaps in Digital Rights Governance]],
  [[2026-03-07 Nigerians Groan Under Digital Identity Regime That Turns Basic Rights into Privileges]].
- **Humanitarian data — the extreme-vulnerability case (2026-07).** The AI-governance gap in
  humanitarian/nonprofit work: the **WFP Gaza breach** exposed ~600,000 households' data (IDs,
  phones, locations); 93% of aid workers use AI but only 22% have formal policies (75% of those
  surveyed in SSA/MENA/Asia-Pacific); UNHCR holds ~19m records incl. 15.8m facial images, WFP 31m
  people across 59 countries. The **"unconsenting beneficiary"** — refugees and famine survivors
  who cannot meaningfully refuse data collection — is the sharpest edge of data protection. See
  [[dpi.id]], [[tech.ai]], [[XSS]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-09 Uncovering the Humanitarian and Nonprofit Sector's AI Governance Crisis]].
- **Continental framework → national action — São Tomé (2026-07).** The **[[au-data-policy-framework]]**
  (AUDPF) moving from continental reference to national institutional reform: **[[STP]] São Tomé and
  Príncipe** — one of Africa's smallest states — held a Data Governance Capacity Building Workshop
  (9–10 Jul 2026) on national AUDPF adoption, run by the **[[d4d-hub]]** (#D4DataGOV / Team Europe)
  with **[[giz]]**, **[[auda-nepad]]** and **[[kictanet]]**. The demand-side counterpart to the
  data-localisation-stack thread above — small states operationalising the AU's data-governance
  norms. See [[gov.policy]], [[gov.regional]], [[capacity.training]], [[XWA]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-13 D4D Hub on Sao Tome and Principe]].
- **[[CAF]] Central African Republic — law without an authority.** Law No. 24.001 (2024,
  [[car-data-protection-law-24-001]]) is comprehensive — tiered CEMAC/ECCAS transfers, resident DPO,
  fines to 5% of turnover — but the supervisory authority it mandates is **still not appointed** (per
  the [[data-protection-africa]] factsheet, Jan 2026). → [[caf--gov-protect]].
- **[[AGO]] Angola — law *with* an authority, now under revision.** Law 22/11 (2011,
  [[angola-data-protection-law-22-11]]) is enforced by an **active [[apd-angola]]** (sanctions issued
  2024) — the opposite of CAR's dormant regime; Malabo ratified. The government's **revision cleared
  public consultation (announced 2026-06-17)** and heads to the Council of Ministers then National
  Assembly next legislative year — adding a **DPO role**, stronger sanctions, **fines re-denominated
  into Kwanza**, and AI provisions. Separately, a UNITA bill to **open electoral personal data**
  passed committee 10–0–12 (2026-04-13) but was **rejected in plenary (2026-06-25)** on
  constitutional-privacy grounds — a clean transparency-vs-privacy reversal. → [[angola--gov-protect]].
  Sources: [[2026-06-17-nova-lei-de-proteccao-de-dados-proximo-ano-legislativo]],
  [[2026-06-25-parlamento-rejeita-proposta-proteccao-de-dados-pessoais]].
- **[[BWA]] Botswana — six years of a law that never took effect.** The 2018 Data Protection Act sat
  un-commenced (its date pushed back by ministerial orders) until the **[[botswana-data-protection-act-2024]]**
  (effective 14 Jan 2025) finally switched the regime on and empowered the Information & Data Protection
  Commission. → [[botswana--gov-protect]].
- **[[SEN]] Senegal — data-protection law and the New Deal's rights gap (2008 → 2026).** Senegal's
  regime rests on **[[senegal-data-protection-law|Law No. 2008-12]]** (2008; in force 2014) and the
  **[[cdp-senegal|CDP]]**, with a revision reported underway (2025); Paradigm Initiative argues the
  **[[new-deal-technologique|New Deal Technologique]]** omits explicit digital-rights provisions. See
  [[gov.legislate]], [[gov.policy]], [[SEN]]. Source:
  [[2025-03-27 Senegal's Technological New Deal – A Digital Ambition Tested by Digital Rights]].

- **[[STP]] São Tomé and Príncipe — a 2016 data-protection law.** São Tomé's **Personal Data Guarantee
  and Protection Law** (Law 03/2016) is enforced by the **ANPDP** data-protection authority. See
  [[gov.legislate]], [[STP]].
- **[[SOM]] Somalia — a law and an authority, with gaps.** Somalia's **Data Protection Act**
  (Law 005/2023) is backed by a DPA inaugurated **Feb 2024**, but "public data" is left undefined and the
  regulator's independence is qualified (as of 2026). → [[somalia--gov-protect]] ([[SOM]]).
- **[[SLE]] Sierra Leone — a combined bill pre-enactment (2025).** Sierra Leone validated a combined
  **Data Protection & Right-to-Access-Information Bill 2025**, awaiting enactment. See [[gov.legislate]],
  [[SLE]].
- **[[SSD]] South Sudan — no regime yet (2025–26).** South Sudan has no comprehensive data-protection
  law; a **first Data Protection Bill** was promised for **2026** (Paradigm Initiative / Koneta Hub
  convening, Oct 2025). See [[gov.legislate]], [[SSD]].
- **[[SWZ]] Eswatini — law in force, office not stood up.** The
  [[data-protection-act-2022-eswatini]] (Act No. 5 of 2022) has been effective since **4 March
  2022**, with the sector regulator [[esccom]] designated the data protection authority and fines to
  **E5,000,000 or 2% of turnover** — but the World Bank reports the **Data Protection Office is not
  yet operationalised (2025-07-31)** and the national data policy was still being **procured
  (2025-07-25)**. [[apc]] flags s.36 and the certificates of urgency behind all four 2022 Acts.
  → [[eswatini--gov-protect]].
- **[[SYC]] Seychelles — a 2003 law replaced, and the transition already elapsed.** The
  [[data-protection-act-2023-seychelles]] repeals a 2003 Act "enacted but never implemented,"
  designating the pre-existing [[information-commission-seychelles]] as authority (s.5) — the same
  designate-an-existing-body model as [[ZAF]]. Enacted **19 December 2023**; the 18-month transition
  ran from **22 December 2023** and **elapsed around 22 June 2025**, so the Act now binds in full.
  → [[seychelles--gov-protect]].
- **[[TCD]] Chad — a data-protection law *being finalised*, not enacted (2025-12).** Minister
  **[[boukar-michel]]** announced (**9 Dec 2025**, third Chadian School of Internet Governance) that
  Chad was **finalising** a digital law incorporating international standards on cybersecurity, data
  protection and internet governance — **no text, enactment date or gazette reference is held**, so
  it is not law here. His framing is the sharpest ministerial statement of the
  [[sovereignty]] lens in this batch: *"In the digital age, a country that does not control its data
  does not control its destiny."* See [[gov.legislate]], [[TCD]]. Source:
  [[2025-12-10 Chad Finalizes Digital Law to Align National Framework With Global Standards]].
- **[[TGO]] Togo — the DPA gets into the broadcast regulator's monitoring room (2025-12).** Togo's
  authority **[[ipdcp-togo]]** — created alongside a **data protection law of September 2019** (per
  the World Bank's WURI ISR; **neither the 2019 text nor the IPDCP's establishing act is held**) —
  signed an accord with broadcast regulator **[[haac-togo]]** on **10 December 2025**
  ([[haac-ipdcp-accord]]) whose operative mechanism is that **the HAAC authorises the IPDCP to
  access its control and monitoring room**, enabling tracking of broadcast content and coordinated
  reaction to personal-data breaches; both presidents argued press freedom "cannot hold if the
  protection of digital rights remains fragile." The sole account is **sourced to the IPDCP and
  carries no critical framing** of a data-protection authority acquiring monitoring-room access —
  the absence is recorded, not filled in. See [[gov.legislate]], [[infra.cybersec]], [[TGO]].
  Sources:
  [[2025-12-12 Personal data and digital rights in Togo - HAAC and IPDCP seal institutional cooperation accord]],
  [[2023-12-21 ISR - WURI Phase 2 Seq 7 (P169594)]].
