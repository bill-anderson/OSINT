# Data protection (`gov.protect`)

Personal-data regimes across African jurisdictions — and the friction their
**fragmentation** creates for cross-border digital businesses.

## Key material — continental & thematic

- **Analysis — "surveilled into compliance" (Justin Brown, Silicon Canals, 2026-02-27).**
  [[colonialism]] Contributed op-ed: biometric ID, cashless rails and AI credit scoring across the
  Global South as surveillance infrastructure presented as inclusion — data flowing to Silicon
  Valley, populations unable to opt out. Cited by author; polemical about systems, on the wiki's
  standing lens. See [[dpi.id]], [[include.access]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-27-siliconcanals-surveilled-into-compliance]].

- **The surveillance side of the ledger — two 2025 civil-society baselines (ingested 2026-07-21).**
  [[sovereignty]] *Both are 2025 sources arriving late: baselines, not current state.*
  **CIPESA (2025-03-12, Edrine Wanyama)** reports a **Digital Rights Alliance Africa** learning session
  on surveillance threats to civil society. DRAA was created by **ICNL** and **[[cipesa]]** in response
  to rising digital authoritarianism and spans **more than 12 countries**. Three figures worth holding,
  each dated and attributed: **Richard Ngamita (Thraets)** is reported putting African government
  spending on surveillance technology at **~US$1bn annually**. **★ Corrected within this same run
  (acquire, 2026-07-21) — the underlying IDS release is now held and the figure does not mean what the
  secondary implies.** IDS's *Mapping the Supply of Surveillance Technologies to Africa*
  (**2023-09-27**, Tony Roberts and 11 others, with the African Digital Rights Network) finds that
  **five named countries — [[NGA]], [[GHA]], [[MAR]], [[MWI]] and [[ZMB]] — collectively spend at least
  US$1bn a year**, on contracts with US, UK, Chinese, EU and Israeli suppliers. It is **not a
  continental total**, and it is a **2023** figure, not a 2025 one. Carry it as *five-country, 2023*.
  The same release gives figures the wiki did not hold: **Nigeria alone at ≥US$2.7bn** of known
  contracts over a decade (**~US$12 per citizen**), the continent's largest buyer; **Ghana, Morocco and
  Zambia each over US$250m** on Chinese safe-city packages; **[[EGY]]** running the **US firm
  Honeywell's** safe-city package; and **Malawi the only one of the five to have rejected** the Chinese
  offer. Its supply-chain finding is the sharper one: **US and European firms are losing their historic
  dominance of phone and internet surveillance to [[huawei]] and [[zte]]**, while **the USA and UK
  dominate social-media surveillance and "political marketing" consultancy**, and **Germany, Italy and
  Israel are the major exporters of phone-hacking malware** — so "Chinese surveillance" names one layer
  of a market the West still leads elsewhere. *Distinguish this 2023 study from IDS's separate
  **March-2026** Smart City Surveillance report — same lead author, different work, different
  figures.* Source: [[2023-09-27-ids-mapping-supply-surveillance-technologies-africa-1bn]];
  the **[[malabo-convention|Malabo Convention]] stood at 16 ratifications of 55 states**; and **only 36
  states had enacted specific data-protection laws** *(both as at 2025-03 — the wiki separately holds
  Equatorial Guinea ratifying on 2026-03-25, so the ratification count has since moved)*. The framing
  it supplies is the mechanism: surveillance is **"often facilitated by biometric data collection
  systems"** — national ID, voter cards, travel documents, mandatory SIM registration, smart-city CCTV
  — which places it directly on top of the DPI stack this wiki tracks, rather than beside it.
  **[[unwanted-witness|Unwanted Witness]] (announced 2025-06-13; launched Harare 2025-06-10)**, with
  **MISA Zimbabwe**, the **Open Society Foundations** and the **Ford Foundation**, released
  *"Surveillance/Spyware: An Impediment to Civil Society, HRDs & Journalists in East & Southern
  Africa"* — case studies in **[[KEN]], [[RWA]], [[UGA]], [[ETH]], [[ZWE]], [[ZAF]] and [[MWI]]**,
  documenting **Pegasus, FinFisher, IMSI catchers, biometric systems and real-time communication
  intercepts**, and the misuse of **cybersecurity and anti-terrorism law** to justify them. Executive
  Director **Dorothy Mukasa**: *"this right is under siege as states and non-state actors deploy
  increasingly sophisticated tools to monitor, intimidate, and silence dissent."* **The report itself
  is not held** — this is the launch announcement only, and nothing here should be cited as its
  findings. **Acquisition attempted and failed 2026-07-21** (`unwantedwitness.org` download path returns
  not-found); **not held, and dropped rather than parked.** The **Spaces for Change** report on dual-use
  surveillance in Nigeria, which CIPESA cites for its finding on weak regulation and unaccountable state
  practice, was **also attempted and dropped the same day** — its page serves metadata only behind a
  74 MB gated download (created 2024-10-28), so **that report is not held either**. Sources:
  [[2025-03-12-cipesa-surveillance-footprint-africa-privacy-data-protection]],
  [[2025-06-13-unwanted-witness-surveillance-spyware-report-launch]].
- **Enforcement is the frontier, not legislation (2026 H1).** The gap is now implementation, not
  statute: Unwanted Witness’s **Privacy Scorecard 2025** ([[privacy-scorecard-report]]) puts
  continental compliance at just **46%** (health sector 22%), and a 13-CSO coalition led by
  [[media-rights-agenda]] argues Nigeria’s crisis is one of **horizontal accountability** — the NDPA,
  NDPC and a 121m+ digital-ID system coexist with NINs sold for ₦100 and an INEC breach via leaked
  credentials. DPAs in Kenya, Nigeria and Tanzania ramped up enforcement in early 2026 (Kenya 110+
  decisions in 2025). Sources:
  [[2026-05-18 Africa’s Digital Economy Is Expanding Faster than Its Privacy Protections]],
  [[2026-06-22 MRA, Civil-Society Coalition Call on Nigerian Government to Strengthen Horizontal Accountability for Citizens Data]],
  [[2026-06-23 Bimonthly Update on Privacy in Africa (January & February 2026)]]. See [[gov.legislate]], [[dpi.id]].

- **Continental state of play — 44 laws, 38 authorities (end-2025).** The authoritative annual
  roundup ([[2026-01-11 The Year of the Teeth Data Protection in Africa Roundup, 2025, Projections for 2026]],
  [[digital-policy-alert]] × [[tech-hive-advisory]]) counts **≥44 African countries with
  data-protection laws** (~80% of AU members) and **≥38 fully operational DPAs**, leaving **6 with a
  law but no regulator** and **11 yet to legislate**; it projects **>50 laws** by end-2026. New laws
  came into force in **Djibouti** (Digital Code), **The Gambia** and **Botswana**;
  Algeria/Ghana/Kenya/Mauritius/Nigeria/Senegal are amending existing statutes.
  [[code-for-africa]] ([[2026-05-05 Africa's data protection laws began to bite in 2025]]) corroborates
  the same 44/38 figures and frames 2025 as the year enforcement moved from "paper law" to real
  "teeth." This anchors a count analysts reported variously as **44** (Adebowale) vs **36** (Mbaya) —
  the primary trackers give **44** *(bears on REV-001(b))*. See [[gov.legislate]], [[dpi.id]].
- **Fragmentation as a valuation problem (2026-07, P.M. News / Okorie).** As **AfCFTA**
  expands, incompatible national regimes — **Nigeria's Data Protection Act 2023**,
  **Kenya's Data Protection Act 2019** — turn "governance" into a deal-killer: "a data
  governance framework built for Lagos may create legal exposure in Nairobi." Regulators
  "are no longer passive"; investors now treat data-processing architecture and privacy
  compliance as a **defensibility** question in due diligence. See [[gov.legislate]],
  [[dpi.exchange]], [[gov.regional]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-03 AfCFTA, Data Governance and the Hidden Legal Gaps Reshaping Africa's Digital Economy - P.M. News]].
- **Enforcement against big tech, and its ceiling (2026-06).** Following a **2025 court-approved
  settlement**, **[[meta]]** now funds the **[[ndpc]]**'s two-year M-SIDP programme
  ([[ndpc-meta-settlement]]) — an African regulator compelling platform compliance. But
  [[2026-06-08 The Promise and Limits of African Data Sovereignty]] shows the ceiling: under
  the **2018 US CLOUD Act**, US-parent firms ([[microsoft]]/Azure via **[[g42]]** in Kenya)
  can be compelled to hand over data regardless of location, and Nigeria's **$220m FCCPC Meta
  fine** reportedly went unpaid. Jurisdiction asserted; enforcement uneven. See [[geopol.usa]],
  [[infra.store]], [[nigeria--gov-protect]]. Sources:
  [[2026-06-09 Meta backs NDPC in landmark privacy initiative]],
  [[2026-06-08 The Promise and Limits of African Data Sovereignty]].
- **Governance > technology (2026-07).** A Warwick/Turing study finds cross-border
  digital-ID interoperability fails at the **governance and assurance** layers (data
  protection among them), not technical standards, recommending supranational governance
  via bodies like **[[afcfta]]**. See [[dpi.id]], [[gov.standards]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-03 Study finds governance, not technology, is blocking digital ID interoperability]].
- **The DPA-capacity formula (2026-05, ID4Africa AGM).** A [[world-bank]]-led session put
  **data-lifecycle trust** at the centre of DPI, cataloguing **six data-storage risks** (vendor
  immaturity, single points of failure, function creep, cryptographic gaps, insider threat, absent
  redress). African DPAs are "frequently under-resourced": **[[MUS]] Mauritius** mandates chief data
  officers and runs ~**3,000 enforcement actions/yr, 99% resolved amicably** (now adding
  administrative fines); **[[BEN]] Benin's APDP** has a dedicated **AI policy** with strict
  purpose-limitation; **[[LBR]] Liberia's** data-protection bill is before its legislature; and the
  **[[eac]]** created a body for its **eight** national DPAs to align cross-border. See
  [[id4africa]], [[gov.regional]], [[UGA]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-16 World Bank, African DPAs outline formula for trusted digital identity, DPI]].
- **Payment data as a monetary-sovereignty battleground (2026-04→07).** Localization now reaches
  money itself: SARB's Kganyago warned (Oct 2025) that **dollar stablecoins** could cost African
  states **monetary sovereignty**, with **$205bn** moving through SSA digital rails Jul 2024–Jun
  2025 (+52%), much via USD stablecoins outside central-bank oversight. Nigeria's [[cbn]] answered
  with a 15-member task force and the regulated **cNGN**; meanwhile **[[visa]]** is settling
  **[[safaricom]] M-Pesa** top-ups in stablecoins in **[[COD]] DR Congo** via Visa Pay with **[[onafriq]]**
  ([[visa-mpesa-onafriq-drc-stablecoin-pilot]] — core mechanism corroborated on record; no jointly-announced
  pilot and USD peg inferred). Governing transaction data becomes inseparable
  from governing the currency. See [[dpi.pay]], [[geopol.usa]]. Sources:
  [[2026-04-23 Africa's $205 Billion Digital Shift Is Redefining Money, and Central Banks Are Losing Control]],

- **Health data-sharing as the sharp new data-protection frontier (2026-02→03).** US bilateral
  health MOUs under the **[[america-first-global-health-strategy]]** (which replaced USAID/PEPFAR)
  make *health data* — sensitive under most African regimes — the hardest live test of these
  laws. Each reportedly bundles a **Data-Sharing Agreement** giving the US
  access to national surveillance and lab databases (10-yr post-termination) plus specimen/genomic
  sharing (25-yr), for five years of *declining* funding. **[[KEN]] Kenya** signed first
  (**[[us-kenya-health-framework]]**, ~$2.5bn, Rubio–Mudavadi 2025-12-04) but its **High Court
  suspended** the deal after the Omtatah / Consumer Federation petition (injunction 2025-12-19): a
  legal opinion found it breached the **Data Protection Act 2019** and **Digital Health Act 2023**
  (both classify health data as sensitive and require localisation absent reciprocal safeguards) and
  bypassed Parliament. **[[ZMB]] Zambia**'s draft MOU ([[zambia-us-health-mou]]) has the most
  extractive terms yet — **$1.012bn/5yr** for **10-yr health-data access** and a
  **25-yr pathogen-data pledge** (the longest of any signatory), financing reportedly tied to
  **copper/cobalt mining**; minister Muchima denied the mining link (15 Feb) and was fired
  three days later by President Hichilema, who frames the cuts as a chance to "take care of our
  own affairs" and has requested "revisions." The **[[odpc-kenya]]** and courts had
  already primed this by ordering **Worldcoin** to stop processing and delete ~**300,000**
  Kenyans' biometric data (2023 ODPC suspension, 2025 court ruling) — precedent that
  data-protection law can halt an external data-extractive project. **[[ZWE]] Zimbabwe rejected**
  its ~$367m deal as "clearly unbalanced" (Mnangagwa) and **[[ZMB]] Zambia** pushed back on the
  data clauses; **[[africa-cdc]]**'s Kaseya declined a US observer role, insisting "we want to own
  our data." Domestic data protection as a constraint on foreign policy, with a demonstration effect
  across the 22+ signatory states (Senegal's later MOU adds that data "remain governed by Senegalese
  law"). Full Kenyan case: **[[kenya--gov-protect]]**.
  See [[geopol.usa]], [[dpi.mis]], [[finance.mou]], [[SEN]], [[ZMB]]. Sources:

- **Washington's counter-offensive against data sovereignty (2026-02→03).** As African states build
  localisation regimes, the US works to roll them back. A State Department cable (**18 Feb 2026,
  signed Rubio**), classified as an *action request*, directs diplomats worldwide to monitor and
  **counter data-localization and data-sovereignty laws** — naming the EU's GDPR — and to promote
  the US-led **Global Cross-Border Privacy Rules Forum** as the "trusted" alternative
  ([[us-department-of-state]]). It sits inside a wider campaign (a Section 301 investigation against
  Brazil, anti-DSA lobbying, a WTO e-transmission duty-moratorium push opposed by India, South
  Africa and Indonesia). The bilateral **health MOUs** above are the same doctrine in practice: data
  sovereignty branded a barrier to American cloud/AI. See [[geopol.usa]], [[gov.policy]],
  [[gov.legislate]], [[XGL]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-12 How Trump's Data Regulation Crackdown Undermines Digital Sovereignty]].
- **The DPA/institutional build-out across smaller states (2026-01→02).** Even with the framework
  patchwork uneven, the *institutions* are being stood up: **[[COG]] Congo-Brazzaville** installed a
  **[[national-personal-data-protection-commission-congo]]** (~22 Jan 2026) — an independent body
  empowered to advise, warn and sanction, framed as "digital rule of law"; **[[SOM]] Somalia**'s
  Council of Ministers approved a **Regulatory Framework for the Public Data Protection Law** (early
  Feb 2026, [[somalia-ministry-communications-technology]]) to operationalise its existing statute;
  **[[MOZ]] Mozambique**'s **[[intic-mozambique]]** launched a pilot **data-breach reporting
  platform** (Feb 2026); and **[[MWI]] Malawi** used Data Privacy Week (Jan 2026) to declare
  "Privacy by Design" and its **[[malawi-data-protection-act-2024]]** "no longer optional" — and is
  now operationalising it (registration regulations, an independent DPA: **[[malawi--gov-protect]]**).
  Enforcement capacity, not statute count, is the live question (cf. the ID4Africa formula above).
  See [[dpi.govtech]], [[gov.legislate]], [[somalia--gov-protect]]. Sources:

- **Cross-border identity as a data-governance problem (2026-07, DIAL).** A [[dial]] fellowship
  frames portable identity around three governance problems — **sufficiency** (jurisdictions
  disagree on what proves identity), **adequacy** (registries collect different attributes) and
  **sovereignty** (whose law applies once identity data leaves its country). National ID cards are
  mutually recognised for *travel* among **[[KEN]]/[[RWA]]/[[UGA]]** yet often inadequate for
  cross-border *financial services* — so AfCFTA-scale integration ([[afcfta]]) hits data protection,
  not technology, as the binding constraint (reinforces "governance > technology" above). See
  [[dpi.id]], [[gov.regional]], [[gov.standards]], [[XAF]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-10 Every country defines identity differently. Can digital public infrastructure bridge those differences]].
- **Chinese surveillance as data protection by its absence (2026-04).** An IDS "Smart City
  Surveillance in Africa" survey (11 countries) found Chinese firms — **[[zte]]**, **[[hikvision]]**,
  **[[huawei]]** — supplying facial-recognition and ANPR "safe city" systems in *every* country
  surveyed, >**$2bn** spent, typically via ~$250m China Eximbank loans bundling cameras + a national
  data centre + training ("integrated governance packages" creating institutional lock-in). Nigeria
  is largest (China Exim advanced $399m of $470m; 10,000 Lagos cameras). The data-protection story is
  what is *missing*: systems "repurposed to monitor political opposition… in the absence of adequate
  legal oversight" (Zimbabwe facial-recognition fears; Mozambique cameras in opposition strongholds),
  and — Georgetown's Jili warns — formal legal frameworks can *legitimise* rather than constrain
  surveillance — the missing layer beneath biometric mass surveillance. See
  [[infra.cybersec]], [[geopol.china]], [[tech.ai]], [[NGA]], [[ZWE]], [[ZMB]], [[SEN]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-04 Chinese surveillance rolled out in Africa ZTE, Hikvision, Huawei at the helm]].
- **Facial recognition — build the rules the tech lacks (Nagadya, 2026-06).** Building on
  Buolamwini's bias research (34.7% error on dark-skinned women), Nagadya catalogues large African
  biometric-surveillance spends with no legal safeguards: **Uganda $126m**,
  **Nigeria >$470m**, **Kenya $219m**, across **11 African countries** — an **IDS (Institute of
  Development Studies) March-2026 report** finding *no adequate legal framework in any*.
  **Corrected 2026-07-21 (reconcile).** This page previously carried "**1,800 Kampala cameras**"
  alongside Uganda's $126m. **The 1,800 figure is Kenya's, not Uganda's**, and its appearance here was
  a **transposition**: the analysis it came from lifted Uganda's $126m, Nigeria's >$470m and Kenya's
  $219m verbatim from the IDS press release — **which carries no camera counts at all** — and attached
  Kenya's camera count to Uganda's row. Uganda's own figures, from contemporaneous Ugandan reporting of
  the police account: **5,552 cameras nationwide, of which 3,233 planned for Kampala Metropolitan**
  (2018-08-02), under the **Uganda Police National CCTV Network Expansion Project** supplied by
  **[[huawei]]**. On money: the police sought **UGX 458bn (then given as ~$124m)** in three
  instalments; **US$126m is the figure that settled in later reporting (2019-08)** and is a
  **conversion of the UGX contract value**, which is the announcing party's own currency. See
  [[UGA]], [[geopol.china]]. Sources:

  Counter-references: the **[[eu-ai-act]]** banned real-time public facial recognition for law
  enforcement (Feb 2025); Rwanda's AI policy carries a human-oversight requirement; Nigeria's CBN
  requires fintech AI explainability. She proposes pre-deployment accuracy testing on African faces,
  appeal mechanisms and tightened national-security exemptions. Surveillance is used against
  opposition in Uganda/Zimbabwe/Mozambique (complements the Chinese-surveillance bullet). See
  [[tech.ai]], [[infra.cybersec]], [[UGA]], [[NGA]], [[KEN]], [[ZWE]], [[MOZ]]. Source:

- **Humanitarian data — the extreme-vulnerability case (2026-07).** The AI-governance gap in
  humanitarian/nonprofit work: the **WFP Gaza breach** exposed ~600,000 households' data (IDs,
  phones, locations); 93% of aid workers use AI but only 22% have formal policies (75% of those
  surveyed in SSA/MENA/Asia-Pacific); UNHCR holds ~19m records incl. 15.8m facial images, WFP 31m
  people across 59 countries. The **"unconsenting beneficiary"** — refugees and famine survivors
  who cannot meaningfully refuse data collection — is the sharpest edge of data protection. The
  Zambian refugee-register transfer (**[[zambia--gov-protect]]**) is the continental instance. See
  [[dpi.id]], [[tech.ai]], [[XSS]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-09 Uncovering the Humanitarian and Nonprofit Sector's AI Governance Crisis]].
- **Two regulators funded from the population or the offence.** Gabon's ordonnance of 2020 levies
  **1,000 FCFA per person per year**, collected by controllers, split **40% state / 60% authority**;
  Angola's **DP 263/25 art. 25** splits receipts **40% Treasury / 30% the Centro Nacional de
  Cibersegurança / 30% a *Fundo de Cibersegurança*** — the Centro retaining a share of the fines it
  levies. Both are self-funding models with an obvious incentive question, surfaced independently.
  See [[gabon--gov-protect]], [[angola--gov-protect]].
