# Connectivity (`infra.connect`)

Broadband and network access as the base layer of the digital economy.

## Key material — continental & thematic

- **[[NAM]] Namibia — a private challenger, and the density problem (2025-2026).** **[[paratus]]** launched
  **Namibia's first privately-owned mobile network** (Sep 2025; LTE + 5G) against the state duopoly
  **[[mtc-namibia|MTC]]** (>90%) and Telecom Namibia — but the binding constraint is geography: at **3.76
  people/km²** (among the world's lowest), fibre backhaul and towers are expensive, so **5G is phased and
  city-first** and rural areas stay on 2G/3G. Targets: internet **53%→90% by 2030** (NDP6); 25 new PowerCom
  towers; a demand-side push (free school/clinic internet; 4G-device tax breaks). Full picture:
  **[[namibia--infra-connect]]**. Sources:
  [[2025-09-04-paratus-launches-namibia-first-private-mobile-network-connectingafrica]],
  [[2026-02-10-towards-5g-enabled-digitalized-namibia-2026-telecomreviewafrica]].
- **[[MWI]] Malawi — wholesale gains, distribution gap (2025–2026).** The World Bank **Digital Foundations
  Project** collapsed wholesale bandwidth from **~$460/Mbit/s to below $10** and brought **~7m** new users
  online (users/100 rose 17.6→38.3), but the last mile lags: Malawi needs **1,000+ towers** (DMAP procuring
  ~90–100; USF under-resourced), **85% of 9,000 schools** have limited/no internet, and a consumer **petition**
  is pressing MACRA on retail prices and data-billing transparency — with **[[macra-malawi|MACRA]]** then
  ordering **both operators to compensate customers** for a **seven-day tariff-notice** breach (s.76,
  Communications Act 2016; 2026-07-17). **[[tnm-malawi|TNM]]** (5G first, Jul 2025)
  and **[[airtel-malawi|Airtel]]** (5G Nov 2025; 111 sites/10 districts) are rolling out 5G and eyeing
  satellite. Full picture: **[[malawi--infra-connect]]**. Sources:

  [[2026-04-17-school-connectivity-unicef-giga-report-malawi24]],

  [[2026-07-17-malawi-macra-telecom-tariff-compliance]].
- **Mauritius as an Africa–Asia cable gateway (2025-2026).** **[[mauritius-telecom|MT]]**'s "Bridging Africa &
  Asia" strategy positions the island as a low-latency corridor between the two continents: existing **SAFE,
  LION/LION2 and METISS** landings, the new **T4** cable set to replace SAFE (retiring ~2027), planned **SEACOM
  2.0** landings, **5G-Advanced** rollout, and the **MIXP** exchange — ~**1.01M** internet subscriptions (Jan
  2025), **3rd in Africa** on the ITU ICT measure. See **[[mauritius--infra-store]]**, [[MUS]], [[infra.store]].
  Source: [[2026-01-21-mauritius-telecom-strategy-2026-2029-bridging-africa-asia-lexpress]].

- **Localise the traffic — resilience and the sovereignty cost (2026 H1).** Internet Society evidence ties
  **IXP/peering** to affordability — in 40 of 50 grant countries the income share for basic broadband fell
  >10% (Burkina Faso 13%→**0.2%**) — while a new Pulse content-locality measure shows most African traffic
  still leaves the continent (Malawi **3% local**; South Africa a 66% regional hub). The ATU/AFRINIC/ISOC
  **[[internet-resilience-framework-africa]]** gives regulators a six-step resilience template (53 IXPs in
  36 countries). GSMA maps Congo-B's connectivity/tax-reform gap and the governance conditions for using
  MNO data in crises (the Ebola cross-border divergence). ODI documents China's fibre/data-centre finance
  (Ethiopia; Kenya's [[konza-technopolis]]) and its sovereignty/debt tail. Sources:

- **Global pledges vs the gap (2026-07, ITU).** **[[partner2connect]]** ([[itu]]) crossed
  **$100bn** in commitments (Africa a leading implementation region — incl. [[microsoft]]'s
  450 Kenya rural hubs via Azure Space), but ITU puts 2030 universal meaningful connectivity at
  **$2.6–2.8tn** — a fraction pledged. See [[include.divides]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-09 ITU's Partner2Connect crosses $100 billion in global connectivity pledges]].
- **The infrastructure layer under DNS (2026-07).** The **[[smart-africa]]**–**[[afnic]]**
  MoU targets African **ccTLD** capacity and DNS resilience — the naming/routing layer beneath
  connectivity. UNDP's DPI paper counts connectivity/devices/data centres as DPI's **enabling
  infrastructure** layer ([[2026-01-01 Digital Public Infrastructure in Africa - UNDP]]). See
  [[infra.cybersec]], [[gov.standards]]. Source:

- **Continental backbone (2026-07, [[seacom]]).** **Seacom 2.0** — a 25,000 km, 48-fibre-pair
  system, **20 landing points across 15 countries** (Marseille to Singapore and South Africa,
  looping to West Africa) — is pitched for **AI workloads**, its landing stations reframed as
  "AI communication nodes" linking "African nations' sovereign AI infrastructure to global data
  hubs": a private, Africa-anchored bid for digital-destiny control, complementing the
  data-centre build-out ([[infra.store]], [[tech.ai]]). Source:
  [[2026-07-09 Seacom plans 25,000km subsea cable from France to Singapore and South Africa]].
  An **ATF 2025 interview** (Prenesh Padayachee) adds economics: **phase one by early 2030**,
  original 2009 cable **retired 2035**, build cost **$1.5bn–$2bn** via a
  shareholder-plus-new-investor raise, stressing **data-centre neutrality**. Source:
  [[2025-11-01 Seacom's Prenesh Padayachee on the 'Seacom 2.0' subsea cable]].

### Submarine cables as contested infrastructure

The subsea layer is where connectivity, geopolitics and the `colonialism` lens converge — the
political-economy spine to the entries above.
- **The content-provider ownership shift (2024–2026).** Content providers have moved from cable
  *customers* to *owners*: **[[google]]**, **[[meta]]**, **[[microsoft]]** and
  **[[amazon-web-services]]** now own or lease **~70%** of undersea bandwidth (Atlantic Council,
  2022) and drive **~75%** of international-bandwidth demand — **[[telegeography]]** puts global
  demand at **>6.4 Pbps** at end-2024 (tripled since 2020), **Africa fastest-growing at >40%
  CAGR**. The peer-reviewed **[[cable-wars-abels-babic-2026]]** dataset (622 systems) finds
  **Google the single most prominent owner** — 16% of core (>5,000 km) cables, in 13 of the 50
  highest-capacity systems, sole owner of ten — though telecom carriers (Orange, Tata, Vodafone)
  still hold much of the network. Cable *supply* is a tighter oligopoly: the "big four" (SubCom,
  ASN, NEC, HMN Tech) lay **~85%**, and US sanctions (HMN Tech onto the Commerce entity list, Dec
  2021) pushed the Chinese supplier from large contracts toward Africa/Asia (the PEACE cable). See
  [[geopol.usa]], [[geopol.china]], [[infra.capacity]]. Sources:
  [[2026-01-20 Cable wars Mapping the political economy of submarine cables in an era of geoeconomic competition]],
  [[2024-06-27 A (Refreshed) List of Content Providers' Submarine Cable Holdings]],
  [[2025-05-12 International Bandwidth Demand Surpasses 6.4 Pbps]].
- **Equiano, MainOne and the "making of middlemen" (2025, [[equiano-mainone-nwankwo-2025]]).**
  Google's **[[equiano-cable]]** (144 Tbps, sole-owned, Portugal→South Africa via Togo, Lagos,
  Namibia) alone exceeds every older African cable's combined capacity; Google lands it through
  partners (WIOCC/OADC) to stay in international waters, sidestepping country-by-country regulation
  while capturing users and their data for its cloud. **[[equinix]]**'s **$320m** acquisition of
  Nigeria's MainOne (Funke Opeke's 7,000 km open-access cable, 2008 — the largest Nigerian tech
  deal) is the counter-move: terminating cables inside co-located data centres, *remaking* the
  terrestrial "middlemen" rather than removing them. The `colonialism` reading — better-capitalised
  US platforms disintermediate local infrastructure owners. See [[infra.store]], [[NGA]], [[TGO]],
  [[CMR]]. Source:
  [[2025-10-20 Redefining competition and submarine cable ownership Equiano, MainOne, and the making of middlemen]].
- **Google's four connectivity hubs + [[umoja-cable]] (2025-09).** Google will build **four
  subsea-cable connectivity hubs** (north/south/east/west Africa — landing stations + data centres,
  ~3-year build) tying [[equiano-cable]] and the new **Umoja** system into its Africa Connect
  programme. Umoja — the **first cable connecting Africa directly to Australia** (RFS 2027) — runs
  a terrestrial spine ([[KEN]]→[[UGA]]→[[RWA]]→[[COD]]→[[ZMB]]→[[ZWE]]→[[ZAF]]) built with
  [[liquid-c2]]/[[cassava-technologies]] before crossing the Indian Ocean, turning inland capitals
  (Kampala, Kigali, Lubumbashi, Lusaka, Harare) into backbone nodes. With Meta's **[[2africa-cable]]**
  (~45,000 km, encircling the continent, 33 landings), the two US platforms now anchor Africa's
  highest-capacity routes. See [[tech.ai]], [[geopol.usa]], [[infra.store]]. Sources:
  [[2025-09-22 Google plans to build four subsea cable connectivity hubs for Africa]],
  [[2024-05-28 Google invests in terrestrial-subsea cable linking Kenya to Australia]].
- **Cable-cut vulnerability and the resilience agenda ([[carnegie-endowment]], [[jane-munga]]).**
  **77** cable systems land in Africa (37 of 38 coastal states; Eritrea the exception),
  concentrated on five hubs ([[EGY]], [[ZAF]], [[DJI]], [[NGA]], [[KEN]]). The **March 2024**
  four-cable break (WACS/ACE/MainOne/SAT-3) knocked out **10 countries** and cost Nigeria **>$590m
  in four days**; a Feb-2024 Red Sea anchor-drag and recurring West-coast cuts expose the thinness
  of routes — only **three** repair ships serve the continent (one African-ported, the *Léon
  Thévenin*), and single-cable states ([[LBR]], Sierra Leone, The Gambia, Guinea, [[MRT]]) go dark
  when their one link fails. Governance is fragmented across 38 coastal jurisdictions; the fixes
  are redundancy (route/landing-station diversity, cross-border circuits) plus harmonised
  governance via the **[[international-cable-protection-committee]]** (founded 1958, >235 members)
  and the ITU–ICPC **[[international-advisory-body-on-submarine-cable-resilience]]** (Nov 2024),
  co-chaired by Nigeria's [[bosun-tijani]] — whose **final report was approved 10 July 2026** at WSIS
  Forum 2026: a three-pillar roadmap (faster deploy/repair, risk monitoring, route diversity), after
  Abuja-2025 and Porto-2026 summits, with seven priorities including **streamlined permitting**,
  **addressing the needs of vulnerable regions** (LDCs/SIDS explicitly) and **climate integration**.
  **[[watra]]** picked it up regionally within days (2026-07-15), pressing its 16 regulators toward
  **regulatory harmonisation** and reading the March-2024 West African cable break as the case for it.
  See [[infra.cybersec]], [[gov.regional]], [[gov.standards]], [[XWA]]. Sources:
  [[2025-04-03 Beneath the Waves Addressing Vulnerabilities in Africa's Undersea Digital Infrastructure]],
  [[2025-09-01 About the ICPC]],
  [[2026-07-13 ‘How Nigeria, others can secure undersea Internet cables’]],

- **The colonial-continuity critique (2024–2026).** Two named-analyst essays read the cable map as
  imperial: cables land at former colonial trading ports (Djibouti, Mombasa, Lagos, Dakar, Cape
  Town) and retrace 19th-century telegraph — even trans-Atlantic slave-trade — routes, while
  private content-provider ownership replaces the old distributed telco consortia, concentrating
  control over who gets bandwidth and on what terms. [[jess-auerbach]] (aboard the *Léon Thévenin*,
  2023) sharpens the sovereignty stakes: poorer single-cable states must accept the terms of
  Alphabet, Meta and Huawei. Continuous with Africa's own cable history and open-access advocacy
  ([[jagun-sat3-open-access-2008]], [[internet-society-africa-history]], [[nsrc]]). See
  [[colonialism]], [[include.divides]]. Sources:
  [[2026-03-02 I traced who owns the undersea cables that carry 95% of global internet traffic — the map is a colonial one]],
  [[2024-03-15 Undersea cables for Africa's internet retrace history and leave digital gaps as they connect continents]].
- **Liquid–Google hybrid cloud networking (2024-06).** [[liquid-c2]] ([[cassava-technologies]])
  became the **first Google Cloud Interconnect provider in Africa** ([[liquid-c2-google-cloud-mou]])
  — a private, low-latency link into Google Cloud that lets African enterprises bypass the public
  internet — extending the Liquid–Google partnership that built Umoja's overland route. See
  [[infra.store]], [[geopol.usa]]. Source:
  [[2024-06-06 Liquid C2 becomes the first provider in Africa to support hybrid network connections to Google Cloud]].
- **Terrestrial & regional backbone (2026-07 batch).** [[seacom]]'s **Nairobi–Kampala** route
  (1 Tbps → 30 Tbps, sub-50ms auto-reroute) upgrades the East-African inland corridor; the
  **[[powertel-paratus-fibre-corridor]]** lights up a Botswana–Zimbabwe–Zambia spine (>10
  Tbps-capable); **[[telecom-namibia]]** integrates the SARSSy/Equiano subsea route and Angosat-2
  satellite via Angola. See [[XEA]], [[XSA]], [[infra.capacity]]. Sources:
  [[2026-06-17 Seacom deploys a new high-capacity Nairobi–Kampala route to strengthen East Africa's digital backbone]],

- **Satellite (LEO) as the disruptive layer (2026-06).** Ookla data show **[[starlink]]** beating
  most terrestrial ISPs on download across SSA (>500k subs); "**Who Owns Africa's Sky**" argues
  LEO operators cherry-pick high-value users while paying a fraction of local costs — threatening
  the cross-subsidy that funds rural build-out — amid divergent national responses (SA/Namibia
  blocked entry; Zimbabwe booming). Amazon **Kuiper** entering. See [[starlink]], [[gov.policy]],
  [[include.divides]]. Sources: [[2026-06-15 Starlink drives African broadband speeds higher]],
  [[2026-06-16 Who Owns Africa's Sky]].
- **The usage gap & operator financing (2026).** GSMA: coverage gap down to **9%** but the
  **usage gap widened to 63%** — the frontier is adoption/affordability ($40 handsets), not
  coverage. **[[ifc]]** lent **$150m** to [[airtel-africa]] for network expansion.
  [[mtn-group]]/[[vodacom]] pivot from connectivity to platforms. See [[include.access]],
  [[gsma]]. Sources:
  [[2026-06-18 Africa's $240 Billion Mobile Economy A Checkpoint on Africa's Digital Transformation Journey]],
  [[2026-07-01 IFC and Airtel Africa Expand Digital Connectivity to Strengthen Jobs and Inclusion]].
- **New Atlantic corridor (2026-05, [[via-africa-cable]]).** [[orange]] leads a consortium
  building **Via Africa**, a **>20,000 km** Atlantic subsea system linking Nigeria and ~19 other
  African/European countries (landings including Senegal, Guinea, Côte d'Ivoire, Mauritania),
  a **3–4-year** build adding route diversity and drawing hyperscalers to African landing data
  centres. Context: Africa has **77** active/planned cable systems, yet **>half** its
  international bandwidth flows through just five countries ([[NGA]], [[ZAF]], [[EGY]], [[DZA]],
  [[KEN]]). First announced 12 May 2026 at the Africa Forward Summit (Nairobi; Macron present),
  where Orange also pledged to train **>3m** youth by 2030. See [[XWA]], [[geopol.eu]],
  [[infra.store]]. Source: [[2026-05-23 Orange-backed subsea cable to connect Nigeria, 19 other countries]].
- **Seacom 2.0 — the primary launch (2025-09).** The formal announcement (Submarine Networks
  World, Singapore) confirms the **48-fibre-pair**, AI-tuned Indian-Ocean-Basin system, its
  landing stations reframed as **"AI communication nodes"** linking African sovereign AI
  infrastructure to global hubs — corroborating the continental-backbone entry above ([[seacom]],
  [[infra.capacity]]). Source: [[2025-09-24 Unveiling Seacom 2.0 - ​ ​A new digital renaissance and subsea network to power the Indian Ocean Basin's digital and AI future]].
- **LEO revenue leakage (2026-05).** An Africa CEO Forum/Askya report warns offshore LEO
  operators capture high-value customers under far lighter obligations: [[starlink]] is
  authorised in **≥25** countries (~66k Nigeria users, >67k Zimbabwe), paying **~$150k** for a
  Senegal licence versus **>$50m** for 5G — eroding the cross-subsidy that funds rural build-out.
  It urges a **hybrid** model positioning satellite as a wholesale/coverage layer, not a
  premium-retail competitor. See [[data.satellite]], [[gov.policy]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-26 Africa risks losing billions to satellite internet operators, report says]].
- **SpaceX's quiet Africa bet (2026-05).** SpaceX's **S-1** (277 pages, **$1.75tn** valuation)

- **Penetration statistics: the measure decides the number.** Three families circulate and are routinely
  confused. **Usage** (individuals using the internet — ITU, Kepios) is the lowest. **Subscriptions ÷
  population** (regulator series) runs far higher because one person may hold several, and can *fall when
  population grows faster than subscriptions* — Zimbabwe's POTRAZ series did exactly that in Q1 2025,
  −1.37pp, which a usage measure cannot do. **Coverage** (population within range of a signal) is higher
  again and moves when transit fibre is laid, not when anyone connects. Worked cases: **Zimbabwe** 84.55%
  subscriptions (Q4 2025) against 38.4% users (Oct 2025); **CAR** ministry ~30% coverage (Jul 2025) against
  13.8% ITU usage (2024); **Zambia** ZICTA 56.8 per 100 inhabitants (end-2022) against 33.0% users (Oct
  2025). Never compare across families. See [[zimbabwe--infra-connect]], [[caf--infra-connect]],
  [[zambia--infra-connect]].

  attached to two funded phases (750 by end-2026, a further 750 announced for 2027, as of 2026-04-06), while

  confirmed 2026-02-18) — the ministry's own Transition Digitale 2030 roadmap year, of which this is the
  first of twelve chantiers. Financed at **75bn FCFA**, split evenly between the state and ONATEL/Orange
  Burkina Faso/Telecel; **370 localities connected by early 2026**. Sources:

  **12.63% (2024)**. The same publisher's earlier **22.5% (Jan 2024)** was rebased away by DataReportal

- **[[CAF]] Central African Republic — coverage quoted as usage.** Internet **use** is **13.8% (2024, ITU)**

  **network-coverage** figure — the minister described it rising to 50% then 70–80% as backbone fibre
  reaches new regions, against a target of 70% *couverture nationale*. Kepios's 15.5% (Jan 2025) has been
  **withdrawn by the publisher**. See [[caf--infra-connect]].
