# Digital Payments and Fintech (`dpi.pay`)

Interoperable digital payments and financial inclusion.

## Key material — continental & thematic

- **Instant payments: from building rails to scaling them (2026).** SIIPS 2025 records **36 live IPS**
  ($1.98tn / 64.6bn transactions in 2024; Nigeria's NIP the first rated "mature"), with ownership pivoting
  to central banks (17 of 36) partly to avoid "loss of digital sovereignty." AfricaNenda's *Scaling Instant
  Payments* and non-bank-participation notes argue usage, trust and fair competition — not the rail — are
  now the constraint; **Liberia** stood up a [[mojaloop]] IPS in **73 days**. But affordability is under
  threat: GSMA finds mobile-money levies above **~0.2%** of value harm inclusion (Cameroon −40% taxed
  value), and Mo Ibrahim/Affinity show >90% of mobile-money value is still cashed out. Sources:
  [[2025-11-01 SIIPS 2025 State of Inclusive Instant Payment Systems in Africa]],
  [[2026-03-01 Scaling Instant Payments in Africa Policy Choices for Central Banks]],
  [[2026-03-01 Mobile Money Taxes and Affordability in Sub-Saharan Africa]],
  [[2026-06-01 The State of Cash Dependency and Digital Financial Inclusion in Africa]]. See [[dpi.exchange]], [[include.access]].

- **Continental rail reaches Central Africa — but Nigeria pulls back (2026-07).** CEMAC
  central bank **[[beac]]** joined **[[papss]]** (2026-07-11), extending the rail into
  **[[XCA]] Central Africa**; Nigeria's [[nibss]] launched an ISO-20022 **National Payment
  Stack** and NIP became the first African instant-payment system rated "Mature" on
  [[africanenda]]'s spectrum. Nigeria footnote: **a reported PAPSS line fell 53%** (to ₦5.6bn,
  ~$3.7m) in H1'25 per CBN's Financial Stability Report — a real but **narrow-scope** metric, not
  Nigeria's total throughput and **not a counter-signal** to the rail's scaling (REV-002 resolved
  2026-07-16). Paper **cheques still
  move ~$17bn/yr in Kenya** even as Burkina Faso, Zambia, Seychelles and Ghana phase them out.
  See [[gov.regional]], [[XCA]], [[NGA]], [[KEN]]. Sources:
  [[2026-07-11 BEAC joins PAPSS and accelerates cross-border payments integration in Central Africa]],
  [[2026-02-02 How Strategic  Shift of NIBSS Is  Rewiring Nigeria's Payments System]],
  [[2026-01-27 Nigeria's PAPSS cross-border payments falls 53% to N5.6bn]],
  [[2026-01-13 The rise of cheques as Kenya's preferred payment method]].
- **Continental interoperable payments (2026-07).** The
  [[equity-africanenda-gates-dpi-partnership]] centres on interoperable payment systems
  (government/merchant/cross-border) to scale across **20–30 markets**, with [[africanenda]]
  (30+ central banks) and [[equity-group-holdings]] as private execution arm — DPI as the rail
  beneath AfCFTA cross-border payments. **~400m** Africans remain unbanked. See
  [[include.access]], [[dpi.exchange]], [[XAF]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-07 Equity, AfricaNenda and Gates Foundation Partner to Advance Digital Public Infrastructure Across Africa]].
- **Cross-border payment rails (2026-07, AfCFTA).** The Digital Trade Forum put **[[papss]]**
  at the centre of the digital single market — cross-border payments so money "moves as freely
  as goods." See [[gov.regional]], [[afcfta]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-07 African nations recommit to operationalize AfCFTA digital single market framework]].
- **Payments-adjacent, this ingest (2026-07).** Ethiopia's **Fayda** rollout is pitched as a
  **financial-inclusion** lever ([[ETH]], [[dpi.id]]); Ghana plans a **national fintech fund**
  ([[GHA]], [[finance.new]]); Nigeria's payment *data* is the asset the [[cbn]] localization
  directive forces onshore — payments as the "wedge" for wider cloud migration ([[NGA]],
  [[gov.protect]]). Banks, fintechs and payment firms must **onshore transactional data by 1
  Jan 2027**; NIBSS logged **~$702bn** (₦1.07 quadrillion) in e-transactions in 2024, up 80%.
  Sources:

- **Afreximbank's continental payments suite (2026-05).** [[afreximbank]] unveiled a payments
  stack in Egypt — **[[papss]]**, the **PAPSSCARD** continental card and an **African Currency
  Marketplace** — settling trade in **local currencies to cut USD dependence**; PAPSS has
  integrated with Kenya's **Pesalink**. AfCFTA's financial rail, so value "circulates within
  Africa rather than being routed through external centres." See [[dpi.exchange]],
  [[gov.regional]], [[afcfta]], [[KEN]], [[EGY]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-30 Afreximbank Banks on Digital Payments to Unlock Africa's Trillion-Dollar Trade Potential]].
- **Mobile money is becoming tax infrastructure (2026-05).** Under fiscal pressure, Kenya,
  Nigeria and Senegal are folding mobile-money rails into **tax collection** — the traceability
  that drove inclusion now serves revenue. With the informal sector ~**90% of SSA
  employment**, higher transaction fees risk pushing low-value users **back to cash**. See
  [[gov.policy]], [[include.access]], [[KEN]], [[NGA]], [[SEN]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-29 Africa's Mobile Money Networks Are Becoming Tax Infrastructure]].
- **Scale — and its AI blind spot (2024 figures, 2026-04 audit).** Global mobile money hit
  **2.1bn accounts / 108bn transactions / $1.68tn** in 2024 (SSA >1bn accounts). But AI is
  woven into **fraud detection, credit scoring and customer service** with no independent bias
  audit: fraud models trained on urban male transactors flag first-time rural women as
  anomalous, and the mobile-money gender gap **widened to 36%** in 2024 — an inclusion story
  with a [[colonialism]] underside. Entities [[safaricom]], [[mtn-group]], [[airtel-africa]],
  [[gsma]], [[huawei]]. See [[include.divides]], [[tech.ai]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-16 3 Reasons Mobile Money Programs Have a $1.7 Trillion Problem]].
- **GSMA State of the Industry 2026 — the anchor figures (2025 data, pub 2026-03).** The
  [[2026-03-01 GSMA State of the Industry Report on Mobile Money 2026]] anchors this batch's
  numbers: **2.3bn registered accounts / 593m active / >$2.091tn** transacted globally in 2025
  (value doubled in four years). **Africa is the epicentre** — ~**$1.432tn** (+27%, ~66% of
  global value), ~74% of ~125bn transactions and ~1.2bn accounts (52% of global, 347m active).
  Merchant payments +42% to **$155bn** and bank-to-wallet transfers **$167bn** put the sector
  past P2P. Adoption lags value: SSA smartphone ownership **~33%**, with
  digital-financial-literacy gaps (Ethiopia: 60% of women / 54% of men "don't
  know how to use it"). Sector context: Africa's **$240bn** mobile economy, operators
  ([[mtn-group]], [[vodacom]]) pivoting from connectivity to payments/platforms, and the
  [[dzap]]-backed ZamPay inclusion strand. See [[include.access]], [[include.divides]]. Sources:
  [[2026-03-01 GSMA State of the Industry Report on Mobile Money 2026]],

- **Stablecoins vs monetary sovereignty (2026-04).** **$205bn** moved through SSA digital
  rails Jul-2024–Jun-2025 (**+52%**), much via **dollar stablecoins** outside central-bank
  oversight; SARB's Kganyago warned countries "might lose monetary sovereignty," and Standard
  Chartered modelled up to **$1tn** in deposits leaving EM banks. [[cbn]] responded with a
  15-member task force and the regulated naira stablecoin **cNGN**. See [[gov.protect]],
  [[gov.legislate]], [[NGA]], [[ZAF]], [[KEN]]. Source:
  [[2026-04-23 Africa's $205 Billion Digital Shift Is Redefining Money, and Central Banks Are Losing Control]].
- **PAPSS Nigeria→Ghana naira rail (2026-02).** [[onafriq]] and **[[papss]]** launched a pilot
  for wallet-based outbound payments from **[[NGA]] Nigeria to [[GHA]] Ghana fully in naira** —
  no hard-currency conversion — CBN-approved, six months from 1 Dec, aimed at SMEs. PAPSS now
  spans **160+ banks / 400m+ accounts across 19 countries**; Onafriq cites 1bn mobile and 500m
  bank wallets. Operationalises [[afcfta]], making "Africa's borders invisible to payments."
  See [[dpi.exchange]], [[gov.regional]], [[XWA]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-04 PAPSS, Onafriq launch new trade rail for West Africa]].
- **PAPSS baseline (2022 backfill).** PAPSS launched Jan 2022 (HQ **Cairo**), developed by
  [[afreximbank]] with the AfCFTA Secretariat on a StoneX platform, to let ~42 African
  currencies settle intra-African trade locally and cut correspondent-banking reliance —
  aspiring to save businesses **~$5bn/yr**. Afreximbank approved **$500m** for the West African
  Monetary Zone pilot and earmarked a further **$3bn** for continental rollout; 25 of the
  largest commercial banks connected in phase one, fintechs/mobile money later. As of 2022. See
  [[gov.regional]], [[finance.new]], [[EGY]]. Sources:
  [[2022-05-11 Pan-African Payment and Settlement System]],
  [[2022-08-30 Pan-African Payment and Settlement System drives Africa's banking integration]].
- **National switches join the continental rail (2026-02/03).** Kenya's **[[pesalink]]** (80+
  banks/fintechs/SACCOs) joined **[[papss]]** as a **Technical Connectivity Provider** —
  PAPSS's first pilot of a national switch for transaction termination — giving Kenyan
  institutions instant local-currency cross-border settlement without new correspondent
  relationships. **[[bank-of-ghana]]** pressed the same case at the African Prosperity
  Dialogue: intra-African payments still cost 7–10% (vs ~3% global) and >80% route through
  correspondent banks abroad (~$5.3bn/yr), so it promotes PAPSS, a Ghana–Rwanda
  fintech-passporting pilot and a Virtual Asset Service Providers law to settle [[afcfta]]
  trade on African rails. See [[dpi.exchange]], [[gov.regional]], [[KEN]], [[GHA]], [[RWA]].
  Sources: [[2026-03-02 Pesalink and PAPSS link networks for African payments]],
  [[2026-02-05 Bank of Ghana Calls for Payment System Overhaul to Unlock AfCFTA Trade]].
- **The "payments end-game" thesis (analysis, 2025–2026).** Frontier Fintech essays by **Samora
  Kariuki** reframe African payments: as low-cost central-bank **instant rails become
  utilities** (NIP, TIPS, GhIPSS, PayShap, Kenya's in-development Faster Payments — AfricaNenda's
  SIIPS 2025 puts Africa's cross-domain IPS at **~$1.98tn** in 2024, a 35% volume CAGR),
  aggregator processing margins collapse — as for Brazil's StoneCo/PagSeguro and India's
  PhonePe/Paytm — so durable value migrates to **credit, data and compliance** layers, not
  processing. Kariuki scores six markets on a 1–5 **DPI-maturity rubric** (Ghana 40, South
  Africa 39 leading; Nigeria, Egypt, Kenya, Rwanda profiled), contrasting closed "Ecosystem
  Debt" platforms (M-PESA) with "generative" public rails (India Stack, Pix). One analyst's
  framework — cite as Kariuki's, not an authoritative index. See [[dpi.id]], [[dpi.exchange]],
  [[geopol.china]]. Sources:
  [[2026-02-23 111 - Winning But Losing - The Payments End Game]],
  [[2025-08-04 93 - Ecosystem Debt or Generative Platforms A Case for Digital Public Infrastructure]],
  [[2026-04-20 117 - Payments Follow Trade]]; earlier backgrounder (2021, figures dated — do not
  harden) [[2021-08-30 31 First we must build Infrastructure]].
- **Instant-payment rails as national utilities — eKash and the CBN circular (analysis, 2025–2026).**
  Rwanda's **[[ekash]]** (RNDPS 2.0; launched 5 Dec 2025, powered by [[rswitch-rwanda]] on the
  [[mojaloop]] open-source switch — RSwitch + WiredIn, P2M/P2B, SACCO/MFI; insider-authored,
  so volume figures are project-stated) exemplifies the interoperable-utility model — and reached
  **full interoperability effective 14 Jul 2026** under **[[national-bank-of-rwanda]]** Directive
  45/2026 (any-bank/any-wallet A2A + merchant pay, **RWF 20** fee cap, **RWF 10m** limit;
  aggregator-sourced, verify vs the directive) — while
  Anjola Adebowale's two-part read of the **[[cbn-payments-circular-2026]]** (data localisation
  by **1 Jan 2027**) argues Nigeria's payments-*data* mandate outruns its data-centre and grid
  capacity and should be sequenced against infrastructure milestones. Attributed to Ishimwe
  (eKash) and Adebowale (CBN). See [[RWA]], [[NGA]], [[infra.store]], [[dpi.exchange]]. Sources:
  [[2025-12-05 From Access to Empowerment Introducing Rwanda's Instant Payment System — eKash]],
  [[2025-03-10 Powering Rwanda's Payments Future How Mojaloop Enables RNDPS 2.0 and eKash]],
  [[2026-07-13 Rwanda's eKash System Achieves Full Interoperability for Digital Payments]],
  [[2026-07-01 Strategy Ahead of Infrastructure, Part I Inside the CBN's June 2026 Payments Circular]],
  [[2026-06-19 Strategy Ahead of Infrastructure, Part II The Opportunity and the Case for Sequencing]].
- **[[SDN]] Sudan (2026) — a sovereign hub with competing private spokes.** The intended national
  payments architecture is **not single-provider**: CBoS tendered the **National Instant Payment System**
  (20 Apr – 4 May 2026), and as of **2026-07-08** a national switch is **contracted but not launched**, to
  be operated by **EBS** as a "switch of switches" above multiple licensed bank- and fintech-operated
  switches. CBoS had licensed **≥4 switch operators** by mid-2026 and **revoked one (Al-Asjad) on
  2026-07-04**, breaches undisclosed. Wartime concentration on Bankak (2025) was **practice under
  collapse, not design** — a qualification of, not support for, the concentration reading.
- **[[SSD]] South Sudan (2025-10-08).** SSRA + NCA six-year **build-operate-transfer** with Ensoft
  (N-Soft in other reporting) for telecom revenue assurance: **vendor 3.56%, government 96.44%**, NCA
  hosts and oversees, full transfer after six years, no fixed contract value.
- **[[CAF]] Central African Republic (2026-04) — digitising revenue collection, not just services.**
  *Pata Polélé* (Sango: *pata* = money, *polélé* = clear/open) digitises the **"mini-recettes"** —
  administrative fees levied across some **twenty ministries** — replacing cash and manual *quittances*
  with digital receipts and mobile-money settlement, including traffic fines recorded in-platform. Pilots:
  Justice, Finance, Interior. **EU-financed via Expertise France** (€17.5m over four years).
