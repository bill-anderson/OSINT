# Legislation and regulation (`gov.legislate`)

Binding law and regulation — data-protection statutes, cross-border enforceability,
localization mandates, and the competition/antitrust angle on cloud concentration.

## Key material

- **[[NGA]] Coordination as a regulatory form — Nigeria's virtual-assets Executive Order
  (2026-07-20).** The **[[virtual-assets-executive-order-2026|Presidential Executive Order on Virtual
  Assets Coordination, 2026]]** (s.5, 1999 Constitution as altered; effective immediately) is
  notable for what it refuses to be: **no new regulator, no transfer of statutory powers**. It builds
  a **Virtual Asset Council** ([[cbn]] chair; **[[nigeria-revenue-service|NRS]]** and
  **[[sec-nigeria|SEC]]** vice-chairs; **[[nfiu|NFIU]]**, **[[onsa-nigeria|ONSA]]**) over the
  existing mandates, splitting registration by **activity type** — securities-like to the SEC,
  non-security payment/settlement/custody to the CBN — with the Council adjudicating the residue. A
  **Harmonised Implementation Framework** is due **within 30 days** (~2026-08-19); the sandbox, tax
  policy and White Paper it announces are **unpublished as of 2026-07-20**. Contrast the statutory
  route taken elsewhere (e.g. [[RWA]] Rwanda's virtual-assets bill,
  [[2026-04-01-virtual-assets-draft-law-techafricanews]]). See [[NGA]], [[dpi.pay]],
  [[nigeria--dpi-pay]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-20-nigeria-coordinated-regulatory-framework-virtual-assets]].
- **[[DZA]] An image is personal data — ANPDP délibération (adopted 2026-07-15).** Algeria's
  **[[anpdp]]** adopted a délibération on **Wednesday 15 July 2026** ruling that **any photograph or
  video permitting direct or indirect identification of a natural person is personal data** under
  **[[algeria-law-18-07|loi n°18-07]]**, so its collection, storage, retention and publication need a
  legal basis and must meet lawfulness, transparency, proportionality and purpose-limitation. Public
  posting to social or digital platforms now requires **free, explicit, prior and verifiable
  consent** absent a statutory exception, with prior notice of controller identity, purpose,
  distribution, retention period and the rights of access, rectification, objection and withdrawal.
  The sovereignty clause is the sharp one: **publishing images on platforms whose infrastructure is
  hosted outside Algeria may be treated as an international transfer of personal data**, triggering
  **arts. 44–45** adequacy obligations on the controller — an extraterritorial reading that in
  practice reaches ordinary social-media use. Complaints lie to the ANPDP alongside the courts. It
  follows ANPDP texts on **workplace video surveillance** and **DPO designation**. See
  [[gov.protect]], [[algeria--gov-protect]], `sovereignty`. Source:
  [[2026-07-20-algerie-anpdp-regles-publication-images-plateformes]].
- **A "second-wave" reform surge across the continent (2026 H1).** Trackers converge on a wave of new
  and amended data-protection statutes: **Burundi** enacted (15 Jan 2026), taking Africa to **~45
  laws**; **Djibouti, The Gambia, Botswana** newly in force; **Angola, Ghana, Kenya, Mauritius,
  Nigeria, Seychelles, Algeria, Tunisia** amending or overhauling. Drivers: local-nexus/accountability
  rules for foreign platforms, AI regulation via data-protection law, and interoperability with
  international standards; **Ghana** would become one of few states globally to recognise personal data
  as *property*. Sources:
  [[2026-03-12 Africa’s Data Protection Reforms A Continental Perspective on the Drivers of Change in Legal Frameworks]],
  [[2026-06-23 Bimonthly Update on Privacy in Africa (January & February 2026)]],
  [[2026-01-29 Press release Paradigm Initiative applauds countries with data protection laws]]. See [[gov.protect]], [[tech.ai]].

- **African data-law patchwork (2026-07, Okorie).** Nigeria DPA 2023 and Kenya DPA 2019
  are becoming "enforcement-driven"; cross-jurisdictional enforceability of IP and data
  assets is now a gating factor for scale-ups. See [[gov.protect]], [[dpi.exchange]].
- **New national statutes (2026-07).** **Nigeria's NIMC Act 2026** (enacted June 2026,
  replacing the 2007 Act) makes the NIN the foundational credential and underpins "trusted
  DPI" ([[nimc]], [[dpi.id]], [[nigeria--dpi-id]]); the **[[cbn]] data-localization
  directive** is the binding regulatory instrument behind Nigeria's onshoring push
  ([[gov.protect]], [[nigeria--gov-protect]]); and **Ghana** is drafting an **Innovation
  and Startup** framework to give its planned fintech fund a legal foundation and set
  startup eligibility ([[GHA]], [[tech.innovate]], [[samuel-nartey-george]]). Sources:
  [[2026-07-09 Nigeria seeks support to operationalize new digital ID law as NIN tops 136M]],
  [[2026-07-07 CBN's local data order puts Nigeria's data centres to test]],
  [[2026-07-07 Ghana and UNDP Strengthen Partnership on Digital Transformation Projects]].
- **[[NGA]] competition law meets AI, and a privacy-policy suit (2026-07).** President Tinubu
  directed the **[[fccpc]]** to investigate [[meta]], Alphabet/[[google]], X and generative-AI
  platforms for **abuse of dominance and copyright** (FCCP Act 2018) over uncompensated training
  on Nigerian journalism, after a Nigerian Press Organisation petition — **competition and
  consumer-protection law, not data law**, as the enforceable lever over foreign tech, building on
  the FCCPC's contested **2025 $222m Meta penalty**. In parallel,
  [[digital-rights-lawyers-initiative]] sued [[inec]] under the
  **[[nigeria-data-protection-act-2023]]** (ss.24/27) for publishing no privacy policy —
  regulation increasingly tested through the courts, not just agencies. See [[gov.protect]],
  [[tech.industry]], [[nimc]]. Sources:
  [[2026-07-10 Why Nigeria Is Investigating Meta, Google, X and AI Firms]],
  [[2026-07-10 FIJ Showed That INEC Broke Data Law. Digital Rights Lawyers Sue the Commission 10 Months Later]],
  [[2026-07-10 Nigeria tests data sovereignty as foreign tech firms facing probe]].
- **New statutes and DPAs across smaller states (2026-01→02).** The legislative layer is thickening
  beyond the big markets: **[[ZMB]] Zambia** tabled an **Electronic Government (Amendment) Bill**
  (revising Act No. 41 of 2021) to give **electronic submissions and digital authentication** binding
  legal standing and unblock end-to-end digital services ([[smart-zambia-institute]]); **[[SOM]]
  Somalia**'s Council of Ministers approved a **Regulatory Framework for the Public Data Protection
  Law** ([[somalia-ministry-communications-technology]]); **[[COG]] Congo-Brazzaville** installed an
  independent **[[national-personal-data-protection-commission-congo]]** empowered to advise, warn and
  sanction; and **[[MWI]] Malawi** anchored its privacy push on the **[[malawi-data-protection-act-2024]]**.
  Law as the enabling — and enforcement — layer under DPI. See [[gov.protect]], [[dpi.govtech]],
  [[gov.standards]]. Sources:
  [[2026-01-23 Zambia begins amending Electronic Government Act]],
  [[2026-02-06 Somalia adopts data regulatory framework]],
  [[2026-01-22 Congo-Brazzaville intros data protection commission]],
  [[2026-01-28 Malawi positions data privacy as cornerstone of digital development]].
- **Internet shutdown without legal basis — Uganda (2026-01).** The **[[uganda-communications-commission]]**
  ordered a **four-day internet blackout** from the evening of **13 Jan 2026**, two days before the
  presidential election; **>$70m** biometric voter-verification kits failed on polling morning, and the
  cut cost **[[mtn-group]]**/**[[airtel-africa]]** ~**$15m**. The **Uganda Law Society** declared the
  order **illegal, null and unconstitutional**: Section 87 of the UCC Act permits suspension only under a
  declared **state of emergency** (Art. 110), and none existed; a lawsuit against UCC, the telcos and the
  Attorney-General seeks compensation and a bar on future arbitrary shutdowns. Regulatory power exercised
  outside its own statutory limits — the legality question at the sharp end. See [[UGA]],
  [[infra.connect]], [[gov.protect]], [[sovereignty]]. Source:
  [[2026-01-26 The cost of Uganda's internet shutdown]].
- **US pressure against sovereign data law (2026-02→03).** A State Department cable (18 Feb 2026, signed
  Rubio), classed as an *action request*, instructs diplomats to monitor and **counter**
  data-localization and data-sovereignty legislation in host countries — naming GDPR — and to push the
  US-led Global Cross-Border Privacy Rules Forum instead. Binding law elsewhere reframed as a trade
  barrier; part of a campaign spanning a Section 301 case against Brazil and a WTO e-transmission duty
  moratorium (opposed by South Africa, India, Indonesia). See [[gov.protect]], [[gov.policy]],
  [[geopol.usa]], [[XGL]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-12 How Trump's Data Regulation Crackdown Undermines Digital Sovereignty]].
- **Regulation as market-shaping (Eaves & Bester, "Parting Clouds").** The competition
  case: three hyperscalers hold ~66% of global public cloud (85% in Canada). Argues for
  interoperability mandates (S3-compatible storage) and procurement leverage over
  bespoke "sovereign stack" industrial policy. See [[tech.industry]], [[gov.standards]].
- **EU statutory push (Verhulst & Gonzalez Otero, "Triad").** The EU **Technological
  Sovereignty Package** (IP/26/1187, 2026-06-03) — Chips Act 2.0, Cloud & AI Development
  Act, Open Source Strategy — is strong on *dependency* and *openness* but weak on
  **agency** (decisions relegated to national administrations, thin redress). See
  [[geopol.eu]].
- **Cybersecurity statute — Mozambique (2026-04-17).** Parliament **unanimously** passed a **Cyber
  Security Law** plus a separate **Cybercrime Law** ([[mozambique-cyber-security-law]]), naming the
  **INCM** as technical coordinator and creating a **Cyber Security Fund** — but with a notable
  securitisation clause: in a state of siege/emergency the National Cyber Security Authority's
  functions pass to the **Defence and Security Forces' Cyber Coordination Centre**. See [[MOZ]],
  [[infra.cybersec]]. Source: [[2026-04-17 Mozambique parliament unanimously approves Cyber Security Law]].
- **Digital-ID lawmaking by regulation — South Africa (2026-05).** DHA gazetted draft **Identification
  Regulations** amendments (2026-05-04; comment to 2026-06-06) creating voluntary digital-ID
  credentials, "trusted entities" with API access to the population register, and tiered
  identity-assurance levels — with a **launch timeline** (hosting complete by 31 Mar 2027; first
  verifiable credentials 2027/28). Analyst Mark Burke warns the regs may be **ultra vires**:
  substantive digital-ID policy made under the pre-smartphone **Identification Act 1997** ahead of
  the **National Identification and Registration (NIR) Bill** now before Parliament that should be
  their proper statutory foundation ([[sa-digital-id-regulations]]). See [[ZAF]], [[dpi.id]],
  [[gov.protect]]. Sources:
  [[2026-04-21 South Africa's digital ID gets a targeted launch date]],
  [[2026-05-29 Opinion South Africa's Digital Identity Regulations A Governance Crossroads]].
- **Rewriting the telecom rulebook — Nigeria (2026-05).** The **[[ncc]]** is rewriting Nigeria's
  **National Telecommunications Policy** for the first time since **2000**, stakeholders warning that
  a licensing regime built for voice/SMS no longer fits cloud, AI, data centres and smart cities —
  regulatory obsolescence cited as a reason hyperscalers route investment to Kenya and South Africa.
  See [[NGA]], [[gov.policy]], [[infra.store]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-22 Why global hyperscalers are avoiding Nigeria despite Africa's biggest market]].
- **Mandating an infrastructure database — South Africa (2026-04).** **[[icasa]]** gazetted draft
  rapid-deployment regulations whose **regulation 7** compels every licensed operator to submit
  **geo-referenced data** on all passive infrastructure (fibre, towers, ducts, poles, base stations)
  plus service-availability and forward roll-out plans to a **national GIS database**, bi-annually,
  to prescribed standards (**WGS84**; Shapefile/GeoJSON/GeoPackage). Fines up to **R1m** for
  non-compliance; GIS duties bite six months post-publication. Regulation forcing disclosure that
  operators long resisted on competitive grounds. See [[gov.standards]], [[infra.connect]],
  [[data.open]]. Source: [[2026-04-12 Icasa moves to mandate national infrastructure database]].
- **Licensing as a sovereignty gate — Namibia (2026-03).** **[[cran]]** **denied** [[starlink]] both
  an operating and spectrum licence, finding it met only **three of six** statutory criteria — failing
  **ownership/control** (100% foreign, breaching the **51% Namibian-ownership** rule with no
  ministerial exemption), **national security / data sovereignty**, and **compliance history** (a
  Nov-2024 cease-and-desist for operating unlicensed). Licensing law wielded as a data-sovereignty and
  local-ownership instrument; CRAN left the door open to compliant LEO providers. See [[NAM]],
  [[gov.protect]], [[infra.connect]], [[digital.rural]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-26 Starlink blocked from operating in Namibia]].
- **Opening the payment system by statute — South Africa (2026).** A pending amendment to the
  **National Payment System Act 1998** (per SARB Vision 2025) will open the NPS to **non-bank PSPs**
  from 2026 via an **activity-based licensing** model (seven categories — e-money, acquiring,
  remittances, clearing/settlement…), replacing bank-sponsorship. An interim **2025 Exemption Notice /
  Draft Directive** set prudential terms; **[[sarb]]**/Prudential Authority supervise. Statutory
  re-plumbing of who may run payment rails (PayShap >100m txns; cross-border via SADC TCIB / ISO
  20022). See [[XSA]], [[dpi.pay]], [[gov.policy]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-17 South Africa's National Payment System PSP licences and the 2026 payments reform]].
- **A single-identity statute — Rwanda (2026-03).** Rwanda's **Single Digital Identification Law**
  bars any citizen from holding two government-issued identities and **sunsets all legacy ID cards by
  June 2027**, giving the biometric e-Indangamuntu ([[nida-rwanda]]) its legal foundation, with
  statutory consent (self-consent from 16) and revocable, logged data access. Law as the enforcement
  layer under a national digital ID. See [[RWA]], [[dpi.id]], [[gov.protect]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-16 Digital IDs How registration is progressing so far]].

- **AI regulation and NDPA enforcement — Nigeria (2026-02/03).** The **[[ndpc]]** joined 60+ regulators
  via the Global Privacy Assembly's International Enforcement Cooperation Working Group against
  deepfakes/synthetic media, pushing privacy-by-design, watermarking and "algorithmic accountability
  like financial reporting" (Commissioner Vincent Olatunji) — the enforcement arm of Minister
  **[[bosun-tijani]]**'s national AI strategy. But the **[[nigeria-data-protection-act-2023]]** itself
  is criticised as structurally weak: it created the NDPC yet placed it **under ministerial control**,
  and analysts document uneven enforcement (breaches rarely translating into publicised sanctions,
  the NDPR-era gap persisting). Statute enacted; regulator independence and enforcement contested. See
  [[gov.protect]], [[tech.ai]], [[NGA]]. Sources:
  [[2026-03-04 Nigeria takes new measures to regulate AI]],
  [[2026-02-27 Who Protects Nigeria's Digital Citizens Unmasking Accountability Gaps in Digital Rights Governance]].

- **AI law — the EU AI Act, Kenya's transplant, and corporate lobbying (2026, Substack batch).** The
  **[[eu-ai-act]]** (in force 2024-08-01; prohibited-practice bans from 2025-02-02; full high-risk
  enforcement 2026-08-02) is analysed by Nagadya as **extraterritorial** — its "market-placement"
  test binds African businesses whose AI output lands in the EU — with a call to *extend* rather than
  copy its risk tiers. Munyua reads Kenya's **[[kenya-ai-bill-2026]]** as a wholesale EU-AI-Act
  transplant (self-declared, Memorandum of Objects p.73) that regulates a stack Kenya did not build:
  a presidentially-appointed AI Commissioner, a **KES 5m (~$40k) cap** vs the EU's 3%/7%-of-turnover
  penalties, no extraterritorial reach, no impact assessment, no community standing. Okolo warns
  **corporate lobbying** already shapes African AI law (Google/Meta/Microsoft supported drafting
  Nigeria's strategy; Brazil diluted its 2025 AI Act after OpenAI/Meta/Amazon/Google lobbying),
  prescribing independent publicly-funded regulators and mandated lobbying transparency. See
  [[tech.ai]], [[geopol.eu]], [[gov.regional]]. Sources:
  [[2026-05-18 Europe Wrote The World's Most Advanced AI Law. The EU AI Act]],
  [[2026-05-19 GATEKEEPERS XIV]],
  [[2026-02-17 Corporate Interests Must Not Co-Opt Africa's AI Moment]].

- **New statutes across the smaller states — STP, SSD, SLE, SOM (2016–2026).** The legislative layer
  thickens in four more states: **[[STP]] São Tomé and Príncipe** rests on its data-protection
  **Law 03/2016** plus an **Interoperability Framework (Law 1/2024)**; **[[SSD]] South Sudan** passed a
  **Cybercrime & Abuse of Computer Bill 2025** (awaiting presidential assent); **[[SLE]] Sierra Leone**
  validated a combined **Data Protection & RTI Bill 2025**; and **[[SOM]] Somalia** approved a
  **Cybersecurity Law** (**26 Jan 2026**) establishing a national CIRT (**SOM-CIRT**). See
  [[gov.protect]], [[infra.cybersec]], [[STP]], [[SSD]], [[SLE]], [[SOM]].

- **Statute enacted, regulator borrowed — Eswatini and Seychelles (2022–2023).** Two small states
  legislated by **designating an existing body** rather than building a privacy regulator.
  **[[SWZ]] Eswatini**'s [[data-protection-act-2022-eswatini]] (Act No. 5 of 2022) was gazetted and
  effective **4 March 2022**, one of **four ICT Acts** passed that year — all, [[apc]] notes,
  **tabled with certificates of urgency**, compressing stakeholder input — and made the sector
  regulator [[esccom]] the data protection authority, with fines to **E5,000,000 or 2% of
  turnover**; the World Bank reports the **Data Protection Office not yet operationalised
  (2025-07-31)**. **[[SYC]] Seychelles**'s [[data-protection-act-2023-seychelles]] (enacted **19
  December 2023**) repealed a **2003** Act "enacted but never implemented," designating the
  Access-to-Information-Act-constituted [[information-commission-seychelles]] as authority (s.5) —
  the model TechHive Advisory likens to **[[ZAF]]**'s; its 18-month transition ran from **22
  December 2023** and **elapsed around 22 June 2025**. Enactment is the easy part; the enforcing
  institution is the lag. → [[eswatini--gov-protect]], [[seychelles--gov-protect]]. See
  [[gov.protect]].

- **Francophone lawmaking — Chad drafting, Togo passing (2025).** **[[TCD]] Chad** is
  **finalising, not enacting**: Minister [[boukar-michel]] announced (**9 Dec 2025**) a digital law
  to incorporate international standards on cybersecurity, data protection and internet governance —
  **no text, enactment date or gazette reference is held**. Its National Assembly did approve, on
  **22 October 2025**, an **ordinance amending the 2014 law** on electronic communications and
  postal services. **[[TGO]] Togo** legislated for real: the [[togo-aml-law-2025]] (National
  Assembly, Dec 2025 budget session) **replaces a 2018 statute**, importing updated **[[fatf]]**
  recommendations and a **2023 [[waemu]]/UEMOA directive**, introducing a **risk-based approach**,
  extending due diligence to **virtual asset service providers** and strengthening **beneficial
  ownership** identification — the data-governance hook in an AML instrument. Separately, Togo is
  **aligning** with WAEMU's **June 2023** consumer-protection directive via a **20–21 March 2025**
  Lomé stakeholder workshop — **consultation, not an adoption instrument**. See [[gov.protect]],
  [[gov.standards]], [[gov.regional]], [[dpi.registry]], [[XWA]]. Sources:
  [[2025-12-10 Chad Finalizes Digital Law to Align National Framework With Global Standards]],
  [[2025-12-30 Togo Adopts New Law to Strengthen Anti-Money Laundering Rules]],
  [[2025-03-24 Togo Adopts WAEMU Consumer Protection Framework]].

- **A whole digital-law package moving at once — Angola (2026 H1).** Angola is legislating the digital
  stack in parallel, with uneven results. Its **cybersecurity bill** ([[angola-cybersecurity-law]]) won
  general approval **23 Jan 2026 (105–1–75)** but was **suspended in committee (7–8 Jul 2026)** over
  drafting non-conformities; a **"fake news" law** ([[angola-fake-news-law]]) **passed definitively
  21 May 2026** (1–10 years); a **Digital Government framework bill** ([[angola-digital-government-law]])
  entered **public consultation (6 May 2026)** expressly to end "dispersão legislativa" since 1997; and
  the government's **Law 22/11 data-protection revision** cleared consultation (2026-06) — while a UNITA
  electoral-data amendment **passed committee then failed in plenary (2026-06-25)**. Fragmentation is
  both the diagnosis and, in the stall of the cyber bill, the symptom. See [[infra.cybersec]],
  [[gov.protect]], [[AGO]], [[angola--infra-cybersec]]. Sources:
  [[2026-01-23-proposta-lei-ciberseguranca-aprovada-generalidade]],
  [[2026-07-08-deputados-suspendem-discussao-lei-da-ciberseguranca]],

- **[[LBR]] Liberia — rule by executive order, ahead of statute.** Liberia's digital agenda advances
  largely by **executive order** while primary law lags: **EO 147** (Apr 2025) mandated biometric ID and
  **EO 163** (Apr 2026) created the [[otdi-liberia|OTDI/NDMI]] digital-government framework, each promising
  follow-on legislation (EO 163 gives 12 months to draft an institutionalising law). The
  **[[liberia-data-protection-bill|Personal Information Act]]** reached a House committee (Jun 2025) but is
  unpassed; a **Cybercrime Act 2025** exists; and the [[lta-liberia|LTA]] is rewriting the licensing regime
  under the 2007 Telecom Act (splitting infrastructure/service licences, satellite and IMEI rules). See
  [[dpi.id]], [[gov.protect]], [[LBR]]. Sources:

- **Regulators using statutory powers against licensees (2026-07).** Two contemporaneous cases show
  African telecom regulators exercising codified authority rather than informal pressure — and
  surviving the procedure. In **[[GHA]] Ghana**, the **[[national-communications-authority-ghana|NCA]]**
  amended **[[ngic-ghana|NGIC]]**'s wholesale 5G licence to strip its exclusivity (**effective
  2026-07-15**) under **Article 6.1.2 of the licence** and **s.14, Electronic Communications Act 2008
  (Act 775)**, running the full statutory sequence — Notice of Proposed Amendment (2 Mar), NGIC
  objection under **s.14(4)** (18 Mar), meeting (1 Apr), oral representations to the Governing Board
  (28 May) — before finding removal to be in the public interest; the amendment is deliberately
  narrow, leaving the balance of the licence and NGIC's **spectrum assignment** intact. In **[[MWI]]
  Malawi**, **[[macra-malawi|MACRA]]** found **[[airtel-malawi|Airtel Malawi]]** and
  **[[tnm-malawi|TNM]]** in breach of **s.76, Communications Act 2016** (approved tariff changes must
  run in two daily newspapers **seven days** before effect) and ordered customer compensation for the
  **26 Jun – 2 Jul 2026** window, to be completed with evidence by **31 July 2026**. MACRA (DG
  **Mayamiko Nkoloma**) framed the remedy in explicitly **restitutional** terms — to "restore consumers
  to the position they would have been in had the statutory notice requirement been observed" — which is
  the doctrinally interesting part: the regulator treats a **procedural** breach as generating a
  compensable loss, not merely a sanctionable one. Note the shape of
  both: Ghana reverses **its own** market design through process; Malawi enforces **notice**, not
  price — the approved increase stands. See [[infra.connect]], [[ghana--infra-connect]],
  [[malawi--infra-connect]], [[include.access]]. Sources:
  [[2026-07-17-ghana-ends-ngic-exclusive-5g-licence]],
  [[2026-07-17-macra-airtel-malawi-tnm-tariff-compensation]],
  [[2026-07-17-malawi-macra-telecom-tariff-compliance]].
- **[[DZA]] Algeria, Presidential Decree n° 25-320 (30 Dec 2025).** 20 articles, four chapters, signed
  Tebboune; gazetted in *JO* n° 87 alongside Decree 25-321 (National Information-Systems Security Strategy
  2025–2029). Splits supervision three ways: the National Council for Information-Systems Security on the
  dispositif's operation (Art. 14), [[anpdcp|ANPDCP]] on personal-data conformity (Art. 15), [[assi|ASSI]]
  on security-level classification (Art. 16). Source:
  [[2025-12-30-joradp-decret-25-320-gouvernance-donnees]].
- **[[MWI]] Malawi's Data Protection Act 2024 — a worked example of the commencement gap.** s.1 left entry
  into force to a ministerial Gazette notice (exercised **2024-06-03**, four months after gazetting), and
  s.53 then deferred general compliance a further **twenty-four months to 2026-06-02**. **Enactment date,
  commencement date and enforceability date are three different things** — conflating them produced a live
  error in this corpus, a trade source asserting in May 2025 that Malawi had no enacted law when the Act had
  been in force eleven months. Source: [[2024-02-02-malawi-data-protection-act-2024]].
- **[[GHA]] Ghana — national-ID statutory stack (corrected 2026-07-20).** **NIA Act 2006 (Act 707)**,
  **National Identity Register Act 2008 (Act 750)**, **NIR (Amendment) Act 2017 (Act 950)**, and **NIR
  Regulations 2012 (L.I. 2111)** as amended by **L.I. 2356 (2018)** and by the **NIR (Amendment)
  Regulations 2026 (L.I. 2523)**. Acts 707 and 750 were each also
  amended by the Data Protection Act 2012. A **"National Identification Act 2009" — previously carried in
  this wiki — does not exist**; no Ghanaian statute bears that title or year.
- **[[GHA]] Ghana L.I. 2523 — the three-dates trap, and the announcement date as a fourth.** A worked
  instance of the rule above. **L.I. 2523** was **signed and gazetted on 27 March 2026**, **came into force
  on 9 June 2026**, and was **publicised by the NIA on 16 July 2026** — the day after the Executive
  Secretary's own Facebook post of **15 July 2026** stated the instrument had been "gazetted today". Three
  outlets each anchored on a different date and each called it the gazetting: two on 15 July (the post),
  one on "last month" (the June commencement). **None was right, and the error originated with the
  announcing official, not the press** — a reminder that an institution's own social post is not the same
  evidentiary tier as its signed press release, and that a *publicity* date is a fourth thing again,
  arriving here nearly four months after gazetting and five weeks after the law already bound anyone
  subject to it. Sources: [[2026-07-16-nia-li-2523-biometric-verification-press-release]],
  [[2026-07-15-biometric-verification-mandatory-ghana-card-myjoy]],
  [[2026-07-15-photocopying-ghana-card-illegal-fines-graphic]]. *The gazette notice itself is not held
  (stated 2026-07-20).*
- **[[UGA]] Uganda — retroactive commencement as a validation device.** **SI 67 of 2024** (2024-08-12)
  amends reg. 19 of the Registration of Persons Regulations 2015 to extend national ID validity **from
  ten to eleven years**, and is **deemed in force from 20 November 2015** — a retroactive commencement of
  eight years nine months whose effect is to validate cards already expired on their printed term. The
  state extended the life of expired documents rather than reissuing them. *(This wiki previously
  described SI 67 as the instrument preserving ID use for KYC and financial transactions under ss.65–66;
  the instrument says none of that, and that reading is withdrawn.)*
- **[[TCD]] Chad — legislating the state's use of biometrics before the citizen's protection from it.**
  A law on **biometric data in judicial procedures** was passed **2026-04-10 (138–1)** while Chad holds
  **no general data-protection law and no supervisory authority**. The sequence is the finding.
- **[[TGO]] Togo — a prior-authorisation regime that bites on MOSIP programmes.** *Loi n° 2019-014* of
  **2019-10-29** (not September, as previously carried) requires **IPDCP authorisation** for processing
  biometric data **and** for use of a national identification number (art. 8). Togo's MOSIP-based e-ID
  does both, and **no such authorisation is held (as of 2026-07-20)**. The IPDCP is created by **art. 55
  of the law itself** — there is no separate establishing act, though its organisation decree is unheld.
