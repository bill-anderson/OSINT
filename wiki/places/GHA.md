# Ghana (GHA)

## Recent developments

- **2026-07-01** — **[[google]]** announced **Africa's first applied AI lab in Ghana**
  (Accra AI Community Center; applications close 31 Aug 2026), part of the five
  initiatives unveiled at its Johannesburg Africa Cloud Summit. Source:
  [[2026-07-01 Google Cloud Summit in Africa Highlights the Continent's Digital Transformation and Unveils New Agentic AI and Infrastructure Investments]].
  See [[tech.ai]], [[tech.innovate]], [[capacity.training]].
- **2026-07-07** — Ghana deepens its **[[undp]]** partnership on digital transformation.
  Minister **[[samuel-nartey-george]]** confirms use of the **UNESCO Readiness Assessment
  Methodology (RAM)** to sequence initiatives, previews a **national fintech fund** tied
  to a forthcoming **Innovation and Startup** legislative framework, and flags an **AI &
  digital-governance capacity-building programme for the Civil Service** (multi-cohort,
  gender-inclusion emphasis). UNDP cites three flagships — an Italian-supported digital
  project, an MSME digital platform, and a Japanese-backed AI programme — plus a proposed
  **Digital Youth Village**. Source:
  [[2026-07-07 Ghana and UNDP Strengthen Partnership on Digital Transformation Projects]].
  See [[gov.policy]], [[capacity.training]], [[gov.legislate]].
- **2026-04-03** — **$250m AI computing centre + National AI Strategy.** Cabinet approved a
  **$250m national AI compute centre** and the **[[ghana-national-ai-strategy]]** (2023–2033;
  eight pillars incl. data governance; a Responsible AI Office; public launch set 24 Apr 2026),
  developed with **[[smart-africa]]**, GIZ FAIR Forward and The Future Society; Ghana ranks
  72nd globally / 6th in Africa on the 2025 Global AI Index. Minister
  **[[samuel-nartey-george]]**. Source:
  [[2026-04-03 Ghana approves $250M AI Hub & National AI Strategy]]. See [[tech.ai]],
  [[finance.new]], [[gov.policy]], [[infra.store]].
- **2026-04-01** *(correction)* — **Ghana Card does NOT support payments.** TechCabal
  **retracted** its 1 Apr 2026 story claiming the **[[ghana-card]]** could now make payments;
  the claim was inaccurate and the article was removed. Recorded so the wiki carries no
  reversed claim of Ghana-Card payment functionality. Source:
  [[2026-04-01 RETRACTION Ghana's National ID cards can now make payments]]. See [[dpi.id]], [[dpi.pay]].
- **2026-03-11** — **NSOs as data custodians (RIA report).** [[research-ict-africa]] published
  a report on the evolving role of National Statistics Offices, with **Ghana among four NSO
  case studies** (ZAF/GHA/KEN/BEN) being displaced as primary data producers by platform,
  satellite and private digital data; it calls for legal reform to formalise NSOs as data
  custodians. Source:
  [[2026-03-11 The evolving role of National Statistics Offices (NSOs) as data custodians in the digital age]].
  See [[data.statistics]], [[data.open]], [[gov.policy]], [[gov.legislate]].
- **2026-03-05** — **Huawei to train 3,000 girls in AI.** On the MWC 2026 (Barcelona)
  sidelines, [[samuel-nartey-george]] secured a commitment from **[[huawei]]** to give free
  AI training to **3,000 girls** in Ghana's Girls in ICT programme (which has trained ~18,000
  since 2012), feeding into the One Million Coders programme; talks also covered the $250m AI
  Compute Centre, 5G, rural telephony and a possible smartphone-assembly plant. 4G reaches
  >90% of Ghanaians but usage is <60%. Source:
  [[2026-03-05 Ghana strikes AI training deal with Chinese multinational amid $250m tech investment push]].
  See [[capacity.training]], [[tech.ai]], [[geopol.china]], [[include.divides]].
- **2026-02-23** — **MTN Ghana's $1.1bn three-year investment.** During a working visit by
  MTN Group CEO **[[ralph-mupita]]**, **[[mtn-ghana]]** ([[mtn-group]]) pledged to reinvest
  **~$1.1bn over three years** in network resilience, 4G/5G, hyperscale data centres and
  AI/cloud — pitching Ghana as a West African digital hub on the back of subsea-cable
  capacity. MTN Ghana holds ~**58%** market share (>25m subscribers); its **MoMo** platform
  has **>11m** active users. Echoes a 2022 >$1bn/5yr pledge (with GIFEC). Source:
  [[2026-02-23 MTN Ghana secures $1.1bn boost as AI push deepens]]. See [[infra.connect]],
  [[infra.store]], [[tech.ai]], [[dpi.pay]], [[capacity.training]].
- **2026-02-06** — **Nigeria's fintech-passporting bid names Ghana as a pilot partner.**
  Commentary on the **[[cbn]]** **[[cbn-policy-insight-series-2025]]** (18-month roadmap;
  Single Regulatory Window; Responsible AI in Finance Hub) reads it as regional architecture
  for **[[ecowas]]** / **[[afcfta]]**: proposed bilateral **regulatory passporting** pilots
  with Ghana, Kenya, Senegal and South Africa would let a CBN-licensed fintech operate in
  Ghana without re-licensing, collapsing cross-border compliance friction. Source:
  [[2026-02-06 Nigeria just raised the bar for West African Fintech]]. See [[dpi.pay]],
  [[finance.new]], [[gov.regional]], [[gov.standards]], [[XWA]].
- **2026-02-05** — **Mobile-money interoperability +87%.** Bank of Ghana data
  (**[[bank-of-ghana]]**, African Prosperity Dialogue) show interoperable mobile-money value
  rising **GH¢3.1bn (Dec 2024) → GH¢5.8bn (Dec 2025), +87%**, transactions ~20m → 29m (+45%),
  with higher-value payments increasingly on interoperable rails (launched 2018). BoG flagged
  **fintech passporting with Rwanda**, a passed **Virtual Asset Service Providers Act**, and
  Ghana's role in **[[papss]]** as a platform for AfCFTA financial integration. At the same Dialogue,
  Second Deputy Governor Matilda Asante-Asiedu called for a cross-border **payment-system overhaul**
  to unlock AfCFTA's ~**$2.8tn** market: intra-African payments cost **7–10%** (vs ~3% global) and
  **>80%** route through correspondent banks abroad, costing Africa ~**$5.3bn/yr**. Sources:
  [[2026-02-05 Ghana Mobile Money Interoperability Nearly Doubles in 2025]],
  [[2026-02-05 Bank of Ghana Calls for Payment System Overhaul to Unlock AfCFTA Trade]]. See [[dpi.pay]],
  [[dpi.exchange]], [[data.statistics]], [[gov.regional]], [[finance.new]], [[infra.cybersec]].
- **2026-02-04** — **PAPSS–Onafriq Nigeria→Ghana wallet rail.** **[[papss]]** and
  **[[onafriq]]** launched a **6-month pilot (from 1 Dec)**, CBN-approved, for wallet-based
  outbound payments **Nigeria→Ghana fully in naira** with no hard-currency conversion —
  bridging bank and mobile-money silos across the corridor. PAPSS spans **160+ banks /
  400m+ accounts / 19 countries**; framed as operationalising AfCFTA. *(Background, as of
  2022: PAPSS launched Jan 2022, HQ Cairo, developed by **[[afreximbank]]** with AfCFTA,
  ~42 currencies, Ghana access via GhIPSS —
  [[2022-05-11 Pan-African Payment and Settlement System]].)* Source:
  [[2026-02-04 PAPSS, Onafriq launch new trade rail for West Africa]]. See [[dpi.pay]],
  [[dpi.exchange]], [[finance.mou]], [[gov.regional]], [[afcfta]].
- **2026-02-02** — **After Access gender study, Ghana report (RIA).** [[research-ict-africa]]
  published its **[[after-access]]** qualitative gender study Ghana report: Ghana rates among
  stronger continental performers on connectivity/affordability, yet women's access remains
  uneven by geography, income, education and gender norms. Source:
  [[2026-02-02 After Access qualitative study on gender and digital technologies Ghana report]].
  See [[include.divides]], [[include.access]], [[capacity.literacy]].
- **2026-01-28** — **Margins ID Group — Africa's certified ID producer (profile).**
  **[[margins-id-group]]** (Accra, founded 1990) positions as a fully certified identity/
  payment infrastructure provider; its **ICPS** arm is the only West African **ISO 14298**
  government-grade security-printing facility (Mastercard/Visa/Europay-certified) and produces
  the **[[ghana-card]]**, while **MIDSA** runs digital-ID/biometric platforms — an African
  alternative to overseas card production, positioned for AfCFTA-interoperable systems and
  SDG 16.9. Source:
  [[2026-01-28 Margins ID Group emerges as Africa's leading provider of certified digital identity solutions]].
  See [[dpi.id]], [[dpi.pay]], [[infra.cybersec]], [[gov.standards]].
- **2026-01-22** — **.GH domain to state control.** Minister **[[samuel-nartey-george]]**
  announced plans to transition the **.gh** root — managed privately since 1996 by internet
  pioneer Prof. Nii Narku Quaynor — to government ownership via the
  **[[ghana-domain-name-registry]]** (GDNR), and to auto-assign **.com.gh** domains to newly
  registered businesses, framing the domain as "our national identity on the internet." A
  digital-sovereignty move. Source:
  [[2026-01-22 Ghana to take state control of .GH domain to strengthen digital identity]].
  See [[gov.policy]], [[dpi.id]], [[infra.connect]].
- **2026-01-20** — **Margins exports Ghana's national-ID model to The Gambia.**
  **[[margins-id-group]]** signed a **DBFOT contract** ([[gambia-national-id-contract]]) to
  build The Gambia's National ID + Integrated Digital ID system — the **first African-owned
  firm to export a full national ID system** (tender opened Jul 2024; 11 bidders → Margins).
  Ghana's own system (built with the NIA) has **19.2m registered** and integrates **262
  institutions**; The Gambia's third national-ID attempt. Africa-to-Africa, AfCFTA-aligned.
  Source: [[2026-01-20 Margins takes Ghana's ID success to The Gambia]]. See [[dpi.id]],
  [[finance.new]], [[tech.industry]], [[GMB]].
- **2026-01-15** — **Voice AI to bypass the feature-phone barrier (analysis).** In a
  TechAfrica analysis on voice/NLP AI extending digital services to feature-phone users,
  Ghana's Communications Minister [[samuel-nartey-george]] features among named voices on
  inclusive AI (alongside MTN's "Miss Baza" and Viamo). Source:
  [[2026-01-15 Leveraging AI to Bypass the Smartphone Barrier and Advance Digital Inclusion in Africa]].
  See [[tech.ai]], [[include.divides]], [[include.access]].
- **2026-01-13** — **Ghana tightening cheque rules.** Amid reporting on Kenya's persistent
  cheque usage, Ghana is noted (with Seychelles) among African markets tightening cheque
  rules, against a continental trend toward phase-out. Source:
  [[2026-01-13 The rise of cheques as Kenya's preferred payment method]]. See [[dpi.pay]].
- **2025-10-08** — **After Access 2022 Ghana report (RIA).** [[research-ict-africa]] published
  *Digital Africa post the pandemic: Ghana report* (**[[after-access]]** series), comparing
  2022 survey data with the 2012/2018 waves on ICT development and digital inequality;
  recommends updating ICT4AD and the National Telecommunications Policy and finalising the
  Digital Economy Policy. Source:
  [[2025-10-08 Digital Africa post the pandemic Ghana report]].
  See [[include.divides]], [[include.access]], [[dpi.pay]], [[gov.policy]], [[infra.connect]].
- **2025-08-04** *(analysis)* — **Ghana a joint-leader on Kariuki's DPI-maturity rubric.** Samora
  Kariuki's six-country read scores **Ghana ~40/60 (joint-top with South Africa)**: the
  **[[ghana-card]]** as sole financial *identifier*, fully interoperable mobile-money/bank payments
  via **GhIPSS**, offset by a weak data-exchange layer and the e-levy as a cost barrier. His later
  "payments end-game" essay lists GhIPSS among the instant switches commoditising processing. One
  analyst's rubric, attributed to Kariuki — not an authoritative index. Sources:
  [[2025-08-04 93 - Ecosystem Debt or Generative Platforms A Case for Digital Public Infrastructure]],
  [[2026-02-23 111 - Winning But Losing - The Payments End Game]]. See [[dpi.pay]], [[dpi.exchange]].
- **2016–2017** *(background; publ. 2021-09-13)* — **Health-data recording burden.** A
  five-country WHO study ([[rhis-recording-burden-5country]]) found Ghana's PHC facilities
  used ~**16 registers** (the fewest of the five) but carried the **highest per-worker
  monthly reporting burden — ~15 hours** (tied-top with Tanzania), with recording consuming
  ~**31%** of consultation time. Source:
  [[2021-09-13 The burden of recording and reporting health data in primary health care facilities in five low- and lower-middle income countries]].
  See [[dpi.mis]], [[data.statistics]], [[gov.standards]].
