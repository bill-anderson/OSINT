# Central African Republic × GovTech / e-Government

In barely a year (Mar 2025 → Feb 2026) CAR stood up **three parallel e-government platforms**,
all under the **[[pnd-2024-2028-central-african-republic]]** digitalisation agenda and mostly
donor-financed — an ambitious dematerialisation push in a country where ~90% of people are
offline, raising the obvious question of reach.

## What we know

**Three platforms, three owners:**

- **e-Tax** (launched **24 Mar 2025**) — online tax declaration and payment, four modules
  including registration for the unique identification number (NIU); run by **[[dgid-central-african-republic]]**
  (tax administration). Prompted by a Jan 2025 **IMF** mission urging faster public-finance
  digitalisation. Source: [[2025-03-25 La Centrafrique lance officiellement e-Tax, sa plateforme de paiement d'impots en ligne]].
- **"Gouvernemental Centralisé"** unified services portal (launched **10 Dec 2025**) — a single
  entry point for administrative tasks, request/complaint tracking and access to official
  documents; **[[world-bank]]**-financed (Public Sector Governance Programme), overseen by the
  Ministry of Finance, implemented by **EY Tunisie Medianet**; planned expansion to health,
  education and tax. Source: [[2025-12-12 Central African Republic Launches Unified Digital Portal for All Public Services]].
- **DUNIA** (launch **23 Feb 2026**) — **[[mepci-central-african-republic]]**'s platform to
  dematerialise administrative procedures, launched under President **[[faustin-archange-touadera]]**'s
  patronage. Source: [[2026-02-11 La digitalisation des services publics et la gouvernance numerique du PND 2024-2028]].

**A fourth platform, on the revenue side: Pata Polélé (2026-04).** [[dpi.pay]] Where the three above
dematerialise *services*, **Pata Polélé** (Sango: *pata* = money, *polélé* = clear/open) digitises
**public revenue collection** — the "mini-recettes," the administrative fees and duties levied across
some **twenty revenue-generating ministries**. A harmonisation and restitution workshop ran
**14–16 April 2026** at the Hôtel Kitika, Bangui, with **Justice, Finance and Interior** as pilot
ministries. It replaces cash payment and manual *quittances* with digital receipts, allows traffic
fines to be recorded by officers and settled online via mobile money, and covers issuance of the
*certificat de nationalité*. It is **EU-financed and delivered by Expertise France** — a **€17.5m**
programme over four years, of which **€8.5m** sits in the "Justice pour l'Ouest 1" component — and was
built by a **Tunisian expert** with the **Office National de l'Informatique (ONI)**. The revenue-side
counterpart **Patapaye** (civil-servant salary payment by mobile money) was already running by 2024.
Source: [[2026-04-18-pata-polele-digitalisation-paiements-ndjonisango]]. (Provenance established by the
reconcile pass, 2026-07-20 — the claim previously reached the wiki only through a dropped mirror.)

**The World Bank project underneath much of this — now sourced to its own documents.**
**P174620, the CAR Public Sector Digital Governance Project (PSDGP)**, is an **SDR 25.1m / US$35m** IDA
grant, appraised **7 Mar 2022** and approved **5 May 2022**, closing **31 Dec 2026**, implemented by the
**Ministry of Finance and Budget**, with **SDR 4.8m / US$6.4m** of additional financing appraised
**21 May 2025** for a mining-governance component (total **US$41.4m**). Its stated objective is to
"(i) improve efficiency and transparency in key areas of public resources management, and (ii) lay
foundations for a trusted and inclusive digital government."

As of **15 April 2025** it had disbursed **US$11.35m (33%)** and was rated *Moderately Satisfactory* on both
PDO progress and implementation, with **five of six PDO indicators on track** at the **September 2024**
midterm review. Recorded achievements (as of the **May 2025** paper) are: modernised customs systems; a
payroll clean-up removing **more than 2,000 ghost workers**; mobile-money salary payment for **1,702 civil
servants** in regions without banking services outside Bangui; implementation of the integrated FMIS; the
enactment of **two laws — cybersecurity and data protection**; strengthened capacity at the regulator
**[[arcep-central-african-republic]]**; a cross-agency working group on identification and civil
registration; and civil-servant training (a third of them women). The Bank also records **persistent
procurement failure**: planned dates not respected, weak document quality, evaluation committees not held on
time, and STEP not regularly updated — a candid counterweight to the *Moderately Satisfactory* headline.
It is **distinct from [[cardip]]**, the regional six-country program. Sources:
[[2022-03-07-worldbank-pad4661-car-public-sector-digital-governance]],
[[2025-05-21-worldbank-project-paper-af-car-public-sector-digital-governance]].

**What the project documents do *not* establish.** The wiki previously read the **"Gouvernemental
Centralisé" portal** as a P174620 deliverable. **Neither project document names that portal**, and the May
2025 paper's enumeration of Component 2 achievements does not include a unified public-services portal. The
portal's World Bank funding is asserted only by the launch report
([[2025-12-12 Central African Republic Launches Unified Digital Portal for All Public Services]]), which
names a "Public Sector Governance Programme" without a project ID. The attribution is **plausible but
unconfirmed** — sub-component **2.3 "Facilitating access to select public services" (US$3.5m)** is the
obvious budget home, and the portal launched **10 Dec 2025**, *after* the May 2025 paper, so its absence
from that achievements list is expected rather than disconfirming. Recorded here as **unestablished as of
2026-07-20**, not as a fact. The April 2025 état-civil diagnostic **is** attributed to P174620, but by the
diagnostic's own reporting rather than by these documents (→ [[caf--dpi-id]]).

Stated aims are common: cut office visits and paperwork, speed processing, improve transparency
and the business climate, reduce corruption. Whether the three converge or fragment — and how
they reach a population that is ~90% offline with ~14% electricity access — is the open
question. See [[dpi.govtech]], [[gov.policy]], [[CAF]].
