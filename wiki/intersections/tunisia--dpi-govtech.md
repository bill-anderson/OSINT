# Tunisia × E-Government & Interoperability

Tunisia treats digital government as a **whole-of-state programme** — a cabinet-monitored portfolio inside
the 2026–2030 Development Plan, with an Estonian-lineage interoperability backbone now extending from
government to the business economy. The framing (a minister's "historical necessity", a "regional digital
hub") is ambitious; the substance is a real, EGDI-anchored delivery machine.

## The portfolio — cabinet-level monitoring

- **192 projects, 2026 as the launch year (2026-02-13).** A ministerial council at the Kasbah palace,
  chaired by Head of Government **Sarra Zaafrani Zenzri**, put **192 digital-transformation projects
  under national follow-up (as of Feb 2026)** and declared 2026 the year of the *"démarrage effectif de
  la digitalisation complète"* of the administration. Delivered by end-2025, per Communication
  Technologies Minister **Sofiene Hemissi**: the **single administrative-services portal**, remote
  vehicle-tax payment, the **electronic fiscal stamp**, the **[[digital-hospital-project-tunisia|digital
  hospital]]** (phase 1), **Najda.tn**, the national project-monitoring system, the **national
  interconnection platform** ([[uxp-tunisia]], phase 1), the community-enterprise register (phase 1),
  online student ID-card applications, the ***Taamir*** building-permit platform, the cross-border
  circulation card, social-affairs interconnection services, **"Easy Export"** and the **"Nationalité"**
  citizenship system. PM directives: expand e-payments, interlink citizen services, embed cybersecurity,
  AI and **open data**. See [[gov.policy]].
  **The governing instrument is the plan, not a strategy.** Zenzri stated the State has adopted its
  digital-transformation vision *"dans le cadre du plan de développement 2026-2030"* — so the successor
  to the lapsed **[[digital-tunisia-2020-2025]]** is a **chapter of a whole-of-government five-year plan
  coordinated by the Ministry of Economy and Planning**, not a renewed sectoral ICT strategy owned by the
  communication-technologies ministry. A change of instrument type, and what the EU ambassador loosely
  called Tunisia's "National Digital Strategy" in the Jan 2026 EBRD announcement. *As of 2026-07-20 no
  extension, repeal or outcomes report for Digital Tunisia 2020-2025 is held.*
  Source: [[2026-02-13-tunisie-conseil-ministeriel-digitalisation-complete]] (the Head of Government's
  own portal — replaced a TechAfrica News re-report of 2026-02-16 as the record of this council).

## The interoperability backbone — UXP goes to the business economy

- **UXP Phase 2 (May 2026).** **[[cybernetica]]** signed a Phase-2 contract (with the Ministries of
  Communication Technologies and Economy & Planning) extending **[[uxp-tunisia]]** — the X-Road-lineage
  Unified eXchange Platform — from the five Phase-1 institutions to the **business-environment bodies**:
  RNE, TIA, APII, CEPEX, the Ministry of Finance and the **[[central-bank-of-tunisia|CBT]]**. Adds
  high-availability modules, integrates government databases, and **explicitly targets a higher UN EGDI
  ranking**. Funded by **[[ebrd]] + SECO**; PwC local partner; completion **2027**. The platform moving
  from a five-ministry pilot to the plumbing under economic services. See [[dpi.exchange]],
  [[gov.standards]], [[geopol.eu]]. Source: [[2026-05-14-cybernetica-national-interoperability-platform-phase2]].

## The shop window

- **Tunisia Digital Summit, 10th edition (Apr 2026).** Minister **Sofiene Hemissi** framed digital
  transformation as **"no longer a choice but a historical necessity"** and set the goal of a **regional
  digital hub** radiating across the Maghreb, Africa and the Mediterranean — pledging continued legal/
  regulatory development, especially on **AI and data protection**. ~2,000 experts, 80 exhibitors; Tunisia
  entered the 15th Startup World Cup (San Francisco, Nov 2026) for the first time. See [[tech.innovate]].
  Source: [[2026-04-22-assabah-tunisia-digital-summit-10]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: Tunisia's e-gov build is coherent and state-owned in the ways that matter — a national
  interoperability layer it governs (UXP under its own central authority), a cabinet-tracked project book,
  and an explicit EGDI target it already scores well on (0.6935, 3rd in Africa; see [[TUN]] hub). The
  reliance is European (Estonian tech, EBRD/SECO money, GIZ capacity) rather than Gulf or Chinese here, and
  the sovereignty pitch is real — but note the recurring gap the ID chunk flags: the same summit that
  promises AI and a "digital hub" is still *pledging* the data-protection framework, not enforcing it.
