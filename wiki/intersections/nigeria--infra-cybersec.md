# Nigeria × Cybersecurity

Nigeria is **among Africa's most-targeted cyber environments** — **4,000+ attacks weekly** — even as
its police cyber-unit tops continental rankings. The policy response is hardening (a telecoms cyber-
resilience framework, a National Cybersecurity Centre) but is shadowed by a **dual-use Cybercrimes
Act** used against dissent and by a **data-offshoring** dependence that is itself framed as a security
risk.

## Threat scale and the offshoring risk

- **4,000+ cyberattacks weekly**, with **~₦12bn** in estimated 2024 losses (NDPC, IoT West Africa
  2026). **~90% of Nigeria's data is hosted outside Africa**, implying **~$850m/yr** on foreign cloud
  — an economic-leakage and sovereignty concern feeding the localisation push (see
  [[nigeria--gov-protect]], [[nigeria--infra-store]]). "Data ... has become critical infrastructure"
  (Vincent Olatunji), for a digital economy put at **$18.3bn (~20% of GDP)**. Source:
  [[2026-05-06-cybercrime-n12bn-90pct-data-offshore]].
- The **financial sector alone lost ₦53.4bn** to cybercrime in 2024. Source:
  [[2025-08-01-cybercrime-act-misuse-n1-1trn]].

## Enforcement — the police cyber-unit

- The **Nigeria Police Force National Cybercrime Centre (NPF-NCCC)** was named **INTERPOL's Best
  Cybercrime Unit in Africa for 2024** (1st of 54). In 2024 it recovered **₦8.82bn + 115,237 USDT +
  $84,000**, arrested **751+ suspects**, and seized 685 devices (incl. four Starlink units), 16 houses
  and 39 plots. Source: [[2025-01-09-nigeria-police-cybercrime-unit-interpol]].

## The Cybercrimes Act — dual-use concern

- Despite the **Cybercrimes (Amendment) Act 2024**, civil-society lawyers argue enforcement agencies
  **misapply the Act to suppress dissent** (harassing critics) rather than pursue cyber threats. SAN
  Kunle Edun notes most such cases collapse because complainants (officials, wealthy individuals)
  never testify — "abuse of power." See [[gov.legislate]]. Source:
  [[2025-08-01-cybercrime-act-misuse-n1-1trn]].

## Institutions and frameworks

- **Cyber Resilience Framework (CRF-NCS, Feb 2026).** The **[[ncc]]** now requires major operators
  (MTN, Airtel, Globacom) to report significant incidents to its **CSIRT within four hours**; a
  12-month compliance runway. It treats communications as **critical national infrastructure**, under
  the National Cybersecurity Policy & Strategy. Source: [[2026-03-05-ncc-4-hour-cyber-incident-reporting-crf]].
- **National Cybersecurity Centre, BUK (May 2025).** **[[nitda]]** commissioned a National
  Cybersecurity Centre at Bayero University Kano — "a national asset" for cyber education, research and
  talent. See [[capacity.training]]. Source: [[2025-05-28-nitda-cybersecurity-centre-kano-buk]].
- **Cyber-insurance market.** NAICOM is pressing local insurers to build **cyber-insurance products**,
  working with [[nitda]] and [[ndpc]]. Source: [[2025-02-27-local-cyber-insurance-products]].
- **Finland MoU (Mar 2026).** Nigeria and Finland signed a digitalisation/innovation MoU prioritising
  **cybersecurity**, digital governance and infrastructure — building on Helsinki talks around the Data
  Exchange Platform and Finnish (Finnvera/Finnfund) participation in Project BRIDGE. See [[geopol.eu]].
  Source: [[2026-03-23-finland-nigeria-cyber-digitalisation-mou]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: cybersecurity and data-localisation are being fused into one argument — offshored data
  as a *national-security* exposure, not just an economic one — the same logic driving the CBN/NDPC
  onshoring mandates. The Cybercrimes Act shows the flip side: security powers turned inward.
