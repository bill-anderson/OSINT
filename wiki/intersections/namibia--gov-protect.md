# Namibia × Data Protection

Namibia has no data-protection statute — a bill **~12 years** in the making has still not been
enacted — yet the state is already exercising data sovereignty as a **licensing veto** against a
foreign operator. Collection and gatekeeping run ahead of the citizen's protection.

## A 12-year bill, still short on rights (2025-2026)

- Namibia's **Data Protection Bill** — **~12 years** in the making — was set for tabling
  (Sept 2025) and, by mid-2026, was still being finalised alongside a **Cybercrime Bill**.
- The **IPPR** (Frederico Links) warns it may **fall short on data-subject rights**: a rights
  section was removed in 2020 and seemingly not reintroduced, and the public has not seen the
  latest draft.
- The gap is not academic: it sits under a live biometric **e-ID** rollout — statute lagging the
  DPI it should govern. See [[namibia-data-protection-bill]], [[namibia--dpi-id]],
  [[gov.legislate]]. Sources:
  [[2025-08-17-data-protection-bill-returns-to-parliament-namibiansun]],
  [[2026-07-14-namibia-pushes-cyber-security-reforms-itweb]].

## Data sovereignty as a licensing veto (2026-03)

Namibia's **[[cran]]** denied **[[starlink]]** an operating and spectrum licence, meeting only
3 of 6 statutory criteria. Two failures were governance ones — **ownership** (100% foreign,
breaching the 51%-Namibian rule, no ministerial exemption) and **national security / data
sovereignty**, CRAN holding that 100% foreign ownership undermines "jurisdiction, enforceability
of compliance obligations, and… effective oversight." Data sovereignty deployed as a hard
gatekeeping tool against a foreign operator (cf. the same fight in [[ZAF]]). See
[[infra.connect]], [[gov.legislate]], [[sovereignty]]. Source:
[[2026-03-26 Starlink blocked from operating in Namibia]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: the state can already refuse a licence on data-sovereignty grounds while its
  citizens have no enforceable statutory data rights — sovereignty asserted outward before
  protection is granted inward.
