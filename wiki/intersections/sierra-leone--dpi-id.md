# Sierra Leone × Digital Identity

The **NIN** issued by the **[[ncra]]** is the spine of Sierra Leone's digital state — payments,
banking, agriculture and public services wire onto it. Coverage headlines are strong (**93%
registration**, 2025) but **card issuance, affordability and last-mile human intermediation** are the
binding realities. The build is deliberately **open-standards** (MOSIP, W3C verifiable credentials).

## The spine and its gaps

Foundational facts (see [[SLE]] hub for sources): **6.4m NIN holders (~8 in 10)** toward an 8.8m target,
but **<0.5m physical ID cards issued (2024)**; the card is **mandatory** for movement and services since
Jan 2025; the new digital card costs **~165 Leones (~$8)** — **seven times** the old paper card. Coverage
≠ issuance ≠ affordability — each dated metric kept distinct.

## Open-standards build

- **MOSIP brownfield migration.** Sierra Leone is **migrating enrolled records onto open-source MOSIP**
  (piloted early 2024) — a *brownfield* migration where, NCRA Deputy Director Moses Vibbie notes, a
  replacement must at least match the legacy system's service delivery (phased rollouts won't satisfy
  stakeholders already relying on it). Situated alongside Uganda (28m records migrated) and Nigeria
  (~120m, the largest). See [[gov.standards]]. Source: [[2026-02-19-mosip-brownfield-sierra-leone-migration]].
- The prospective national platform pairs MOSIP-style openness with **W3C verifiable credentials** and
  national ownership (Bhutan-NDI/Sign template — [[sierra-leone-digital-id-mou]], see [[SLE]] hub).

## "Brokers of citizenship" — the human last mile

- An ethnographic study (Laura Lambert) documents the **"invisible yet inevitable" intermediaries** —
  chiefs, teachers, family, document brokers and **justices of the peace** — who help the
  underdocumented enrol. Those lacking papers obtain an **affidavit** (small informal fee) from a JP,
  which NCRA generally trusts at face value ("what you put on it is what we now believe in"). The JP is
  a **colonial-era institution** that can both reinforce discriminatory ideas of belonging *and* keep
  people from statelessness. Lambert's conclusion: **"digital identities do not abolish intermediaries"**
  — the state depends on their work (relevant to SDG 16.9). See [[include.access]]. Source:
  [[2026-03-18-brokers-of-citizenship-digital-id]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: the open-standards choice (MOSIP, W3C VCs, national ownership) is a deliberate bid to
  own the ID stack rather than rent it — the clearest sovereignty marker in Sierra Leone's DPI.
- `colonialism`: yet the system's last mile runs on **colonial-era brokers** (justices of the peace) and
  a card priced out of reach for the poorest — a reminder that a "modern" digital ID inherits and
  depends on older structures of recognition rather than replacing them.
