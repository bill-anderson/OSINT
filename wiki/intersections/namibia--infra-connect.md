# Namibia × Connectivity

Namibia's connectivity posture cuts both ways: the regulator has refused a 100%-foreign-owned Starlink on data-sovereignty grounds, while the ICT ministry pushes a free-access counter-move to close the divide from the demand side.

## What we know

**Sovereignty vs access, both ways (2026-01/03).** [[cran]] **declined** [[starlink]]'s comprehensive licence and spectrum on **100% foreign ownership** (no Namibian shareholding), mirroring South Africa and leaving a 90-day reconsideration window (Starlink now licensed in **27** African countries). On access, ICT minister **[[emma-theofelus]]** committed **free internet for 7 years** to schools/clinics under **N$80m / nine RAN towers**, while [[cran]] proposed **tax breaks on 4G devices** after MTC reported **<50%** 4G-tower utilisation in Kunene — coverage without affordable devices is not transformation. See [[data.satellite]], [[gov.legislate]], [[include.access]], [[include.divides]].

## The Starlink saga (2025-06 → 2026-06)

A protracted, twice-decided refusal — the clearest African case of **local-ownership + data-sovereignty** rules
being used to keep a 100%-foreign LEO operator out.

- **Ownership-exemption stage (2025-06).** [[starlink]]'s bid to retain majority foreign ownership went to a
  **ministerial exemption review** (Min. [[emma-theofelus]]) ~9 months before the formal denial. Source:
  [[2025-06-30-theofelus-starlink-ownership-exemption-under-review-namibiansun]].
- **The denial (gazetted 23 Mar 2026).** **[[cran]]** declined both Starlink's **Class Comprehensive
  Telecommunications Service Licence (ECS/ECNS)** and its **Spectrum licence** (Downlink 10.7–12.7 GHz / Uplink
  14.0–14.5 GHz). Starlink met only **3 of 6** criteria, failing on **ownership/control** (0% Namibian-owned vs
  the **51%** rule; no exemption requested), **compliance history** (prior unlicensed operation) and **national
  security / oversight**. CRAN concurrently approved a transfer for **Q-Kon Telecom Namibia**; s.31 of the
  Communications Act allowed a **90-day reconsideration**. Sources:
  [[2026-03-24-cran-rejects-starlink-licence-namibian]],
  [[2026-03-24-cran-rejects-starlink-ownership-security-namibiansun]].
- **Starlink pushes back (Mar 2026).** Starlink called the decision "disappointing" and **misleading**,
  vowing to appeal; director Ben MacWilliams cited **98.6%** public-consultation support, thousands of deposits,
  a three-year effort to establish a local company, tax compliance "in 164 markets," and claimed CRAN
  misunderstood how Starlink "protects data sovereignty." Min. Theofelus and CRAN chair Mufeti held the line
  ("met only half"). Sources: [[2026-03-27-starlink-to-appeal-namibia-licence-rejection-namibian]],
  [[2026-03-31-starlink-says-cran-denial-unjustified-namibiansun]].
- **Reconsideration rejected — the denial upheld (2026-06-24).** CRAN (CEO Emilia Nghikembua) received **624
  reconsideration requests**; **622** failed the legal threshold and **2** were heard on merits but showed "no
  new evidence nor a material error." The **March denial stands**; Nghikembua stressed the issue "has never been
  whether satellite technology is beneficial" but statutory compliance. Starlink filed a **fresh application on
  8 June 2026**. Source: [[2026-06-24-namibian-regulator-rejects-starlink-again-connectingafrica]].

## Telecom market & 5G

- **A private challenger enters (2025-09).** **[[paratus]]** launched **Namibia's first privately-owned mobile
  network** (2 Sep 2025; LTE + 5G; data-only), challenging the state duopoly — **[[mtc-namibia|MTC]]** (majority
  state-owned; **2m+** subscribers / **>90%** share) and **[[telecom-namibia|Telecom Namibia]]**'s TN Mobile
  (~336,000). Paratus (Namibia its "power base") has invested **N$1.42bn since 2018** plus **N$600m** on the new
  network (built with **Nokia** + **Cerillion**). Sources:
  [[2025-09-04-paratus-launches-namibia-first-private-mobile-network-connectingafrica]],
  [[2025-08-19-paratus-to-launch-mobile-network-namibiansun]].
- **Towers into the underserved (2025-08).** **25 PowerCom towers** (**N$40m**, with Telecom Namibia) were
  inaugurated across **nine regions** (symbolic launch at Omusati) — part of the drive to raise internet
  penetration **from ~50% to 90% by 2030** (digital-tech access 28%→70%; 100% service digitisation by 2029/30).
  See [[digital.rural]], [[include.access]]. Source:
  [[2025-08-24-namibia-25-new-network-towers-powercom-namibiansun]].
- **5G against a density constraint (2026-02).** Namibia's **3.76 people/km²** (one of the world's lowest) makes
  fibre backhaul and tower rollout expensive and leaves rural areas on 2G/3G; the **5G** rollout is therefore
  **phased and city-first** (SDN, virtualisation, edge, site upgrades). It sits under the OPM's **Digital
  Government Strategic Roadmap 2024–2026** ("Digital First Services for All"; digital ID to ≥25% by 2026), the
  **NDS (2025)** and **NDP6** (internet 53%→90% by 2030). See [[gov.policy]], [[dpi.id]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-10-towards-5g-enabled-digitalized-namibia-2026-telecomreviewafrica]].
- **Regional backbone.** Telecom Namibia's **SARSSy / Equiano** integration with Angola Telecom + SATCAB (MSA
  signed 12 Jun 2026, 1 Tbps) extends the sovereignty-minded regional-corridor play — see
  [[telecom-namibia-angola-deals]].
