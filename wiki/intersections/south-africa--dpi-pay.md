# South Africa × Digital Payments (`ZAF` × `dpi.pay`)

South Africa is opening its bank-only National Payment System to non-bank PSPs through activity-based licensing under [[sarb]] Vision 2025, while a broader 2026 fintech rulebook — an AI-in-finance framework, crypto licensing and the COFI Bill — builds the scaffolding beneath the payments reform.

## What we know

- **[[ZAF]] Opening the National Payment System (2026 reform).** South Africa is opening its
  bank-only NPS to **non-bank PSPs** via activity-based licensing (7 categories) under [[sarb]]
  Vision 2025 — a 2025 Exemption Notice/Draft Directive ahead of the legislative amendment.
  **PayShap** (instant payments, live 2023) crossed **>100m transactions** in year one, raised
  its per-txn limit to R50,000, and will integrate into third-party wallets; cross-border via
  SADC TCIB on ISO 20022. [[sars]] (which crossed **R2tn** annual revenue) is driving a single
  biometric digital ID and partnering [[sarb]] on a cost-effective instant-payment system to
  cut cash and widen inclusion. See [[gov.legislate]], [[dpi.id]], [[finance.new]]. Sources:
  [[2026-03-17 South Africa's National Payment System PSP licences and the 2026 payments reform]],
  [[2026-04-03 Say goodbye to SARS tax numbers in South Africa]].
- **[[ZAF]] South Africa — the 2026 fintech-regulation agenda.** A year-ahead outlook (Webber
  Wentzel) maps SA's pending fintech rulebook: an **AI-in-finance regulatory framework**
  expected 2026 (after the FSCA/Prudential Authority AI survey), the **[[sarb]]** Payments
  Ecosystem Modernisation opening the NPS to non-banks, **300 crypto-asset-service-provider
  licences** issued by end-2025, "**2026 the year of stablecoins**" for cross-border flows, and
  the pending **COFI Bill** consolidating conduct rules (open finance, BNPL) — the scaffolding
  under the payments reform. See [[gov.legislate]], [[gov.policy]], [[finance.new]], [[popia]].
  Source: [[2026-01-30 2026 fintech year ahead]].

## PayShap becomes a national utility — and the fee it can't shake (2025–26)

- **Central bank co-owns the rail.** The **[[sarb]]** took a **50% stake in [[payinc|PayInc]]** (ex-
  BankservAfrica, Oct 2025), and Godongwana's Feb-2026 Budget recast it as **"open, shared national payment
  infrastructure"** — treating instant payments as a **public utility, not a bank product**. **[[payshap]]**
  reached **~R403bn / 461m transactions** (Mar 2026), then **6m users / 905m transactions** (May 2026), and
  is **pivoting from P2P to merchants/e-commerce**. Sources:
  [[2026-04-02-payshap-r100bn-national-payment-utility]], [[2026-05-21-payshap-pivots-to-merchants]].
- **The conflict-of-interest brake.** Adoption lags Pix/UPI because the **banks that own PayInc price the
  rail against themselves** — fees **R1–R10**, Discovery **0.5% (cap R35)**, GoTyme **free**; **no USSD** for
  feature phones. Whether the merchant pivot works "will depend on what PayInc and the Reserve Bank can
  extract from the participating banks — the same institutions whose pricing has been holding it back."
  Sources: [[2026-03-26-payshap-conflict-of-interest]], [[2026-07-14-why-sa-banks-charge-instant-payments]].
- **QR+ — one code for all (Jul 2026).** The SARB gazetted a **national interoperable QR standard (QR+,
  v1.2)** under Payments Ecosystem Modernisation — one merchant QR usable by any participating app; PayShap
  is the **first rail** to adopt it; cohort-one is the eight major banks. Pradeep Maharaj: **62% of South
  Africans still use cash** daily. Foundational infrastructure, not a consumer product. Source:
  [[2026-07-13-sarb-qr-plus-checkout]].

## The reform's legal and monetary frame

- **The draft NPS law (2025).** SARB is drafting the **biggest payments reform in a generation** — a **2030
  strategy** elevating payments modernisation to a core pillar (with price/financial stability), estimating
  **+0.5% GDP** and attacking a **~R30bn/yr cash-management cost**. Activity-based licensing will let
  **non-banks issue e-money and access clearing/settlement directly** (pre-funded settlement model), via an
  exemption notice and the forthcoming **NPS Bill** — undercutting the big-five banks' monopoly (Governor
  Kganyago). Source: [[2025-07-21-sarb-payments-reform-draft-law]].
- **Payments-first, CBDC later.** Deputy Governor **Rashad Cassim** (BIS) set out the sequencing: **SAMOS**
  RTGS (**~R584bn/day**, real-time since 1998); DLT experiments **Project Khokha 1 (2018)** and **Khokha 2
  (2022)** (tokenised securities, a wholesale CBDC, a stablecoin-like token); and a published position that a
  **retail CBDC has "no compelling case"** yet — **fix the rails first**, especially as stablecoins pressure
  monetary sovereignty. Sources: [[2026-06-16-cassim-money-in-motion-sarb]], [[2026-07-02-sarb-payments-first-over-cbdc]].
- **AI enters prudential supervision (Jul 2026).** The SARB **Prudential Authority**'s 2025/26 report
  embeds **AI, cloud, cyber and data-offshoring** into bank supervision — new cyber/cloud standards, a **first
  nationwide AI-adoption survey** (barriers: regulatory uncertainty, AI skills, governance), as the big five
  scale AI from pilots into fraud/risk/lending. See [[tech.ai]], [[gov.standards]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-16-banks-stricter-ai-scrutiny-regulator]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: South Africa is building a **state-anchored payments commons** — a central bank that now
  co-owns the instant rail, sets a national QR standard, opens clearing to non-banks, and deliberately
  **sequences CBDC behind fixing the everyday rails**. That is a coherent, sovereignty-minded design. The
  binding constraint is not the state but the **incumbent banks**: they own the rail and price it to protect
  card/EFT revenue, so a system built for inclusion still charges the poor to move their own money, and 62%
  stay on cash. Whether "national utility" governance can override owner-bank incentives is the open
  question — the SARB's leverage over its own co-shareholders.
