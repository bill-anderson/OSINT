# Tunisia × Data Protection & Legal Frameworks

This is the guardrail half of Tunisia's "**capability ahead of safeguards**" story (see [[tunisia--dpi-id]],
[[TUN]] hub). Mandatory Mobile ID, a centralising health identifier and now blanket e-invoicing are all
arriving while the governing data-protection statute is still the **2004 law**
([[organic-law-2004-63-tunisia]]) — and 2026 is the year parliament finally moves to replace it.

## The data-protection overhaul (Feb 2026)

- **A 123-article organic law in committee.** The **[[assembly-of-peoples-representatives-tunisia|ARP]]**'s
  Rights and Freedoms Committee (chair **Thabet Abed**) reviewed the draft opinion on a proposed **organic
  law on personal data protection** — **123 articles across 6 titles** (general provisions; processing
  principles; data-subject rights; processing systems; the protection authority; penalties + transitional).
  Aim: a **modern framework aligned with international standards**, replacing the 2004 law. Source:
  [[2026-02-20-africanmanager-data-protection-law-parliament]].
- **What it targets.** Sponsors cite an **institutional gap** — the **limited role of the
  [[instance-nationale-protection-donnees-personnelles-tunisia|INPDP]]** — and the **absence of clear rules
  for sensitive domains: the press, surveillance cameras, AI/algorithms, and cross-border data transfers**.
  The bill introduces a **Data Protection Officer** role, obliges **public institutions** to obtain
  declarations/authorisations for any processing, and shifts enforcement toward **financial penalties** via
  a **dedicated sanctions department**. Chairman Abed framed it against "the growing influence of algorithms
  and surveillance technologies." See [[tech.ai]], [[gov.legislate]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-20-africanmanager-data-protection-law-parliament]].

## E-invoicing — the data-protection tension made concrete

- **Article 53 amendment (Feb 2026).** A joint session (Finance & Budget + Administration/Digitisation/
  Governance & Anti-Corruption committees) debated bill **2026/012** amending **Article 53** of the 2026
  Finance Law, which **generalises compulsory e-invoicing to all service providers** — liberal professions,
  telecom operators, insurers, hotels, transport, small trades — **from 1 Jan 2026, regardless of invoice
  value or firm size**. 12 MPs sponsored the amendment (single article); the Finance Ministry had already
  promised (13 Jan) **flexible application to avoid disruption**. Committees heard the Ministry of
  Communication Technologies, the DG of **[[ance-tunisia|ANCE]]** (e-certification), Finance, and Tunisie
  Trade Net. Source: [[2026-02-10-assabah-parliament-e-invoicing-law-amendment]].
- **Why it belongs here.** Parliament's own reasoning ties the two threads together: the memo notes **no
  unified national e-invoicing system yet exists** to absorb the diversity of services, and that mass
  e-invoicing **"raises serious personal- and professional-data-protection issues."** The amendment
  therefore **mandates a 30-day joint ministerial report** assessing technical/organisational readiness,
  **the adequacy of data-protection systems and their compliance with DP legislation**, and a realistic
  phased timeline — i.e. the state pausing a mandatory-digitisation measure specifically to check it against
  data-protection law. See [[dpi.govtech]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-10-assabah-parliament-e-invoicing-law-amendment]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: Tunisia is doing the right thing late. The 123-article bill is a serious, EU-standard
  instrument that names exactly the right risks (surveillance cameras, AI, cross-border transfers, a weak
  regulator) — but it is still *a bill*, and the mandatory-digitisation push (ID, health, invoicing) is
  *already live*. The e-invoicing episode is the tell in both directions: parliament is alert enough to
  halt and demand a data-protection readiness report, yet the underlying measure was legislated to take
  effect before that check existed. The open question for the wiki is timing — whether the safeguards land
  in 2026 before the compulsory-ID and centralised-registry build-out makes them retrofit rather than
  design.
