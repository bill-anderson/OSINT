# Sierra Leone × Connectivity

Sierra Leone has spent **~$400m** on telecom infrastructure yet still faces a **60% usage gap** —
**20.8% internet penetration (Oct 2025**, 1.85m users, **7.02m offline)** — with affordability, not
coverage, the binding constraint. The response mixes **regional interconnection**, **satellite**, and a
**state-telco rescue**.

## The affordability gap

- **$400m invested, 60% usage gap (2026).** The national fibre backbone reaches **14 of 16 districts**,
  but usage lags; Minister Salima Bah called for infrastructure-sharing and industry accountability to
  cut costs. Source: [[2026-05-05-sierra-leone-400m-telecom-usage-gap]].
- **Among SSA's most expensive data.** A partisan-but-sourced critique notes **1GB ≈ Le 18,000–25,000
  (2025–26)** — roughly **tripled since 2018** — leaving Sierra Leone in the **top-5 most expensive**
  data markets in sub-Saharan Africa (A4AI 2024); the 2021 "Smart Learning" school-connectivity
  programme is documented as having largely failed (modems "lasted two months," prepaid-SIM buses).
  Attributed opposition analysis, not neutral fact. See [[include.divides]], [[capacity.literacy]].
  Source: [[2026-01-14-broken-signals-ict-critique-forumnews]].

## Regional interconnection — the Guinea axis and ECOWAS roaming

- **SOGEB–Leonecom fibre (Jun 2025).** Guinea's backbone operator SOGEB and Sierra Leone's **Leonecom**
  interconnected their fibre networks at **Pamelap** (border), **42.5 Gbps → 100 Gbps by 2026**, under
  the **Guinea–Sierra Leone Digital Pact 2024** — cheaper transit and redundancy (Orange/MTN gain),
  with extension toward Liberia discussed. See [[dpi.exchange]], [[GIN]]. Source:
  [[2025-06-23-sogeb-leonecom-fibre-interconnection]].
- **Second submarine cable via Guinea (2025).** An MoCTI delegation assessed a **second subsea-cable
  landing** (feasibility, PPP models) for redundancy — a **$15m** investment cited at 2026 stakeholder
  meetings. Source: [[2025-07-11-sierra-leone-second-submarine-cable]].
- **Cross-border roaming.** A **Liberia–Gambia–Sierra Leone** ECOWAS free-roaming MoU took effect **2
  May 2025** (home SIM at local rates), and **[[natca]]** signed a **free-roaming deal with Guinea** at
  **CAR-15 / Transform Africa 2025** (Smart Africa "Single Digital Market") — the second SLE–Guinea
  telecom pact in a year. See [[gov.regional]], [[LBR]], [[GMB]], [[GIN]]. Sources:
  [[2025-02-05-west-african-roaming-mou-lbr-gmb-sle]], [[2025-11-17-natca-guinea-free-roaming]].

## Satellite

- **Starlink.** Licensed and operating since **June 2024** (2 years by 2026), with **three local
  resellers**; via **ELCOME SL** the government connected **12 universities** (National Broadband
  Strategy 2023–2028), extending to vocational schools — enabling remote clinics, schools and farmers.
  See [[data.satellite]], [[digital.rural]], [[capacity.training]]. Sources:
  [[2025-01-31-starlink-university-internet-elcome]], [[2026-03-13-natca-spectrum-pricing-mwc-barcelona]].

## Regulation and the state telco

- **Spectrum policy (MWC 2026).** [[natca]] DG **Amara Brewah** pressed for **15–20-year licences**,
  **staggered spectrum fees**, **NGSO** guidelines, technology-neutral frameworks and an **LS Telcom**
  Automated Spectrum Management System — prioritising **low-band for rural** and UADF solar community
  sites. See [[gov.policy]]. Source: [[2026-03-13-natca-spectrum-pricing-mwc-barcelona]].
- **Sierratel relaunch as an MVNO (Apr 2026).** The government appointed **[[africell]]** as investment
  partner to relaunch troubled state telco **[[sierratel]]** on an **MVNO** (revenue-share) model —
  avoiding heavy capex after failed privatisations — targeting students/young professionals. Sierratel
  carries **~$6.3m** employee obligations and **~$35m** external debt; Africell covers 92% (2G) and held
  **54.51% market share** (end-2024). Source: [[2026-04-22-sierratel-mvno-africell]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: connectivity policy leans hard on **regional integration** (the Guinea axis, ECOWAS
  roaming, a second cable through a neighbour) and on **foreign satellite** (Starlink) to reach the
  offline majority — pragmatic for a small market, but it makes resilience contingent on partners and a
  single LEO operator. The Sierratel MVNO is the sovereignty hedge: keep a state brand without the capex.
