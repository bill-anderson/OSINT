# Uganda × Digital Payments

Uganda is one of the world's largest mobile-money markets, and its payments layer is being rebuilt
around two levers at once: **the national ID as the gate to a financial account**, and **digital
payment rails as an instrument of tax visibility**. Both are explicit, both are stated by the
authorities themselves — and both run ahead of the register that is supposed to admit people to them
(see [[uganda--dpi-id]]).

## The rails: BoU, FY2024/25 (2025-10-06)

**[[bank-of-uganda]]**, under Governor **[[michael-atingi-ego]]**, reported for **FY2024/25**:

- **E-money +28.6% to UGX 326 trillion**
- **Financial inclusion 70%**; **e-payments index 37%**
- **Agent banking +76.1% to UGX 29.4tn**; agents **+49.1% to 22,793**
- **Debit card use fell 5.1% to 3.6m**; **internet banking users fell 6.6% to 0.9m** — attributed to
  **cyber-security fears**

**The composition is the finding: growth is in agents and mobile, not in cards or internet banking.**
Uganda is not digitising along the account-and-card path; it is digitising along the agent-and-handset
path, and the two channels that require a customer to trust a screen both **contracted**.

**In train (as of Oct 2025):** an **RTGS replacement and ISO 20022 migration set to go live November
2025**; a **National Payment Switch** in progress; a **third ACH session from February 2025**; and a
**CBDC study planned**. Regionally, **[[eaps]]** inflows reached **UGX 1.3tn (+17%)**, with Kenyan and
Tanzanian currency **99%** of activity — East African settlement in practice means two counterparties.
Source: [[2025-10-06 BoU's New Payment System Cuts Delays and Boosts E-Money by 28.6%]]. *Further
figures on [[bank-of-uganda]].* See [[gov.regional]].

**Carried, not reconciled.** The same reporting gives national e-money of **UGX 326tn (+28.6%,
FY2024/25)** and **[[mtn-uganda]]** at **UGX 319.6tn (+28.6%)** while stating MTN handles **40%** —
**internally inconsistent**. **No derived shares are computed from it.** See [[UGA]] → Contested
figures.

## The ID gate on payments

**[[bank-of-uganda]]'s April 2024 directive** requires ID verification for **all financial transactions
of UGX 1 million (US$260) and above** — *"met with criticism as some citizens said it would slow down
business."* **74 financial institutions** held NIRA database access for digital KYC **as of June 2024**
(per the BoU 2023 annual report; *the primary is not held*): commercial banks, MFIs, a forex bureau,
fintechs, insurers and **one telco**. Source:
[[2024-10-14 Uganda financial institutions sign on for digital KYC through national ID]]. **Full
treatment: [[uganda--dpi-id]].**

## Payments as a tax instrument

**2025-12-23 — tax on MoMo.** [[ura-uganda]] and **[[mtn-uganda]]** launched tax payment via USSD
***165\*18#**, free, on any phone (***285#** retained). **URA collected UGX 15.25 trillion net in H1
FY2025/26 against a UGX 14.93tn target — 102%**, a **UGX 322bn** surplus. Commissioner General
**[[john-musinguzi]]** stated the rationale without euphemism: *"By shifting transactions from cash to
digital platforms, economic activity becomes visible and traceable. Informal traders can be identified
and gradually integrated into the tax net."* Source:
[[2025-12-23 URA Launches Enhanced Tax Payment Services on MTN MoMo to Boost Digital Compliance]]. See
[[dpi.govtech]].

**2025-04-21 — the quantified case ([[imf]] analysis — attribute).** Uganda targets **tax-to-GDP of 13%
→ 18% by 2030** against a decade near **13%** (SSA average **16%**, OECD **34%**). The IMF records:
**TINs integrated with the national ID registered over 350,000 informal taxpayers by 2022**;
**[[efris]]** drove a **12% rise in VAT collections**; **Payment Registration Numbers** took **25% of
tax payments digital**. Source:
[[2025-04-21 Leveraging Digital Public Infrastructure to Enhance Uganda's Tax Revenue Potential]].

*Read with [[ura-uganda]]'s **TIN-into-NIN merger** (2025-11-11) on [[uganda--dpi-id]]: the tax number
and the identity number are converging into one object.*

## Strategy: NFIS II

**[[uganda-nfis-ii]]** — the **Uganda National Financial Inclusion Strategy 2023–2028** — is the
governing framework, notable for **widening its priority groups to add refugees and persons with
disabilities**. Source:
[[2023-11-01 Uganda National Financial Inclusion Strategy 2023-2028 (NFIS II)]].

Against that ambition, **[[asamo-hellen-grace]]**, Minister of State for Disability Affairs, reported
(**2025-12-02**) that Uganda is **estimated to have more than 1.3 million persons living with
disabilities (as of December 2025)**, with complaints of barriers to **loans, banking and essential
financial services**. Source:
[[2025-12-02 Minister Asamo Calls for Stronger Financial Inclusion for Persons with Disabilities]]. See
[[include.access]].

## Two governance gaps

**2026-04-16 — the data is not available, and the lending is not governed.** Ugandan mobile-money
operators **refused to share real transaction data** with researchers, forcing the use of a synthetic
dataset. And Uganda — among the world's largest mobile-money markets — has **no central-bank guidance
on AI in mobile lending**, even as algorithmic profiling is already deployed in digital lending and
mobile money. Source: [[2026-04-16 3 Reasons Mobile Money Programs Have a $1.7 Trillion Problem]]. See
[[gov.protect]], [[capacity.research]].

## The rails are only as good as the network

**2026-01-26.** The four-day internet blackout ordered on the evening of **13 January 2026**, two days
before the presidential election, **crippled what the reporting calls the "mobile-money economy"** and
cost [[mtn-group]] and [[airtel-africa]] an estimated **~$15m**, spilling into Northern Corridor trade.
**A payments layer built on agents and handsets inherits the connectivity layer's single point of
failure — and that point is political, not technical.** Source:
[[2026-01-26 The cost of Uganda's internet shutdown]]. *Shutdown detail on [[UGA]].* See
[[infra.connect]].
