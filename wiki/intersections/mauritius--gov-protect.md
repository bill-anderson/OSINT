# Mauritius × Data Protection & Privacy

Mauritius runs one of Africa's most mature data-protection regimes — yet the same period exposes a sharp
**gap between the compliance apparatus and the state's own surveillance conduct**. Both halves are unusually
well-documented.

## The regime (strong on paper and in enforcement)

- **Legal lineage.** Mauritius legislated data protection early — a **first law in 2004**, partial
  implementation in **2009**, and the current GDPR-aligned **Data Protection Act 2017**. It is enforced by the
  independent **[[data-protection-office-mauritius|Data Protection Office]]** under Commissioner **Drudeisha
  Madhub** (also President of the Francophone Association of Data Protection Authorities). Source:
  [[2026-05-27-no-digital-id-without-data-protection-madhub-biometricupdate]].
- **Enforcement intensity (2026).** The DPO issues roughly **3,000 enforcement actions a year (99% resolved
  amicably)**, mandates **chief data officers**, and is amending the law to add **administrative fines** — among
  the most active DPAs on the continent (ID4Africa 2026 AGM). Source:
  [[2026-05-16 World Bank, African DPAs outline formula for trusted digital identity, DPI]].
- **EU adequacy (2026).** Madhub confirmed that **in 2026 Mauritius is pursuing the EU adequacy assessment,
  fully aligning its law with the EU GDPR** — one of the few African states to seek adequacy. She frames privacy,
  cybersecurity and governance as **foundational** to digital identity ("two sides of the same coin"), warning
  digital-ID vendors against "marketing trust" without genuine privacy-by-design. See [[geopol.eu]], [[dpi.id]],
  [[mauritius-mobile-id]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-27-no-digital-id-without-data-protection-madhub-biometricupdate]].
- **DPO Regulations 2026.** The Cabinet approved the **Data Protection (Designation, Tasks and Position of Data
  Protection Officers) Regulations 2026** (promulgated 12 Jun 2026) — formalising DPO designation,
  qualifications, independence and reporting duties across public and private sectors, with a six-month
  compliance window and entry into force **1 January 2027**. See [[gov.legislate]]. Source:
  [[2026-06-12-dpo-regulations-2026-lexpress]].
- **Reform runway (2025).** A year earlier, Minister Ramtohul had flagged the regime's weaknesses — a **slip in
  the UN EGDI ranking**, dispersed personal-data holdings, and a lagging legal framework — calling for a
  **citizen "data hub"** and a dedicated ICT strategy (the precursor to the National Data Strategy). Source:
  [[2025-04-09-ict-data-protection-strategy-in-preparation-lexpress]]. See
  [[mauritius-national-data-strategy|National Data Strategy 2025-2029]] and its **Freedom of Information Bill**.

## The counter-current (state surveillance)

- **The VERINT / PertSol interception saga.** Parliamentary revelations (2025) describe **large-scale
  communications interception since 2017**: a surveillance system procured from the Israeli firm **[[verint]]**
  (further batch 2022), branded **PertSol**, monitoring calls, internet traffic and social/messaging platforms
  (Facebook, TikTok, WhatsApp, Signal, email) **without judicial oversight**, wired into major telecom operators
  and accessed by the **Counter Terrorism Unit** and **National Security Services**. PM Ramgoolam confirmed
  illegal wiretapping; a large volume of intercepted data was reportedly **wiped from government servers
  immediately after the 2019 general election**, raising questions of political targeting of opposition figures,
  journalists and activists. See [[infra.cybersec]], [[sovereignty]]. Source:
  [[2025-02-07-in-the-shadows-of-mass-surveillance-mauritiustimes]].
- **"Moustass Leaks" and communications trust (2026).** Leaked audio recordings — the ongoing **Moustass Leaks**
  saga — continue to test the framework: the revocation of the SIT CEO over a circulated recording prompted legal
  analysis of **electronic-evidence admissibility** (relevance, authenticity, integrity, legality) and whether
  unauthorised recording and dissemination constitutes **unlawful processing under the DPA 2017**. The rise of
  **AI-generated deepfakes** is flagged as a new challenge to evidentiary standards. See [[tech.ai]],
  [[gov.legislate]]. Source: [[2026-07-13-privacy-of-communications-moustass-leaks-mauritiustimes]].

The tension is the story: a GDPR-adequacy-seeking regime whose credibility is measured against a state
interception apparatus that operated outside it. See [[MUS]], [[gov.protect]], [[sovereignty]].
