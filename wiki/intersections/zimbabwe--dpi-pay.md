# Zimbabwe × Digital Payments & the ZiG

Zimbabwe — the country that made mobile money famous (**[[ecocash|EcoCash]]**, ~43% of GDP by 2014) — runs a
digital-payments boom on top of a **broken currency**. The paradox that organises this page: the
**gold-backed ZiG is legal tender yet barely works online**, so a market drowning in digital transactions
still runs a **physical cash-out war** — and the state's own wallets sit idle while private ones fight.

## The boom, and the RBZ's modernisation

- **Explosive volumes (2025).** RBZ digital retail transactions hit **$5.93bn in Q2 2025 (+34%)**, with
  **152,000 POS** deployed in the quarter on RTGS/EFT rails and **[[zimswitch|ZimSwitch]]** interoperability —
  the ZiG boosting volumes despite IMF criticism, mobile money thriving amid cash shortages. Source:
  [[2025-09-15-digital-finance-sector-explosive-growth]].
- **A fintech department, and charge caps.** The **[[reserve-bank-of-zimbabwe|RBZ]]** stood up a **Digital
  Transformation & Fintech department** (sandbox, Innovation Hub, NFIS II, **CBDC exploration** signalled),
  then in the **2026 MPS** capped charges (**2% withdrawal, 1.5% POS**), raised **[[zipit|ZIPIT]]**/mobile
  limits **ZiG 8,000 → 13,000** and issued larger ZiG notes — all to push ZiG usage. See [[gov.legislate]].
  Sources: [[2025-11-13-rbz-digital-transformation-fintech-department]], [[2026-02-27-rbz-cuts-bank-charges-expands-zipit-limits]].

## The ZiG-online paradox

- **Legal tender that fails at checkout.** ZiG cards "should" flow through ZimSwitch, but in practice ZiG
  **fails online while USD Visa/Mastercards breeze through**: online gateways (Paynow, Pesapal, DPO) are
  configured USD-first for "revenue certainty," ZiG cards need separate online activation, and ZiG settlement
  is opaque (no public breakdown vs USD's T+2/T+3). And on the POS fee caps, "a directive is not
  implementation" — the governor himself conceded limited enforcement capacity. **Currency sovereignty on
  paper, USD dependence in the checkout.** See [[include.access]]. Source: [[2026-05-07-zig-legal-tender-why-cant-you-use-online]].

## The cash-out war goes physical

Because digital ZiG is unreliable and cash is scarce, the competitive frontier is **getting physical notes
into a hand without an agent**:

- **[[o-mari|O-Mari]]** (Old Mutual/CABS) lets holders **withdraw at CABS ATMs** via interbank settlement — no
  new machines. **[[ecocash|EcoCash]]** answered by wiring withdrawals into TN CyberTech Bank's ATMs.
  **[[innbucks|InnBucks]]** built its own **cash-recycler ATMs** (10 installed, 78 pipeline) at Simbisa
  outlets. Meanwhile **[[netone|NetOne]]**'s **One Wallet sits unactivated** and a potential **AFC Holdings**
  integration (both state-owned via Mutapa) that could reach farming communities has **not moved** — the
  clearest rural-inclusion opportunity, unused. And **ZIPIT still isn't seamless**, so network effects keep
  favouring EcoCash's scale. Sources: [[2026-07-02-o-mari-effect-mobile-money-war-cashout]],
  [[2025-06-12-econet-fintech-comeback-ecocash-growth]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: Zimbabwe's payments story is where **monetary sovereignty and digital usability pull hardest
  against each other**. The state made the ZiG legal tender to reclaim its currency, and stood up the rails
  and rules to push it — yet the market routes around ZiG to USD at every online checkout, and the *physical*
  cash-out war shows how little the digital layer is trusted for value that must ultimately be spendable. The
  private sector innovates hard (O-Mari, InnBucks, EcoCash) while the **state's own wallets (NetOne One
  Wallet, AFC) — the ones best placed for rural inclusion — sit idle**, the same statist paralysis seen in
  connectivity. A cashless pioneer, re-learning cash, because the sovereign currency won't hold digitally.
