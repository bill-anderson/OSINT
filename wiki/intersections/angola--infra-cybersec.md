# Angola × Cybersecurity

Angola is building a cybersecurity apparatus on **two parallel tracks that both stand up the same institutions**, while its *practice* — Predator spyware against a journalist, a "fake news" law the opposition calls a muzzle — runs ahead of, and against the grain of, its stated rights safeguards. The country entered this build near the **bottom of the ITU Global Cybersecurity Index (Tier 4, 2024)**, the second-lowest of five bands.

## The two-track institution build

**Legislative track — approved, then stalled.** The Executive's **[[angola-cybersecurity-law]]** (Proposta de Lei de Cibersegurança) won **general approval on 23 Jan 2026 (105–1–75)**, creating a **National Cybersecurity Council** and a **[[centro-nacional-de-ciberseguranca-angola]]**. **UNITA's 75 abstentions** were grounded in fear of **election-day/protest-day internet shutdowns**, citing 2024 precedents in Tanzania, Kenya and Uganda. Committee-stage discussion was then **suspended, unanimously, on Tuesday 2026-07-07** (the event date; Angop reported it same-day, *Novo Jornal* the following day) over "inconformidades na redacção, na forma e no conteúdo" — specifically the **preamble and chapters 1–2**. UNITA's **Mihaela Webba** argued the bill's purpose is security and that "telecommunications are secondary questions"; MPLA's **António Paulo** said the joint sitting issued the rapporteurs a mandate to improve the text with an Executive team yet to be named, and UNITA warned that a redraft this deep risks becoming a **new bill requiring fresh procedure**. No resumption date was set. Minister **[[mario-oliveira]]** called it strategically important; the opposition warned it could become "an instrument of abusive surveillance and digital repression" absent strong judicial guarantees. **The bill is stalled as of the window's close.**

**Decree track — enacted, not draft.** Angola's **National Cybersecurity Strategy** ([[national-cybersecurity-strategy-angola]]) was **approved by Presidential Decree 256/25**, adopted in the Council of Ministers session of **2025-10-27** and published in *Diário da República* Iª Série n.º 227 of **2025-12-03** — in force from publication, grounded in Law 7/17, with six strategic objectives. Two companion decrees from the same session followed: **DP 258/25** creating the **Conselho Nacional de Cibersegurança** (a 21-member *consultative* body chaired by the President, published 2025-12-03) and **DP 263/25** creating the **[[centro-nacional-de-ciberseguranca-angola|Centro Nacional de Cibersegurança]]** (published 2025-12-10). **All three decrees are now held in full text**, DP 263/25 including its complete 31-article *Estatuto Orgânico*: sources
[[2025-12-03-decreto-presidencial-256-25-estrategia-nacional-ciberseguranca-angola]],
[[2025-12-03-decreto-presidencial-258-25-conselho-nacional-ciberseguranca-angola]] and
[[2025-12-10-angola-decreto-presidencial-263-25-centro-nacional-ciberseguranca]]. Each decree records
Council-of-Ministers approval on **2025-10-27** and entry into force on publication — three distinct dates,
none of which is the publication date of any secondary report. (DP 256/25 and DP 258/25 carry presidential
signature **2025-11-26** and publication **2025-12-03**; DP 263/25 was signed **2025-12-02** and published
**2025-12-10**.)

**What DP 263/25 actually establishes** (no longer secondary reporting). The Centro is an **instituto
público with legal personality and administrative, financial and patrimonial autonomy** (Art. 2), seated in
Luanda with power to open local services (Art. 3), under the **superintendence of the minister responsible
for telecommunications and IT** — [[ministerio-das-telecomunicacoes-tecnologias-de-informacao-e-comunicacao-social-angola|MINTTICS]] — who appoints and dismisses the Director-General, approves its plans
and budget, and may suspend or revoke its acts (Art. 4). Three points sharpen the wiki's reading:

- **It is a regulator, not just a CERT.** Art. 6(a) gives it **regulation, rule-making, supervision,
  inspection and *sanctioning* powers** over cybersecurity; Art. 7 lets it issue binding circulars,
  deliberations and *instrutivos* on supervised entities. This is materially more than the "specialised
  body to protect national cyberspace" the secondary accounts described — and it complicates the
  two-track reading: the decree already confers a supervisory regime, leaving the stalled bill to supply
  the *statutory* obligations and offence framework.
- **CERT.ao is a department inside it** (Arts. 9, 17), not a separate agency — national incident response,
  CSIRT-network coordination and international representation sit in one executive department, alongside a
  critical-infrastructure protection department and a **24/7 security operations department** (Art. 19).
- **It is funded partly by its own fines.** Art. 25 splits receipts **40% Treasury / 30% the Centro / 30% a
  *Fundo de Cibersegurança*** — a sanctioning body retaining 30% of the penalties it levies, with the
  Cybersecurity Fund itself not otherwise established in any instrument the wiki holds *(as of 2026-07-20)*.

Art. 6(c) requires the Centro to **act in articulation with the [[apd-angola|APD]]** where an incident has
caused a personal-data breach — the statutory seam between the cyber and data-protection regimes. The staff
tables (Anexos I–II) and organigram (Anexo III) are tabular/graphic and **not held as of 2026-07-20**; the
normative text is complete.

> **Resolved (2026-07-20): the two tracks are sequential, not rival.** The relationship is stated inside the primaries. ENCS **§7.1.1** lists creating the Conselho, the Centro and CERT.ao/CSIRTs as **strategic actions of the strategy itself**; **§7.4** separately commits to a two-phase legislative reform whose phase 1 is passage of laws *by the legislature* — that is the bill. So the decrees **built the policy architecture**, while the law would supply the **statutory regime**: operator obligations, incident notification and the offence framework. *(Refined 2026-07-20 on the DP 263/25 primary: the earlier formulation — that supervision and sanctions could only come from the law — is **wrong on the decree's own text**. Art. 6(a) of the Estatuto Orgânico already vests regulation, supervision, inspection and **sanctioning** powers in the Centro, and Art. 25 presupposes fine revenue. What the decree cannot do is create criminal offences or bind operators by statute.)* The strategy even refers forward to a "Lei da Cibersegurança" that does not yet exist. President **[[joao-lourenco]]** listing the *Centro* among digital-sovereignty investments (2026-03-03) postdates its creation by DP 263/25 on **2025-12-10**. **As of 2026-07-20 the decree track is fully in force and the bill remains suspended in committee with no resumption date.** *(Note the ITU GCI score of 39.5/100 the decree quotes as its own justification is a **2024** figure — superseded by design, and must be written dated.)*

## The US vendor inside the institution

The national cybersecurity *agency* is being financed from outside: **[[cybastion]]**, a US technology firm, signed a **$170m** agreement with **[[angola-telecom]]** at the **US–Africa Business Summit (Luanda, 24 Jun 2025)**, a **$25m first phase** funding the **establishment of a national cybersecurity agency** and Cisco-Networking-Academy training under its "Digital Fast Track," plus a Luanda–Lobito undersea cable. A **US pole** thus sits inside Angola's cyber-institution build — a fourth external actor alongside the China/India/UAE contest the wiki reads over the ID stack.

## Content control as cybersecurity

The **[[angola-fake-news-law]]** (Lei contra Informações Falsas na Internet) **passed definitively on 21 May 2026** (105 MPLA/PRS/PHA votes; UNITA against): a base penalty of **1–5 years**, rising to **10 years** for hate incitement, violence, discrimination or **compromising electoral processes**. UNITA and civil society read it as a tool to criminalise journalists and repress expression; the government frames it as combating structured disinformation.

## Practice ahead of protections

The sharpest evidence is a **surveillance case**: Amnesty International's Security Lab gave the **first forensic confirmation** that Intellexa's **Predator** spyware ([[predator-spyware-angola]]) was **active on journalist [[teixeira-candido]]'s phone on 4 May 2024**, with the state intelligence service **[[sinse-angola]]** named as the software's end-user in a contract dated to April 2021. Cândido filed a criminal complaint; this is the adversarial counter-story (reported by *Novo Jornal* / *Expresso*) to the state's digital-sovereignty framing.

**International cooperation.** President Lourenço reported (2026-03-03) that Angola has **signed the UN Convention against Cybercrime** (ratification pending) and that ~**$20m** in cybercrime-related assets were seized in 2025 (illegal crypto-mining, bank-system attacks, comms diversion).
