# Zambia × Cyber Law & Surveillance

Zambia's **2025 cyber Acts** are the sharpest sovereignty-and-rights fault line in its digital transformation
— framed by the state as protection, by the US as a surveillance mandate, by lawyers as a democratic
backslide, and by the opposition as something to repeal — a contest running straight into the **13 August
2026 election**.

## The law, and the interception mandate

The **[[cyber-security-act-zambia|Cyber Security Act No. 3 and Cyber Crimes Act No. 4 of 2025]]** were signed
by President Hichilema on **8 April 2025**. Act No. 3 establishes the **[[zambia-cyber-security-agency|ZCSA]]**
and a critical-infrastructure regime; Act No. 4 criminalises unauthorised access, identity offences,
computer fraud, "deceptive electronic communications," malware and **"false information."** The contested
core (per the US alert) is a requirement that **ICT providers proactively intercept all electronic
communications** to detect "critical information," via a Central Monitoring Centre (see [[ZMB]] hub).

**What the gazetted Act No. 4 text actually contains (now held, 2025-04-15).** With the statute in hand
([[2025-04-15-zambia-cyber-crimes-act-no-4-of-2025]]) the enforcement clause is identifiable: **s.10** makes
it an offence to **record a private conversation without notifying the parties** (whether or not you are one),
with law-enforcement and lawful-interest exceptions — this is the "recording/publishing private communications"
warning issued on 2026-07-18. Its investigatory powers (Part III) are **court-gated**: warranted search and
seizure (s.27), a 7-day preservation notice extendable only by a judge (s.30), warranted traffic-data
disclosure (s.31), and *ex-parte* judicial orders for real-time traffic-data collection (s.32). **The
blanket "intercept all communications / Central Monitoring Centre" mandate is not in Act No. 4** — it is
attributed to **Act No. 3 (Cyber Security Act)**, whose text the wiki does not hold. The distinction matters
to the surveillance reading: Act No. 4's own reach is narrower and more judicially supervised than the US
alert's framing of the regime as a whole.

## Four framings of the same Act

- **The US alert (Apr 2025).** The **US Embassy** warned its citizens that the new law **"requires the
  interception and surveillance of all electronic communications."** Home Affairs Minister
  **[[jack-mwiimbu]]** countered that the laws "are meant to protect." See [[geopol.usa]]. Source:
  [[2025-04-18-us-warns-citizens-surveillance-law]].
- **The legal challenge (Apr 2025).** The **Law Association of Zambia (LAZ)** moved to mount a **legal
  challenge**, framing the cyber laws as a democratic backslide. *(Lusaka Times; body needs a manual clip.)*
  Source: [[2025-04-22-laz-legal-challenge-cyber-laws]].
- **The opposition's repeal pledge (Jul 2026).** NRPUP running mate **Makebi Zulu** pledged that an elected
  government would **repeal** the Cyber Crimes and Cyber Security Acts, saying they have **"gagged"** Zambians
  — a pre-election campaign line. *(News Diggers lede-only.)* Source: [[2026-07-04-well-repeal-cyber-law-makebi]].
- **The state's protective framing (Jul 2026).** The **ZCSA** urged **journalists** to learn the laws ahead
  of the election, casting them as protection for media houses against ransomware and disruption — the
  government's counter-narrative to the surveillance reading. Source:
  [[2026-07-15-journalists-urged-learn-cybersecurity-laws]]. The Ministry of Information and Media's
  enforcement-commencement statement (issued 2026-07-18) extends the same framing: the Act "applies equally
  to every individual without exception", enforcement will be "fair, impartial, without fear or favour" —
  and **public servants are singled out for their duty to protect confidential information held in the
  course of their duties**, folding civil-service information control into the compliance message. Source:
  [[2026-07-22-zambia-cyber-crimes-act-compliance]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty` / `colonialism`: the cyber Acts are where Zambia's "sovereignty" language turns
  double-edged. Cast as protecting national critical infrastructure and citizens, the same interception
  mandate builds a **legible, monitorable network** just as the state rolls out a national digital ID, a
  government-owned 4G network (GovLink), and — via **Huawei** — a "sovereign" AI data centre. Whether this is
  sovereignty *for citizens* or *over* them is exactly the question the LAZ challenge, the US alert and the
  opposition pledge each answer differently; the wiki records the dispute rather than resolving it, and notes
  the timing — a surveillance-enabling regime bedding in right before a contested election.
