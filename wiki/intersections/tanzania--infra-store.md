# Tanzania × Data Centres & Cloud

Tanzania's data-centre story is driven by a **data-sovereignty argument turned commercial**: the
**[[tanzania-pdpa|PDPA]]** and a **localisation policy** are pushing organisations to host sensitive data
in-country, and operators (**[[wingu-africa|Wingu]]**, **[[liquid-intelligent-technologies|Liquid]]**) are
building the local-cloud and carrier-neutral capacity to catch that demand — on top of the
**[[nictbb|NICTBB]]** backbone and four new public data centres. The recurring caveat: **adoption is still
early** (in-house server rooms dominate), and **energy and skills** constrain the build.

## Compliance as the demand driver

- **Localisation policy + PDPA.** National policy already requires **some institutions to host data
  locally** (while not barring cloud learning), and the **PDPA is "already encouraging organisations to
  keep sensitive data within the country"** — Wingu's Kyriacou calling **compliance "a major driver,"** with
  businesses wary of the legal/operational risk of offshore hosting. The gap operators name: **further
  regulatory clarity on cloud services and cross-border data flows** would lift investor confidence. See
  [[gov.protect]], [[tanzania--gov-protect]]. Sources:
  [[2025-11-23-watunza-taarifa-cloud-storage]], [[2026-04-30-wingu-africa-targets-tanzania-regional-digital-hub]].

## Wingu — local cloud and the regional-hub pitch

- **Wingu Cloud Exchange (WCX).** Wingu's **locally-hosted private cloud** (Wingu Compute, Kubernetes,
  Wingu Security) offers a compliant alternative to offshore — **data within regional borders**,
  **local-currency pricing**, **carrier-neutral** peering that breaks single-ISP monopolies, hybrid with
  AWS/Azure (CSO Nicholas Lodge). *(The hub records WCX debuting in Tanzania Nov 2025, after a $60m raise;
  this is the platform detail.)* Source: [[2026-04-23-wingu-africa-launches-cloud-exchange-wcx]].
- **The regional-hub assessment (Apr 2026).** Deputy CEO **Demos Kyriacou**: the **fundamentals are in
  place** (geography, NICTBB, SEACOM/EASSy/2Africa cables), Dar es Salaam the anchor of Wingu's East
  African operations, its flagship carrier-neutral DC hosting the **Tanzania Internet Exchange (TIX)** to
  keep domestic traffic local — but **international capacity is not yet matched by internal distribution**,
  outsourced-DC adoption is early, and **energy reliability + a specialised-skills shortage** are the
  binding constraints. See [[infra.connect]]. Source: [[2026-04-30-wingu-africa-targets-tanzania-regional-digital-hub]].

## Liquid enters the frame

- **Liquid–government alignment (Jun 2026).** **[[liquid-intelligent-technologies|Liquid]]** (a
  **[[cassava-technologies]]** subsidiary, fresh off an **$855m recapitalisation**) aligned with Minister
  Kairuki on connectivity, cloud, cybersecurity, data centres and payments under the **Digital Economy
  Strategic Framework 2024–2034** — as Tanzania invests in **public data centres in Dodoma and Zanzibar**.
  See [[gov.policy]]. Source: [[2026-06-19-liquid-tanzania-government-align-digital-transformation]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: this is the cleanest example in the Tanzania material of **data-protection law functioning
  as industrial policy**. The PDPA plus a localisation rule create in-country demand; local operators build
  to serve it; the internet exchange keeps traffic domestic. That is a coherent sovereignty flywheel — the
  law is not just a rights instrument but a reason to build the racks at home. The honest limits are the
  ones the operators volunteer: the demand is still nascent, cross-border-flow rules are unclear, and the
  physical constraints (power, skills, internal distribution) are exactly the ones a hub ambition has to
  clear. Cite the market-size projections (Arizton second-tier, on the [[TZA]] hub) but don't absorb them.
