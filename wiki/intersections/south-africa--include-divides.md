# South Africa × Digital Divides (`ZAF` × `include.divides`)

South Africa is the continent's most digitally capable state and the place where **digital-by-default
exclusion is being litigated with numbers**. In January 2026 the state was simultaneously claiming a
record of inclusion and defending, at the Supreme Court of Appeal, an online-only welfare system a
high court had already found unlawful. The two claims are days apart and point in opposite
directions — and the government's own arithmetic corroborates the plaintiffs.

## The collision

### The state's own arithmetic (2026-01-19)

**[[sassa]]**'s own media statement — issued to rebut a social-media claim that it had loosened
eligibility for foreign nationals — carries, incidentally to that purpose, the most damaging number
in the file. **As of 1 January 2026**, of **14,135** COVID-19 SRD applications from permanent
residents, refugees and special-permit holders, **8,368 were approved but only 2,690 were paid — a
32% approved-to-paid ratio.** CEO **[[themba-matlou]]** confirmed no regulations or eligibility
criteria had been amended. The same statement establishes the "**unique system-generated identifying
number for people without identity documents**" clause on the DHA or SASSA database that underpins
the alternative-ID eligibility category. Source:
[[2026-01-19 SASSA on qualification criteria for COVID-19 SRD]].

### The litigation, nine days later (2026-01-28)

The **[[institute-for-economic-justice]]** and **[[paythegrants]]** filed opposition to the state's
**Supreme Court of Appeal** appeal against a **January 2025** Pretoria high court ruling that found
several SRD regulations unlawful — specifically the **online-only application system**, the **R624
monthly income threshold** and the **grant's value**. Their charge, **from their court papers**, is
that the government made the grant "**inaccessible to millions of people who need it, refused it to
eligible beneficiaries based on inaccurate information, reduced its value and the means threshold for
eligibility, and persistently failed to pay hundreds of thousands of approved beneficiaries**". They
argue the online-only system was adopted to "serve the improper purpose of reducing uptake", reject
Treasury's unaffordability claim, and ask that the appeal be dismissed with costs. **A hearing date
was not set** as of 2026-01-28. *These are the parties' pleaded arguments, attributed — the judgment
itself is not held (GAP-ZAF-004).* Source:
[[2026-01-28 State is blocking access to SASSA grants, argue activists]].

### The state corroborating the plaintiffs — the analytical spine

**SASSA's 32% is independent, dated, primary evidence for the precise claim the state is appealing.**
The activists allege the state "persistently failed to pay hundreds of thousands of approved
beneficiaries"; nine days earlier the agency itself published, in its own name and for an unrelated
purpose, a cohort in which **two-thirds of approved applicants went unpaid**. The cohort is small and
non-citizen — 14,135 applications — so it cannot be scaled to the national caseload without evidence
the wiki does not hold. But it is the government's own number, on the government's own site, and it
does not contradict the pleadings. It confirms them.

## Scale — on the department's own figures

On the Department of Social Development's **June 2022** figures, as cited in the court papers:
**13.4 million** working-age people had **no income**, and **18.3 million** working-age people had
incomes **below the food poverty line**. The plaintiffs' allegation is fiscal, not administrative:
"**Treasury persistently allocates funding that is sufficient to fund payment to fewer than half of
the 18.3-million people who the Department of Social Development has estimated would qualify for
it**" ([[national-treasury-south-africa]]). The grant "provides less assistance than it did when it
was introduced" and reaches **significantly fewer people than at its March 2022 peak**. Source:
[[2026-01-28 State is blocking access to SASSA grants, argue activists]].

## The exclusion mechanism

- **Income defined as any inflow.** Any money entering an applicant's bank account is treated as
  income. The groups argue this "unjustly excluded many people".
- **Bank verification on a one-month snapshot.** The groups say the government's "**unchallenged
  evidence**" shows bank verification wrongly excludes eligible applicants by **misclassifying funds**
  and relying on **a single month's snapshot** — a design that has "often excluded people receiving
  occasional financial help from friends or relatives".
- **A monthly re-test.** Qualification requires earnings **under R625 a month**, with SASSA
  conducting **monthly bank checks**.

## The tell

In its papers the department argued that **complaints posted by applicants on platforms like X prove
there is no barrier to online access**. The groups countered that while some applicants have social
media, **a significant number have no internet access at all**, and that the system is "**an
insurmountable hurdle**" for people **without digital literacy or with disabilities**. The argument
is self-refuting on its face: it can only sample the connected. See [[capacity.literacy]],
[[digital.rural]]. Source:
[[2026-01-28 State is blocking access to SASSA grants, argue activists]].

## Grant parameters (dated)

- **Introduced 2020** as a temporary emergency COVID-19 measure; **extended annually**, the current
  extension running to **31 March 2027**.
- Value raised **R350 → R370 in April 2024**.
- Means threshold: **under R625/month (as of 2026-01)**, monthly bank checks. The struck threshold
  litigated is **R624**.
- Eligibility (per the **2022** COVID-19 SRD Regulations): **18–60**, resident in South Africa, not in
  a state-funded institution, not unreasonably refusing work or education; citizen, permanent
  resident, refugee, special-permit holder (Angolan / Lesotho / Zimbabwe Exemption Permit
  dispensations), valid s.22 asylum seeker, **or** registered with a system-generated number for
  people without identity documents.

## Biometrics as a second gate

Biometric enrolment sits directly on the alternative-ID category the SRD rules exist to protect.
**From 5 May 2025**, SASSA required compulsory biometric enrolment of **new clients using
alternative identification** (sworn affidavits, DHA application receipts), **existing clients under
review or changing details**, and **proxies** — explicitly **not** existing recipients using a
standard 13-digit ID
([[2025-04-24 New ID verification process for Sassa grants - Here's who's affected]]). Trade press
of **2025-08-25** headlined mandatory enrolment for "all grant beneficiaries" **from 1 September
2025**, while its own body says "all **new and updated** grant applications"; Matlou conceded the
FY2025/26 start slipped — "we hit a snag"
([[2025-08-25 SASSA to Launch Mandatory Biometric Enrollment for Grant Beneficiaries on September 1]]).
**Resolved (2026-07-20):** biometrics gate **new applications and triggered reviews**, not everyone —
existing recipients using a 13-digit SA ID are not required to enrol unless a review or change of details
triggers it (acting CEO van Vrede to the Portfolio Committee, 2025-08-27). *Correction:* the SCA matter
(hearing 2026-08-25) is the state's appeal in *Institute for Economic Justice v Minister of Social
Development* [2025] ZAGPPHC 29, on **SRD-grant** exclusion barriers — it is not a challenge to biometric
enrolment, which post-dates the judgment. Thematically adjacent, legally distinct. See [[dpi.id]],
[[south-africa--dpi-id]].

## Inclusion claimed and exclusion litigated, the same day

On **2026-01-28** — the day the SCA opposition was reported — the **[[home-affairs-south-africa]]**
published its statement that it had issued a record **4,002,964 Smart ID cards in calendar 2025**,
**+17%** on **3,427,468** in 2024, with **[[leon-schreiber]]** framing it as proof that "our
commitment to digital transformation is expanding inclusion and access at a scale never seen before"
([[2026-01-28 Home Affairs sets new record by delivering over four million Smart IDs in 2025]]).
Same state, same week: **inclusion claimed on the identity layer, exclusion litigated on the payment
layer.** Both can be true — an ID is a gate, not a grant — and the juxtaposition is the point: the
capability to enrol millions is not, by itself, the capability to pay them.

## Coverage is not use

The hub holds **4G reaching 98% of the population** (**[[sadis-2025]]**, 2026). **[[datareportal]]**'s
Digital 2025 report puts **13.6 million people — 21.1% of the population — offline at the start of
2025**, against **50.8 million internet users (78.9% penetration, Jan 2025)** and **124 million mobile
connections (193% of population, early 2025)**. *Reference study: cited, not absorbed
([[2025-03-03 Digital 2025 - South Africa (DataReportal)]]).* **The two are compatible and must be
read together**: near-universal 4G coverage does not mean near-universal use, and a fifth of the
population being offline is exactly the population an online-only application system excludes. This
is the empirical floor under the department's X-complaints argument — and under
**[[solly-malatsi]]**'s warning (2026-07-13) that "digital-first must not become digital-only" and
that "the digital divide becomes a democratic divide"
([[2026-07-13 Digital-first must not become digital-only, says Malatsi]]). Malatsi's is the
rhetorical version; the SRD case is the litigated one. See [[include.access]], [[infra.connect]].

## Open questions

- The **high court judgment of January 2025** and the SCA papers themselves are not held —
  the wiki reads the litigation only through GroundUp's reporting (**GAP-ZAF-004**).
- **No hearing date** was set as of 2026-01-28; outcome unknown.
- Whether the **32% approved-to-paid ratio** in the non-citizen cohort holds across the citizen
  caseload is **not established by any source held**.
