# Kenya × Cybersecurity

Kenya is building **institutional** cyber capability faster than it is demonstrating **operational**
resilience. In the space of eight months the state stood up a central cyber authority, took EU money
to harden critical infrastructure, and published an attack-volume figure in the billions — and in the
same window **the presidency's own website was defaced twice**. The gap between the architecture and
the estate is the story on this page.

## The president.go.ke defacements

- **2026-07-18 — homepage defaced, 5 BTC demanded** *(reported 2026-07-20; **the event date is
  2026-07-18**, not the publication date)*. Attackers replaced the homepage of **president.go.ke** with
  messages addressed to President William Ruto and a **cryptocurrency wallet address**, threatening to
  leak unspecified information about him unless paid **by 6pm the same day**. The demand was **five
  Bitcoin** — reported as **KSh 41.3 million / ~USD 320,000 as at 2026-07-20** *(the BTC figure is the
  demand; the fiat figures are a dated conversion and move with the exchange rate — do not carry them
  forward undated)*. The defaced page read in part: *"This message is the third time for you; before we
  leak everything about you. Do a payment of 5 bitcoins to the Bitcoin wallet... if you want peace
  before 6 o'clock this evening."* — **"the third time" implies prior contact the government has not
  addressed publicly.**
- **The response, and what it does not say.** **State House** confirmed the breach; its ICT team,
  working with the **National KE-CIRT/CC** (National Kenya Computer Incident Response Team Coordination
  Centre), **restricted access to the site** shortly after discovery to contain it, and the site was
  restored. ICT Cabinet Secretary **William Kabogo Gitau** said incident-response protocols were
  activated and that **there was no evidence sensitive government data had been accessed, exfiltrated
  or lost**, posting that "the responsible Agencies that protect websites are on top of stuff."
  Unaddressed on the record: **whether the intrusion reached back-end systems**, **how access was
  obtained**, and the meaning of the attackers' "third time". **No group has claimed responsibility and
  the government has made no attribution (as of 2026-07-20).** Source:
  [[2026-07-20-kenya-presidential-website-defacement-ransom]].
- **2025-11-17 — the first one, and it was broader.** A coordinated attack took president.go.ke offline
  alongside the ministries of **Interior, Energy, Labour, Health and Education**, plus the Immigration
  Department, the Directorate of Public-Private Partnerships, the **DCI**, the **Hustler Fund**, the
  Government Press and **Nairobi City County**. Platforms were restored after emergency cyber-response
  procedures, and investigations were opened under the **Computer Misuse and Cybercrimes Act**. Source:
  [[2025-11-17-hackers-government-websites-presidency-nation]].
- **Read the pair, not the incidents.** Two compromises of the presidency portal **within twelve
  months**, the second **eight months after** the first triggered emergency procedures and a criminal
  investigation. Whatever was done in between did not prevent a recurrence on the same asset. *Note the
  asymmetry in what is public: the government has disclosed **outcome** ("no evidence of data loss")
  but not **cause** — and it is cause that would tell you whether the second event was a repeat of the
  first.*

## The institutional build

- **National Cyber Security Agency (2026-06).** Parliament approved the **NCSA Order, 2026**,
  centralising a previously siloed, reactive posture: protection of critical digital infrastructure,
  incident-response coordination, national cyber strategy, and resilience audits of key government and
  private systems. Oversight concerns attach to the breadth of its mandate and the concentration of
  security chiefs within it — see **[[national-cyber-security-agency-kenya]]**. Source:
  [[2026-06-23-kenya-approves-national-cyber-security-agency-itweb]].
- **Kenya Cyber Resilience (KCR) Project (2026-01).** An EU-funded programme to harden critical
  information infrastructure and build national cyber skills — **USD 3.56m (EUR 3m) over 36 months**,
  funded by the EU, implemented by Expertise France with ESTDEV. See
  **[[kenya-cyber-resilience-project]]**. Source: [[2026-01-27 Kenya launches project to boost cyber security]].
- **The attack-volume figure (2026-06).** A report from the **National Computer and Cybercrime
  Coordination Committee (NC4)** recorded **more than three billion cyber attacks** on Kenyan
  government systems, cloud infrastructure and critical digital services **over a three-month period**,
  with **Nairobi logging the highest number of cybercrime cases nationally**. *Carried as the NC4's own
  dated claim, attributed, not adopted: the report's methodology and its definition of an "attack" are
  **not established in held material**, and event counts at this magnitude typically aggregate
  automated probe traffic. **The NC4 report itself is not held** — it is on the acquisitions list.*
  Reported in: [[2026-07-20-kenya-presidential-website-defacement-ransom]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: Kenya's cyber posture is being built **top-down and institutionally** — a central
  agency, a national strategy, donor-funded CII hardening — while the **operationally embarrassing**
  failures keep landing on the most symbolic asset the state owns. A defaced presidency homepage does
  not by itself indicate deep compromise; a **twice**-defaced one, with cause undisclosed and the
  attackers claiming prior contact, indicates that **basic web-estate hygiene sits outside whatever the
  new architecture currently reaches**. The sovereignty question is not whether Kenya can write cyber
  policy — it demonstrably can — but whether the agency it has created has **operational authority over
  the estate**, or only strategic authority over the policy. Nothing held answers that yet.
