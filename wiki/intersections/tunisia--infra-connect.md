# Tunisia × Connectivity, 5G & Submarine Cables

Tunisia entered 2025 with all three operators live on 5G and a mature national fibre backbone, and is
adding **Mediterranean subsea capacity** framed explicitly as *digital sovereignty*. The connectivity base
is strong, and the Medusa landing — briefly claimed by both national operators — is settled: Orange owns the
station, Tunisie Telecom buys capacity on it.

## 5G — three operators at once (Feb 2025)

All three MNOs launched 5G within days, **<3 months after end-Nov-2024 licensing** (15-year licences; 5 MHz
in 700 MHz + 100 MHz TDD at 3.5 GHz):

- **[[tunisie-telecom]] (14 Feb 2025).** National operator; CEO **Lassâad Ben Dhiab** stressed its fibre
  base — **61,000+ km of optical fibre**, 90% of base stations fibre-connected, 4G ~98% coverage; **~90,000
  users on 5G by launch evening**. First operator to test 5G on its network (2020); mobile + "BOX 5G" offers.
  Sources: [[2025-02-14-assabah-tunisie-telecom-launches-5g]], [[2025-02-17-developingtelecoms-three-operators-launch-5g]].
- **[[orange-tunisia]].** 5G mobile + Wi-Fi 6 FWA router; **400 5G sites** at launch; opened a **5G Lab**
  (Berges du Lac). Source: [[2025-02-17-developingtelecoms-three-operators-launch-5g]].
- **[[ooredoo-tunisia]].** FWA "Fix Jdid 5G" (up to 100 Mbps); the Gulf-group operator. Source:
  [[2025-02-17-developingtelecoms-three-operators-launch-5g]].

## Submarine cables — Mediterranean capacity as sovereignty

- **Medusa lands at Bizerte (2025-10-31).** The cable's **first African landing**, marked by a ceremony both
  operators' CEOs attended — the source of the ownership confusion that followed. Announced by
  **[[afr-ix-telecom|AFR-IX]]/Medusa's own newsroom** with both CEOs, EU Ambassador **Giuseppe Perrone**
  placing it under **[[global-gateway|Global Gateway]]**. Sources: [[2025-11-01-medusa-lands-in-bizerte]]
  (the announcer of record), [[2025-11-03-zawya-tunisie-telecom-medusa-landing-bizerte]] (TAP/Zawya
  re-report).
- **Orange owns the station; TT buys capacity (settled 2026-07-20).** **[[orange-tunisia|Orange]]** is the
  landing party and **owns and operates the Bizerte landing station** and the ~1,050 km Tunisian segment
  (**ViaTunisia**), under a hosting agreement of **2023-05-11** and an **EU CEF-Digital grant of €10m**
  (signed Dec 2022, 30% of construction and management costs); maximum capacity to Orange **24 Tbps
  (2025-11)**, ready-for-service **2026-06-03**. **[[tunisie-telecom]]** is a **capacity customer**, having
  agreed on **2025-02-07** a dedicated Bizerte–Marseille pair of **20 Tbps (2025-02)** — the basis of its
  "eightfold capacity increase" claim (2025-11), which is about its own capacity, not ownership. TT
  separately owns the Hannibal and SEA-ME-WE 4 cables. Sources:
  [[2023-05-11-orange-tunisia-medusa-bizerte-hosting-agreement]] (the hosting agreement itself),
  [[2025-02-10-tunisie-telecom-joins-medusa-fibre-pair]] (TT's pair, 20 Tbps),
  [[2025-11-01-medusa-lands-in-bizerte]], [[2026-06-05-connectingafrica-viatunisia-subsea-live]],
  [[2026-06-26-techafricanews-orange-medusa-cable-operational]].
  - *Capacity note:* Medusa's own record gives TT's pair as **20 Tbps (2025-02)**; trade press and the
    TAP/Zawya re-report say **22 Tbps (2025-11)**. The announcer of record is preferred.
- **The 2023 hosting agreement, in Medusa's words.** Signed **11 May 2023** by Orange Tunisia (then-CEO
  **Thierry Millet**) and Medusa CEO **Norman Albi** to land the cable at Bizerte and "transform the
  northernmost region of the continent into a new gateway to Africa." Medusa's 2023 statement already
  framed Orange as the party that would *develop* the infrastructure — the ownership question was never
  genuinely open in the primary record. Orange had previously built the **Didon** cable (2014) with
  [[ooredoo-tunisia]]. Source: [[2023-05-11-orange-tunisia-medusa-bizerte-hosting-agreement]].
- **Superseded framing.** Both operators' PR re-cut the shared landing event around itself. Raised as
  **CONTRA-TUN-001** and **resolved 2026-07-20** against Medusa/AFR-IX's own newsroom; both accounts kept
  dated here rather than one overwritten.

## Market shape and the numbers

- **Data-driven shift (2025).** **[[instance-nationale-des-telecommunications-tunisia|INT]]** data: mobile
  internet and subscriptions rising sharply, voice plateauing (OTT displacement), **mobile broadband the
  dominant access point**, active SIMs rising (multi-device/IoT). Source:
  [[2025-04-11-techafricanews-telecom-data-driven-growth]].
- **Penetration (as of 2025, per a 2026 overview).** ~**80% internet users**, **mobile subscriptions >
  population**, **4G ~95%** of inhabitants, commercial 5G rolling out from 2025; **ICT ~11% of GDP**,
  **2,200+ firms**, ~40% of tech employment under-30 (Telecom Review Africa feature, dated figures — not
  bare present). Note: the same feature's "**~$8.8bn electronic transactions in 2024, +10.6%**" is
  *all* e-transactions and must not be conflated with the mobile-payments-specific +81% figure on the
  [[tunisia--dpi-pay]] page. Source: [[2026-03-09-telecomreviewafrica-north-africa-digital-generation]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: connectivity is the strongest, most self-consciously *sovereign* leg of Tunisia's digital
  build — a state operator with a deep fibre backbone, simultaneous tri-operator 5G, and Med-cable capacity
  narrated in the language of "digital sovereignty" and a "regional hub." The dependence is European (EU-
  funded Medusa/ViaTunisia, EBRD money) rather than Chinese here. The one blemish is not capability but
  **clarity of ownership**: two national operators claiming the same landing is exactly the kind of
  provenance ambiguity a sovereignty story should not carry unresolved — hence the flag.
