# Uganda × Cybersecurity & Cyber-Law

Two currents run through Uganda's cyber domain: a **defensive build-out** (a new national security framework,
against a rising threat curve that reached the central bank) and a **contested body of cyber-law** — where
the courts just **rolled back the state's online-speech powers** even as regulators reach for new
content-blocking tools against piracy. Protecting the system and controlling the discourse are not the same
project, and 2026 pulled them apart.

## The threat curve

- **Financial sector under pressure (Jan 2026).** Banks, insurers, fintechs and mobile-money operators are
  prime targets; Ugandan firm **Milima Security** took ISO/IEC 27001 certification, its CEO citing a **skills
  shortage** as the top constraint. Police recorded **446 cybercrime cases in 2024** (UGX **72.1bn** lost,
  ~$20m; down from 523 in 2023) with an **887-case backlog** — the "last mile" mobile-money users most
  exposed. See [[dpi.pay]]. Source: [[2026-01-30-experts-urge-tougher-cybersecurity-financial-sector]].
- **NISF 2026 (Jul 2026).** The Ministry of ICT launched an updated **National Information Security
  Framework** (minimum controls, critical-infrastructure protection) under the Uganda Digital Acceleration
  Project, citing a **60% YoY rise in cyberattacks** including a **UGX 62bn ($16.8m) central-bank incident**.
  Minister Lumumba: information security **"is a matter of national security… everyone's responsibility"**;
  NITA-U ED Hatwib Mugasa framed it as moving "from awareness to implementation." See [[gov.policy]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-16-uganda-updated-national-information-security-framework]].

## Cyber-law rolled back: the Computer Misuse Act ruling (Mar 2026)

- **Constitutional Court nullification.** A five-justice panel (led by **Justice Irene Mulyagonja**) struck
  down **Sections 11, 23, 26, 27, 28 and 29 of the Computer Misuse Act** — the provisions criminalising
  unauthorised access/recording, "offensive"/"malicious"/"unsolicited" information and social-media misuse —
  on **quorum** grounds and for being **"overly broad, vague and ambiguous."** It also voided **Penal Code
  ss.162–163 (criminal libel)**. These were the tools **"widely used to regulate online behaviour and
  prosecute offenders"** (the same law a minister invoked at the border-Wi-Fi launch, see
  [[uganda--infra-connect]]). A significant digital-rights win that **removes the state's main online-speech
  weapon** — even as it kept the network kill switch (see [[uganda--infra-connect]]) intact. See [[gov.legislate]],
  [[uganda--gov-protect]]. Source: [[2026-03-17-constitutional-court-nullifies-computer-misuse-act]].

## New enforcement reach: anti-piracy and content-blocking (Jul 2026)

- **National anti-piracy framework.** The **[[uganda-communications-commission|UCC]]** convened
  broadcasters, MTN/Airtel/Lyca, URA and URSB to build a **national anti-piracy coordination framework**,
  exploring **Dynamic IP and DNS-blocking** technologies (Canal+ presentation). Legitimate pay-TV
  subscriptions **fell from ~1.65m (2021) to ~800,000 (Mar 2026)**, hitting the creative economy and
  revenue. Participants flagged the need to **balance enforcement with protection of legitimate internet
  services** — the live concern being that IP/DNS-blocking infrastructure built for piracy is the **same
  capability** used to throttle speech (cf. the election shutdown). Source:
  [[2026-07-17-ucc-national-push-against-digital-piracy]].

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: Uganda's cyber posture is a study in the two meanings of "control." On defence, it is
  building sensibly — a national framework, ISO-certified local firms, a threat picture honest enough to name
  a central-bank breach. On law, the picture inverts: the **courts are dismantling** the vague speech-crime
  provisions the executive relied on, while the **regulator is assembling** new content-blocking
  infrastructure (for piracy, ostensibly) whose dual use is obvious given the January shutdown. The open
  question is whether "cybersecurity" in Uganda ends up meaning *securing the network* or *securing the
  narrative* — the same instruments serve both, and the institutions are pulling in opposite directions.
