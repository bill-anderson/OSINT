# South Sudan × E-Government & Revenue Digitisation

South Sudan ranks **192/193** on the UN EGDI (2024), and its e-government push is driven above all by
**revenue**: digitising licensing, tax and permits to plug leakages. A new **ICT Authority** is meant to
give the fragmented effort an institutional home. (Full-digitisation decree, Jan-2026 deadline — see
[[SSD]] hub.)

## Revenue-first digitisation

- **NCA e-Services Platform (Feb 2026).** The **[[national-communication-authority-south-sudan|NCA]]**
  launched an **e-Services Platform** ("Digital Transformation for Transparency and Accountability")
  digitising licensing, payments and regulatory workflows — cutting leakages, improving traceability and
  aligning to **regional digital tax systems**. See [[dpi.pay]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-13-nca-eservices-platform-revenue-onecitizendaily]].
- **SSRA "Digital First" tax strategy (Mar 2026).** The **[[south-sudan-national-revenue-authority|South
  Sudan Revenue Authority]]** is going **digital-first** — e-tax, **e-visa** and **electronic work
  permits** (early success reported) — to plug leakages, though hampered by an **ICT-infrastructure
  deficit and a shortage of specialised personnel** (working with the NCA on connectivity). See
  [[infra.capacity]]. Source: [[2026-03-10-ssra-digital-first-tax-strategy-onecitizendaily]].

## Institutional and partner moves

- **ICT Authority signed into law (May 2026).** President Kiir **signed the legal framework** for a
  national **ICT Authority** to oversee digital regulation, **data protection**, communication services
  and emerging-technology coordination — the institutional anchor the digitisation drive has lacked (the
  Data Protection Act to take effect alongside it — see [[south-sudan--gov-protect]]). Source:
  [[2026-05-21-ict-authority-established-szn]].
- **Crawford Capital e-services partnership (May 2026).** The ICT Ministry (MICT&PS) confirmed an
  official collaboration with **Crawford Capital Ltd** for e-services research/technical support, said to
  follow "established institutional procedures" and Council-of-Ministers resolutions. *(Capture is not
  endorsement.)* See [[finance.mou]]. Source:
  [[2026-05-15-crawford-capital-eservices-partnership-citizendigital]].

## Crawford Capital: revenue collection in private hands *(established 2026-07-20)*

The ministry's May-2026 framing of a research-and-technical-support "collaboration" understates what the
record shows. Per the **UN Commission on Human Rights in South Sudan**, *Plundering a Nation*
(**A/HRC/60/CRP.5**, 16 Sep 2025), **Crawford Capital Ltd** — **UK-registered**, payments arm
**CapitalPay** — has since **November 2019** been the **exclusive provider of e-Government Services** under
a contract with MICT&PS, covering trade permits, visas, tax payments, customs, work permits and the
**Electronic Crude Oil Accreditation Permit**.

- **Procurement.** No competitive tender on the public record, against the Public Procurement and Disposal
  of Assets Act 2018. The Commission found it endorsed by key ministries **and by the National Security
  Service's Internal Security Bureau**, whose then-director-general wrote personally in support and
  proposed Crawford work with the intelligence service's ICT department **because the company was building
  large databases and integrating online services**. That is the load-bearing fact for this wiki: the
  security service backed the contract *on database grounds*, making the platform a data-collection asset
  as much as a fiscal one.
- **Terms.** Crawford **retains 75% of the profit on taxes it collects**, 25% to the ministry; extended in
  2024 to an **unlawful levy on tax-exempt humanitarian agencies** that forced suspension of food aid. A
  **0.3%-of-cargo-value** crude-oil accreditation fee (2023) is split with the ministry — Crawford's share
  estimated at **US$9.6-11.5m/yr**.
- **Custody of public money.** The Commission found the **[[south-sudan-national-revenue-authority|SSRA]]**
  complicit in letting Crawford **hold public revenues in company bank accounts** outside the treasury —
  "in clear violation of articles 9 and 10 of the UN Convention against Corruption."
- **Crawford's account (22 Jun 2026).** The 75/25 split applies "only to a separate convenience fee"; the
  company says it takes a **2% platform service fee**; funds sit in **regulated trust/collection accounts**
  under the Electronic Money Regulation 2017 with central-bank oversight. It claims monthly non-oil revenue
  rose from ~**US$648,000 (2020)** to ~**US$28m (2025)**, and did not address the oil-sector findings.
- **Sanctioned, and politically protected.** The **US State Department sanctioned Crawford in May 2026**.
  Trade Minister **Atong Kuol Manyang Juuk**'s 90-day review order (5 Mar 2026) was **overruled within 24
  hours** by VP **James Wani Igga** on the ground that the contract was cabinet-approved (Cabinet
  Resolution No. 3/2024; Presidential Order No. 35 of 2025).
- **Ownership.** The Commission's published findings say Crawford is "owned and run by **family members of
  national political elites**"; they **do not name Adut Salva Kiir Mayardit**, the attribution carried in
  secondary reporting (which also names **Garang Mayom Kuoc Malek, 68%** and **Jeremy Gisemba, 26%**).
  Keep the distinction.

**The control case.** On **8 Oct 2025** the SSRA and **[[national-communication-authority-south-sudan|NCA]]**
signed a **six-year Build-Operate-Transfer** deal with **Ensoft** (**N-Soft** in other reporting) for
telecom revenue assurance: vendor takes **3.56%**, government **96.44%**, **NCA hosts and oversees**, and
the system **transfers to the state after six years**. Same country, same year, same revenue-digitisation
rationale — and terms an order of magnitude different. The contrast is the argument: nothing about
digitising revenue collection *requires* the Crawford structure.

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: e-government here is **fiscally motivated** — the state digitising to *capture revenue*
  before it digitises to *serve citizens*. That is a rational sequencing for a cash-strapped state, but
  it concentrates sensitive fiscal/permit data in new platforms (and private partners like Crawford)
  ahead of the ICT Authority and Data Protection Act meant to govern them — the recurring "build ahead of
  governance" pattern (cf. [[south-sudan--gov-protect]], [[south-sudan--dpi-id]]).
- `sovereignty`: the Crawford arrangement inverts the usual sovereignty worry. The standard concern is a
  **foreign state or hyperscaler** holding a country's data; here a **domestically-connected private
  company, registered abroad**, holds both the revenue and the records — and was sponsored into the role
  by the **national security service** precisely because it would build the databases. Sovereignty was not
  lost to an outside power; it was **allocated internally**, to a contractor, on terms the state has since
  defended against its own trade minister. Note too that South Sudan is drafting a Data Protection Bill
  while its most data-rich fiscal platform has operated **outside any data-protection regime for six
  years** — the governance gap is not prospective, it is retrospective.
