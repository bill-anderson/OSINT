# South Sudan × E-Government & Revenue Digitisation

South Sudan ranks **192/193** on the UN EGDI (2024), and its e-government push is driven above all by
**revenue**: digitising licensing, tax and permits to plug leakages. A new **ICT Authority** is meant to
give the fragmented effort an institutional home. (Full-digitisation decree, Jan-2026 deadline — see
[[SSD]] hub.)

## Revenue-first digitisation

- **NCA e-Services Platform (Feb 2026).** The **[[national-communication-authority-south-sudan|NCA]]**
  launched an **e-Services Platform** ("Digital Transformation for Transparency and Accountability")
  digitising licensing, payments and regulatory workflows — cutting leakages, improving traceability and
  aligning to **regional digital tax systems**. See [[dpi.pay]]. Source:
  [[2026-02-13-nca-eservices-platform-revenue-onecitizendaily]].
- **SSRA "Digital First" tax strategy (Mar 2026).** The **[[south-sudan-national-revenue-authority|South
  Sudan Revenue Authority]]** is going **digital-first** — e-tax, **e-visa** and **electronic work
  permits** (early success reported) — to plug leakages, though hampered by an **ICT-infrastructure
  deficit and a shortage of specialised personnel** (working with the NCA on connectivity). See
  [[infra.capacity]]. Source: [[2026-03-10-ssra-digital-first-tax-strategy-onecitizendaily]].

- **The e-visa portal starts charging EAC citizens (20 Jul 2026).** Revised fees **published on South
  Sudan's electronic visa portal on 2026-07-20**, implementing an immigration policy introduced the
  preceding week (*week of 13 Jul 2026 — the policy's own date is not established in held material*),
  put a **USD 50** visa fee on citizens of **[[KEN]], [[UGA]], [[RWA]] and [[COD]]**, and **USD 100** on
  Somali, Burundian and Nigerian citizens. **Tanzanians and Egyptians enter visa-free**; South Africans
  visa-free for stays **under 30 days**. Applications are online-only — apply on the portal, pay online,
  download the approved document before travel — with a stated **72-hour** processing time. *(Fees are
  denominated in USD by the issuing authority itself; no local-currency figure is published.)* The
  e-visa is one of the SSRA "Digital First" rails above and sits inside the Crawford Capital
  e-Government concession documented below — so the charge is levied through a **privately operated
  channel**, which is the part worth watching. See [[gov.regional]], [[south-sudan--dpi-pay]]. Source:
  [[2026-07-20-south-sudan-e-visa-fee-eac-labour-mobility]].

## Institutional and partner moves

- **ICT Authority signed into law (May 2026).** President Kiir **signed the legal framework** for a
  national **ICT Authority** to oversee digital regulation, **data protection**, communication services
  and emerging-technology coordination — the institutional anchor the digitisation drive has lacked (the
  Data Protection Act to take effect alongside it — see [[south-sudan--gov-protect]]). Source:
  [[2026-05-21-ict-authority-established-szn]].
- **Crawford Capital e-services partnership (statements 14 May 2026).** The ICT Ministry (MICT&PS)
  confirmed an official collaboration with **Crawford Capital Ltd** for e-services research/technical
  support, said to follow "established institutional procedures" and Council-of-Ministers resolutions;
  ICT Minister **Ateny Wek Aten** framed it as delivering "a modern, efficient, and accessible
  government". The SSRA separately reported **electronic cargo tracking** for inbound goods, introduced
  to curb diversion and smuggling — a second data layer beyond tax and permits. *(Capture is not
  endorsement.)* See [[finance.mou]]. Sources:
  [[2026-05-14-south-sudan-defends-crawford-capital-deal-eastleighvoice]],
  [[2026-05-15-crawford-capital-eservices-partnership-citizendigital]].

## Crawford Capital: revenue collection in private hands *(established from the primary, 2026-07-20)*

The ministry's May-2026 framing of a research-and-technical-support "collaboration" understates what the
record shows. The **UN Commission on Human Rights in South Sudan**, *Plundering a Nation*
(**A/HRC/60/CRP.5**, 16 Sep 2025), devotes a titled section to **Crawford Capital Ltd** — payments
subsidiary **Capital Pay Ltd** — which under a **16 November 2019** contract with MICT&PS (then minister
**Michael Makuei**) is the **exclusive provider of e-Government Services**, covering trade permits, visas,
tax payments, customs, work permits and the **Electronic Crude Oil Accreditation Permit**. The report is
the wiki's primary of record for this arrangement; Crawford and the government contest it (both below).

- **Procurement, and the intelligence service's sponsorship (para. 154).** No competitive tender. The
  Commission's government correspondence shows that in **October 2019** the **Ministry of Finance and
  Planning approved a single-sourcing procurement**, noting "*the National Security concern attached to the
  project*". That approval **followed written advice from Akol Koor Kuc, then Director-General of the
  Internal Security Bureau (ISB) of the National Security Service**, proposing that Crawford "*should work
  in partnership with the ISB ICT Department since they are aiming to create **huge databases** and
  integration of services that can be accessed online*."
- **The Commission's own surveillance inference (para. 154).** The Commission does not leave that
  implication to the reader. It concludes that, beyond the corruption concerns, "*the nature of the
  intelligence service's involvement in the single-source procurement process suggests that 'e-Services'
  are also likely to be used **without any consideration for personal data protection to facilitate
  unlawful State surveillance of citizens, an established tactic of repression in South Sudan**.*" This is
  the UN Commission's finding, not this wiki's inference — and it is the sharpest data-governance
  statement in the corpus: a national fiscal platform read by a UN inquiry as latent surveillance
  infrastructure.
- **Terms — 75/25, revised upward from 65 (paras. 145, 155; fn. 222).** Crawford takes **75% of the
  'eServices' profit share**, the Ministry 25% — the same split as the **0.3%-of-cargo-value** crude-oil
  accreditation fee (agreed between the ICT and Petroleum ministries in **November 2022**, directive
  **29 Aug 2023**), on which Crawford stood to gain **US$9.6–11.5m/yr** and the Ministry US$3.2–3.8m
  (2023 production and price levels). Footnote 222 records that "*although the initial contract indicated
  a **65 per cent** share to Crawford, the subsequent references to profits are for it to receive 75 per
  cent*" — the split moved **in the contractor's favour** after signature. The Ministry's 25% "does not
  appear to go to the national government budget". The contract also purports to **exempt Crawford from
  corporation, import and value-added tax for its first ten years**, and the Commission found Crawford
  proposed the Ministry "*be the face of the project*" while the company "remained in the background but
  still retaining majority shares".
- **The separate 2% e-Tax entitlement (para. 157)** — a **November 2020** contract with the National
  Revenue Authority makes Crawford its 'e-Tax' collector with a legal entitlement to **2% of all digitally
  assessed taxation**, likely **>US$5m/yr**. Commercial banks receive **0.5%** for physically collecting
  and transporting cash revenue. *This is the reconciliation of the two figures the wiki carried as a
  possible conflict: **the 75% and the 2% are different contracts**, not competing accounts of one.*
- **Custody of public money (paras. 24, 157).** The Commission found the
  **[[south-sudan-national-revenue-authority|SSRA]]** complicit, "in clear violation of articles 9 and 10
  of the UN Convention against Corruption". In **late 2023**, after a change of Commissioner-General,
  commercial banks were instructed to deposit non-oil collections into **accounts controlled by Crawford
  rather than Authority accounts as required by law**; bankers described Crawford representatives making
  **irregular withdrawals exceeding the 2% retention** before remitting. Banks had **not seen the e-Tax
  contract** directing them to do so.
- **A borrowed platform (para. 156).** The e-Visa portal Crawford set up runs on the **same 'e-Citizen'
  platform developed by Webmasters Kenya for the Government of Kenya**. Crawford "did not build the
  platform software, nor significantly customize it", yet takes 75% of the profits, with Capital Pay
  charging a further **US$20.16** on a US$100 visa.
- **Crawford's account (22 Jun 2026).** The 75/25 split applies "only to a separate convenience fee"; the
  company says it takes a **2% platform service fee**; funds sit in **regulated trust/collection accounts**
  under the Electronic Money Regulation 2017 with central-bank oversight. It claims monthly non-oil revenue
  rose from ~**US$648,000 (2020)** to ~**US$28m (2025)**, and did not address the oil-sector findings.
  *Against the report, the 2% claim describes the e-Tax contract and does not answer the eServices or
  crude-oil permit splits.*
- **The state's account (14 May 2026).** The SSRA and MICT&PS called the engagement lawful, transparent
  and endorsed by the Council of Ministers. Neither addressed the 75/25 split, the humanitarian levies or
  the private-account finding.
- **Sanctioned, and politically protected.** The **US State Department sanctioned Crawford in May 2026**.
  Trade Minister **Atong Kuol Manyang Juuk**'s 90-day review order (5 Mar 2026) was **overruled within 24
  hours** by VP **James Wani Igga** on the ground that the contract was cabinet-approved (Cabinet
  Resolution No. 3/2024; Presidential Order No. 35 of 2025). *These two remain secondary-sourced.*

### Ownership, as documented (paras. 164–165, fn. 273)

**The report does not name Adut Salva Kiir Mayardit — the string does not occur in its 101 pages.** The
attribution to the president's daughter, which the wiki previously carried from secondary reporting, is
**retired, not merely qualified**. The documented on-paper structure is:

- **Garang Mayom Kuoc Malek** — **68%** of Crawford Capital Ltd, 95% of Crawford Laboratory Ltd, 61.2% of
  Capital Pay Ltd; son of a former Deputy Minister for Transport and MP for Twic County.
- **Jeremy Gisemba**, a Kenyan — **26%** of Crawford Capital Ltd, 23.4% of Capital Pay Ltd.
- **Ruey Majok Guandong** — previously held **50%** at incorporation; son of South Sudan's Ambassador to
  Turkey. (The Commission notes shareholdings are subject to change.)
- On paper **at least seven individual shareholders, three primary**; the Commission states Crawford's
  financial beneficiaries "extend further and include political elites and their close relatives", and
  that the company "is likely linked to more senior political elite, based on consistent reporting from
  numerous, confidential sources" — **explicitly a hedged characterisation, not a documented ownership
  finding**.

**The only Kiir-family link the Commission documents is to a different company.** South Sudan Business
Registry records show Garang Malek and Ruey Guandong formed **Air Afrik Aviation Limited with President
Salva Kiir's son, Mayar Salva Kiir, in 2013** — an aviation company, **not Crawford**, and a different
Kiir child from the one secondary reporting named. The two must not be conflated.

**Horizon (2026-07-20).** Footnote 249 records that "in addition to revenue collections there have been
plans to extend Crawford's operations to the **administration of South Sudan population data, including
birth registration, passport applications**" and a national identification function — the footnote is
**truncated at a page break in the captured text**, so the final clause is not established. If those plans
advanced, the arrangement reaches the civil registry itself (cf. [[south-sudan--dpi-id]]); nothing on file
establishes that they did. Note also an internal inconsistency in the report: the body dates the
single-sourcing approval to **October 2019** while fn. 247 dates the underlying correspondence to
**October 2023**; the body date is used here.

**The control case.** On **8 Oct 2025** the SSRA and **[[national-communication-authority-south-sudan|NCA]]**
signed a **six-year Build-Operate-Transfer** deal with **Ensoft** (**N-Soft** in other reporting) for
telecom revenue assurance: vendor takes **3.56%**, government **96.44%**, **NCA hosts and oversees**, and
the system **transfers to the state after six years**. Same country, same year, same revenue-digitisation
rationale — and terms an order of magnitude different. The contrast is the argument: nothing about
digitising revenue collection *requires* the Crawford structure.

*Naming unresolved (2026-07-20):* the vendor is **Ensoft** in Eye Radio's account and **N-Soft** in One
Citizen Daily's; the agency appears as both **South Sudan Revenue Authority (SSRA)** and **National
Revenue Authority (NRA)**. Both variances are recorded rather than silently picked — no source on file
settles either. The Eye Radio report also gives the SSRA Commissioner-General as **Simon Akuei Deng**
(Oct 2025), where the Jan-2026 material gives **William Anyuon Kuol**; read as succession, undated.

## Reads

- `sovereignty`: e-government here is **fiscally motivated** — the state digitising to *capture revenue*
  before it digitises to *serve citizens*. That is a rational sequencing for a cash-strapped state, but
  it concentrates sensitive fiscal/permit data in new platforms (and private partners like Crawford)
  ahead of the ICT Authority and Data Protection Act meant to govern them — the recurring "build ahead of
  governance" pattern (cf. [[south-sudan--gov-protect]], [[south-sudan--dpi-id]]).
- `sovereignty`: the Crawford arrangement inverts the usual sovereignty worry. The standard concern is a
  **foreign state or hyperscaler** holding a country's data; here a **domestically-connected private
  company, registered abroad**, holds both the revenue and the records — and was sponsored into the role
  by the **national security service** precisely because it would build the databases. Sovereignty was not
  lost to an outside power; it was **allocated internally**, to a contractor, on terms the state has since
  defended against its own trade minister. Note too that South Sudan is drafting a Data Protection Bill
  while its most data-rich fiscal platform has operated **outside any data-protection regime for six
  years** — the governance gap is not prospective, it is retrospective.
