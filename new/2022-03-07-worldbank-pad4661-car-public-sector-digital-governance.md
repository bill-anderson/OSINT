I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

A. Country Context

1. Central African Republic (CAR) is one of the poorest and most fragile countries in the world. In 2019, per capita gross domestic product (GDP) averaged US$468 —much lower than the average of US$1,130 in countries affected by fragility, conflict, and violence (FCV) in Sub-Saharan Africa. The extreme poverty rate remained high at 71.4 percent in 2020.

2. CAR’s political and governance context remains extremely fragile. Throughout its history this landlocked country located in the heart of Africa has suffered from cycles of violence. While the adoption of the 2015 Constitution began a new process of stabilization, the December 2020 presidential election was marked by violence following the creation of a rebel coalition and the subsequent government counter-offensive. The Government had regained control over most of its territory by end 2021. While the country has recently seen important gains in terms of consolidation, fighting continues in some areas of strategic interest. Government troops and their allies are accused of human rights violations. Furthermore, the choice of bilateral partnerships in the security sector is creating tensions with CAR’s traditional political, development and humanitarian partners. The ability of CAR and the international community to establish an effective and sustainable arrangement to address chronic instability will determine the prospects for poverty reduction and shared prosperity and strengthen the resilience against state collapse.

3. Insecurity and forced displacement have severely aggravated poverty and contributed to the acceleration of urbanization, placing additional strain on already-limited infrastructure and services in host communities. According to the 2021 Human Development Report, CAR has one of the lowest levels of human development, ranking 188 th out of 189 countries. Poverty rates range from 45 percent in Bangui - which accounts for 11 percent of the country’s poor, to 78 percent in the northwest of CAR, the area mainly controlled by armed groups 1. The World Food Program (WFP) estimates that 45 percent of the population face food and nutritional insecurity. CAR’s population is young (60 percent under the age of 25) and increasingly urban (with an urban growth rate of 2.8 percent per year), though most Central Africans continue to reside in rural areas. With a land area of about 620,000 square kilometers and an estimated population of 4.7 million, CAR is sparsely populated (approximately 8 people per square kilometer), making it even more difficult to provide public services across the country.

4. The COVID-19 pandemic, political instability, and security threats have contributed to a deterioration of CAR’s economic performance. Due to a fragile security environment and persistent structural issues, 2 CAR has experienced a deceleration of its growth trend, which reached 3.1 percent in 2019 and 0.8 percent in 2020 (compared to a peak growth rate of 4.8 percent in 2015). Per capita income decreased by 1.1 percent and 2.8 percent in 2020 and 2021, respectively. Successive crises and the pandemic exacerbated existing vulnerabilities, worsened the precarious humanitarian situation and pushed more Central Africans into extreme poverty 3. 5. The Government’s ability to provide public services, and address the population’s grievances, is curtailed by limited State presence throughout the territory, constraining the capacity and legitimacy of State institutions across the territory. The State is present in the capital, Bangui, and surrounding localities, but absent from an estimated 50 percent of the national territory (largely exacerbated by the 2013 political crisis, after which civil servants migrated to the capital and urban dwellings) which undermines the Government’s capacity to address the population’s grievances and leads to territorial disparities in service provision. Low population density, compounded by persistent security and resource constraints, has led to weak investments in infrastructure and service coverage. Extensive infrastructure gaps continue to hamper economic activity, access to markets, and service coverage. Approximately 55 percent of health facilities are functional, 21 percent of the population have access to drinkable water, and it is estimated that 80 to 90 percent of the population lack formal identification. Weak governance has been a major challenge to sustainable economic growth and shared prosperity. CAR ranked 154th out of 180 countries in the 2021 Transparency International Corruption Index. CAR’s 2.6 score on the World Bank Group (WBG) 2019 Country Policy and Institutional Assessment (CPIA), is also below the Sub-Saharan Africa’s (SSA) fragile countries’ average of 2.8. The social contract between the population and the Government remains weak due to unequal access to public services across the country and a perception of widespread corruption and impunity.

6. As CAR has been identified as highly vulnerable to climate change, supporting investments in climate-smart infrastructure, digitally enabled efficiency gains, and increased response capacity will be critical. CAR ranks 181 out of 182 in the Notre Dame Global Adaptation Index, indicating high vulnerability and low readiness to combat the effects of climate change. 5 Heavy rains and floods are recurrent, and the frequency and impact of these events are expected to increase. 6 The impacts of climate change will disproportionately affect the poorest and most vulnerable and hinder CAR's development agenda and its efforts towards economic sustainability and political stability. Factors contributing to low preparedness to adapt to the impact of climate stress include: (i) limited use of energy-efficient and resilient infrastructure (including digital); (ii) limited integration of climate data and climate risks into sectoral (including digital) policies, regulations, and infrastructure planning; (iii) weak capacity to manage and analyze climate data, including its cross-sectoral impacts on sectors such as health; and (iv) limited management of e-waste.

Public Financial Management (PFM)

Organic Law (LOLF) 7, the legal and regulatory framework has been overhauled to bring it into compliance with the Central African Economic and Monetary Community (CEMAC) harmonized fiscal framework. In line with these reforms, authorities have adopted new budget classification and accounting charts, initiated budget deconcentration 8, and a plan to transition towards program budgeting. SIMBA, the recently launched Financial Management Information System (FMIS), will improve efficiency and transparency of budget management by progressively supporting the transition and facilitating production of periodic reports. Reforms have also been initiated to digitalize tax administration: Using its own resources, the Government is developing an online tax declaration system to enhance efficiency of the declaration process for large taxpayers, while deterring graft within the administration. This digital declaration system is expected to later evolve into a digital payment system and possibly broaden its reach beyond large taxpayers. With support from the European Union (EU), the customs administration is planning to update the Automated System for Customs Data (ASYCUDA) to ASYCUDA World.

8. Significant efforts have also been undertaken to improve accounting and internal control rules and procedures, including the implementation of the Treasury Single Account (TSA). The Ministry of Finance and Budget (MFB) has closed over 400 commercial accounts of public entities and transferred the balance to the TSA which is held at the Bank of Central African States (BEAC). The Central Treasury Accounting Agency (ACCT) has been created to validate expenditures and account for transactions. Reforms have resulted in a significant reduction of use of exceptional procedures, from 27 percent in 2016, to 16 percent in December 2020, and increased the rate of compliance by line ministries. In 2020, the Treasury became a direct user of the real time gross settlement system (SYGMA- Système de Gros Montants Automatisé) and the automated clearing system (SYSTAC-Système de Télécompensation en Afrique Centrale) operated by BEAC that supports bulk payments at a country level, including civil servants’ salaries payments 9. The regional payment systems are still at a critical stage of transition from deployment of infrastructure to widespread adoption and usage of electronic payment instruments and services.

9. Despite such progress, there is still significant room to improve overall PFM efficiency. Lack of resources and efficiency of PFM systems generally affect financing and efficiency of service delivery. In 2020, overall expenditure execution stood at 77 percent and internally financed investment budget execution stood at 95 percent. Nonetheless, efficiency of expenditures targeting and planning, as well as involvement of line ministries in the budget management cycle remain limited. This contributes to the increasing burden on households to shoulder the cost of basic public services – for instance in the health sector where their financial participation amounted to 44 percent 10. Procedures are cumbersome, line ministries lack the required information on cash flows to plan service delivery, and resources are limited throughout the budget execution chain. The situation is compounded by the weak capacity, from budget planning to execution, of budget actors, including their ability to monitor and report on annual performance, both within the MFB and in line ministries. Future reforms will require continuing the deconcentration of budget functions and control, and the use of SIMBA within line ministries to allow for increased budget responsibilities by the ministries.

10. CAR has among the lowest levels of tax collection in Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). Overall domestic revenues amounted to 7.8 percent of GDP in 2019 – significantly lower than the average for regional peers, reducing the country’s capacity to deliver basic services. Weak revenue mobilization is due to the existence of considerable tax exemptions 11, important leakages largely through the misvaluation of goods at the borders, and low capacity in the administration of tax and customs. Customs is saddled with outdated practices and deficient infrastructure (of the country’s seven customs offices, only three are currently connected to the customs data management system ASYCUDA). While customs revenues accounted for an average of 45 percent of domestic revenue over the 2019-2020 period, a significant portion remains untapped, with an estimated 10 percent lost to fraud and smuggling during the same period. In May 2021, the Government signed a Memorandum of Understanding for technical assistance in the context of its cooperation agreement with the Federation of Russia. The objective was to support customs anti-fraud mechanisms. After an evaluation, the Government ended the technical assistance in September 2021 without any visible positive impact on the mobilization of revenues from customs.

11. The operationalization of the TSA and the ACCT is also incomplete. The TSA’s scope and comprehensiveness, as well as its on-time accounting of operations, is still inadequate. Many public accounts continue to fall outside the perimeter of the TSA (including those of public agencies and non-fiscal revenues), and its current structure does not follow best international practices, hindering the individualized tracking of revenue and expenditure flows for each accredited public accountant. The current accounting system between the ACCT and the network of deconcentrated public treasury accounting posts is not optimized. At present, the latter act in the execution of the state’s financial operations on behalf of the ACCT, which remains the sole principal accountant for these operations 12, clogging up the ACCT’s services and resulting in a considerable workload.

12. External audit of public finance is limited by weak capacity of the Court of Accounts and limitations of the current information system of the MFB, impacting timely drafting of financial reports. While efforts have been deployed to increase budget oversight functions and bolster transparency and accountability, shortcomings remain substantial. The Government resumed the publication of financial reports from 2016 onwards, albeit with some delays, for auditing and scrutiny by the Court of Accounts and Parliament 13, and the quality of accounting information has also been progressively improved. Human resources management (HRM) and payroll

13. CAR’s inadequate HRM and payroll undermine public sector performance and constrain effective service delivery, especially outside Bangui. CAR employs an estimated 34,200 civil servants (of which only 19 percent are women), with an average 10 percent annual increase in the last 5 years 15. The wage bill constitutes 44 percent of the Government’s primary expenditure yet only 20 percent of the administration is posted outside of Bangui 16, where approximately 80 percent of the population resides. Information systems and processes are outdated and inadequate to effectively support the management of HR and payroll, leading to further inefficiencies and leakages. GIRAFE, the integrated payroll management system developed by the National IT Office (Office National de l’Informatique – ONI), lacks web access – curbing its deployment to ministries – and is strictly limited to the payment of civil servant wages, while all other remaining payments are subjected to complex manual procedures 17. Overlapping controls in payroll management result in cumbersome procedures, inadequate verification, and an unreliable payroll. GIRAFE also lacks HRM functionalities, creating the risk of multiplicity of inefficient systems as various departments develop HRM tools in silo. Recruitment is not aligned with policy priorities, while absenteeism 18 remain a challenge 19. These issues lead to a lack of motivation, hinder the productivity of CAR’s civil service, and result in extremely poor service delivery. Absenteeism, particularly of teachers and health workers, has repercussions on educational outcomes and the wellbeing of the population.

14. Lack of incentives for civil servants to relocate and difficulties accessing their salaries, further exacerbate weak service delivery outside Bangui and the slow (re)deployment of civil servants. Assignments outside of Bangui do not come with any career or financial incentives, such as bonuses or special allowances to support civil servants in moving, despite the harsh living conditions 20. In addition, many provincial banking agencies closed following the eruption of violence, leading civil servants to travel long distances to Bangui to collect their wages. This generates further costs (between 5 to 10 percent of their salary), security risks, inconveniences, and increases absenteeism (in some cases, absences can be as long as two weeks).

Public Procurement

16. Weak capacity, compounded by an incomplete legal and institutional framework, lead to poor procurement practices and outcomes in CAR. These include inflated costs, leakages, and limited opportunities for the domestic economy. There are only five procurement services, each embedded within a ministry, handling the procurement for all 31 ministries. Their capacity is stretched, resulting in backlogs and delays. Rules and regulations are not systematically applied, as demonstrated by the significant portion of single source contracts procured without suitable justification 23. All procedures are conducted manually, preventing real time monitoring and audits, and increasing the risks of corruption. The Procurement General Directorate (Direction Générale des Marchés Publics - DGMP) and Procurement Regulation Authority (Autorité de Régulation des Marchés Publics -ARMP), responsible for ex ante control, and ex post control and regulation - respectively, lack the resources to fulfil their mandates, including IT equipment and connectivity. In addition, while the legal framework is sound with regards to general procurement principles, successive amendments have led to overlapping mandates and legal gaps, especially with regards to conflict resolution, and there are no specific provisions for digitalization and data publication. There is also little transparency, and procurement plans are not published in a timely manner, constraining accountability, interactions with the private sector, as well as state budget preparation.

Civil registration (CR) and identity (ID) management

17. The largest part of the population lacks legal identification restricting access to, and efficient delivery of public services, and exacerbating disenfranchisement 24. Estimates from 2015 show that national birth registration collapsed below 20 percent, and below 40 percent in Bangui. There are significant regional birth registration variations between urban and rural areas according to 2010 government statistics: 78.4 percent in urban areas, compared with 51.6 percent in rural areas. This deprives poorer and more vulnerable segments of the population, including women, from the right to an official identity, preventing them from accessing schooling, and, later in life, financial services, pensions, formal jobs, entitlement claims and property transactions. The legal framework underpinning CR processes poses important registration challenges, including for instance the relatively short legal period for birth registration, as well as the judicial process imposed for late registration and for the reconstitution of destroyed registers. More broadly, because there is currently no data protection law, there are inadequate safeguards for both ID and CR data (see Annex 6: Summary of Legal Analysis). 18. Strong civil registration and identity ecosystems are essential to CAR’s economic development, security, governance, and efficient delivery of services. Digitalizing CR and ID systems will generate significant benefits across the public and private sectors by facilitating registration and storage of data, increasing accountability (chiefly through the reduction of fraud, leakages, and waste) and driving innovation in service delivery (through the use of mobile or digital payments, for instance). Having a reliable digital identity can also empower individuals and enhance their access to their rights, to online and offline services, and to the formal economy. The absence of a reliable CR and ID ecosystem that would be supported by a solid legal enabling environment constitutes a major roadblock for the modernization of public administration and the simplification of service delivery online. The Government has recently shown interest in reforming the CR and ID ecosystem with the support of the African Development Bank, United Nations Children’s Fund (UNICEF) and the EU. However, overall support for systemic reform of CR and ID management systems, legislation, regulations, and institutional coordination and processes is required and remains unfunded. While implementing inclusive CR and ID systems is an enormous task in the short term, implementing structural reforms laying solid legal, regulatory, institutional, and operational foundations is a reasonable approach to pave the way for the future inclusive national digital identity ecosystem.

Digital Transformation 19. CAR still has important gaps to bridge and digital foundations to lay before it can fully leverage digital technology as an engine of growth and a tool to support poverty reduction. According to the Digital Economy diagnostic for CAR (2020) 25, many of the essential foundational blocks for the required digital ecosystem are at a nascent state. These include broader access to broadband connectivity among public institutions and their beneficiaries, digital skills, digital government infrastructure, shared platforms, as well as the enabling environment to ensure digital safeguards (such as data protection and cybersecurity) and expand adoption of digitalized solutions. Limited progress in establishing these foundational blocks for digital transformation largely explains why digitalization of public service delivery is so underdeveloped in the country, as it directly impedes the way government functions, shares information, and delivers services to external and internal customers.

Building block 1: Connectivity

20. Connectivity challenges, resulting in low levels of broadband access and adoption by consumers, businesses, and Government, prevent the country from leveraging digitally enabled solutions for expanded service delivery. CAR is one of the least connected countries in the world and is characterized by significant infrastructure gaps along all segments of the broadband value chain. At present, CAR lacks an international and national fiber optic backbone network, and therefore almost all of CAR’s current international capacity is supplied via satellite connections, which pushes up wholesale and retail broadband prices. As a result, CAR offers some of the most expensive broadband services in the world in relation to purchasing powers (1GB=19.4 percent of GNI per capita 26). Building block 2: Digital skills

21. Most of the population is not familiar with digital tools and systems and has not acquired the basic skills needed to use digital technologies. According to the recent MICS6 survey (2018-2019) 31, 36.9 percent of men own a cell phone, compared to 20.6 percent for the women. Among individuals aged 15-49.8 percent of men and 4.8 percent of women have used a computer, while 7.6 percent of men and 4.8 percent of women have used the internet (well below the regional average). Furthermore, the percentage of the population that has conducted computer related activities or even basic computer tasks is less than 4.1 percent for men and 1.5 percent for women. High drop-out and low completion rates limit the role that the formal education system can play in bridging existing digital skills gaps. At the same time, there are substantial deficiencies in education and training for information technology and systems, and very few higher institutions offer continuous training for young people.

Building block 3: Digital government infrastructure, shared systems, and platforms

22. The use of digital systems and tools in improving core government functions in CAR remains limited. Most platforms in use support core back-end administrative systems (as described in the PFM section). Front-facing services delivery is limited and fragmented. There is no cohesive strategy, nor architecture to provide user-friendly access to government information and services. Moreover, GoCAR lacks the capacity to effectively procure and manage digital systems due to a lack of institutional coordination and the limited skills of public officials to support the adoption and use of digital solutions. Only a few public institutions have dedicated internal IT functions which vary from covering multiple areas of expertise to very basic support by one or two people with limited resources and equipment. The situation is aggravated by the absence of government-wide planning and monitoring of IT investments and spending. This results in costly acquisitions by individual MDAs which fail to deliver the expected results, and problematic proprietary arrangements under poorly designed contracts.

Building block 4: Digital safeguards

24. Legal and regulatory gaps constitute a cross-cutting constraint for the pursuit of digital adoption and transformation in CAR. Law 18.002 on electronic communications establishes the main legal framework governing the telecommunications sector. The law covers the regulation of licensing, interconnection, infrastructure sharing, roaming, meaningful market power and basic data governance. However, there is currently no authorizing body, and the reporting mechanism is still under development. Access to international bandwidth is largely not regulated. Likewise, the regulatory framework for the wholesale market is yet to be fully drafted. These constraints are compounded by the limited capacity of the regulator, the Electronic Communications and Post Regulatory Authority (Autorité de Régulation des Communications Eléctroniques et de la Poste- ARCEP). Additionally, CAR does not have a comprehensive data protection body and lacks cybersecurity and cybercrime legislation which would foster trust in computer networks, sanction any misuse or illegal interceptions of computer systems, and protect government digital infrastructure. Finally, there is lack of laws and regulations on electronic transactions and e-commerce, key enablers of the digital government agenda.

25. In addition to legal and regulatory gaps, the country does not have capacity to mitigate, detect and respond to cybersecurity incidents. CAR rates 3.6 on the 2018 Cybersecurity index, way below the 29.5 rating for SSA. There is no national cybersecurity strategy. Public administration information systems are therefore vulnerable to cyber threats. There is neither a computer emergency response team (CERT) nor a security operations center (SOC). Sub-optimally designed contracts for the procurement of digital systems have led to issues of control and ownership over data, as well as general cybersecurity concerns, and have in some instances contributed to data breaches. Ratifying the Malabo convention would allow CAR to develop its institutional and legal frameworks for digital transactions, cybersecurity, and cybercrime based on the harmonized regional cooperation standards.
