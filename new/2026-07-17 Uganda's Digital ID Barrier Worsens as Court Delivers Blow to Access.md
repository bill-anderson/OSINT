The High Court of Uganda on 10 June 2025 dismissed the case of Initiative for Social and Economic Rights (ISER) & 2 Others v Attorney General & NIRA (HCT-00-CV-MC-0066-2022). The applicants had challenged the mandatory requirement of a National Identification Number (NIN) or National ID card (Ndaga Muntu) under the Registration of Persons Act to access services such as healthcare and social protection, arguing the system disproportionately excludes vulnerable populations, including older persons and women.

Despite expert opinions by Prof. Tom Fisher, Dr. Reetika Khera and Prof. Philip Alston submitted in an Amicus Curiae by CIPESA, ARTICLE 19 and Access Now, the Court held that "Uganda's ID system is not 'digital' because it operates primarily offline. A person cannot be denied access simply because of lack of internet connectivity," a narrow interpretation the author argues ignores its biometric backbone, centralized database and digital interfaces.

Access to services: The author states the ruling legitimizes a system that denies access for those without a National ID, estimated at 18 million (about 40%) of the population. The Ministry of Education has mandated all schools to register learners on a portal under the Education Management Information System (EMIS), requiring learners and staff to possess a NIN; those without are excluded from enumeration, planning and budgeting. The Social Assistance Grants for Empowerment (SAGE), which offers financial assistance to vulnerable elderly individuals, only benefits those on the National Identification Register with a national ID.

The Court noted the deponents "appear to me to be few and isolated cases" and that applicants had not adduced sufficient evidence of "actual and systemic exclusion" from SAGE benefits.

Undermining digital transformation: The Government launched a 5-year Digital Transformation Roadmap on 17 August 2023, shifting to service provision via Digital Public Infrastructure including the National ID System, mobile money, EFRIS, Express Penalty Scheme, Digital Tax Stamps and Payment Registration Numbers — all of which require authentication, undercutting the finding that the ID system is "offline and non-digital."

Failure to provide alternatives: The Court relied heavily on respondents' submissions and did not provide alternatives (e.g. baptism cards, voters' cards, residence identity cards) to remedy exclusion. The author warns the ruling deepens digital exclusion of rural communities, older persons, women, the digitally illiterate and those without ICT access, and highlights that Uganda's DPI lacks strong legal safeguards, comprehensive data protection and independent oversight, risking surveillance, discrimination and misuse of personal data. It also challenges Uganda's compliance with the World Bank's ID4D principle of universal coverage from birth to death, free from discrimination.
