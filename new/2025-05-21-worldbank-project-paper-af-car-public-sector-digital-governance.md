I. BACKGROUND AND RATIONALE FOR ADDITIONAL FINANCING

1. The present Project Paper requests the approval of the Division Director for an additional grant in the amount of US$6.4 million equivalent to support the inclusion of a mining sector governance component of the project. The additional financing (AF) is needed due to changes in project design and includes an accompanying Level 2 restructuring to: (i) revise the Results Framework without changing the Project Development Objective (PDO); and (ii) modify the implementation arrangements. The AF aims to enhance transparency, accountability and data management in the mining sector with a view to improving public revenue mobilization from mining sector operations. The proposed AF will expand the scope of the project by introducing a fifth project component and adding new indicators to track implementation progress. The additional activities are in line with the original PDO and the original closing date of December 31, 2026, will be maintained.

2. The Public Sector Digital Governance Project (PSDGP, P174620) is on track to achieve the PDO. The parent project was approved on May 5, 2022. Although the project disbursement has only reached US$11.35 million (33 percent) as of April 15, 2025, the Midterm Review (MTR), held in September 2024, confirmed that five out of the six PDO indicators are on track and the overall progress towards attainment of the PDO and Implementation Progress (IP) are rated as Moderately Satisfactory (MS) - for further details, see December 2024 Implementation Status and Results Report (ISR). Under Component 1, the project achieved (i) modernized customs systems; (ii) improved civil servant census and payroll database clean-up (removal of more than 2,000 ghost workers); (iii) expanded access to mobile money payment of salary for 1,702 civil servants located in regions without banking services outside the capital city; (iv) implementation of the Integrated Financial Management and Information System, and technical assistance to the customs administration to strengthen risk management and customs control capabilities with the objective of increasing efficiency and reduce revenue leakages by better detecting potential frauds. Under Component 2, the project achieved the following: (i) the enactment of two laws (cybersecurity and data protection); (ii) strengthened capacity of the regulator, ARCEP (Electronic Communication and Postal Services Regulatory Agency, Autorité de Régulation des Communications, Electronique et de la Poste); (iii) improved cross-agency coordination through the working group for strengthening the identification and civil registration; and (iv) strengthened competencies of civil servants (a third of whom are women) to exploit and deliver digitalized public services. In addition to the supplementary mining sector component, The MTR recommended streamlining of some activities (not impacting the Financing Agreement), which require revisions to the results framework.

3. The World Bank previously supported mineral sector development in the Central African Republic (CAR) through the Natural Resource Governance Project (NRGP, P161973), for which remaining International Development Agency (IDA) funds were cancelled on June 30, 2024 1. The NRGP was instrumental in formalization of a large number of small scale mining operations and in bringing their diamond production to the international market. The project also provided legal advice to the revision of the Mining Code, which was adopted in August 2024. 2 Notwithstanding, there continues to be unmet needs for digital data management and transparency in natural resource extraction. Notably, CAR’s re-admission to international verification initiatives, including the Kimberley Process (KP) Certification Standard 3 and the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative 4 (EITI) will need to be integrated into national administration such as customs declarations, public revenue collection and more. 4. The second half of 2024 saw a set of positive developments, predicting a stronger governance of the mining sector. In August 2024, the government promulgated the revised Mining Code, confirming its commitment to sector strengthening and reform. Subsequently, CAR completed its first validation of the EITI, although with numerous qualifications that require corrective actions, most prominently among these are the required strengthening of stakeholder engagement and enhanced production and license data accessibility. 5Finally, in November 2024, the KP fully lifted the embargo on the trade of diamonds from the remaining regions in the country where diamonds were considered to fuel the on-going conflict. Thus, there is strong reason to believe that the mining sector can regain its position as a central pillar of economic activity in the country. For that reason, AF under the auspices of the PSDGP is a natural next step in the World Bank’s support to mineral sector reform. Activities to be financed include: (i) development of a digital platform for mineral license management; (ii) monitoring and reporting of mineral extraction and revenue collection through the internationally recognized KP and the EITI; and (iii) geological data acquisition and management through a digital geological and mineral information system (GMIS). Moreover, the additional activities will draw on experience from the early stages of the PSDGP in terms of good practices for digitization of the public administration in CAR. Specifically, it is expected that economies of scale can be achieved as the fiber-optic network is rolled out. Moreover, data management will continue to require tailor-made designs given the limited IT infrastructure which persists in CAR. The proposed budget of US$6.4 million is considered adequate in comparison to costs of similar activities in other countries. This AF and the inclusion of the Ministry of Mines and Geology (MMG) require changes to the existing institutional arrangements of the parent project.

5. The CAR mining sector has a long history of gold and diamond extraction which dates to the beginning of the 20 th century. The industry has continuously employed five to ten percent of the population. However, the national conflicts over the past decades have dramatically reduced official mineral production, while illegal extraction and smuggling to neighboring countries is reported to have surged. This means that only a fraction of the country’s mineral production is captured by the government. However, as indicated above, recently this trend has reversed. Since 2020, CAR has enjoyed a substantial increase in official gold and diamond production. The combined production value of gold and diamonds was less than US$20 million in 2019, while that value had surged to almost US$100 million in 2023. 6It is likely that production volumes will continue to increase since current levels are between three to eight times lower than historical averages. 7For that reason, there is an urgency in supporting government efforts to enhance sector governance and oversight.

6. A climate change risk assessment of the parent project concluded that CAR is particularly vulnerable and has a low readiness to the effects of climate change. 8 Relevant risks related to the mining sector include (i) flooding; (ii) erosion; (iii) landslides in and around mine pits; as well as (iv) heat-related stress factors on workers and equipment. These findings are consistent with the CAR Country Climate and Development Report (2024). The proposed activities are aligned with the goal of the Paris Agreement. The project also aligns with CAR’s Nationally Determined Contributions of 2022 which identify the mining sector as one of the impact areas to enhance spatial planning and limit deforestation and water scarcity.

8. Component 5: Mining Sector Governance Support (US$5.9 million equivalent). The proposed AF will support mineral sector governance in areas of digital data management, regulatory strengthening, international transparency initiatives, as well as social and environmental management. Equipment and technical assistance will enhance geological knowledge of the nation’s sub-soil resources, while legal support will guide the implementation of the recently promulgated legal framework. In parallel, resources will be allocated to the roll-out of the KP and the EITI activities which are intended to facilitate a more equitable development of the mining industry. This will be accompanied by a strategic and institutional analysis of the environmental and social challenges facing the sector.

9. Sub-component 5.1: Geo-data knowledge management (US$2.0 million). The data management activities will design and install a digitized GMIS to monitor and disseminate sector statistics through a publicly accessible web-portal. The system will also compile and convert existing paper-based reports and geological maps. These efforts will be further expanded through acquisition of new geological data from remote sensing and limited soil sampling in prospective areas. CAR is considered to host substantial deposits of minerals which are critical for the energy transition, including cobalt,

copper, coltan and rare earth elements. Data acquisition and geological modeling of these resources will form the first steps towards inclusion of CAR in the supply chain for critical minerals that are necessary for the low-emission economy. The AF will support exclusively non-intrusive geological campaign techniques such as ground based geophysics (using hand-held equipment), light-scale geochemical sampling and analysis using only hand-held tools, structural mapping of geology, and the use of remote sensing to deliver updated topographical mapping. All this information will be combined with existing geological information (using modern state-of-the-art techniques) to provide an updated understanding of geology which will assist the government to understand high priority areas, attract investor interest, and inform other land-use planning. The statistics to be monitored and disseminated through the GMIS, will include data on climate risks and hazards such as landslides and floods. The improved knowledge of the national geology will help identify high risk zones of climate hazards that will be a valuable contribution to strengthening climate mitigation and adaptation.

10. Sub-component 5.2: Legal and institutional development (US$1.9 million). The paper-based mineral licensing system will be digitized and integrated with a geographical information system (GIS) to allow geographical referencing of mineral permits. The digitization will allow systematic and secure storage of licensing documents to increase efficiency and reliability in data management. This upgrade will require the purchase of office hardware and off-the-shelf software packages. Technical assistance will also be required to support the Department of Geology and Mines in the installation and management of the systems. Processing of license applications will be automated to minimize discretionary decision making or unintended delays in approvals. The system will include automated monitoring of payments, reporting and other obligations on the part of the license holders. Legal and regulatory advice will support the drafting of implementing regulations to underpin the Mining Code (2024). 9 This will include regulation on climate-smart mining practices, including energy efficient operations and safeguarding climate resilient natural and physical assets. In combination, the planned activities are expected to improve the investment climate and facilitate regulatory oversight. In addition to tracking production and revenue data, the mineral licensing system will also enable a stronger enforcement of mine closure obligations which are meant to bring abandoned mine sites back to productive alternative land uses and mitigate climate change impacts such as deforestation, soil erosion and water contamination.

11. Sub-component 5.3: Promotion of sector accountability (US$1.5 million). Resources will be allocated to the implementation of the KP, which is an essential prerequisite for CAR’s official export of and revenue collection from diamond production. To complement KP, CAR has also re-engaged in the EITI implementation which covers all extractive industries in the country. Examples from countries, such as Senegal, Sierra Leone, Zambia, and others, show that the combination of electronic public revenue management and systematic data disclosure through the EITI has increased efficiencies in public revenue management and overall trust in natural resource management. 10 The proposed activities will finance office equipment, operational expenses and basic digital infrastructure of regional offices of and the KP Secretariat. In the case of CAR, the adoption of electronic management systems and adherence to international reporting standards strengthen the traceability of mineral production and solidify collaboration between mining sector agencies and customs authorities. Consultations and training programs will be provided to public and private sector representatives as well as civil society. This is expected to lead to a reduction in the illegal trade of precious stones and metals and an increase in revenue collection.

The project will strengthen the government's digital payment systems and financial inclusion among civil servants thereby reducing the burden, cost and absenteeism for collection of salary especially for women civil servants. At the same time, the scaling-up of mobile salary payment requires a local network of mobile money agents. The AF will continue supporting an approach that includes targets for women mobile money agents, specific trainings for women and measures that allows for better inclusion of women in such training (see Project Appraisal Document from parent project for additional details).

III. KEY RISKS

18. The overall residual risk of the operation, after accounting for the mitigating factors, is considered Substantial given CAR’s volatile political, security and macroeconomic situation. These risks will require deepened supervision and possibly flexibility in design and implementation if the context changes during implementation.

19. The political and governance risk is rated High because the governance framework and political climate remain fragile after years of conflict and instability. Multiple alliances in the security sector are creating tensions with CAR’s traditional political and development partners, both domestically and externally. Access to mineral resources plays a significant role in these partnerships because control of land and resources is a prerequisite for political power. Mitigation: In light of these concerns, the AF will focus on activities which enjoy broad-based support, including the KP and the EITI as they aim to normalize and legitimize the mineral sector activities.

20. Sector strategies and policy risk is rated Substantial. The AF activities address mining governance challenges which are sensitive as they relate to some of the root causes of conflict in CAR. Any engagement in natural resource management will challenge the intersectoral dynamics and affect the interests of some stakeholders with vested interests who may oppose change. Mitigation: The AF is largely technical assistance, capacity building, and institutional support, and the proposed activities will have limited exposure to higher risk elements in the sector which would be associated with more activities in the field. A key lesson learnt from the NRGP was that local-level outreach activities were time-consuming and carried a high risk of non-completion. Moreover, the AF focuses on implementation of the mineral law and will support institutional and organizational structures to underpin sector governance. Finally, the support of EITI and KP introduces mechanisms of accountability and transparency with the backing of international oversight mechanisms.

21. Institutional capacity and sustainability risks, as well as macroeconomic risks remain Substantial. The macroeconomic situation continues to be volatile and may impact on the purchasing power of the project. Institutional capacity and sustainability risks relate to the ambitious reform agenda underpinning the project including the introduction of new technologies. Mitigation: To mitigate the macroeconomic risk, the project will rely on larger long-term contracts that can lock in the expected prices at an early stage. The project design will maintain a relatively simple organizational

IV. APPRAISAL SUMMARY

### A. Economic and Financial Analysis

23. The original project appraisal document estimated that economic benefits of the project would be up to three fold the initial investment. These estimates remain on track thanks to the successful roll-out of mobile payment to more than 1,700 government staff outside Bangui and the removal of more than 2,000 “ghost workers” which received monthly salary without a valid contract. Reducing civil servant travel and absenteeism could yield returns up to 206 percent. Tax and customs reforms may raise at least US$4.5 million annually. Digitization will lower administration costs and fraud, while procurement reforms and centralized digital services will add savings. Given the nature of the proposed AF activities, i.e. technical assistance, the AF components do not easily lend themselves to a standard economic or financial analysis. The attribution of project activities to increased output or revenue collection from the mining industry will be indirect since it will depend on private sector investments, which are likely to occur only after project closure. Geoscience data acquisition to detect mineral occurrences will be attractive for prospective investors and will support authorities in making informed decisions about planning and allocation of mineral licenses. Eventually, better geological knowledge will better equip decision-makers in negotiations with investors, although future investment decisions cannot be quantified at this stage.

24. The above-mentioned issues, notwithstanding, benefits are expected to accrue through various channels. First, successful expansion of the KP to a larger number of sub-prefectures than the eight which were until November 2024 considered compliant would substantially increase the sales and revenue potential. The official exports of diamonds from CAR in 2022 and 2023 stood at approximately US$15 million per year, according to BECDOR. However, other trade statistics 13 indicate exports of US$35 million. This means that CAR has significant upside in revenue projections if only a fraction of the illegal trade in diamond and gold is captured. Second, the introduction of a digitized mineral licensing system is expected to increase the number of legally registered mineral permit holders which, in turn, would facilitate an expansion of the duly reported mineral production. The total value of mineral output in 2023 was reported at approximately US$100 million.

B. Technical

25. The proposed AF activities draw on lessons from similar technical assistance projects in comparable countries. System designs for GMIS will deploy proto-type systems based on open-source software to guarantee compatibility and sustainability of operations beyond the lifetime of the project. Similar IT system upgrades have been implemented with success in other countries in the region, including Nigeria Mineral Sector Support for Economic Diversification Project (MinDiver) (P159761), Sierra Leone Extractive Industries Technical Assistance Project Phase 2 (P160719), and Mauritania Public Sector Governance Project (P146804). Experience from the referenced projects points to the importance of intensive capacity building and on-the-job training of government staff to ensure that systems can be operated and maintained after project completion without dependence on external expertise. The planned legal and regulatory advice is grounded in the Mining Code which was supported through the World Bank-financed NRGP (P161973) and reviewed by the World Bank in line with international good practice. The drafting of implementing regulations and institutional support will follow the same international benchmarks, most notably the African Mining Legal Atlas. 14 Activities related to accountability and transparency will follow international norms (EITI and KP) and pre-established performance criteria to validate CAR’s compliance with principles of good sector governance. Importantly, the international initiatives will also identify weaknesses in sector management and suggest action plans for improvement.
