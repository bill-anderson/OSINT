The Zimbabwean government, through the Ministry of ICT, Postal and Courier Services, has donated 250 Starlink kits to the Ministry of Local Government, part of a drive to bring internet access to underserved communities where fibre and mobile coverage have failed to reach or aren't viable. This follows earlier government involvement in Starlink deployment under the Presidential Internet Scheme, with free WiFi hotspots at post offices and community halls. This time the kits go to local authorities (councils) rather than schools or health centres.

Techzim notes the logic is sound — Starlink needs no ground infrastructure, ideal where traditional rollout is expensive and slow — but the responsibility to deploy, manage, maintain and secure the kits now falls on local authorities, whose track record is often one of mismanagement. Deployed correctly, the kits could enable real-time communication, digitised records, online payment portals and more efficient service delivery; deployed poorly, they risk sitting uninstalled for lack of solar power or expertise, or "disappearing." The piece frames Zimbabwe's official recognition of Starlink as a connectivity option as positive, while cautioning that "hardware is never the issue" — systems, people and accountability determine the outcome. A commenter references POTRAZ's earlier prosecution of a man in Umzingwane District who connected six schools using one Starlink kit (later acquitted).
