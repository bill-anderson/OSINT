In 2024, 19 African countries are recognized as strong performers in e-government, with scores of at least 0.5 out of 1. South Africa and Mauritius lead the rankings, while Tunisia has surpassed Seychelles.

South Africa (0.8616), Mauritius (0.7506), and Tunisia (0.6935) top the African continent in the latest E-Government Development Index (EGDI), per a new report by the United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs (UN DESA): "E-Government Survey 2024: Accelerating Digital Transformation for Sustainable Development." The EGDI is a composite index of three components: the Online Service Index (OSI), the Telecommunications Infrastructure Index (TII), and the Human Capital Index (HCI).

Globally, e-government development improved, with the average EGDI score rising to 0.6382 in 2024, up from 0.6102 in 2022. Asia saw the highest increase (7.7%), followed by Africa (4.8%), the Americas and Oceania (4.1%), and Europe (2.3%).

In addition to the top three African countries, Morocco (0.6841), Seychelles (0.6773), and Egypt (0.6699) made the global top 100, with EGDI values above the global average. The African average EGDI score increased to 0.4247 in 2024, from 0.4054 in 2022. The most significant factor driving Africa's improvement is the TII, which grew 27.8%, rising from 0.3546 in 2022 to 0.4534 in 2024, attributed to increased investment in digital infrastructure as countries recover from COVID-19.

South Africa and Mauritius are the only African countries with very high EGDI scores (above 0.75), among global leaders. Seventeen African countries have high scores (0.50-0.75), 28 scored medium (0.25-0.50), and seven received low scores. The lowest-ranked include Burundi, Niger, Chad, Eritrea, Somalia, South Sudan, and the Central African Republic. The digitization of public services is part of Africa's 2020-2030 Digital Transformation Strategy.
