# Microsoft says AI could add $136 billion to Africa if data can move freely

By Frank Eleanya — TechCabal, 19 February 2026

Artificial intelligence could add as much as $136 billion in productivity gains across Africa, according to Microsoft — but unlocking that value depends on whether countries can enable the secure, seamless flow of data across borders. "We only have about 1% or 2% of global compute power in Africa," Akua Gyekye, Government Affairs Director for Microsoft Africa, told TechCabal. "If we're serious about diffusing AI across the continent, we need infrastructure. But we also need the right policy environment."

As of mid-2025, Africa has 223 data centres across 38 countries, less than 0.02% of the world's total of more than 11,800. Microsoft was the first hyperscale cloud operator to launch local data centres in Africa (2019), organised into South Africa North (Johannesburg) and South Africa West (Cape Town). Gyekye argues a regional rather than purely national approach often makes more economic sense.

## Data sovereignty vs. data silos

Africa has seen a rapid surge in data-protection regulation: in 2012 only 12 countries had laws; at least 46 have now enacted legislation, and more than 40 operate dedicated Data Protection Authorities. The AU's Malabo Convention, which came into force in 2023, has helped speed alignment across regional blocs. Enforcement has intensified — Nigeria's FCCPC and Data Protection Commission fined Meta $220 million in July 2024; Kenya's ODPC fined digital lenders for "shaming" borrowers.

Yet Gyekye warned that well-intentioned sovereignty policies can create data silos: "cybersecurity threats don't respect borders." The issue, she argued, is modernising sovereignty so that "a country's laws can travel with its data" through contractual, technical and regulatory safeguards, making physical location less critical. Ethiopia's strict localisation requirements complicate regional expansion for banks, fintechs and e-commerce firms.
