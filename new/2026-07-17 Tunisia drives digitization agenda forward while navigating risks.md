Tunisia's digital transformation has reached a crucial stage with the execution of E-Houwiya, a state-recognized digital identity system and key pillar of the country's 2025 digital strategy, aiming to improve financial inclusion by providing secure access to banking and FinTech services while facilitating authentication for digital government platforms. The broader push for digitization, including biometric ID efforts and digital service platforms, has raised concerns about privacy and data security. Civil society organizations warn that a lack of transparency and outdated data protection legislation might expose millions to identity theft and mass surveillance.

Tunisia's digital identity revolution: E-Houwiya, launched in August 2022, is a countrywide digital identity system. Its development was made possible by collaboration between the Tunisian government and the Estonian e-Governance Academy (eGA); in 2022, Tunisia's Ministry of Communication Technologies and eGA signed a memorandum of understanding to advance digital transformation and develop the eID landscape. Marit Lani, Tunisia's smart government program director, said they are using the Estonian example to organize existing building blocks into a well-functioning digital identity ecosystem. E-Houwiya, developed under Dr. Nizar Ben Neji's supervision, has been integrated into the banking sector, allowing remote onboarding, KYC compliance, and secure financial transactions. It has 200,000 mobile ID customers, including 15,000 Tunisians living overseas, and functions as an SSO for portals such as E-Bawaba and E-CNAM.

Digitization plans raise privacy concerns: Per Access Now's report, three major programs — the Biometric ID project, Mobile ID E-Houwiya, and the Subsidies Compensation Platform — were developed with minimal transparency and public consultation and carry risks of mass surveillance, identity theft, and data exploitation. The proposed biometric ID would force citizens to provide biometric data to the Ministry of Interior; Tunisia's weak data protection regulations and history of cyberattacks make the biometric database a security concern. The subsidy compensation platform, designed for direct cash transfers, allegedly violated data privacy laws, as authorities failed to consult Tunisia's data protection agency for impact assessments.

Digital transformation and AI strategy: Per Communications Technology Minister Sofiene Hemissi, Tunisia is evolving its digital ID and AI plans, focused on digitizing government services, improving digital infrastructure, and boosting AI in sectors such as health and education. The AI strategy, scheduled for 2025, will support open data and technology industries. Tunisia is ranked 8th in Africa for ICT development (ITU) and leads North Africa in e-government (UN).

Key global partnerships: Collaborations with the EU, AfDB, and accelerators such as Flat6Labs and Seedstars supported fintech, agritech, and healthtech innovation; fintech raised US$150 million. Over 40 firms expanded across Europe, MENA, and Sub-Saharan Africa via the Startup Tunisia Global Program. Tunisia and China discussed digital transformation cooperation (telecom infrastructure and AI); Tunisia and Italy explored collaboration on innovation and startups. Uqudo has partnered with Tunisia to provide secure digital identity verification for passports and national IDs, using AI, biometrics, and cognitive document analysis to verify identity data against the MRZ field for KYC/AML. The GIZ-founded Digital Transformation Centre Tunisia works with the Ministries of Communication Technologies and Industry.
