# Somalia's eAqoonsi app shows progress, gaps in Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) push

Hiiraan Online, April 24, 2026

The app is called eAqoonsi, part of Somalia's push to move public services online. In theory it should save the time and cost of travelling to a registration center; in practice weak internet and limited data have turned a simple task into a struggle. DPI includes core systems like digital ID, digital payments and secure data-sharing that help governments deliver services at scale — but if not built for real conditions, it can exclude more people than it helps. The Somalia Digital Public Infrastructure Summit 2026 communique warned these tools risk excluding the very people they are meant to help if not designed for Somalia's fragile and unequal conditions.

eAqoonsi is linked to the national identification system and designed to give users a digital version of their ID and, eventually, access to a wider range of government services. Registration still requires an in-person visit to a NIRA center before users can activate the digital ID. "First, a person must go to the NIRA centers and register physically and be given an 11-digit national ID, then you can make the ID card itself digital, and then you can access government services," said Mohamed M. Mohamed, NIRA's director of enterprise services and digital partnerships.

Somalia aims to issue 15 million national IDs by 2029, and nearly two million people have registered so far. NIRA has not publicly disclosed how many digital IDs have been downloaded through eAqoonsi, despite Google Play indicating more than 50,000 downloads. Associate Professor Dr. Yahye Abukar Ahmed said the e-ID system faces challenges in internet availability and digital literacy: "There is a risk that this system will leave women, the elderly, and people with disabilities behind." He argues Somalia should follow the "5A" framework — availability, affordability, awareness, ability, accessibility — and recommends multi-channel access including SMS/USSD.

Somalia's main legal framework for personal data is the Data Protection Act, 2023, supported by an independent authority. While NIRA has published a privacy policy, there is little public information on security audits, technical standards or how breaches are reported. Across East Africa governments are expanding digital service delivery; in Kenya the eCitizen portal integrates thousands of services. Somalia's eAqoonsi points in a similar direction but from a more constrained starting point shaped by limited infrastructure, uneven internet access and incomplete national ID coverage.
