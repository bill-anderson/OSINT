# Literature catch-up: African DPI, digital transformation & data governance

**Window: 2026-01-01 to 2026-06-30, UTC — sourced via Exa.** Run 2026-07-14.
**Coverage:** the 107-source literature subset of Bill's registry — 38 academic journals + 69 think tanks/organisations. Big-three countries (Nigeria, South Africa, Kenya) are included but ranked below other countries and regional/continental work, per the standing watchlist rule.

---

## The half-year in one read

Two currents dominate the six months. First, **payments and identity DPI moved from pilots to policy**: AfricaNenda's SIIPS 2025 recorded 36 live instant-payment systems and the first African IPS to reach "mature" inclusivity, while central-bank playbooks, a proposed pan-African Payment Service Directive, and live foundational-ID milestones (Benin's WURI rollout, Ethiopia's Fayda self-assessment, São Tomé's citizen portal, Somalia's civil registration) show the building blocks being wired into government. Second, **data-protection law is quietly becoming Africa's de facto AI-governance instrument** — the FPF's "second wave" analysis, a run of new laws (Burundi, Djibouti, Gambia), and a wave of national AI bills and strategies (Kenya's AI Bill 2026, South Africa's short-lived draft AI Policy, Zimbabwe's AI Strategy) all point the same way.

Underneath, three tensions recur: **sovereignty vs. dependency** (compute, cross-border data flows under the AfCFTA, pushback on importing India's DPI model, mineral-data rights); **inclusion vs. surveillance** (election-year data governance in Uganda, SIM/ID centralisation in Kenya, biometric reliance without safeguards); and **connectivity as the unglamorous floor** under all of it (internet-resilience frameworks, peering economics, localisation of hosting, and a spate of election-period shutdowns). The academic journals were strikingly thin on Africa-specific, in-window work — only seven qualifying articles, mostly South African public-sector digital-transformation studies — so the intellectual running this half-year was made by the think tanks and organisations, not the peer-reviewed press.

A note on the journals: several titles had 2026 issues built from articles first posted online in 2023–2025 (out of window), and *Information Technology for Development* migrated off its Taylor & Francis platform on 1 Jan 2026, so its 2026 content could not be verified on the old domain. Details in the "nothing new" list.
