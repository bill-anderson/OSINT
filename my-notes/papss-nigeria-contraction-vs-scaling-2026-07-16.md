# PAPSS Nigeria: contraction or scaling? — provenance check

**As-of date: 16 July 2026.** Search engine: Exa. Prepared for the "Exa Ad-hoc searches" project.

## Bottom line

The "contraction vs scaling" framing is a false binary — both are true at different levels of the system, and they do not contradict.

* **Nigeria-specific, H1 2025:** a genuine pullback. The one figure on file (₦5.6bn, −53%) is confirmed and is **not** an FX/denomination artifact — transaction *count* also fell 29%, and the naira was flat-to-stronger over the period, so the real-terms (USD) decline is essentially as large as the naira decline (\~53%).
* **Continental PAPSS:** unambiguously scaling, and this is now sourced (it was a synthesised narrative before). Country coverage, bank count and aggregate volume are all rising fast through mid-2026.
* **The important caveat:** ₦5.6bn is only \~US$3.7m for six months. That is far too small to be "Nigeria's PAPSS cross-border flows" in aggregate for the platform's self-described largest participant, on a rail doing $600m+ per quarter continentally. The CBN figure is almost certainly a **narrow line item**, not total Nigerian throughput. Treat it as one specific, small-scoped metric — not a proxy for whether Nigeria is on or off the rail.

## 1\. The contraction figure — verified, with one unresolved ambiguity

The ₦5.6bn / 53% figure traces to the **Central Bank of Nigeria Financial Stability Report for the period ending 30 June 2025** (H1 2025). Every write-up attributes it to that report; the report itself was not directly retrievable (the CBN FSR index page returned no document), so the provenance is still one step removed — news coverage of the CBN report, as flagged in the original task.

What the report reportedly shows for Nigerian participants in H1 2025:

|Metric|H1 2025|Base period|Change|
|-|-|-|-|
|Transaction value|₦5.6bn|₦11.97bn|**−53%**|
|Transaction count|3,246|4,600|**−29%**|
|Implied avg ticket|₦1.73m|₦2.60m|−34%|

**Unresolved ambiguity — what the base period is.** The press disagrees with itself. Vanguard's report says the ₦11.97bn/4,600 base is *"the preceding half-year"* (i.e. H2 2024, a half-on-half comparison). Mobile Money Africa, Bam Magazine and others say it is *"the corresponding period of 2024, H1'24"* (a year-on-year comparison). These imply different things about seasonality, and only the primary CBN table resolves it. It does not change the direction or rough magnitude, but flag it before quoting the comparison basis.

## 2\. Real volume drop, not an FX/denomination effect — decisive

The naira leg fell 53%. Two independent checks show this is a real contraction in usage, not a currency-denomination illusion:

* **The count fell 29%.** Transaction count is currency-independent. Fewer actual transactions were settled. A pure denomination effect would leave the count flat and move only the naira value. It did not.
* **FX pushes the other way.** Over H1 2025 the naira was flat-to-appreciating (official rate \~₦1,475–1,530/$, parallel market +5.7% in H1; 2024 average \~₦1,480, 2025 average \~₦1,528). A stable/stronger naira means the naira figure is *not* being deflated by devaluation. Converting to USD under either base assumption gives a **52–55% USD decline** — essentially identical to the naira decline:

  * base = H2'24 (\~₦1,570): US$7.6m → US$3.6m, −52.5%
  * base = H1'24 (\~₦1,480): US$8.1m → US$3.6m, −55.2%

**Verdict: genuine volume contraction.** If anything, the naira number slightly *understates* the real-terms drop rather than exaggerating it. The separate 34% fall in average ticket size means the mix also shifted toward smaller transactions — consistent with (though not proof of) the April 2025 CBN rule change that eased documentation for low-value transactions ($2,000 individual / $5,000 corporate), or with a loss of a few large corporate flows. That split can't be disaggregated without the primary data.

## 3\. The scale mismatch — the finding that matters most

₦5.6bn ≈ **US$3.7m for a full half-year.** Set against the continental picture:

* PAPSS processed **US$600m+ per quarter by Q1 2026**, up from \~US$200m/quarter in 2023 (TechCabal 4-year review; Algeriatech).
* Nigeria is described in every account as **"the largest participating country,"** with 22+ integrated banks.

US$3.7m/half-year for the *largest* participant cannot be reconciled with US$600m/quarter continentally if ₦5.6bn were Nigeria's total PAPSS activity. So it is not. The most likely readings — pending the primary CBN table — are that the figure captures only a specific slice: transactions *originated* by Nigerian participants and settled through the CBN's own reporting window / naira leg, or one transaction category, rather than all inbound-plus-outbound Nigeria-linked PAPSS value. **The practical implication: do not treat ₦5.6bn as "Nigeria's cross-border flows." It is a narrow, small-denominator metric, which is also why a modest absolute swing shows up as a dramatic-looking 53%.**

## 4\. The scaling reading — now sourced (previously a synthesised narrative)

The countervailing "continental rail is scaling" reading now has multiple dated sources, not just a synthesised story:

* **Country coverage roughly tripling.** Live in 19–20 countries by early 2026 (TechCabal, 31 Mar 2026); Algeria joined Aug 2025 as the 18th central bank; **BEAC (CEMAC, 6 Central African states) joined 9 July 2026, taking coverage to 28 countries, 190+ banks and 250+ institutions** (Ecofin Agency, Business Verge, 10 Jul 2026); BCEAO (Francophone West Africa) expected later in 2026.
* **Volume \~3× since 2023** — US$600m+/quarter by Q1 2026 vs \~US$200m/quarter in 2023.
* **Reach** — 500m+ mobile money users brought into the ecosystem per Afreximbank's 2025 annual report (High Street Journal, 10 Jul 2026).
* **CEO framing** — Mike Ogbalu III cites "2,000% growth," targets 80% of African commercial payments within five years (BFT / Africa Briefing, late Jan 2026). Treat these as directional advocacy figures, not audited.
* **Nigeria-specific tailwinds that post-date the H1 dip** — April 2025 documentation easing; the **Nigeria–Ghana wallet corridor launched Feb 2026** (first wallet-based outbound pilot from Nigeria).

## 5\. Is there later data confirming contraction or rebound?

**No post-H1 2025 Nigeria figure is available as of 16 July 2026.** The next data point is the **CBN Financial Stability Report covering H2 2025** (would be dated \~31 Dec 2025); it has not surfaced with a PAPSS line. So the Nigeria-specific series still ends at the H1 2025 dip — we cannot yet confirm a rebound. The reforms and the Feb 2026 Nigeria–Ghana corridor are plausible catalysts for a recovery, but that is forward-looking, not in the data. **Watch item: the H2 2025 CBN FSR PAPSS table** — it both extends the Nigeria series and, if it carries footnotes, would resolve the scope question in §3.
