## Summary

Over the past three years, the Central African Republic (CAR), a deeply unstable country in the grip of transnational organized crime and foreign state influence, has implemented a series of cryptocurrency ventures that endanger its economic future and create an open invitation to foreign criminal organizations to capture the country and its economy. Launched in the midst of a war waged by Russian mercenaries and national armed forces against civilian populations perceived to be affiliated with rebel groups, the CAR government claimed that its cryptocurrency programme was designed for the benefit of the people and would boost economic growth and infrastructure development across the country. But that statement seems far from reasonable. An impoverished population, exposed to mass executions, torture and gang rape, with limited access to electricity, mobile phones and the internet, cannot engage in crypto investments in any meaningful way. Instead, the cryptocurrency programme launched by the CAR government appears to be tailored more to the interests of foreign investors than to the needs of its own population.

Cryptocurrencies are known to be exploited by transnational criminal networks for money laundering, drug trafficking, fraud, ransomware, sanctions evasion and the financing of terrorism, and the CAR's cryptocurrency initiatives lack the regulation and oversight needed to protect the system from such illicit financial flows. The CAR's two main crypto initiatives, first **Sango Coin** and then the **$CAR meme coin**, were launched hastily and appear to have performed poorly.

The country's crypto policies — particularly the decision to make cryptocurrency legal tender — have been criticized by international financial institutions, regional banking regulators and the country's own constitutional court. In particular, a **July 2023 law that facilitates the tokenization of national resources** — allowing digital tokens issued on a blockchain to represent ownership rights, usage rights or revenue streams linked to oil, gold, timber or land — lacks critical governance safeguards and threatens the country's sovereignty. The involvement of foreign private individuals who have been charged with or implicated in fraud and other criminal acts in the development of the country's crypto programme further undermines its already dubious credibility.

Ultimately, the CAR's cryptocurrency initiatives — incongruously imagined, poorly executed and riddled with red flags — in effect favour elite, and possibly criminal, interests at the expense of the wider population. Rather than being a means for monetary innovation, they are yet another avenue through which the country can be captured and exploited so that those in power can maintain their status. In recent years, the space for independent research and civil society in the CAR has significantly shrunk. Access to information has become increasingly limited.
