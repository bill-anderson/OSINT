# Africa's data is worth $400bn. We're giving it away

Opinion by Sam Tayengwa, Group Executive: Global Markets, Mettus — ITWeb Africa, 8 May 2026

Africa is now the world's largest producer of the most valuable currency of the 21st century — data — and has no reserve to back it. Across fifteen African markets, policymakers speak about "leveraging data," but nobody is pricing it as a tradeable, internationally recognised asset. Conservative Western platform valuations suggest Africa's ~600 million active digital citizens generate data worth $30–60 billion annually, largely flowing offshore. Including alternative data (800 million monthly mobile money transactions, agricultural patterns, a $600 billion informal economy), the author argues the asset class is worth $200–400 billion annually.

The author proposes five structural pillars for a continental "data reserve":

- **Identity infrastructure is the mint** — Ghana's National Identification Authority and Rwanda's foundational digital identity layer show what a biometrically anchored identity ecosystem unlocks; the rest of the continent must accelerate.
- **Data governance is the central bank** — 36 African countries now have data protection legislation, but legislation without enforcement, a continental interoperability framework, or a shared data sovereignty doctrine is a central bank with no tools. Africa needs a *Continental Data Governance Compact* establishing ownership, consent, cross-border data flow rules, and value-sharing.
- **Bureau interoperability is the exchange rate mechanism** — a Kenyan with 15 years of credit history becomes financially invisible in Tanzania. A Unified Credit Information Platform (being architected in Tanzania) is a proof of concept for federated bureau infrastructure.
- **AI is the refinery** — turning unstructured African data into tradeable creditworthiness signals.
- **National Data Trusts are the sovereign wealth fund** — sovereign vehicles licensing enriched, privacy-preserving, aggregated data products. The author models initial annual licensing revenue of $15–40 billion, rising to $200–400 billion at maturity.

The framing: Africa watched this story with minerals and agricultural commodities and cannot afford to repeat it with data.
