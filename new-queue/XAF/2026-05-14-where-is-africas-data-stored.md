# Where Is Africa's Data Actually Being Stored?

By Joyce Onyeagoro — TechAfrica News (#TechTalkThursday), 14 May 2026

When a banking transaction is processed in Lagos or a payroll system runs in Cape Town, the data often leaves the continent before the task is complete — routed through subsea cables to global hubs: Frankfurt, London, Amsterdam, Paris, Dublin, and increasingly Northern Virginia. This is the contradiction at the centre of Africa's digital sovereignty ambitions: over the past decade more than 40 African countries have introduced data protection and localization frameworks, yet estimates from the IFC and GSMA suggest **more than 80% of African data is hosted outside Africa**.

The issue is not a lack of legislative ambition but the absence of enough local infrastructure. Europe's major internet hubs became default destinations because they offered reliable power, dense connectivity, mature ecosystems, and stability; local alternatives often struggle with power instability, limited rack density, cooling challenges, and financing constraints.

The capacity gap is stark: Africa's estimated demand for data-centre infrastructure is approaching **1,000 megawatts**, while active installed capacity remains closer to **360 megawatts** — nearly half of it in South Africa alone. That leaves governments mandating local data residency while lacking the infrastructure to support it, making sovereignty difficult to enforce physically even where it exists legally. The article argues the real challenge is no longer legislation but whether the continent can build enough power, infrastructure, and regional trust to keep its data — and eventually its AI systems — at home.
