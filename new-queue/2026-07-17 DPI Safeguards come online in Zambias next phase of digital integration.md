Zambia embarked on its digital public infrastructure (DPI) journey to ensure that all adults among its 20 million population have a national digital ID. The Department of National Registration, Passport, and Citizenship (DNRPC) has digitized nearly 81 percent of legacy civil registration records and enrolled 1.5 million people with biometrics by building the Integrated National Registration Information System (INRIS). Currently, over 88 percent of adults aged 16 and older have a physical National Registration Card (NRC)—a relatively high coverage for a Sub-Saharan African country.

Zambia is now in the strategy and design stage of its DPI life cycle, a crucial phase for shaping robust institutional structures around its digital ID systems. Before deploying the system, DNRPC and other government agencies dedicated resources to ensure the appropriate governance and technical capacity were in place. As part of its commitment to a safe and inclusive digital transformation, in June 2024 Zambia joined the 50-in-5 campaign.

## Strategies for system transition challenges

Technical risks: Migration risks from a legacy NRC system to a new digital ID system, with a transition period where both systems run simultaneously; Infrastructure risks around biometric registration kits, secure data centres and connectivity; Interoperability risks and data protection concerns given that multiple agencies issue their own IDs and collect biometric data using different AFIS and ABIS; Internet penetration risk, as Zambia only has a 56.8 percent internet penetration.

Operational risks: Data safety and privacy risks from breaches and inconsistent data management as biometric information is collected among multiple agencies. Coordination risks among the Ministry of Home Affairs and Internal Security and the Smart Zambia Institute. Digital ID coverage gap: only 1.5 out of 9 million people have been enrolled with biometrics, although 89 percent have a paper-based NRC. Stakeholder commitment and training requirements—training staff in over 145 offices across 10 provinces on the Data Protection Act.

Normative risks: Legal framework updates to the National Registration Act, Citizenship Act, and other regulations; harmonizing data across acts and institutions; and refugee and asylum-seeker inclusion—Zambia hosts over 105,000 refugees, asylum-seekers, and other persons of concern.

## Addressing risks and a path forward

In June 2024, the Ministry of Home Affairs and Internal Security launched the Legal Digital ID Model Governance Assessment, designed by UNDP, which engages government and civil society to evaluate key governance areas for Zambia's digital ID implementation. It marks the beginning of putting the Universal DPI Safeguards principles into practice.

The DNRPC is actively addressing these risks through the next phase of the INRIS project plan which will focus on public awareness campaigns, establishing registration points near populated communities and introducing a unique legal ID number. The old system will be phased out, systems will be integrated with an API manager and child registration for early legal identity will be improved.

A civil society member who participated in the model governance assessment in June, Mbuyu Makayi, Technical Lead at Chipper Cash and Community Member at the BongoHive Technology and Innovation Hub, said: "Working in the fintech industry has made me realize the critical importance of digital identities. They eliminate the friction users experience by enabling compliant, quick and easy onboarding... I am pleased that we are moving towards digital identities, as this will streamline customer onboarding and enhance the Zambian financial sector's efforts to root out bad actors through a single source of truth."
