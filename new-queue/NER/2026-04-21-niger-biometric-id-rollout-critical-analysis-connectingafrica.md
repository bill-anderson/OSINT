# Niger's rollout of biometric IDs gets a thumbs up

Analytical trade-press piece (Daniel Itai, Connecting Africa) on Niger's biometric ID/e-passport rollout, with both endorsement and critical framing.

Facts: biometric IDs now **compulsory for citizens aged 15+**, **10-year validity**; the Dec-2025 Council-of-Ministers resolution, March-2026 enrolment launch and this-month government enrolments; cards feature multifunctional electronic chips storing biometric data for verification across government services, banking and cross-border operations within the AES (Mali, Niger, Burkina Faso). Cost reportedly **~US$6** — potentially beyond reach of many, especially rural residents. **Internet penetration in Niger stood at 23.2% at end-2025**, which "might result in it taking several years before every citizen obtains a biometric ID."

Critical commentary from researcher/advocate **Sani Suleiman**: the technological expansion "comes with profound risks due to weak legal frameworks, limited oversight and a growing reliance on foreign vendors," creating "an ecosystem vulnerable to privacy breaches, state surveillance and systemic exclusion." He warns that biometric systems "increasingly integrate electoral and civil identity data, giving governments vast surveillance capabilities while disenfranchising marginalized groups such as rural communities, migrants, and individuals without foundational IDs," and that fragmented rollout forces repeated submission of sensitive data. Political commentator **Garang Wal** frames the transition as crucial for AES regional integration (Tiani as first enrollee).
