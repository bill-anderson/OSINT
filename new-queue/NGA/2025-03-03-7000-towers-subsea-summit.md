# Nigeria to build 7,000 telecom towers, hosts subsea cable summit

Matshepo Sehloho, Associate Editor, Connecting Africa — 3 March 2025

The Federal Government of Nigeria will build 7,000 telecoms towers across rural areas and is advocating for more investment in subsea cable systems to reinforce resilience.

Nigeria has announced plans to build 7,000 telecom towers across rural areas to bridge the country's connectivity gap.

The announcement comes as the country hosted the International Submarine Cable Resilience Summit in Abuja last week, where delegates discussed investment in new cable systems to reinforce resilience.

Dr. Bosun Tijani, the country's Minister of Communications, Innovation, and Digital Economy, announced that the Federal Executive Council (FEC) had approved the construction of 7,000 new towers as part of a broader strategy to bridge the digital divide.

He said that the investment would complement the ongoing deployment of 90,000km of fiber-optic cables across the country.

Tijani said that the government is committed to ensuring that all Nigerians, especially those in underserved areas, have meaningful access to quality telecommunication services.

He added that investing in rural infrastructure aligns with the administration's broader objective of enhancing digital inclusion and expanding economic opportunities for all citizens.

"The priority for this government is meaningful access. We don't want our people to just have access to telecommunication services; we want it to be of high quality," he explained.

"That is why the Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC) has been working thoroughly to ensure that we shift the focus not just to quality of service, but to quality of experience," the minister said.

## ITU releases subsea cable resilience guidelines

The International Submarine Cable Resilience Summit in Abuja saw delegates adopting a joint declaration recommending investment in new cable systems across different nations.

Moreover, delegates issued a declaration committing to international efforts to reinforce subsea cable resilience, focusing on actions ranging from increased cooperation to technical advancements.

The two-day event was organized by Nigeria's Ministry of Communications, Innovation, and Digital Economy, in partnership with the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) and the International Cable Protection Committee (ICPC).

"Submarine telecommunications cables are a fundamental backbone of our interconnected world. Entire economies feel the impact of disruptions to service," said ITU Secretary-General Doreen Bogdan-Martin.

Minister Tijani said that with an average of 150 to 200 faults reported globally each year, subsea cable disruptions impact economies, access to information and public services, and the daily lives of billions of people.

"This summit and its declaration underscore our commitment to safeguarding the infrastructure that our digital economy is built upon. As a key landing point for submarine cables in Africa, Nigeria is well-placed to contribute to and help shape global efforts to enhance resilience," added Tijani.
