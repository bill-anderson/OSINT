Namibia's Ministry of Information and Communication Technology (MICT) has partnered with Angola to use the Angosat-2 satellite to improve connectivity in the country's remote areas where terrestrial infrastructure remains limited.

The initiative is being spearheaded through a joint effort between Telecom Namibia and Angola's Space Management Office (GGPEN).

MICT said the partnership will explore the potential of satellite connectivity to deliver Internet and voice services to remote areas.

Namibia's MICT Minister Emma Theofelus, during an interministerial phone call between the two countries, added that strengthening this cooperation will expand Namibia's communication alternatives.

Discussions between the two parties began in June 2025 and included the launch of a proof of concept (PoC) to assess the satellite's performance in meeting Namibia's connectivity needs.

This included providing broadband Internet services to remote areas, backhaul solutions to strengthen Telecom Namibia's network infrastructure and cost-effective alternatives to the current reliance on terrestrial systems.

## Angosat-2 connecting underserved communities

Launched in 2023, Angosat-2 is a high-throughput telecommunications satellite (HTS) operating in geostationary orbit.

Angosat-2 delivers C, Ku and Ka-band services, covering the entire African continent and parts of Europe.

It enables high-speed Internet, television and telephone services even in regions lacking terrestrial infrastructure.

In August 2023, Angola initiated technical preparations to extend the satellite's benefits to countries within the Southern African Development Community (SADC).

## Network improvements

Namibia using Angosat-2's services comes at a time when many countries on the continent are either signing deals or launching projects aimed at connecting underserved communities.

In August 2025 Mobile Telecommunications Limited (MTC) launched a strategic rural network optimization initiative called Buffalo Project aimed at boosting rural connectivity in northern Namibia.

Telecoms operator Airtel Congo and Wing Wah, a private Congolese oil company and subsidiary of the Chinese group Southernpec, partnered to deploy a private telecom network at the Banga Kayo oil field in Kouilou in Congo-Brazzaville also in August 2025.

Earlier in August 2025, Airtel Africa signed a strategic infrastructure sharing agreement with Vodacom Group in Mozambique, Tanzania and the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC).

Congo-Brazzaville's Electronic Communications Regulatory Agency (ARPCE) and the Angolan Institute of Communications (INACOM) signed a frequency coordination agreement on their common border, also last week.

In early August 2025, Liberia and Sierra Leone announced the rollout of the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) free roaming initiative, designed to ease cross-border mobile roaming costs.

In March 2025, MTN Group and Airtel Africa agreed to share network infrastructure in Uganda and Nigeria.

In January 2025, Vodacom and Orange announced an infrastructure sharing deal in the DRC, pledging to jointly construct up to 2,000 solar-powered basestations over the next six years.

Similarly, in January 2024, Algerian operators Algérie Télécom (AT) and Ooredoo Algeria agreed to share infrastructure and exchange ideas on innovative services.
