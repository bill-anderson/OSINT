# Six Francophone West African Countries Unite on AI Governance Framework

By : Ange J. A de Berry Quenum
Date : jeudi, 09 juillet 2026 13:33

Benin, Burkina Faso, Côte d'Ivoire, Guinea, Mali and Senegal have adopted shared AI governance guidelines to promote ethical, inclusive and responsible artificial intelligence.

The framework recommends transparent AI systems, stronger data protection, local innovation and digital sovereignty while complementing, rather than replacing, national AI strategies.

The initiative aligns with the African Union's continental AI strategy and has been submitted to the United Nations' inaugural Global AI Dialogue as a written contribution.

Burkina Faso, Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, Mali, Guinea and Benin have adopted common guidelines designed to promote ethical, inclusive and responsible artificial intelligence.

Advocacy organization Niyel led the initiative, which it announced on July 6 on the sidelines of the Global AI Dialogue. The framework aims to help governments design AI policies that reflect African realities while strengthening regional cooperation on AI governance.

"The artificial intelligence revolution is accelerating, and with it every nation faces a choice: either undergo this transformation or become an active participant in shaping it," Aminata Zerbo/Sabane, Burkina Faso's Minister of Digital Transition, Posts and Electronic Communications, said.

She added that the guidelines should enable African countries to build a governance framework based on their own values, languages and development priorities.

Researchers, technical experts and senior officials responsible for digital affairs in the six countries jointly developed the document. The framework serves as a reference to guide AI policies and governance frameworks. It recommends the development of transparent, safe and inclusive AI systems that respect fundamental rights and reflect local contexts.

In addition, the document emphasizes stronger data protection, skills development, mitigation of algorithmic bias, support for local innovation and the promotion of digital sovereignty.

The initiative comes as several participating countries have already begun to formalize national AI policies. Senegal and Côte d'Ivoire have already adopted national AI strategies, while the other participating countries continue to develop or strengthen their governance frameworks.

The Niyel guidelines do not replace those national strategies. Instead, they provide a common foundation that encourages convergence around principles including ethics, inclusion, transparency and digital sovereignty.

The initiative comes as international efforts to establish AI governance frameworks continue to accelerate. UNESCO adopted its Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence in 2021. Subsequently, the African Union launched its Continental Artificial Intelligence Strategy in 2024, encouraging member states to develop policies aligned with Africa's priorities.

Niyel also submitted the guidelines as a written contribution to the United Nations' first Global AI Dialogue on Governance.

Beyond ethical considerations, the framework's promoters said artificial intelligence could help address development challenges in education, healthcare, agriculture and public services.

However, they warned that uncontrolled adoption of AI solutions developed outside Africa could deepen technological dependence and reproduce algorithmic biases that fail to reflect African realities. They said the common guidelines aim to provide Francophone African countries with a shared reference framework for developing AI policies while strengthening regional cooperation.
