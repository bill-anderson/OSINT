Brownfield implementations of MOSIP emerged as a prominent topic at MOSIP Connect 2026 in Rabat, Morocco. Uganda's NIRA has completed the first brownfield implementation of MOSIP, migrating more than 28 million records to its new identity platform, even as it added some 6.7 million new registrations. (Sierra Leone is migrating since a 2024 pilot; Nigeria is undertaking the largest brownfield MOSIP implementation yet, ~120 million people.)

NIRA Executive Director Rosemary Kisembo credited Neurotechnology, which provided Uganda's legacy ABIS, with easing the migration. The biggest problem was vendors considering their product only up to the point of delivery. Loading software from technoforte and Tech5 onto the ~5,000 biometric kits to make them compliant with MOSIP's Secure Biometric Interface (SBI) protocol was one necessary post-procurement step. Uganda issues two distinct ID cards — one for citizens, one for foreign nationals — a capability not native to MOSIP but built by NIRA to comply with Ugandan law.

On biometrics: NIRA deduplicated multiple biometric databases while the legacy platform was still in service, populating a staging environment and graduating records to production only after validation. NIRA found about 200,000 duplicates over ~7 months. The timeframe was compressed by the need to be ready for the national election; NIRA accelerated from ~8,000 records/day to a goal of over 300,000/day. Uganda ran out of old ID cards in May 2025 — urgency Kisembo called "a blessing in disguise." Uganda had unencrypted legacy images checked for quality by the new vendor, avoiding recalling millions to re-enroll biometrics.
