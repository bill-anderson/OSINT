NIRA has dismissed public concerns over the mass collection of personal data during the ongoing national identity card renewal exercise, saying it is legal, secure and necessary for national planning and service delivery. Speaking at Uganda Police Force headquarters in Naguru, Kampala, on June 23, 2025, NIRA registrar Claire Ollama reassured Ugandans that all personal information gathered is protected under the Data Protection and Privacy Act.

"Each employee of NIRA signs an oath committing to safeguard citizens' data. We only share information on a need-to-know basis, for example, when a bank seeks to verify your National Identification Number (NIN). No other personal data is shared," Ollama said. She addressed fears around requests for tribe and origin, stating this is essential to confirming Ugandan citizenship, especially in border districts where indigenous communities span borders — "When someone tells us they are Alur from Zombo, we can confirm they are Ugandan, as opposed to an Alur from Mahagi in Congo."

The number who have renewed National IDs rose to 3.5 million, up from 2.5 million the previous week — "over a million renewals in one week alone." She emphasised the goal is that every Ugandan has a secure, verifiable identity essential for access to government services: "Your ID is not just a card; it is a key to services such as healthcare, education, banking, inheritance, and social protection."

Ollama urged parents to prioritise registering children, warning of complications with intestate inheritance and DNA tests otherwise. NIRA extended operations to weekends. She clarified no parent should be turned away for lacking baptism cards or hospital birth records: "if you know your child's details and are willing to sign a declaration, your own NIN is enough. Citizenship is by descent."
