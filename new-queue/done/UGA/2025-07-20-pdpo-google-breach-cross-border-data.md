The Personal Data Protection Office (PDPO) has found Google in breach of Uganda's data protection laws. In a ruling dated July 18, 2025, acting national PDPO director Baker Birikujja found Google in breach for operating without PDPO registration and for transferring Ugandans' data abroad without demonstrating adequate safeguards. The ruling followed a November 8, 2024 complaint by Frank Ssekamwa, Pamela Sharon Leni, Raymond Amumpaire and Mercy Awino.

Google was given 30 days to register as a data collector, controller and/or processor under the Data Protection and Privacy Act Cap 97, provide its Data Protection Officer's contact details, and submit documentary evidence of its cross-border transfer compliance framework (legal basis and accountability under Section 19 and Regulation 30). No data-localisation order was made, but Birikujja stressed all cross-border transfers must comply with Ugandan law. He declined to award compensation (PDPO lacks that authority; claims go to court under Section 33(1)).

Non-compliance, absent an appeal within 30 days, is an offence under Regulation 48 (fine up to three currency points / ~sh60,000 per day, or up to six months imprisonment). Appeals lie to the Minister for ICT within 30 days. Birikujja emphasised the law applies extraterritorially to any entity monetising Ugandan users, citing Google's local tax presence. In its July 10, 2025 response Google acknowledged processing Ugandan users' data but argued its corporate entities are separate and that Section 19/Regulation 30 apply only to controllers/processors domiciled in Uganda, which Google LLC is not. PDPO — established as an independent office under NITA-U — had acted following a major 2022 Uganda Securities Exchange data breach.
