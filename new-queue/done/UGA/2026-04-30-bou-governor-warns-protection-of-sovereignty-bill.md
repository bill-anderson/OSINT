Bank of Uganda (BoU) governor Michael Atingi-Ego warned Parliament that the proposed Protection of Sovereignty Bill, 2026, could introduce significant risks to Uganda's financial system. Appearing before joint committees on April 27, 2026, he cautioned the Bill "risks introducing regulatory fragmentation and 'voluntary shocks' into the economy." A key concern is a proposed cap on foreign funding of 20,000 currency points (~sh400 million / ~$106,000) annually, beyond which ministerial approval is required — incompatible with the scale of Uganda's banking sector (Tier 1 capital requirements are sh150 billion).

The governor warned of risks to Uganda's external position (2024/25 BoP surplus of $1.5b, FDI $3.4b), diaspora remittances ($1.5b in 2025) and NGO inflows ($420.8 million); Clause 1's definition of "foreigner" includes Ugandans abroad, risking classifying remittance recipients as "agents of foreigners." He flagged risks of offshore investor exit, "de-risking" isolating Uganda from global payment systems, and impact on the digital economy — Uganda has 36.3 million mobile money accounts handling ~27 million transactions daily, incompatible with the Bill's manual approval processes. Roughly Sh5 trillion in external funding and 20,000–50,000 jobs are linked to potentially affected organisations.
