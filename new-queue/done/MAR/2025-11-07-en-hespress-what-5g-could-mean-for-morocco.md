# What 5G could mean for Morocco's economy and its people

Friday 7 November 2025 - 20:33

Morocco's three major telecom operators switched on 5G today, casting the rollout as economic infrastructure meant to boost industry and bridge digital gaps. The government sees the network as an instrument of competitiveness, a productive asset for factories, logistics zones and public administration. Morocco's export sectors depend on timing: a car part delayed in Kenitra or a container stalled at Tanger Med can ripple across Europe's supply chains, and 5G's promise of lower latency fits that logic.

The GSM Association expects mobile-enabled technologies to add $11 trillion to global GDP by 2030. Private 5G systems in European ports have reported productivity increases of about 20 percent and capital savings near 15 percent compared with Wi-Fi networks, according to Ericsson case analyses. A UK government-commissioned review found that a ten-percent increase in penetration correlates with GDP growth of between 0.6 and 2.8 percent.

Morocco's regulator is using regulatory stability rather than public funds to drive its 5G rollout. ANRT's July 2025 licensing round set moderate one-off fees—900 million dirhams for Maroc Telecom and 600 million each for Orange Maroc and Inwi—alongside coverage targets of about 25 percent by 2026 and 70 percent by 2030. Operators have since created joint ventures, tying investment to shared assets, but this risks uneven access as operators weigh costs in low-density regions. ANRT plans to assess operators' progress after the first operational year.

Ericsson's Mobility Report forecasts that by 2030, about 350 million households worldwide will receive broadband through fixed wireless access, more than 80 percent relying on 5G—useful for towns beyond fiber's reach. Across Africa, 5G makes up about 1.2 percent of mobile subscriptions and covers around 11 percent of the population, constrained by high handset prices and limited spectrum, according to the ITU.
