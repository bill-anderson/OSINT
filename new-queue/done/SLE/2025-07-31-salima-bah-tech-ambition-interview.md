Interview by Ganiu Oloruntade with Sierra Leone's Minister of Communication, Technology, and Innovation, Salima Bah. Since the ministry's creation (less than a year old at first interview ~Feb 2024), the government invested more than $132 million directly into technology and innovation, covering policy, infrastructure, and projects like the Universal Access Development Fund. IMF forecasts report internet users rose from 1.84 million in early 2023 to around 2.32 million in 2025. In June 2025 the country unveiled an open-access 5G standalone network.

Key points from Bah:
- Since 2018, over 75% increase in fibre backbone penetration and international bandwidth capacity; rural mobile sites almost doubled.
- Resilience improved by interconnecting with Guinea (emergency bandwidth allocation).
- The undersea fibre cable, formerly managed by state vehicle SALCAB, was privatised; the private partner expanded capacity from 90Gbps to over 500Gbps, built a Freetown metro network, and launched OneMobile, a data-only mobile network on open-access 5G infrastructure ("the first in West Africa").
- Sierra Leone sustains three (now a fourth) mobile operators for a population of ~8 million.

Homegrown government platforms: an HR Hub (geolocates civil-servant attendance) and WanGov, intended as a single-window platform for all government services (apply online, pay, receive documents via national postal service).

On AI: Sierra Leone is conducting an AI Readiness Assessment (infrastructure, policy, data governance, human capacity, startup ecosystem) to inform its national strategy, and supports Smart Africa's AI council launched in Rwanda. The 2018 National Innovation and Digital Strategy positioned Sierra Leone as an "AI lab." Partnering with the World Bank on an AI Innovation Challenge and with MIT on a government AI training program.
