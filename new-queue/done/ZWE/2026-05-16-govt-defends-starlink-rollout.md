The government has defended the entry of satellite internet provider Starlink into Zimbabwe's telecommunications sector, arguing that increased competition is key to expanding connectivity and supporting the ambition to become a digitally driven economy by 2030.

During a National Assembly Q&A session, Witness Jonga asked whether Zimbabwe's growing population (over 15 million per the 2022 census) and mobile usage required more service providers, noting only Econet, NetOne and Telecel serve millions, with Telecel struggling. ICT minister Tatenda Mavetera said the focus was not on increasing the number of telecom companies but on widening access through infrastructure expansion and new technologies. Starlink's licensing was part of broader efforts to open up the sector and improve access in underserved rural communities.

Mavetera said the government was working with TelOne, NetOne and Econet on broadband mapping to identify poor-coverage areas, and was implementing a "tower relocation" programme funded through the Universal Services Fund (administered by POTRAZ) to redistribute infrastructure from overserviced urban areas to marginalised communities.

Legislators warned Starlink remained unaffordable for many: Spencer Tshuma cited subscription costs rising from around US$65 to more than US$100, and questioned reliance on foreign-owned internet while local-currency payment options remained limited. Mavetera acknowledged the concerns but said competition was already forcing local providers to cut tariffs. The debate expanded into data sovereignty and dependence on foreign technology; Judith Tobaiwa challenged the government on measures to develop local solutions. Mavetera pointed to hackathons, ideathons and the recently launched National AI Strategy, including an "AI Grand Challenge" for Zimbabwean innovators and students.
