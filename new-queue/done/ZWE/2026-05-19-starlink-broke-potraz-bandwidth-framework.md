Zimbabwe's Q1 2025 POTRAZ report contains a number that should not be possible: used incoming international internet bandwidth stood at 2,693,127 Mbps, while total equipped international bandwidth capacity was 1,433,865 Mbps — usage exceeding installed capacity by 1,259,262 Mbps. The explanation is Starlink, and it reveals the limits of how Zimbabwe's regulator measured its own market.

Starlink launched in Zimbabwe in September 2024 after years of regulatory obstruction. By Q4 2024, VSAT subscriptions had jumped 513.79% to 23,410; by Q1 2025 there were 30,907 active terminals. The Q1 report attributed 83% of the country's used incoming international bandwidth to Starlink, but did not include Starlink in the equipped capacity table (which lists Liquid, TelOne, Powertel, Dandemutande, Telecontract and Africom). Starlink's 83% share implies roughly 2,235,295 Mbps consumed — 56% more than the entire equipped capacity of all licensed operators combined. Non-Starlink operators used roughly 457,832 Mbps, a coherent ~32% utilisation rate; the Starlink figure exists outside the framework because its satellite network has no Zimbabwean ground infrastructure to measure.

From Q2 2025, POTRAZ added a footnote excluding Starlink's used bandwidth "until a clear position is reached on how to accommodate its capacity," and retroactively restated Q1 to a baseline of 459,157 Mbps. A reader comparing headline figures across quarters, without reading footnotes, sees used bandwidth appear to collapse 81% from Q1 to Q2 — entirely a methodological reclassification. Techzim, reporting the figure in July 2025, had noted the anomaly: "We don't even know how Starlink total available bandwidth and used bandwidth are measured, figures presumably coming from the company itself."
