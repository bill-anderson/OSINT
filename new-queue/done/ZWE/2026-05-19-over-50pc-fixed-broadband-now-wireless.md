At the start of 2025, wireless technologies accounted for 41.84% of Zimbabwe's fixed broadband subscriptions; by the end of the year that figure was 54.54%, with the crossover occurring in Q3. POTRAZ published the numbers; the piece argues Zimbabwe's fixed broadband market structurally became a wireless market within a single calendar year.

Fixed wireless access (FWA), as POTRAZ defines it, is primarily fixed LTE — home internet over cellular infrastructure without a physical cable, distinct from satellite (VSAT/Starlink). Fixed LTE subscriptions grew from 109,504 in Q1 to 143,323 by Q4 (a 30.9% increase), attributed to "accelerated fixed LTE deployment and consumers preferring flexible hardware-light internet solutions."

DSL declined without interruption: TelOne's DSL subscriptions fell from 94,346 (Q1) to 87,713 (Q4), a 7% drop, with ADSL2+ topping out around 4 Mbps and the copper network suffering vandalism (TelOne has acknowledged losses exceeding US$50 million from cable theft alone). Fibre grew from 81,287 (Q1) to 86,225 (Q4), a 6.1% increase — a growth category, but fixed LTE grew five times faster.

Running on a separate axis, Starlink's VSAT subscriptions grew from 30,907 (Q1) to 67,057 (Q4), more than doubling in twelve months. By Q2 2025, Starlink's 40,146 subscribers had consumed 83.9 petabytes of fixed internet traffic — 22.5% of the national total — its impact being pricing disruption in the incumbent ISP market rather than access expansion.
