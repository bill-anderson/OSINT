# Starlink says CRAN licence denial unjustified

Starlink has sharply criticised CRAN's decision to reject its frequency and telecommunications licence, declined on 23 March. CRAN rejected both the class comprehensive telecommunications service licence and the spectrum licence, citing failure to meet the 51% local-ownership threshold, plus compliance concerns (Starlink had previously operated in the country without a licence) and that foreign ownership could impair Namibia's jurisdiction and oversight. ICT Minister Emma Theofelus and CRAN board chairperson Tulimevava Kaunapawa Mufeti said the applicant had met only half of CRAN's requirements.

Starlink director for market access Ben MacWilliams (letter, 23 March): "The overwhelming public support for Starlink's application clearly demonstrates Namibians' urgent demand for innovative, high-speed internet... With over 98% of public comments in support." He called CRAN's explanations misleading, said Starlink had over three years committed to establishing a local company, meeting national-security requirements and paying taxes "in full accordance with its business practices in 164 markets worldwide," and noted the legal framework allows ministerial exceptions — none granted. Starlink said the public-interest concerns reflect "a misunderstanding of how Starlink complies with local laws, protects data sovereignty, and supports national security."
