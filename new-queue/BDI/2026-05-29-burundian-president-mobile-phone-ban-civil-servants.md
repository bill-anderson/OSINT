# Burundian President seeks mobile phone ban for civil servants

Desire Nimubona, Special Correspondent, Connecting Africa. May 29, 2026.

Burundi's President Évariste Ndayishimiye wants to impose a mobile phone ban on government employees during work hours to improve public service performance.

Burundian President Évariste Ndayishimiye is seeking to impose a mobile phone ban on civil servants while at work, citing poor public service performance linked to mobile phone misuse.

"In a recent assessment, the findings show that state workers or civil servants are not working eight hours a day. That is why we have decided that civil servants, without exception, must leave their mobile phones at home and use landlines at the office because it has been discovered that mobile phones distract civil servants," Ndayishimiye said on the sidelines of the CNDD-FDD political party's congress in Gitega.

"Anyone who decides to use their mobile phone at the office is asked to resign and find a job somewhere else. This is a decision that will come into effect as soon as possible to help us accelerate the country's development," Ndayishimiye insisted.

While Burundi wants to restrict mobile phone use, the current mobile phone penetration rate is only 60.7%, making it the lowest among the East African region member states.

Before President Ndayishimiye's statements, discontent was already being voiced in state offices where citizens were complaining about phone misuse.

For some, his declaration represents a will to change public service habits that hamper the country.

According to Martine Nahayo, a patient at a well known public hospital in Bujumbura, there are frequent complaints that healthcare staff don't follow up with patients because of mobile phone use.

"One day, I went to the hospital with my child who had just broken his leg. When I arrived at the emergency room that night, a nurse on duty took a very long time to come to my child's bedside, despite the severe fracture," Nahayo recounted, flagging excessive phone use as the reason for the delay.

"I went to buy a medical dose for my sister who was having amnesia and once there, a pharmacist remained on the telephone, speaking to someone, and I heard that they were talking about useless things. The pharmacist was laughing on the phone, while my sister was in urgent need of the dose," shared Nduwimana Emmanuel, a salesman.

With this decision, the Burundian President aims to restore discipline and rigor to the workplace, as he expressed in a recent speech.

"I really like this decision by the President to limit phone use at work because when you go for important documents in different offices, it takes a long time to be served because workers stay a long time on WhatsApp and other applications just for fun, while we need urgent services," added Ntakamurenga Alexis, a trader.

However, Nahayo believes that some civil servants use mobile phones to make transactions and to monitor their small businesses within the country, such as livestock farming and agriculture, which would make a ban difficult.

## Discipline or backsliding?

The return to landlines in offices is being presented as a matter of discipline, however, according to Faustin Ndikumana, an economist and human rights activist, the decision to ban mobile phones in public spaces would be difficult to implement.

"Indeed, coordination between workers in offices and in the field would be rendered impossible by this decision," Ndikumana explained.

Ndikumana added that some categories of civil servants need to have a mobile phone with them for the efficiency of their work.

He finds it difficult to imagine how a police officer, a soldier, a field worker, or even a minister can work without being able to communicate with anyone, at any time.

State-owned telecommunications provider Onatel has been in financial crisis for years and experts believe that reverting to traditional fixed phone lines would become expensive and reverse digital inclusion gains already made.

"The measure to prohibit the use of mobile phones by public employees during working hours, in favor of landlines, raises significant concerns regarding human rights and digital rights," said Prime Nkezumukama, a digital activist and president of human rights platform Dukingire Isi Yacu (which means protect our earth).

Despite its stated objective of strengthening administrative discipline and productivity, Nkezumukama argued that the measure would limit government employees' access to the Internet, thus violating their rights to information access.

"Since mobile phones are currently the primary means of accessing the Internet, banning them limits access to information, freedom of expression, and the right to private communication. This restriction directly affects fundamental freedoms, especially in a context where digital tools are essential for civic participation," he added.

"While the state can regulate the use of work tools, a total ban on mobile phones is legally precarious and potentially disproportionate. A more balanced approach would be to regulate their use while respecting fundamental rights, including access to information, privacy, and freedom of expression," the digital rights activist added.

## Digital landline alternatives

According to landline and mobile phone expert Hassan Ndayisaba, implementing the President's wish is not without its challenges, given the country's economic situation, and the fact that Onatel, the primary fixed line operator in the country, has been having financial troubles for the past decade.

"Installing a landline would require significant resources because the copper wires used have become prohibitively expensive recently. A ton of copper wire currently costs over US$11,000, whereas it was cheaper 10 years ago in addition to other costly equipment," Ndayisaba argued.

He noted that the installation would also require equipment for telephone exchanges, which are currently outdated and unable to handle the demand.

Ndayisaba explained that these exchanges are located in provincial centers under the old administrative divisions.

With the reduction of provinces from 18 to five in 2025, new installations and configurations will be necessary, resulting in enormous expenses, he said.

Ndayisaba believes that the use of digital alternatives, like Voice over Internet Protocol (VoIP), would be a feasible alternative.

"Onatel can use terminals throughout the country to install digital phones that do not require traditional fixed telephone installations such as copper wires," he said.

He added that even this initiative requires state investment to boost Internet reliability in Burundi, although less investment than that of fixed telephone lines.
