# Bank of Ghana touts inclusion, but more 'architecture' needed to close gaps

*Connecting Africa, by Eden Harris (Chief Special Correspondent), published 12 May 2026.*

At the 3i Africa Summit in Accra, Matilda Asante-Asiedu, second deputy governor of the Bank of Ghana, signaled progress in financial inclusion but called for deeper digital infrastructure.

Matilda Asante-Asiedu, second deputy governor of the Bank of Ghana, said on Friday at the 3i Africa Summit in Accra, that the nation has made gains in financial inclusion, but it must expand its "architecture" investment to further close gaps and attract capital. In her speech, Asante-Asiedu acknowledged the summit's theme of innovation, investment and impact, but painted a picture of structural gaps that still shape Africa's digital economy without expanding architecture.

"Let's be honest, [regarding] capital. It is what it is. Capital flows where systems work, where payment rules are reliable and where regulatory frameworks are clear and trust exists," she said. "Therefore, to attract more investment into Africa's digital economy, we must build the architecture that allows innovation to grow," Asante-Asiedu added. The second deputy governor said that architecture investment means having digital public infrastructure that supports identity and access, regulatory coordination that allows businesses to expand across the continent, and other key systems.

In Ghana, users can send money from a basic feature phone using an Unstructured Supplementary Service Data or USSD code, without needing Internet access, a smartphone, an app, or a data plan, Asante-Asiedu noted during her speech. She also said that this was one of the reasons for financial inclusion in Ghana. In its 2025 Global Findex report, the World Bank's database tracked how the world accesses and uses financial services, finding that Ghana's financial account and mobile phone ownership grew by more than 50% points, reaching 81%. The report also found that sub-Saharan Africa continues to have the highest rate of mobile money account ownership of any world region, at 40% of adults.

Despite this success, Temitope Osunrinde, executive director of Africa Hyperscalers, a company focused on the continent's digital economy, including digital infrastructure, said Ghana has marked a major milestone, "but it also marks a transition point." "The world has moved from access – it is now about infrastructure depth, control and value capture. Despite high connectivity, much of Ghana's Internet architecture still routes traffic and processes workloads externally," he told Connecting Africa. Osunrinde also believes Ghana must consistently provide attractive ways to attract digital infrastructure development.

"Regulators and policymakers such as the Ministry of Communication, Digital Technology and Innovations, and the National Communications Authority must actively incentivize infrastructure buildout through tax incentives, right-of-way reforms and support for local data center and cloud ecosystems," Osunrinde told Connecting Africa. He also said the next phase will require alignment across regulators, banks, infrastructure providers and energy systems.

Asante-Asiedu noted that almost half of the Ghanaian population is female, and the development of technology shouldn't happen without their involvement. In her address on Friday, Ghana's Minister for Gender, Children and Social Protection, Agnes Naa Lartey, called for digital infrastructure to reach women in rural and underserved communities. "I urge all stakeholders gathered here to reaffirm our collective commitment to building an Africa where every woman has access to digital tools and financial resources," she said, adding, "Because when women thrive, Africa thrives."
