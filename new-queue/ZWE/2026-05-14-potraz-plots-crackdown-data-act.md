POTRAZ is preparing to intensify enforcement against companies failing to comply with the Data Protection Act, with large-scale audits expected to begin in the fourth quarter of 2026. The regulator warned that company executives, including CEOs, could face criminal penalties, including imprisonment of up to seven years, for breaching the Cyber and Data Protection Act [Chapter 12:07].

POTRAZ Director for Data Protection Tsitsi Mariwo said the regulator had spent the past three years on awareness campaigns, training and voluntary compliance, but would soon adopt a tougher approach. "At the moment our approach is to ask institutions for compliance. However, from the 4th quarter, our approach is that we are going to enforce compliance, intensify audits," she said. "We have been using a carrot for the past three years, but in the fourth quarter of 2026, we will intensify audits."

The warning targets all organisations handling databases containing personal information for more than 50 individuals. Under Section 4 of SI 155 of 2024, entities processing personal data for commercial purposes must apply for a Data Controller licence (Form DP1), valid 12 months and renewable at least three months before expiry, and appoint Data Protection Officers (DPOs).

Mariwo said POTRAZ had already trained nearly 1,200 DPOs, providing "the critical mass of skills needed" for implementation aligned with National Development Strategy 1 and 2. The law applies to any institution processing personally identifiable information — names, national ID numbers, IP addresses, banking and financial records, political opinions, religious affiliations. Organisations without an internal DPO can outsource to trained part-time professionals. POTRAZ, as Data Protection Authority under Section 5, can conduct audits, issue directives, and initiate criminal proceedings — including a jail term for the accountable CEO.
