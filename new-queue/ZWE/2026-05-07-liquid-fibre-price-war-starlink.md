An opinion-analysis piece on the shifting Zimbabwean internet market. For the past year the "Starlink effect" seemed unstoppable, with dishes appearing on rooftops across Harare and Bulawayo, but a counter-offensive from Liquid Home has turned the "internet war" into a numbers game, and the satellite giant now looks vulnerable in the city.

Liquid Home's Infinity range built a defensive wall around the urban consumer: the Infinity 99 package offers truly uncapped fibre at 500Mbps for US$99, undercutting Starlink's US$100 Priority plan. Starlink urban users face a 1TB data cap and "deprioritisation" during peak hours, while fibre users enjoy unrestricted speeds immune to satellite congestion.

Data from the POTRAZ Q4 2025 report showed Starlink's traffic surged over 42% while Liquid's fixed internet traffic fell more than 10%, as heavy users (gamers, streamers, work-from-home professionals) defected. Liquid leveraged its infrastructure stability (over 86,000 subscriptions) to offer a "power user" experience satellite cannot match in high-density zones. In major cities Starlink's residential tiers are frequently at capacity, forcing new sign-ups onto expensive Priority plans. POTRAZ observers note the competition has ended the "monopoly tax" on Zimbabwean internet. The verdict depends on location: for rural schools and off-grid lodges Starlink remains king, but in Harare and Bulawayo suburbs the tide is turning back toward fibre for heavy users.
