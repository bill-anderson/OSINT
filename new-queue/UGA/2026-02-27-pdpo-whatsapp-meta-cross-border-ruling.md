Note: opinion/legal-analysis column (Joel Basoga and Baraka A. Wanyanga) reporting a PDPO ruling; treat as analysis of a primary decision. On February 20, 2026, Uganda's Personal Data Protection Office (PDPO) delivered a landmark ruling against WhatsApp LLC and parent Meta Platforms Inc., with significant implications for intra-group and cross-border data transfers.

The case stemmed from a complaint by AdLegal Uganda challenging WhatsApp's 2021 privacy policy, alleging it permitted sharing data of ~10 million Ugandan users with Meta without freely given, informed, explicit consent; collected excessive data; offered weaker protections to Ugandans; and transferred data abroad without adequate safeguards. The PDPO upheld the core complaints, finding the January 2021 policy in breach of the Data Protection and Privacy Act — lawful for core messaging/security but violating data minimisation for analytics and ecosystem integration, and lacking a clear link between data categories and purposes/legal bases. It found cross-border transfers done without sufficient evidence of statutory compliance.

The PDPO joined Meta to the proceedings based on the factual data-sharing ecosystem, creating an apparent exception to separate corporate personality. Building on Ssekamwa v Google LLC, it reaffirmed that recipient countries must offer equivalent protections, and newly defined "material harm" to include harms to autonomy, self-determination and dignity. The decision remains subject to appeal but warns multinationals to tailor privacy policies to Ugandan law.
