**Africa is rapidly moving beyond basic connectivity. A strategic shift toward localized high-density compute and “sovereign AI” is currently reshaping the continent’s economic architecture, backed by a projected $20 billion investment surge.**

### Executive Briefing

The African digital landscape is undergoing a structural metamorphosis. The continent is pivoting from a reliance on foreign-hosted data to a “sovereign” model, where data is generated, stored, and processed within its own borders. This transition is fueled by the rise of **Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI)** and a desperate need to internalize the value of African data.

- **Capacity Surge:** Demand for data center capacity is expected to jump from **0.4 GW** today to as much as **2.2 GW by 2030**.
- **Capital Requirements:** Meeting this demand requires an investment of **$10 billion to $20 billion**.
- **Revenue Potential:** This expansion is set to unlock a digital value chain worth up to **$30 billion** by the end of the decade.

### Geographic Concentration: The “Big Five” and Emerging Frontiers

Infrastructure remains heavily concentrated in five key markets which act as regional pillars. However, the “digital frontier” is moving inland as secondary hubs emerge to support the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA).

**South Africa -** 40.7%  
Market ShareContinental leader; host to AWS, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud regions.

**Nigeria -** Fastest Growing (48% CAGR)  
Fintech-led demand in Lagos; aggressive enforcement of data residency laws.

**Kenya -** East African Nexus  
Integration with “Silicon Savannah”; leader in geothermal-powered “green” data centers.

**Egypt -** Mediterranean Gateway  
Leveraging proximity to Europe and submarine cable landings for sovereign compute.

**Morocco -** North African Hub  
$1.1 billion “Digital Morocco 2030” plan targeting public sector modernization.

**Emerging Frontiers:** Significant expansions are currently underway in **Angola**, the **Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC)**, and **Ghana**, where operators like Raxio and ONIX are establishing the first Tier III certified facilities in previously underserved markets.

### Key Players: The Architects of the New Economy

The African data center market is a battleground between established global giants, pan-African conglomerates, and localized specialists.

#### 1\. The Pan-African Giants

- **Africa Data Centres (Cassava Technologies):** Led by billionaire **Strive Masiyiwa**, ADC operates the most interconnected network on the continent. Through **“Project Mufungi,”** Masiyiwa has committed **$720 million** to build Africa’s first “AI Factories” powered by 15,000 NVIDIA H100 GPUs.
- **Raxio Group:** Specializes in emerging markets (Ethiopia, DRC, Uganda), focusing on “edge” infrastructure that brings data closer to the end-user.
- **PAIX Data Centres:** A fast-growing operator with key hubs in Ghana, Kenya, and Côte d’Ivoire.

#### 2\. The Global Entrants

- **Teraco (Digital Realty):** Africa’s largest carrier-neutral provider, Teraco serves as the primary interconnect hub for the continent’s internet backbone from its base in South Africa.
- **MainOne (Equinix):** Following a $320 million acquisition, Equinix is using MainOne’s West African footprint to integrate the region into its global platform.

#### 3\. The Hyperscale Surge

Global tech giants are no longer just using local providers; they are building their own:

- **Microsoft:** Recently announced a **$1 billion** geothermal-powered data center in Kenya.
- **Google Cloud:** Launched its first African cloud region in Johannesburg in early 2024.

### The Technical Challenge: Power and Cooling

AI workloads generate intense heat, making traditional air cooling obsolete. Operators are now transitioning to:

- **Liquid-to-Chip Cooling:** Essential for the heat loads of NVIDIA GPU clusters.
- **Renewable Energy PPAs:** Leading players are bypassing unreliable grids via **Solar Wheeling** and wind power agreements to ensure “five nines” (99.999%) reliability.

### Strategic Outlook

The transition to sovereign compute is the “oxygen” for the next era of African growth. As the **AfCFTA** creates a unified market of $3.4 trillion, the ability to process data across borders without relying on overseas servers will be the ultimate competitive advantage. For investors and policymakers, the message is clear: the African data center market is the foundation upon which all future innovation will be built.
